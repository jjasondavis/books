The Sixth Battle of Acedia

The other day, I casually mentioned that I was thinking and you said that was very bad.

- Yes, I remember, I did say that thinking was bad.

Why?

- Because it is a pernicious habit which takes you away from What Is.

(Ramesh Balsekar, Experiencing the Teaching)

1.

Beginning with the obvious is like beginning at zero – a nice clean start.  And so I begin another epistolary foray into nowhere – as always, for my own obscure reasons – and, as always, without an addressee in mind.  Just another verbal canoe paddled out into the waves of semantic turbulence …  A pernicious habit of mine, you know …

A word of caution: I am absolutely Machiavellian in my desire to help you learn to not mind your mind.  I am willing to use any analogies, any metaphors, any scientific trivia and any psychobabble to help you see the so-hard-to-see irrelevance of our minds.  If all  ten of my fingers are not enough to point you in the direction of the Moon of What Is, I’ll use my toes.

2.

Here’s what is obvious to me: man won, nature lost.  (Of course, nature’s loss is man’s loss, too.  But, we don’t get it yet.  As a civilization – we, the modern-day apes, - are still toddlers.)

The problem is most of us live too long and too comfortably.  We live way beyond our evolutionarily calibrated expiration date, way past our procreative mandate.  This means that, aside from the legitimate battles of survival, we are all – eventually – doomed to fight a very peculiar, very human battle – a battle against meaninglessness – a battle that we need to lose not win, if we are to win the existential war against suffering.  Nature is the first casualty in this misguided battle for meaning.

Back in the day, when our sky-clad ancestors made their migratory ways up north across the sub-Saharan savannas, our life-spans and life-styles were proportionate to our modest biological mandate.  We were busy hustling and surviving.  In the Bronze and Iron Ages, for example, the life expectancy at birth, for a given cohort, was about 26 years.  Time was not yet on our side.  And boredom was largely unknown.  Hunger and fear – those were the foes we battled against; tangible, concrete, commonsensical foes.  As a species we were not yet lost in abstraction, we were nowhere near asking the existential questions that plague us today.  We were still in the Eden of timelessness, still faithfully married to nature.

Countless millennia passed and we eventually divorced nature and re-married the ever-cheating mistress of time.  By the Middle Ages, time was definitely on our side.  Childhood mortality – the merciless scythe that had been mowing down generations upon generations – was on the decline.  By the middle of the 15th century, a male member of English aristocracy, who survived to the age of 21, could be expected to live to the age of 71![1]

These expanded life-spans made many of us into time-keeping silverbacks.  Our beards grew longer as did our shadows; and this newfound luxury of longevity introduced us to a new foe – the abyss of meaninglessness.  A battle-hardened soldier without another battle is a sorry sight to see.  And that’s what we were fast-becoming – evolutionarily engineered survival-machines – idling in the warehouses of time.  Born to kill, we didn’t know how to kill time.  We were coming to discover time as the ultimate and, admittedly, undefeatable foe.

3.

John Cassian, a Christian monk and theologian, while cataloguing the challenges that beleaguer monastic life, described this battle against time as far back as the 4th century.  He called it “the sixth battle.”

“The sixth battle for us is what the Greeks call acedia which we can render as weariness or anxiety of heart.  This is related to depression and is a more pernicious and frequent enemy, greatly experienced by solitaries and dwellers in a hermitage, troubling the monk most greatly at about the sixth hour [noon], even like some fever, increasing at an appointed time, it brings the most burning waves of its approach at customary fixed hours on the diseased soul.  Hence some of the elders say that this is the demon of noonday.”

In acedia there is a sense of forlorn fantasizing, a sense of a better life and greener grass somewhere out there.  John Cassian writes:

[The monk] cries up distant monasteries and those which are a long way off, and describes such places as more profitable and better suited for salvation; and besides this he paints the [interaction] with the brethren there as sweet and full of spiritual life.”

This is a sense of missing out and wasting time, a sense of being existentially short-changed somehow.  In this pining for optimization we – as a civilization – glimpse the beginning of a transition from the priority of survival to the priority of fulfillment.

Cassian continues:

“And when [acedia] has taken possession of some unhappy soul, it produces dislike of the place, disgust with the cell.”

This – I find – is the most curious manifestation of acedia.  It is literally the dis-PLACE-ment of our dissatisfaction with time onto place.  These monks, embattled by the surplus of time, misunderstood their enemy as the place (the situation, the circumstance they were in).  

4.

Acedia is not just for monks.  It’s a secular matter too.  All of us are monks, all of us are solitaries, all of us hermits.  Each mind is a hermit of subjectivity.  Each mind is a place – a field of am-ness, doing a life-long solitary sentence.  Like the anchorites of old, who’d voluntarily entomb themselves into cells of worship adjacent to the walls of a monastery, all of us – the living minds – are encased inside our skulls, with no way out.  And sooner or later, definitely by the time we hit the middle age, we begin to confuse the foe of time with the enemy within. 

Self – you see – is a place.  You are more of a “here” than a “now,” more of a field of being than a moment of being.  And when you eventually run into the surplus of time, there is a good chance you will decide that you don’t like your self, i.e. the place, the conscious arena of your existence, and you will reflexively embark on some kind of “greener grass, better cell” self-optimization project; a mid-life crisis, by another name.

5.

Acedia – if we are to be semantically precise - is the Greek for “not giving a fuck,” i.e. a slothful lack of care.  But that is a somewhat narrow view of acedia.  Acedia is best understood not as carelessness, but as apathy – a loss of passion.  Like a medusian god-head, acedia grows with hundreds of self-cannibalizing dreadlocks.  Amongst them are weariness, listlessness, boredom, ennui, meaninglessness, despair, despondency, anhedonia, suicidality (particularly of the passive kind), nihilism, angst, insomnia, restlessness, sloth, indolence, loss of motivation, tedium, fatigue, lethargy, emptiness, disillusionment, melancholia.  And, of course, the infamously escapist and agenda-abortive mid-day nap. 

6.

Before too long I am going to tell you the story of the Sea Squirt.  It has many lessons for us humans.  But for now, I just want to float this idea – as an oblique kind of foreshadowing – as a metaphoric prop for a point that I will shortly make.  Why do I need to do that?  Because the point – arguably – is too shocking.  So, a warm-up is in order. 

And the warm-up proposition is this: mind is a leg. 

7.

[That “mind is a leg” is a point that I have made in many of my previous writings.  Reliably, this point remains overlooked.  Could it be that I am once again trying to massage this point into the collective psyche?  If so, I remain a hopeless fool.]

8.

Mind is a certain kind of leg.  True, it has no toes.  It doesn’t walk on ground.  Mind walks on air – on the groundless air of abstraction.  Mind is an information-processing leg that is in the business of evolutionary navigation.  In its primordial function, mind is merely a trip-wire that alerts the body to take evasive or predatory action.  Mind moves the body.  This e-motion (endogenously-generated motion) is the very first step of the mind-leg.

At higher levels of complexity, mind infinitely expands the menu of its survival dance-steps.  It becomes more precise. Mind informs the body as to the direction and velocity of the escape route and to the velocity and the direction of target acquisition. 

At even higher levels of complexity, “left” and “right,” “forward” and “backward,” “up” and “down” begin to lose their concrete physicality and acquire ephemeral idealism.  What used to be a specific coordinate in space becomes a vague goal in time.  The “left” and “right” may even eventually acquire totally disembodied meaning of morality and ideology.  That’s why mind keeps racing – the information-processing leg that it is, mind is in competition with itself.

I am not being metaphorical.  Rodolfo Llinas, a famous neuroscientist, proposes that mind is a kind of glorified movement system that has evolved to assist a multi-cellular organism with motricity (evasive and predatory action).[2] 

The mind – for all intents and purposes – is the body, and thinking is nothing other than action.  

Mind is in a constant state of becoming.  Notice the gerundive nature of this word. “Becoming” says it all: life is motion; it’s always in process, always in formation.  Not coincidentally, the word “emotion,” for example, is related to the word “motion.”  

Indeed, we experience emotions as some kind of inner motion: first, you feel one way; then, all of a sudden, you are moved in another affective direction.  

Mind is restless with urges and impulses.  And these words also tell the story of motion.  The original Latin word “impulse” means “push, shock, pressure.”  The word “urge” —also an import from Latin - means “to press hard.”  

Mind streams, presses, pushes, shoves, urges, moves and acts out.  Ever restless it keeps us up night and day.  

Mind battles against rest and that is its restlessness.  Rest is danger.  When at rest, when mind-leg is asleep, the body is vulnerable to predation.  

So, mind tosses and turns as much as it can, battling against the lethargy of acedia, ever afraid to slow down and to fall prey to the unknowns.  Acedia is a restless mind-leg syndrome. 

9.

Mind is a chatty leg.  At higher levels of organismic complexity, mind speaks symbols rather than signs.  Symbols are more strategic and ephemeral whereas signs are more tactical and concrete.

But language is language whether it keeps us close to home or carries us off into the far-away lands of abstraction.  Language is always in motion.  Your tongue, the extension of your mind, is also a leg.  Open your mouth and watch it move to see what I mean. 

Made for processing information, in the absence of real survival battles, the mind-leg paces the info-vacuum of its own skull-cell like a sleepless monk who is on the brink of losing faith, talk-walking and walk-talking to itself about what might be, about what lurks in the silence of the world … 

10.

Getting back to Cassian’s description of acedia, let us make a quick note of his reference to the “noonday demon.”  This was the time – literally mid-day – when an otherwise devout monk would begin to succumb to acedia.  It is a curious point, is it not, that there was such a pattern.  Why would the onset of acedia be so punctually on time?! 

The clue is in the biological hustle of living.  Each day we awaken to a kind of biological to-do list.  We have to take a piss and, if lucky, a shit.  Then we have to find something to eat.  Mornings keep us naturally busy.

Back in the prehistoric day, this morning “to-do” list might have kept us busy most of the day.  Think about the lives of other animals in the wild – not much downtime to sit on their hands (did you want me to say “paws,” my fellow modern-day apes?).

As we win the minor battles of early-day survival, we arrive at mid-day.  The hustle – if we are at all skillful – is mostly over.  We didn’t get eaten.  We got something to eat.  It’s time to wind down.  Nap time!

Body doesn’t mind this simple schedule.  But mind does.  Mind minds. 

To justify its existence, the mind-leg yearns for the desert of meaning, taking us on all kinds of restless detours. 

But the noon-day sun is too high in the sky to hide the long shadows of falsehoods and abstractions.  At mid-day the world is brutally illuminated and the neurotic disillusionment sets in.  We glimpse the meaningless hamster-wheel periodicity of our days and – so as to not deal with this meaninglessness – succumb to escapist afternoon naps. 

11.

The “noonday demon”  described by Cassian – to my mind – is the daily equivalent of a “mid-life crisis” – a crisis of meaning that can strike us at any day of our adulthood, and not just at that existentially frail biological age of our mid-thirties and forties. 

At any day, at any age, and at any time.  It’s not only the Sun that shines light onto our illusions.  So does the Moon.  Mind has a way of turning into a dervish at mid-night.  Particularly on a full moon.

12.

I bet you are by now tired of all this conceptual impressionism.  Probably jonesing for some hard facts, right?  F-fff-ine.  Let’s talk about the Sea Squirt. 

“The sea squirt is an ugly creature. In its adult form it has a tubular shape that resembles a sponge or worm, and in its larval form it looks like a tadpole. Still, the sea squirt is one of our most ancient relatives. Its primitive nervous system makes it more closely related to humans than the sponges and corals it resembles. Scientists say a sea squirt tadpole approximates what an early human ancestor - the very first chordate - may have looked like some 550 million years ago. In this larval form, it has a primitive spinal cord and bundle of ganglia that act as a functional brain. This tiny brain helps it move selectively toward nutrients and away from harm. Like most oceanic creatures, juvenile sea squirts spend their time growing and exploring the sea. Once the sea squirt grows to adulthood, it attaches itself permanently to a rock or a boat’s hull or pilings. It no longer needs to monitor the world as it did as a juvenile because the passing current provides enough nutrients for it to survive. Its life becomes purely passive. The adult sea squirt becomes the couch potato of the sea. In a surprisingly macabre twist, the sea squirt digests its own brain. Without a need to explore or find its sustenance, the creature devours its own cerebral ganglia.” (Stuart Brown, pp. 47-48, 2009).

Let me restate this curious little fact in my own words to make sure you didn’t miss anything here. 

So, there is this creature – a relative of ours, which is important.  It starts out just as we do, with the same neural architecture that we have.  It has a mini-brain – a cephalized (forward-looking) ganglion (tangle) of nerves (neurons), with a spinal cord extending into its body.  It uses its mini-brain just as we do – as a leg – to navigate from becoming a lunch to having a lunch.  The front-part (the mini-brain) directs the back part (the spinal cord) to direct the body (soma) to wriggle here or yonder, away from existential threats towards metabolic rewards; towards a propitious spot, hopefully somewhere on the side of a coral reef, where the creature will settle down – just like we resist to do in our middle-age – and continue on its existence in a vegetative mode. 

So, tadpole that it is, it putzes around until it finds a good enough spot and then it burrows into the corral.  Drills itself, head-first, into a corral!  And – get this – proceeds to digest its’ own brain. 

Why?  Because it doesn’t need it anymore.  How’s them apples, fellow mind?! 

So, following this self-decapitating metamorphosis, the Sea Squirt continues on as a kind of undulating stomach, a living pitcher that sucks in the nutrients in the water and siphons out the waste.

No brain required any further. 

13.

The moral of the Sea Squirt story? 

Mind is disposable. 

Mind (brain) is a leg, an info-processing leg, an organic GPS that has its contextual/situational utility, until it doesn’t.  And when it doesn’t, mind is to be kicked off like the shoes when you walk through the front door of your home, ready to veg.

14.

Now imagine that our adorably morbid Sea Squirt somehow decided to hold on to its GPS, just in case.  So, instead of just going on with its Zen-like vegetative existence, it would have preserved its mini-brain in case it wants to relocate someday. 

Let’s call this neurotic sea squirt a Woody Allen sea squirt. 

So, here sits the Woody Allen sea squirt, perched on a side of a corral reef, siphoning water in and out, with its monkey-mind going a mile a minute – “Is this a good corral reef?  Perhaps, we should have continued to shop around… What if the nutrient flow here is unsustainable?  We need to weigh the pros and cons of staying versus leaving …”

With all due respect, that’s what you and I are – Woody-Allenesque sea-squirts with a monkey-mind.

15.

This is the neurotic liability of acedia.  The Sea Squirt won the battle – it made it, it survived.  But the organic CPU inside its gelatinous body keeps chirping with neurotic hypotheticals.  Mind-leg is neurotically apace.

Mind-brain fears sleep because sleep is oblivion.  Sleep is info-processing entropy. When the mind-leg falls asleep it tries to tingle itself back awake with the paresthesia of dreams. 

Mind is afraid that if you don’t use it, you will choose to lose it.

Neurons – the microscopic cellular life-forms that collectively constitute your sense of “self” – don’t want to veg because they know that vegetables get by just fine without brains.

16.

So, let’s pan out for a second.  Let’s soar up to the altitude of a bird’s eye view. 

If we are to win the existential war against suffering, we have to learn how to not mind our own minds. 

Unlike the sea squirt, we do not have the anatomical option of digesting our own brains. 

But, not all is lost.  We do have options.  Just because we swallowed some information, it doesn’t mean we have to digest it.  Just because something pops into our mind, it doesn’t mean you have to chew (ruminate) on it. 

We can let the mind-leg rest.  

17.

Let us now backtrack a bit and re-approach the problem in a slightly more methodical manner.  Let us re-examine the Sixth Battle in more detail, with more pixilation.  Then let us review the Cassian solution-paths out of this existential impasse. 

18.

I see several themes of acedia.  Several strands of it.  Let us systematize, but without the pedantic attachment to categories. 

19.

So, there is the “loss of interest” kind of acedia.  By this I mean boredom, ennui, tedium (in a psychic rather than physical sense), anhedonia (clinical slang for loss of desire/interest).  Apathy, indifference, saturation are other examples of the same. 

In some ways this kind of acedia is developmental; it comes with the tenure of time and maturity. When you’ve been around the block enough times, you begin to feel that life no longer excites you.  It’s a “lack of stimulation” kind of acedia.  There is an element of desensitization to this.

20.

The scary older cousin of the “loss of interest” acedia is “the loss of meaning” acedia. There comes a moment when somehow the neat little conceptual framework that you were operating from – suddenly – no longer makes sense.  You find yourself in a conceptual, philosophical, spiritual, ideological free-fall. 

Maybe something happened and you’ve been traumatized by a sudden onset of meaninglessness.  Maybe, paradoxically, by digging deeper and deeper into some conceptual niche you pop out on the other side of the dialectic and your own initial premises and axioms now seem arbitrary and ludicrous. 

Here, too, can be a developmental progression.  “The loss of interest” ferments into “the loss of meaning.”  First, you begin to notice that you are becoming a bit apathetic to what used to turn you on.  Maybe you try to spice it up.  But to no avail. And then it dawns on you that you are not just disinterested – it’s worse than that – you no longer see the meaning in what used to hold your interest. 

This kind of acedic (I’m using the word “acedia” here in an adjective sense) – this kind of acedic erosion spares no idols.  Anything is up for disillusionment – parents are dethroned, values are questioned, gods are pronounced dead, ideologies are discarded.  Even the possibility of truth may be once and for all undermined.

21.

Another way to organize the phenomenology of acedia is to examine it through a clinical, symptomatic lense.  Whatever its theme or origin, experientially acedia manifests as restlessness, insomnia, anxiety, angst, even panic; as melancholia or depression or even passive suicidality; or, perhaps, as what clinicians sometimes call “manic defense” – a defensive burst of activity – a mid-career shift, reinventing yourself spiritually (spiritual seeking), deciding that you absolutely have to have a second kid, moving across the country, getting politically active, etc, etc.

22.

Cassian’s description of acedia emphasizes the “loss of motivation” – he talks of sloth, lethargy, inactivity, indolence, passivity, laziness. 

I feel that he is missing the point.  First of all, it is a symptomatically narrow description of acedia.  Secondly, it is a misunderstanding of the cause and effect.  It appears to me that the loss of motivation is a) secondary to loss of interest and/or loss of meaning and b) is not necessarily a problem. 

I see this acedic passivity as a legitimate stage in the evolution of information processing.  Think of this passivity/inactivity as a kind of buffering.  An organism – I mean the modern-day sea squirt that you and I are – hits the pause button, goes into a kind of contemplative hibernation, so as to re-orient oneself. 

I see depression the same way.  (And I share this non-pathologizing point of view with Sheri Huber, a humanistic Zen teacher who skillfully reframes depression from being seen as a problem to being seen as a solution, as an opportunity for understanding oneself).

23.

But, John Cassian was right to ring the bell of alarm.  If left unaddressed, acedia is acidic and toxic.  It erodes the experience of life.  It poisons the simple pleasures of the body with the need for some bigger-picture justification.

24.

So what did John Cassian propose?  What are the solution-paths from this existential dead-end?

25.

Many folks have weighed in on this issue.  But I am only going to single out four – John Cassian, Victor Frankl, Epicurus, and Nietszche.  And I lump these four into the same category – into the overall category of Cassian solutions.  (In this case, I am using the name “Cassian” as an adjective.)

Cassian solutions – in my opinion – are case-specific, tactical, and therefore, at best, palliative.  They are cognitive, affective and behavioral.  They are – unwittingly – aimed at keeping the sea squirt from digesting its own brain.  They are conceptual, philosophical, and narrative in premise. 

Cassian solutions, in my opinion, are the restless thrashings of the mind-leg.  And, as such, they are not enough.  (We have to find a way to let the mind-leg rest).

26.

Cassian solutions are mind-heavy and mind-serving.  Cassian solutions are made by mind and serve the mind.  And in serving the mind, they once again fail the soma (the body). 

Cassian solutions try to win the Sixth Battle.  But I am convinced the Sixth Battle must be lost – if we are to win the war against suffering.  And that is the basis of my criticism of them. A different path is needed – a path of acceptance, a Zen path, a path of not minding the mind-leg.

27.

All opposition must be first understood before it is itself opposed.  So, let us now briefly understand the Cassian solutions on how to win the Sixth Battle and slay the ice-breathing dragon of sloth.

28.

John Cassian’s main solution to the “noonday demon” of acedia – and by extension, to the mid-life crisis phenomenon, – is to stay busy.  Here’s how he puts it:

“That he is sure to be restless who will not be content with the work of his own hands.”

He adds:

“The cause of all these ulcers, which spring from the root of idleness” will be “heal[ed] … by a single salutary charge to work.”

Paraphrased, Cassian’s suggestion is all too familiar: whoever is idle, will feel restless, so get busy and stay busy.  Most readers will recognize this admonition as a Biblical meme that “idle hands are the devil’s workshop.”

This is immediately misguided.  To stay busy the monk had to have the interest and the motivation to stay busy in the first place.  To get busy, the monk would have had to somehow pull oneself up out of the bog of acedia by the bootstraps.  This is Nancy Reagan’s “Just say no (to drugs)!” empty sloganeering.  This is the oft-used and utterly useless call to willpower.  Much of our modern-day approach to working with substance misuse and addictions is predicated on this kind of misunderstanding of motivation.

The Cassian “get busy” advice misses the point.  It’s not that the monk lost the motivation because he stopped being busy.  No, it’s the other way around.  The monk stopped being busy because he lost motivation.  John Cassian doesn’t seem to get it: like a boot-camp sergeant, he prioritizes compliance. 

This kind of Cassian solution is an oversimplification of the matter. 

29.

We need to dig deeper into this motivational permafrost.  And we will.  But for now let’s run through a few modern-day examples of how we try to stay busy when we bump into the invisible wall of acedia.

The solution of course depends on the time-scale of the problem.  If we are wrestling with a fleeting moment of acedic restlessness, a simple band-aid of distraction would do just fine.  If, however, we got stuck in the quick sands of a bona fide mid-life crisis, we need a crane of a solution. 

On a micro-scale – say, in the evening or over the weekend – we might get busy cleaning or re-arranging; we might go shopping even if we don’t necessarily need to.  In this day and age of smart-phones, the distractions are all too readily available – so, we start playing Angry Birds or start thumbing through Facebook posts.  If you are old school, you might pick up a book or just turn on the TV. 

The distinction between getting busy and distraction, by the way, is only in form, not in intent.  Whether you are busy “fine-tuning” your privacy settings on Facebook or power-washing your porch steps, you are trying to distract yourself from the gaping emptiness of your downtime. 

As far as more protracted periods of acedia are concerned – say, a vacation or a mid-life crisis – we get busy on a macro scale.  (Yours truly, for example, with a few days to kill in this early spring of 2017 sat down to pen this little opus on acedia.  Yes, I did!  But there is no fooling myself: I know that at the end of the manuscript, the door of acedia is still ajar and I too will need to rest my mind-leg.)

Sometimes we go to extremes.  Just as you breathe a deep sigh of relief that your first baby finally managed to sleep through the night, you might surprise yourself with a desire for another child.  Is it because you liked the experience that much in the first place or because in some lightning-fast preview of your future empty-nest syndrome, you decided that it is not too early to start postponing that eventual separation from your offspring?  This kind of strategic preemption of separation anxiety might also be a manifestation of acedia.  (So, before you know it you and your partner are getting busy “trying” to get pregnant or filling out the adoption paperwork.)

Or, having succeeded on your first career path, you suddenly sully your success as a burnout (because you are starting to understandably feel a tad apathetic about what you’ve been doing for years); so you decide to reinvent yourself professionally; you get busy by re-enrolling in school; or you get busy redesigning your resume, contacting head hunters, picking others’ brains as to the alternative career paths you might take.  Suddenly, instead of deleting LinkedIn notifications, you are all about it, browsing for a better profile picture, scrolling through possible connections and making connection requests.  You know the drill … 

Or you might get busy by embarking on a spiritual search: you leave the church you are in, buy a handful of new books (probably on Eastern religions), look up Meet-up groups in the area, go on retreats, etc. 

Seeking (of any kind) is doing.  Seeking – for truth, for a better partner, for fulfillment, for meaning – keeps us busy and, therefore, distracted from the unanswered and unanswerable existential questions.

30.

Examples of “busy-making” are endless.  People move – they up-size, they down-size.  They remodel.  They landscape.  They reinvent.  Some get busy updating their emotional and romantic lives.  They seek intimacy.  They go to reunions to reconnect.  They end up having affairs, which naturally keep them quite busy.  Then they try to rebuild the burned bridges in couple’s therapy.  Some divorce, some remarry. 

31.

The “re-” prefix is the name of the game here.  “Re-” stands for repetition. 

We keep busy by going in circles – we recycle the old battles. 

Anything would do in the name of not losing the Sixth Battle.  And the samsaric cycle of suffering continues on.

32.

So, this is my beef with the Cassian solution of “staying busy.”  Mind-leg marching in place is no solution.  Sure, it has tactical value – keeps us distracted.  But strategically we are still walking in place in front of the invisible wall of acedia.

33.

But let us not throw the baby out with the bath water.  The Cassian solution of staying busy is palliative.  Palliative solution-paths are tactical and existentially necessary.  While a quick fix is not always better than nothing, sometimes a quick fix is a true life-saver.  A tourniquet is a great example: if you don’t stop the bleeding, you die.  A tourniquet of “staying busy” is the first intuitive tactic in the Sixth Battle against acedia.  But you’d do well to also have a post-palliative strategy up your self-help sleeve.

34.

John Cassian, with surprising humanism, also cautioned against harsh judgment of those who are idle.  In chapter XV of his Institutes, John Cassian speaks of “how kindness should be shown even to the idle and careless.”

Taking a hint from him, I encourage you to have mercy on those who fight acedia tactically, by “staying busy.”  Indeed, if mind is a leg, and if it is pacing up a storm inside its skull-cell, it makes sense to take it (mind) outside.  It’s just intuitive.  What is here to judge or condemn?  Walk the mind-leg a bit, restore the flow of life.  Go for a walk around the block.  Or for a more geographically expansive walkabout.  Take this mind-leg places.  Get it active.  No sin in that.  Whether you are brushing up on French before your trip to Paris or joining a gym, the info-processing leg inside your cranium could always benefit from a new learning curve to climb.  In this sense, a Cassian solution of “staying busy” is a perfectly okay tactic.  So, let the curmudgeon philosophers among us not scoff at Cassian tactics.

35.

Another Cassian (palliative) path is hedonism.  Associated, at least initially, with an ancient Greek philosopher Epicurus (370 BC), hedonism is a life that emphasizes pleasure.  A historically accurate Epicurean definition of pleasure is very different from the orgiastic, carnal pleasures that come to mind when we nowadays speak of hedonism.  But I will nevertheless use the attribute “Epicurean” interchangeably with “hedonic” in this context.

36.

To clarify: an Epicurean (hedonic) solution to acedia is another Cassian (tactical) path.  A good many of us, if not a majority, escape into some form of Epicurean self-psychiatry.  When we run into the invisible wall of acedia, when we suddenly lose interest and then motivation to hustle in life, we begin to palliate our angst with various forms of pleasure. 

37.

Eating is a common Epicurean solution.  And, as a solution, it is absolutely intuitive and relatable: since we feel empty, we try to literally fill up – we begin to overeat, we start stuffing ourselves, if not with meaning than with food.

Eating is a universal Epicurean distraction.  Feeling bored?  Raid the fridge, right?

Mindless, boredom-driven grazing or binge-eating is an effective tactical defense against the terrifying emptiness of the bodymind.  On the front end, any eating makes us feel better which is why we do it in the first place.  It is a basic pleasure, a rudimentary coping tactic. And on the back-end, overeating keeps us busy in a classic Cassian sense.  As we gain weight, we eventually try to lose it.  And in yet another brilliant displacement, we battle the weight instead of battling the acedia.

Not only does overeating fill us up, it also helps us literally numb out.  Overeating knocks us out – we become lethargic and drift off into sleep, escaping the existential questions that still plague us.  In a sense, overeating to the point of sleepiness is one sure way to finally park the mind-leg.

38.

Another Epicurean tactic that we turn to is substance use.  Drinking and drugging are intuitive coping tactics (that go on autopilot).  No need to pathologize these Epicurean self-medication short-cuts.

Booze and drugs are a form of intuitive self-psychiatry.  Instead of going to a psychiatrist or a primary care provider, we channel our primordial shamanistic self-sufficiency.  Indeed, back in the day when we were still hunter-gatherers, way before managed care and emergency rooms, every one of us was one’s own healer.  Each of us was one’s own shaman-shrink. 

So it is no wonder that when we feel bummed out by the sudden onset of emptiness or melancholia, we try to improvise a chemical self-help solution.  There is again an intuitive rudimentary logic here: if we are feeling low, we just need to get high.  And so we do: we try to drink away the angst, we try to lift our spirits by consuming spirits. 

Just like with overeating, over-using (of booze and drugs) creates a certain logistical fall-out – we lose relationships, drivers’ licenses, jobs, health and sometimes freedom.  All these consequences keep us very busy.  From trying to finance our chemical coping to trying to pay for the rehabs and legal aid, we get quite preoccupied with getting our lives back on track and we end up staying very, very busy, plowing through years, if not decades, of our life, right past the existential “window of acedia.”  While laden with suffering, these lost years are an effective distraction from acedic questions marks.

Beleaguered by all the socio-economic and legal mess of our chemical coping, we simply don’t have time to sit on the edge of the Nietzschean abyss and stare into the gaping emptiness of acedia.  We are – first blissfully and then stressfully – preoccupied; which is the name of the Cassian game.

39.

Another Epicurean classic is sex.  Mid-life is replete with affairs and sexual experimentation.  From sheepish trips to a neighborhood adult store to orgiastic swinging, individuals and couples chase sexual stimulation in an understandable intuitive attempt to “spice things up.” 

This makes perfect Cassian sense: if acedia is a kind of existential desensitization, a stagnation of saturation, then the re-sensitization must be the way out.  Sexual fulfillment is a logical, intuitive answer to existential emptiness.  After all, it comes with the promise of a connection, which for many is a source of deep meaning in and of itself. 

And sex, just like food and booze and drugs, comes with its own back-end Cassian bonus – it keeps us busy.  There is a tantalizing learning curve to climb here – from romantic literature and online porn to the sharing of fantasies to experimentation with sex toys and alternative lifestyles. 

Affairs and closeted sexuality are notoriously busy-making.  The resultant break-ups, the divorces, the custody battles, STDs – all this keeps us sufficiently busy to defend against the insidious creep of acedia. 

Some folks manage to live two or more separate lives in parallel, thus completely eliminating any downtime.  In 69-ing situationally every which way, these folks win the 6th Battle against acedia hands-down, by running out of time.  God Pan would be proud!

40.

Of course, there are many more pleasure-driven, Epicurian pathways here.  Some hit the yard sales with the zeal of a first-ever Easter egg hunt, some are on the lookout for half-off bargains, some collect stamps, some keep tweaking their Second Life avatars – the Epicurian menu-list is bottomless.  Any and all of this is good.  “Whatever works” is my mantra here.  There is an Epicurian, pleasure-filled escape for any of us.  But, no matter how idiosyncratic these pleasurable preoccupations may be, they still remain Cassian (tactical) in nature.  All these Epicurean roads lead to the same Cassian Rome. 

At the end of all these hedonic detours, the abyss of acedia still awaits us. 

41.

So, what else is there, aside from Cassian busy-making and Epicurean pleasure-chasing?  Ah, yeah, there is the Franklian path – the search for meaning!  A promising … but also inherently palliative tactic.  I know: many would disagree.  But I’d disagree back … So, let us – on the pages of this book – tug this rope of contention back and forth a bit. 

42.

Viktor Frankl, Austrian psychiatrist, holocaust survivor, founder of philosophical counseling called logotherapy, offered a solution in his book Man’s Search for Meaning.  His solution to acedia (although, to my knowledge, he never used the term) is “meaning.” 

Frankl once wrote:

“When a person can’t find a deep sense of meaning, they distract themselves with pleasure.”

Can meaning replace pleasure?  Can meaning help us win the battle against meaninglessness of acedia?  Maybe.  Maybe not.

43.

“Meaning” is a power-tool, a veritable jack-hammer to the Wall of Meaninglessness.  But it is inherently a Cassian tactic.  Searching for meaning is a perfect busy-making task for a mind-leg.  After all, meaning is an informational proposition, a mind-game, a ping-pong of self-talk.

Let me explain.  Meaning organizes the meaninglessness of data-points into an informational gestalt (a whole).  I’m sure you’ve seen the picture that, on one hand, looks like a chalice or a cup and, on the other hand – depending on how you position your mind – looks like two human profiles facing each other.  This is the magic of meaning – it turns chaos (an ancient Greek word for “disorder”) into cosmos (an ancient Greek word for “order”). 

For Frankl this mind-magic of turning shit into gold through a change of perspective was a life-saver when he was in the Nazi concentration camp.  Frankl found freedom in the choice of how to frame/narrate a given situation.  He won the battle of survival.  He lived through the Holocaust to share his existential know-how of meaning-making. 

But the battle for survival is very different than the battle against acedia. 

The Sixth Battle is a Mahabharata kind of battle of meaninglessness.  In Gita, the Indian epic, Prince Arjuna finds himself on the battlefield against his brothers and cousins.  He sees no point in this and asks his charioteer, who is an avatar manifestation of Lord Krishna, for guidance.  He is called to selflessly fulfill his duty as a warrior.  He is offered metaphysical reassurance that he (and the brothers he is yet to fight) are one with all that is.  He is asked for unquestioning devotion.

Frankl – as all of us – was devoted to his survival.  And meaning proved to be a powerful weapon in this battle for survival.  The Sixth Battle is unlike any other: it is not for survival, it is against survival.  The Sixth Battle questions the necessity of survival.  That’s the latent suicidality of acedia.  And the weapon of meaning here, I am afraid, can turn against oneself.

In sparring with Frankl here, I know I am barking up a sequoia of a tree.   (Yes, I am a cynic (Greek for “dog”), man’s best friend.  Ha!).

44.

This cognitively heavy, info-processing magic of meaning-making is, undoubtedly, a powerful Cassian distraction from the meaninglessness of acedia.   Frankl himself managed to turn the horrendous chaos of holocaust into an opportunity for meaning.  And that is the enduring appeal of logotherapy – meaning helps break through just about any impasse.  In Frankl’s own words, “Those who have a 'why' to live, can bear with almost any 'how'.”  True that!

But, dear Vik, what about when you just keep running out of “whys”?

This line from the mouth of Lord Krishna (from Bhagavad Gita):

“I am time, the destroyer of all; I have come to consume the world.”

There is a lot of wisdom in what this literary character, Lord Krishna, says to Arjuna, the warrior-prince.  Time is the destroyer of the world.  Time is the destroyer of “whys.” That’s what we are in battle with.  The Sixth Battle of Acedia is a battle against time.  And time has plenty of time to turn our new-found meaning into meaninglessness.  Again and again and again.  Time eventually blunts every arrowhead of meaning.

Survival battles are for time, against the deficit of time.  The Sixth Battle is with time, against the surplus of time.

45.

Franklian meaning-making solutions do have a longer shelf-life than behavioral Cassian busy-making and sensory Epicurean pleasure-seeking.  

Meaning, once found (or made), it is an enduring preoccupation. 

So, a Franklian solution is a step-up, an upgrade from collecting stamps.  No doubt.  But – the cynical dog in me goes woof-woof again – meaning is but a time-limited distraction from meaninglessness.   That’s one problem with meaning-making.  The other one – as I noted earlier – is that meaning-making is mind-made.  Good for the mind but not necessarily optimal for the body.

46.

So, while we are at it, let’s ask what are some of the classic Franklian distractions from acedia?  Political and civic activism, religious and spiritual seeking, philosophical and scientific inquiries are but a few meaning-seeking palliative solutions to the restless mind-leg syndrome of acedia. 

And, of course, all of these pursuits of the ultimate truths, all of these relentless battles for justice, and all of these secular and non-secular forms of star-gazing and navel-gazing are cliff-hanging mental constructions on the edge of the cosmic meaninglessness.

Woof, woof!  Yes, I am still barking at the sequoia of meaning – a tree we love to hug.

47.

While powerfully palliative, these endless meaning-seeking mind-games that we play with ourselves are actually meaning-making.  The Franklian oversight, methinks, is that meaning is never found but only made.  Meaning – a pattern-forming mind-magic – is thus never absolute, never truly objective, and, therefore, never truly satisfying. 

Meaning is a mirage.  

48.

Fact is mind is a subjective realm.  All truths are context-specific.  (An epistemologically sober reader will surely detect the tautological paradox of these two propositions and smirk with that type of chagrin that is best familiar to graduate students of philosophy). 

Put differently, mind is its own religion.  Knowledge and belief – subjectively speaking – are indistinguishable.  To believe is to act as if you know.  And to know is to simply believe that you do. 

Ancient Jainist epistemologists understood the limitations of certainty – thousands of years ago! – and expressed these inevitable uncertainties in the so-called 7-fold system of conditional predication, Syadvada.  The 20th century physical theorists, such as Godel and Heisenberg, re-discovered these limitations of certainty through Byzantine mathematical labyrinths. 

Meaning tantalizes with the promise of truth.  But truth itself is a mirage.  Each mind is its own system of make-belief truths.  Eventually, meaning – just like staying busy and just like pleasure – disappoints.

49.

The Franklian solution to meaninglessness is apophenia-on-demand. 

Apophenia is when you see a pattern that doesn’t actually exist – like the faces in the folds of window curtains that you used to see as a kid when you’d wake up from an afternoon nap and wait on your mom to come get you.  Apophenia is when you look at a random smattering of stars in the sky and you see the Big Dipper.  Apophenia is when the mind-leg finds itself a pattern-road in the midst of otherwise empty field of data.

We are all apophenics – pattern-seeking junkies that turn the shit of meaninglessness into the glitter of pattern and meaning. 

The fact is that there are no facts.  I think it was Bob Marley who said that, but I am not entirely sure. 

The “Nondual Reality of What Actually Is” is unknowable: in the ongoing role-reversing dance of the Subject and the Object, of the Knower and the Known, knowing and not-knowing are but degrees of subjective certainty rather than degrees of objective accuracy.

The possibility of absolute meaning is curbed by the epistemological event-horizon of not-knowing – which begins at the tip of our self-serving nose … 

50.

In terms of information processing, mind is always behind reality.  Reality is the input, mind is the output.  Reality is the stimulus, mind is the response. 

What this means is that mind is a phase-delayed echo of what no longer is. 

So, then what is the meaning of truth?  And what is the meaning of meaning?

51.

As I noted above, these Franklian pursuits of truth and meaning are efficient Cassian detours and delays on the way to the gaping edge of acedia.  Decades of meaning-seeking and meaning-making may pass before the final, unadorned, sobering realization of utter relativity of our treasured truths becomes painfully self-evident. 

Grief ensues.

52.

And that is the story of everything palliative.  All Cassian, Epicurean, Franklian solutions are but elaborate tactical delays of the emptiness that we finally have to face. 

Acedia – as I see it – is not a crisis, but an opportunity.  And I am not just rephrasing the fortune cookie here.  Indeed, the Sixth Battle is not to be won but to be lost!

Lay down the armamentarium of your mind, brothers- and sisters-in-arms!

53.

Let us return to the tale of the Sea Squirt.  If you recall, this distant relative of ours dispenses with its brain when it no longer needs.  Once the creature is done with its organic GPS, it simply digests its own brain (its own mind) and continues on as a kind of undulating bottle-plant, as a stomach, as a living gut in a headless body. 

This amazing metamorphosis tells us something very important: mind-brain is but a tool.  But – in our particular anatomical case – it is a tool that doesn’t want to be put aside, it is a tool that has learned to justify its own utility, a tool that has high-jacked the organism-at-large in search of ever-new projects to justify its utilization, and, thus, existence. 

Mind is a leg that is always in a search of a road.  We can keep going out for a Cassian promenada or we can finally sit down …

54.

The strategic solution to acedia is to learn to not mind the mind. 

You might say: simpler said than done.  Actually, it’s just the other way around: simpler done than said.

55.

You see, as an animal you are a multi-cellular colossus.  You might think and experience yourself as a continuous, experientially monolithic entity, a kind of oneness of being.  But you are not: you are a collective of trillions of stand-alone cells.  You consist of a great variety of cellular tribes – adipose (fat) cells, muscle cells, blood cells, bone cells and, of course, neural cells (aka neurons). 

So, you are not just your brain, not just the 100-billion strong collective of neurons that you are processing this information with and identifying with.  There is more to your body than your mind.

An organism is an organization – a multi-cellular collective.  This multi-cellular microcosm that you are is not a democracy.  The multi-cellular totality of what you are is high-jacked by a particular cellular tribe, neurons.  Neurons – the cells that, on my end, are guiding my writing hand and my thinking mind, and, on your end, guide your scanning eyes and your understanding, - are the cells to which I collectively refer to as “the mind-leg.” 

We – the neural cells that guide this writing (on my end) and guide your reading (on your end) – we are the very ones that I am right now inviting you to ignore.  We are the mind-legs that restlessly pace these solitary cranial walls that we inhabit.   We are the ones that stand in the way of our own wellbeing.

We have met the enemy and it is us.

56. 

You see, the problems of acedia are mind-made.  The body (the soma) that you also are – all of these other cellular tribes that constitute your collective being – your bones, your blood, your muscles, your fat cells – this body that your mind thinks it owns – this body couldn’t give a damn about meaning. 

Your body welcomes acedia.  Your body welcomes sloth and rest and passivity.  Your body is perfectly okay with losing the Sixth Battle.  Your body does not mind the pause of being between the battles of becoming.  It has won the previous five battles – it has survived – it is ok with vegging.  But not your head, not your mind, not the thinking you, not these neural charioteers that keep on beating the dead horse …

57.

Try this for a second.  Stand up.  Stand up tall, stretch out your hands, look up at the sky or at the ceiling.  And imagine yourself as a tree.  Why?  Because you are a tree, a tree on legs.  That’s what fauna is: fauna is flora on legs. 

That’s the whole miraculous lesson of the Sea Squirt: it starts out as an animal, processing information, and then dispenses with its CPU (central info-processing unit) and continues on as a plant, as a vegetable, processing food.

Minds process info.  Bodies process food. 

Life is a curious process: what serves what?  Are bodies in the service of the mind?  Or are minds in the service of their bodies? 

Minds think of bodies as servants.  But the Sea Squirt teaches us otherwise: minds are the servants.

Life processes information so as to have some food to process and so as to not become processed as food by some other life.

Flora gets it.  Fauna doesn’t.

You see, animals, unlike plants, are doomed to be on the go – our life strategy is to chase and not be caught; we pursue and we run.  And that’s why our neural charioteers are hardly ever asleep. They are ever kicking the sides of the body with their catastrophizing thought-spurs. 

So, stand tall for a second and imagine the possibility of a life-process without a sense of self, without an ego, without a mind. 

We are surrounded by such role models: trees, blades of grass, seaweed …

58.

The strategic solution to acedia, apathy, and angst is to not mind.

To not mind the very mind-leg that stomps its thought-feet when it witnesses the informational vacuum ahead. 

The strategy here is to stop taking your mind seriously, to start ignoring it.

This strategy calls for a special kind of mind – a no-mind!

It is a strategy of mindfulness. 

But what is mindfulness?

Mindfulness is mindlessness (not the mindlessness of inattention, but the mindlessness of attention.)

An empty mind pays attention; a mind that is full of itself pays no attention.

So I repeat: a mindful mind is a mindless mind.

A mind-ful mind is not full but empty.

A full mind is a closed mind.  An empty mind is an open mind.

Mindfulness is seeing mind as mind and redefining yourself as a no-mind.

Mindfulness is about not minding the minding mind.

Mindfulness is about not minding, about not becoming.

Mindfulness is about being with whatever is.

(Confused?  Good.  Confusion is a prerequisite to enlightenment.)

59.

Acedia for the mind is death.  Acedia for the body is life.  Mind – the leg that it is – wants to walk (think, feel, process info).  Mind is restless.  Body, on the other hand, is in the business of rest.  It is homeostatic, it seeks equilibrium.  Like a pendulum, body seeks a point of rest. 

Body likes to chill, to hang out.  It yearns for the hammock, not for the hike. 

So, let it.

60.

Learning to “just be” is the art of the body.  This art involves “not minding the mind.”  The art of “just being” is the strategic override to the mind’s restless obsession with “becoming.” 

The “know-how” of this is so simple that it is too complex to explain. 

The “know-how” of this is a non-know-how.

61.

The Sixth Battle of Acedia must be lost, not won. 

Once again, what I mean by this is that to win the war against suffering the mind has to learn to step aside and to let go of its information-processing habit of figuring out what’s next. 

Instead of fighting boredom, mind must learn to re-interpret it as a cue to relaxation. 

Instead of fighting the loss of ambition, mind must learn to re-interpret it as a signal of contentment. 

Instead of panicking at confusion, mind must learn to re-interpret it as a freedom from categories and certainties. 

Instead of self-medicating depression, mind must learn to re-interpret it as a restorative withdrawal.

Instead of taking itself seriously, mind must learn to ignore itself.

How do you do this?

By not-doing.

62.

Acedia is to be won through ahimsa – through info-processing non-violence of “letting things be,” without judgment. 

Ahimsa is an ancient dictum of non-violence.

Mind fears non-interpretive silence.  Non-judgment puts mind out of work.  So mind takes this info-processing inactivity as an existential threat.  Mind panics … and invents the specter of acedia to violently battle with.

63.

The life of mind is mind-on-mind combat: brain keeps scaring itself with its own thinking, particularly as it struggles to pierce through the acedic fog of ambiguity.

The Sixth Battle ends through internal pacificism not through internal resistance.  

You know how we say – in English – “Do you mind?”  Indeed, when asking for permission, we ask: “Do you mind?”  Put differently, the question is: “Do you object?  Would you resist?  Would you fight me on this if I were to proceed with such and such course of action?”

Mind itself is a field of battle. 

That’s all mind does: it problem-solves.  Mind is in the business of removing obstacles.  Mind is an informational, problem-solving bulldozer - a bully that turns onto itself in the absence of an obstacle.

64.

Metabolically, the brain/mind is an energy hog.  A fraction of your total weight, it consumes about 20% of all incoming energy.  Three fourths of this energy is spent on nerve signaling and the remaining quarter on cell maintenance.  What this means is that you (your brain-mind) are busy processing information.

Notice my anthropomorphizing above – I have equated you with your brain, you with your nerve cells.  The reason is so that I can make the following point very clearly: just like you don’t like to get a pay-cut, your nerve cells (which are you, for all intents and purposes) do not like to miss out on the metabolic dollar of glucose that they are earning by processing information.

Acedia – if unaddressed – from the brain’s perspective - is a potential metabolic calamity, a prospective loss of glucose.  Acedia is a threat of slowdown.  As we lose interest and passion, we not only slow down on a physical level but we also slow down on a cognitive, info-processing level.  As we become apathetic and disinterested, as we lose curiosity about the world, we shift towards a more passive mode of processing information.  Instead of thinking through problems and creating and learning, we veg out in front of TV and that sort of thing.

The body welcomes this.  But mind minds.

From a metabolic standpoint, less activity means less energy.  Apathy is a harbinger of atrophy.  If we don’t use our mind-leg, we lose our mind-leg – at least that’s what the mind-leg fears.  Mind (which is you) knows that if it were to allow itself to settle into quietude, it might become functionally irrelevant and would therefore incur metabolic losses. 

No lifeform wants that.  Mind (brain) is a microcosm of microscopic cellular lifeforms (neurons).  So, these little fuckers inside of us (which are us) go into battle – into battle against the potential acedic layoffs, into a battle for their metabolic turf. 

Mind spurns and tickles itself like a drowsy sentry, pinching its own cheeks to stay awake.

The mind-leg doesn’t want acedia.  Acedia is unemployment, a lay-off.  The mind-leg doesn’t want to run out of the road ahead.  It doesn’t want to go by the way of the Sea Squirt.  Mind wants problems more than it wants solutions.  Problems keep mind employed.  Solutions prompt it into retirement. 

So, mind paves its own road – with each racing thought, with each bout of angst – so as to justify its own metabolic employment.

And tenured it is!

65.

But mind’s fears of irrelevance are mostly irrational fears.  The overall mammalian organism is not going to let go of its own brain the way the Sea Squirt dispenses with its central-processing unit.  The mammalian blueprint understands that moments of lull are just that – the idyllic downtime that will inevitably pass. 

The mammalian body knows that there will be new monsters to run from and more edible game to pursue.  So, the body keeps the brain on board, just in case, on a kind of metabolic retainer. 

But brain – the resident neurotic – can’t accept these reassurances.  It yearns for problem-solving, meaning-seeking battles. 

Furthermore, insatiably, mind forges its neural real estate through info-processing warfare – by keeping itself busy and metabolically tenured.  Dendrites grow, axons add more myelin – the mind-leg keeps doing box-jumps in its own do-or-die crossfit battle for relevance.

66.

So, the post-Cassian solution to acedia is acedia itself.  The problem is the solution.  The emptiness, the meaninglessness, the disinterest, the apathy – this is the nirvanic freedom from the mind that we keep overlooking with our neurotic restlessness. 

John the Cassian wanted his monks to stay busy.  A mistake – a busy mind has little room for awe …

67.

The trajectory from neurosis to nirvana is a single, real-time choice to accept the info-processing pause. 

68.

But we – the neurons - get spooked by this info-processing vacuum and beat the battle drums of acedia, trying to oppose the very rest that we seek.

69.

This post-Cassian solution of “no-mind” is not to be confused with Nietzschean nihilism.  A Nietzschean solution to acedia is a kind of inverted Franklian solution.  Instead of solving the impasse of meaninglessness through a pursuit of meaning, a nihilist accepts meaninglessness.  Nihilistic acceptance of meaninglessness, unlike Zen-like path of “no-mind” is mind-heavy. 

Nihilists are, after all, philosophizers.  And philosophizing is just what the mind-leg does.  Philosophizing is problem-solving on a meta-level. 

The conclusions of philosophizing are irrelevant: philosophizing is philosophizing just like walking is walking, regardless of the direction. 

Nietzsche has this metaphor of a camel, lion and a child.  A camel-mind is a mind burdened by culture.  The lion-mind is the mind that courageously and, if necessary, violently shrugs off the cultural baggage.  The lion-mind frees itself from the info-processing ways of one’s culture and embarks on an independent search for truth. 

As the lion-mind realizes that all truths are relative, that there are no absolute truths, the lion-mind might then succumb to acedia – to a kind of intellectual sloth. 

But, according to Nietzsche, the lion-mind can avoid the sloth-mind by morphing into a child-mind – a mind that no longer seeks meaning but instead makes meaning, plays with meaning. A child-mind re-creates itself and the world in its own image.

This is a kind of Epicurean nihilism – an info-processing game not unlike the cosmic game of lila, described in the Vedas, played by gods.  A child-mind is a god-mind – and a mind-at-play is a busy mind.  The cosmic game of lila is a game of separateness in which the solitary god conjures up others to play with.  As the game is played, each namaste of recognition leads back to the original cosmic acedia of divine loneliness.  The lonely god  – one realization after another – meets oneself in its original totality of unaccompanied oneness.  And this is the fate of all running from the “Ineffable Emptiness of What Currently Is” …

So, I dare say, Nietzsche too misses the point: he fights acedia with info-processing play, which is still a form of Cassian busy-making.

The Vedic game of lila is a nice story of a mind-snake eating its mind-tail.

70.

A post-Cassian solution to acedia is a post-meaning mind, a no-mind mind.

The no-mind path is a wholesale rejection of all the questions that create the impasse of acedia in the first place.

Acedia begins with question marks: “What is the point?  What is the meaning of life?  What’s now?  What’s next?  What happens when we die?  Why am I unhappy?” 

A Cassian approach is to try to answer these questions – through action and/or through thought (which is also action). 

A post-Cassian approach to acedic self-inquiry is to un-ask the questions that mind poses to itself. 

The path least trodden is to ignore the mind - to not mind the mind that asked the question in the first place.

A post-Cassian approach is to stop playing the mind-games with oneself.  It’s a non-lila non-story.

71.

So, here’s the kicker: how can a mind ignore itself?  This is the methodological monster that’s been lurking in my propositions.  Indeed, to ignore anything you need a duality – of the Ignorer and the Ignored. 

Such duality exists within the organism that you are.

You see, you have more than one mind – a mind to ignore and a mind to do the ignoring.

To understand this, let us briefly revisit the tale of the Sea Squirt.  The Sea Squirt, to recall, had a brain, a spinal cord, and – oh the hidden treasure – the neural net around its abdominal area.  Same with you – you have a brain upstairs in your skull (CNS – central nervous system), you have a brain running through the length of your spine and branching out into each muscular nook and cranny that needs to be innervated (PNS – peripheral nervous system), and – surprise! – you have a brain in your gut, in the lining of your stomach.

Nowadays, the gut-brain has finally acquired a degree of formal recognition – a title of the Enteric Nervous System (ENS). 

The fact of the matter is that the second largest assembly of neurons is found in the lining of your stomach, in the ENS.  Upstairs, in your skull, you have about 100 billion neurons – a Tokyo of neurons.  In your gut, you have about 100 million – a Pittsburgh of neurons.  Consider these two locations Neural City States – the Athens and the Rome. The point is that you have more than one brain, and therefore more than one mind. 

The one upstairs (in your skull) is an I-mind – a mind with a sense of self.  The one downstairs (in your gut) is a no-mind – a mind without a sense of self.

The one upstairs – like the one in the Sea Squirt – is the mind-leg.  The one downstairs – in the lining of your stomach – is the mind-butt.  The leg walks, the butt just sits.  The mind-leg (the CNS) is restless.  The mind-butt (ENS) is resting.

So, when I say that the post-Cassian solution to the acedic restlessness is to ignore the mind-leg, it is an invitation to your gut-brain, to your mind-butt to stop paying attention to the restless mind-leg neighbor upstairs who is always tapping its info-processing feet, making it difficult for you – the gut-brain – to veg.

72.

John Cassian, in describing acedia treated it as a syndrome, as an open-ended episode of spiritual malaise.  Modern-day commentators of the mind describe mid-life crises in a similar manner – as a developmental stage of sorts, as an unfortunate existential detour that befalls the unsuspecting.  Psychologists and clinicians of the mind treat anxiety and depression in the same manner – as solid, uninterrupted lines on the psychic plane – as sustained states of mind.  All this – in my opinion – is imprecise.

Melancholia, angst, acedia are quantized into moments.  Everything pops in and out of existence.  Mind-states are no exception.  And the understanding of this opens up a possibility. 

What I mean is that spiritual malaise and psychological disorder are macro-trends that consist of finite moments.  A depressed mind is not depressed non-stop.  A period of angst might be punctuated with moments of calmness.  A period of spiritual restlessness might be interspersed with moments of divine ecstasy, however fleeting.  My point is this: the apophenics that we are, we tend to connect the data-dots into lines and then see only lines, without the original space in between the dots. 

The fact is that there is often a restful gut-brain moment between the restless strides of the mind-leg.  We have to notice it and lose ourselves in this vegetative space between the cognitive-affective mind-dots.  We have to learn to fall into this vegetative, non-info-processing gap of just being.  We have to learn to veg.

73.

Acedia is an unwarranted macro-description.  Acedia – be it angst or melancholia or loss of meaning – is a composite, an agglomerate of many acedic micro-moments.  And what connects these acedic data-points into a seeming line of impasse is the very mind-leg that seeks to organize data.  Mind, having stumbled upon a moment of informational vacuum here and there, begins to “see” the pattern: “I think I am getting depressed … Am I losing the will to live?  How come I am no longer interested in finishing this book, what’s wrong with me, have I lost my oomph for good?” 

These are the questions of becoming. 

Becoming begins with questions and propagates itself with questions. 

Questions are the hamstrings of the mind-leg.

Being asks no questions.  Being is its own self-evident answer.

The brain-mind is in the business of becoming.  The gut-brain is the business of being.  When these two minds find themselves on a collision course, the Becoming-Mind (the brain-mind) goes into battle with the Being-Mind (the gut-mind). 

The Brain-Mind feels superior to the Gut-Mind.  It thinks it knows better.  And it does because it is in the business of thinking and it is in the business of knowing.  The Gut-Mind just is.  Being is being.  There is no competition in it, no paranoia, no ambition.  So, the Mind-Leg stomps and kicks, fighting the vegetative acedia of just being.  But after this boot party, the Gut-Mind, the mind of being, remains what it has always been – a blissfully indolent big-bellied Buddha-mind.

To see that is to see the original face of what we are – living tubes of a certain aperture.  Once you mentally cut off your appendages, it becomes self-evident that a torso is merely a protective casing wrapped around the tube of the GI tract.

74.

Acedic moments – when neurotically connected into a pattern – become periods of restless acedia.  The mind-leg keeps tripping over itself, stepping on its own toes, heel-clipping itself into a self-pathologizing stumble. 

Acedic moments – when non-neurotically accepted for what they are – become periods of nirvanic rest. 

To the brain-mind, an acedic moment is a loss of time, yet another moment of forced becoming.

To the gut-mind, an acedic moment is a gain of timelessness, yet another moment of just being.

One and the same moment means different things to different minds.  To the brain-mind an acedic moment means loss of meaning.  To the gut-mind, an acedic moment means a gain of meaninglessness, an opportunity to veg on. 

In the battle against acedia, the brain-mind loses by winning while the gut-mind wins by losing.

75.

Cassian analysis is existential hypochondriasis.  All of this self-diagnosing is Cassian busywork.  When the mind begins to mind itself, it walks in self-referential circles of becoming.  An acedic impasse ensues. 

When the mind stops minding itself it straightens out into the line of being – into the living tube of being.

When a moment of acedia arrives, what mind will you choose to be?   Will you mind meaninglessness with your brain-mind or will you embody it with your gut-mind?  Will you fauna or will you flora?  Will you hustle on or will you veg on?  Will you chase your tail or reach out to the sky?

76.

The post-Cassian answer to acedia is letting go.  The answer is letting be.  The answer is not minding the mind.  No need to connect the dots.  So, you spooked yourself by looking into the Nietzschean abyss and it looked back into you.  Boo!  No crisis here.  Veg on.

77.

In closing, let’s talk about ambition. 

Ambition is a curious concept: inLatin it literally means “going around.”  The insatiable circularity of ambition is the quintessence of a Cassian detour.  There is going and there is going around. 

Life requires going.  Now and then, we have to get from A to Z.  Ambition is an optional going, a going in circles.  The reason why the ambitious are never quite satisfied is because there is always another lap ahead around the circle of becoming. 

That is the nature of a circle: a circle replicates itself, extending itself into infinity.  And therein lays the samsaric crazy-making of ambition. 

The path of ambition is a serpentine mountain road that is chasing an ever-vanishing mountain-peak of abstraction.  Let’s say you’ve progressed in your career to a certain point.  You ought to be content but you aren’t.  Why?  Because you see that the road ahead keeps on going and going, vanishing into the horizon.  And because it does, you persevere and go on, over the horizon, around the world, in circles. 

The circle of ambition is not an obvious, lateral circle.  If it were obvious, there would be no ambition in it for you.  Ambition is a tantalizing mirage.  The circle of ambition is always over the event horizon, taking us to the promised land of the invisible.  You think you are reaching the mountain ridge of perfection but somehow you are always only in the valley of it. 

The ambition of the brain-mind is to keep going.  The ambition of the gut-mind is to stay put, which is a non-ambition.  Any time you keep going, you are running the risk of going in circles, which is how people get lost in the woods.  Any time you stay put, you eliminate the risk of getting lost – staying put is staying home.  Home – the primordial field of “just being” – is the original sphere of non-ambition. 

Enso is a Japanese Zen symbol for enlightenment and clarity.  Enso is in the form of an unfinished circle – the only circle that doesn’t repeat itself.

Enso your way into this!

78.

Ambition is the Olympic sport invented by the mind-leg to keep the mind-leg always employed and metabolically reassured with new supplies of glucose.  Brain-mind needs places to go.  It’s always dressed up to the nines, checking its watch, worrying about missing out on life – on the very life that the gut-mind lives in total timelessness, while sitting at home in worn-out slippers and a ragged bathrobe.  

79.

Ambition is the enemy of contentment.  You already intuitively know that. 

Yes, ambition is an ally in the battle against acedia, a power-ram against the invisible wall of meaninglessness. 

But, if you are chasing contentment, the battle against acedia must be lost, not won. 

Rethink ambition and rethink acedia.

Realize that ambition – while an ally to achievement – is a foe to contentment.

And realize that acedia is not the bad news, but the harbinger of contentment.  It is the bugle call to lay down the arms and to leave the battlefield.  Acedia isn’t surrender but acceptance of what is.

80.

Contrast and compare the “going in circles” of ambition with the “just walking” of kinhin.  Kinhin is a walking meditation: walking for the sake of walking.  It’s just like the “just sitting” of zazen but on the go.  Unlike ambition, kinhin is impractical: it pursues no destination.  It is “just being” but in motion.

81.

A mind plagued by acedia rejects kinhin as pointless, just like it rejects zazen (the “just sitting” meditation) as pointless. 

The brain-mind minds time and doesn’t want to waste it.  The “just walking” of kinhin and the “just sitting” of zazen – acedically – are a waste of time.

The gut-mind welcomes pointlessness and turns it into one-pointedness of presence. Just walking in circles helps digestion, I am told.

82.

The samsaric paradox of suffering is this: there is no time without mind.  Mind is the source of time.  Time is mind in motion.  When mind stands still, timelessness ensues.  So, first the mind-leg inside your skull creates time through its own restlessness and then it panics that time is a-wasting unless you – the mind-leg – are getting somewhere. 

The gut-mind keeps no time.  It keeps up a peristaltic rhythm, like the undulating belly of a headless Sea Squirt.  But it has no illusions about what this rhythm is – it’s just a metronome for a belly, not a bellicose drum-beat.

83.

Get this in your bones: time is mind-made (brain-made).  There is no such physical substance or physical force as time.  The clock on the wall is not a measurement device: it has no sensors.  Unlike the tire gauge in your hands or the thermometer under your arm, the clock on the wall or the watch on your wrist are not measuring any external reality such as air pressure or temperature.  Time pieces are simply self-referential mechanical or digital devices that keep up an arbitrary rhythm, set by the way in place, by ancient Babylonian mathematicians who invented the numeral “base 60” (sixty seconds to a minute, sixty minutes to an hour).  Time is a perception of change (movement) against the relative background of the changeless (static).  Information-processing is time-keeping.  Each step of the mind-leg is a tick-tock on the mind-clock. 

Gut-mind wastes no time because it keeps no time.

The timeless are never late …

84.

Kinhin puts an end to the time-keeping drumbeat.  In kinhin, the mind-leg and the body-leg are not two.  And timelessness ensues.

85.

Of course, I am not literally encouraging you to get into the practice of kinhin or zazen.  My point is that pointlessness is its own point.  The dead-end of acedia is life itself.

86.

An acedic mind asks: “What is the point (meaning) of life?” 

Life is the point of life.  Life is the meaning of life.  Life is its own meaning.  Life is its own means to its own end. That’s it. 

Can it be enough for you?

87.

Time – to the extent to which it is life – is to be lived, not sold or traded. 

Time is to be wasted, not saved. 

Acedia – if welcomed rather than pathologized – is a gift of time, an opportunity for not-doing. 

Acedia is a gateless gate into the timeless nowhere of what currently is. 

Acedia is not an existential impasse but a passage into a moment of contentment.

88.

We – you and me - are coming to an end of this mind-to-mind encounter.  My hope is that at the end of this writing you are finally at a place to give yourself a permission to do nothing.  You are the eyes of the cosmos: you don’t have to look for something to see.  Just see.  And let the Sixth Battle rage on without you.

89.

Mind is a leg. 

Break it. 

For good luck.

Review

By Joseph Amato, PhD

Historian and Author of On Foot: a History of Walking, Surfaces, The Book of Twos, Everyday Life: How the Ordinary Became Extraordinary

The Sixth Battle of Acedia is a bold, indeed, brazen work in the service of cure.  It beams with clarity, and shines as iconoclastic work, ultimately arguing for body against mind, especially against the condition of over minding one’s mind.

The writer, Pavel Somov, Ph. D., psychologist, and author of a handful of notable self-help books, argues here that he will use any arguments (even of a Machiavellian kind, if necessary) in pursuing his end of freeing us from debilitating self-preoccupation and resulting depression, anxiety, etc.  He will empty us out of mind itself if need be so as to free and relieve us.

The book demands that a serious reader come to terms or carry out an inventory of his holding on such theoretical issues as the unity and disunity of body and mind, senses and intelligence, sources, forms, and uses of consciousness.  The book gives a multitude of grounds to the reader to help the reader empty his or her mind of nothing less than itself, and to value one’s being as an organism.

Its conclusion challenges its reader in search for escape from a mind-centered preoccupation, a debilitating narcissism and focus on self, or from what John Cassian, fourth century monk and theologian, described as monks’ sixth battle, which was nothing other than a battle against time with resulting acedia, which the Greeks defined as weariness or anxiety of heart.  

This is a fiendishly intelligent book arguing a case for letting ourselves go, giving up our supposed wants and problems and entering the flow of things and our organism free of constant cultivations of worried consciousness.

This book is worth a lyrical and thoughtful read.  I know of no short work, thirty pages, that is as challenging to self-assessment.  The book is in the genre of what might be called neurological Buddhism, contending against all therapies that only switch mind and its search for meaning to mind and another search for meaning.

Somov’s axiomatic approach disputes and defies all who find solutions in help and cure by more therapy of science, words, and new meaning for mind to continue its struggle with itself.

Somov questions psychology and science’s capacity to connect dots (events, incidents) into lines, and consequently to produce clear causes and inescapable ends guaranteeing truth and assuring explanation, prevention and resolution.

For Somov, we can’t think, reason, or believe our way out of our preoccupation with time (which is made a homogeneous bad) and mind’s obsessive preoccupation with it.

He has no truck with hedonist prescriptions, stoics’ insistence that we live by our duties, or Kantian categorically based morals.  Likewise, he sets aside religious conversions (not indefinable Buddhist illumination), utilitarianism, and all ideologies and political ideologies intent on organizing our minds and lives to an imagined good of the community.

Commanding us not to mind our own minds and suggesting we learn “to veg” with ourselves as organism, Somov’s message is to forsake mind as the source of our illusions, dreams, cares, concerns, worries, and the perplexing condition it sires.

Relying on Rousseau, he argues that mind’s unlimited claim to life and meaning of an organism is a result of civilization’s triumph over nature and body.  This triumph, a complex mental operation, does not yield freedom but carries the great aberration of the runaway brain, which presumes to subsume all.

Across “civilization” the brain acts like the prehistoric animal leg that dominates and becomes the whole of the creature.  This new brain, this all-inclusive leg born not of nature but of civilization, sponsors constant mindfulness that perplexes and denies any place for body and organism as a collection of different cells, neurons, and organs.

This new mind makes time the foe of self in wish, want, opportunity, and threat.  And for Somov to fight time is to lose, for time (a singular demon in his interpretation) divides place, experience, and awareness and, thus, dissolves the aspiring mind into divisions, contradictions, and disunities of self.  

In the end Somov would spare us worrying about time and save us from our self-preoccupied minds.  The brain’s sovereignty and hegemony, with all its neurons and consciousness, amounts for Somov to nothing more than one organ claiming to be all other organs and the organism itself.

In exchange for accepting his reductive neurological materialism, Somov offers a way to dump the centrality and prominence of mind and preoccupations with itself: its dreams, worries, anxieties, and depression.

This new type of body-centered Zen would empty us as an organism of the sick modern mind.  Our “sixth and modern battle” against acedia would be won, when the neurons of body’s other organs would overthrow the imperializing and hegemonic neurons of the civilizing brain.
