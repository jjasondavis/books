# Sorrow / Dejection (lypē) — a diachronic trace

**Research question.** How has the thought/passion of **sorrow-dejection** (Evagrius's *lypē*,
Cassian's "spirit of dejection," Latin *tristitia*) been framed across the Christian centuries,
and where did it drift? The thought is unusual among the eight in having its own **scriptural
fork built in from the start**: Paul distinguishes "godly sorrow" (2 Cor 7:10) from "the sorrow of
the world" within the very passage the ascetic tradition quotes to define the fault. Tracing that
fork — and watching it get replaced by a third, non-moral category ("melancholy" / clinical
"depression") — is the spine of this brief.

**Method.** Corpus-wide, case-insensitive grep of `**/reading/**/*.md` for sorrow, dejection,
sadness, sad, despondency, despair, heaviness, "cast down," melancholy, grief, mourning, and
"godly sorrow." The raw sweep is not a useful signal by itself here: it returns **~21,000
occurrences across 292 files**, because "grief," "mourning," and "sorrow" are common narrative
words in scripture, the Talmud, Josephus, the epics (Mahabharata, Ramayana), and classical
literature having nothing to do with the ascetical *passion* of dejection. The brief below instead
follows the **doctrinal vein** — the texts that treat sorrow/dejection as one of the eight/nine
besetting thoughts or as a named spiritual condition — and reads those in full. Key nodes actually
read: Evagrius *Praktikos* (`patristics/evagrius-ponticus/reading/praktikos-dysinger-english.md`,
4 hits), Cassian *Institutes* IX and *Conference* 5 (`patristics/john-cassian/reading/institutes-09.md`,
19 hits; `conference-05.md`, 9 hits), Athanasius *Life of Antony* (10 hits), Bunyan *Grace
Abounding* (36 hits), Watson *Doctrine of Repentance* (51 hits), Spurgeon *Morning and Evening*
(part of a 14-hit file), Barnes' *Notes on the New Testament* (881 hits corpus-wide, one dense
passage read), and William James's *Varieties of Religious Experience* (130 hits, the "sick soul"
lectures read closely). All quotations below cite a local path and line number; one witness
(Watson) carries visible OCR/transcription gaps and is flagged.

## The arc

### 0. The biblical fork, present from the first citation
> "For godly sorrow worketh repentance to salvation not to be repented of: but the sorrow of the
> world worketh death." — **2 Corinthians 7:10** (KJV, `scripture/kjv/reading/kjv.md:92971`)

Unlike anger or gluttony, sorrow is not condemned outright at the source — Paul splits it in two
in the same verse. Everything that follows in the ascetic and Puritan tradition is essentially
a commentary on how to tell the two apart, and everything in the modern drift is what happens when
a *third*, amoral category (clinical melancholy) displaces the fork entirely.

### 1. Desert root — *lypē* as one of the eight generic *logismoi*
- **Evagrius Ponticus, *Praktikos*** (c. 375–399; trans. Luke Dysinger, 1990;
  `patristics/evagrius-ponticus/reading/praktikos-dysinger-english.md:95`, ch. 10): "**Sadness
  sometimes arises from frustrated desires; but sometimes it is the result of anger.** When
  desires are frustrated it arises thus: certain [tempting-]thoughts first sieze the soul and
  remind it of home and parents and its former course of life... they take and dunk [lit. baptize]
  it in sadness." Evagrius's diagnosis is purely psychological-ascetical: sadness is the *residue*
  of a desire the monk failed to renounce cleanly — it is parasitic on an earlier, unmortified
  attachment, not a free-standing mood.
- Evagrius also names the demonic-military symptom cluster: sadness followed by "**anger and
  sadness and the final evil, utter insanity and madness, and visions of mobs of demons in the
  air**" (`praktikos-dysinger-english.md:123`, ch. 14) — and elsewhere links unresolved sorrow to a
  failure to master the irascible faculty (*thumikon*), noting demons exploit sorrow to make monks
  want to flee community life (`praktikos-dysinger-english.md:161`, ch. 22).
- **Athanasius, *Life of Antony*** (c. 356–360; `patristics/athanasius/reading/life-of-antony.md:101`,
  §36): dejection is listed as a diagnostic *symptom of an evil-spirit visitation*, alongside
  "fear in the heart, tumult and confusion of thought, **dejection**, hatred towards them who live
  a life of discipline, indifference, grief, remembrance of kinsfolk and fear of death" — contrasted
  a few lines later with the *joy, calmness, and courage* that mark a genuine visitation from God.
  Dejection here is not merely an emotion but a **discernment signal**: its presence indicates the
  enemy, not God, is present.

### 2. Cassian systematizes it — Institutes IX and the chain of faults
Cassian (c. 420–426, translating and expanding the desert teaching for a Latin monastic audience)
gives dejection (*tristitia*) its fullest ancient treatment.

- **Structural placement.** Cassian's Serapion enumerates the eight in order — "gastrimargia...
  fornication... philargyria... anger, **fifthly dejection, sixthly acedia**... cenodoxia... and
  eighthly pride" — and then explains *why* that order: "of these eight faults... the six former...
  have a sort of connection with each other, and are... linked together in a chain, so that any
  excess of the one forms a starting point for the next. For from superfluity of gluttony
  fornication is sure to spring, and from fornication covetousness, from covetousness anger, from
  anger, **dejection**, and from dejection, **accidie**" (`patristics/john-cassian/reading/conference-05.md:69`,
  ch. 10). This is a load-bearing structural fact for ChainBreaker: in Cassian's own causal chain,
  **dejection is acedia's immediate parent** — unresolved sorrow curdles into the noonday demon.
- **Institutes IX** (`patristics/john-cassian/reading/institutes-09.md`) is a whole book "Of the
  Spirit of Dejection." Its opening image: "if this... has secured an opportunity of gaining
  possession of our mind it keeps us back at all times from all insight in divine contemplation,
  and utterly ruins and depresses the mind... it makes it impatient and rough in all the duties of
  work and devotion... crushes and overwhelms them with penal despair" (:11, ch. 1). Cassian's moth
  simile: "as the moth injures the garment, and the worm the wood, so dejection the heart of man"
  (:17, ch. 2) — dejection doesn't attack from outside, it *rots from within*, rendering the soul
  unfit material ("moth-eaten... no longer any commercial value") for the "spiritual temple" (:23,
  ch. 3).
- **Etiology without external cause.** "Sometimes without any apparent reason for our being driven
  to fall into this misfortune, we are by the instigation of our crafty enemy suddenly depressed
  with so great a gloom that we cannot receive with ordinary civility the visits of those who are
  near and dear to us" (:29, ch. 4) — a strikingly modern-sounding description of causeless low
  mood, which Cassian nonetheless assigns to the enemy and to buried "seeds of faults" in the monk
  himself, never to an autonomous bodily/medical cause (:35, ch. 5; "the pains of disturbances are
  not always caused in us by other people's faults, but rather by our own").
- **The fork made explicit, with 2 Cor 7:10 as the proof-text.** "There is... another still more
  objectionable sort of dejection, which produces in the guilty soul no amendment of life or
  correction of faults, but the most destructive despair: which did not make Cain repent after the
  murder of his brother, or Judas, after the betrayal, hasten to relieve himself by making amends,
  but drove him to hang himself in despair" (:59, ch. 9) — set directly against "dejection is only
  useful to us in one case, when we yield to it either in penitence for sin, or through being
  inflamed with the desire of perfection... And of this the blessed Apostle says: **The sorrow
  which is according to God works repentance steadfast unto salvation: but the sorrow of the world
  works death.** 2 Corinthians 7:10" (:65, ch. 10). Cassian then gives the two sorrows opposing
  fruit-lists: godly sorrow is "obedient, civil, humble, kindly, gentle, and patient... rejoicing
  and feeding on hope"; worldly sorrow is "rough, impatient, hard, full of rancour and useless
  grief and penal despair" (:71, ch. 11). The therapeutic conclusion: "except that sorrow which is
  endured either for the sake of saving penitence, or for the sake of aiming at perfection, or for
  the desire of the future, **all sorrow and dejection must equally be resisted**, as belonging to
  this world... and must be entirely expelled from our hearts like the spirit of fornication and
  covetousness and anger" (:77, ch. 12) — i.e., unlike grief-for-sin, ordinary dejection is not a
  neutral mood to be endured but a passion to be *fought and expelled*, on the same footing as lust
  or anger. The cure Cassian prescribes is eschatological attention: "keep our mind constantly
  occupied with hope of the future and contemplation of the promised blessedness... not... depressed
  by present accidents, or over-elated by prosperity" (:83, ch. 13).

### 3. Medieval continuity (thin in this corpus)
The public-domain vault has no dedicated medieval treatise on *tristitia* as sharp as Institutes
IX — Aquinas's *Summa* contains scattered scholastic treatment (1,504 raw hits corpus-wide on the
broad term set, mostly incidental to other questions) but was not the load-bearing witness for
this theme the way it was for pride or anger; flagged as a gap below rather than force-quoted.

### 4. The Puritan peak — sorrow re-anchored to repentance, and despair named a distinct danger
The Puritans keep Cassian's fork intact but relocate its stakes: dejection is no longer chiefly a
monk's productivity problem but the layman's test of whether his repentance is real.

- **Thomas Watson, *The Doctrine of Repentance*** (1668; `tradition/thomas-watson/reading/doctrine-of-repentance.md:133`
  ff.): "it is not all sorrow evidenceth true Repentance: There is as much difference between true
  and false sorrow, as between water in the spring which is sweet, and water in the Sea which is
  briny. The Apostle speaks of sorrowing after a godly manner, 2 Cor. 7. 9... There are six
  Qualifications of it." Watson's marks of godly sorrow include grief for "heart-sins" — "the true
  mourner weeps for the stirrings of pride and concupiscence... though it never blossoms into act"
  (:137) — and *durability*: "Godly sorrow is abiding. It is not a few tears shed in a passion will
  serve the turn... true sorrow must be habitual" (:185), pointedly contrasted with "the Papists,
  who leave out the very soul of Repentance... torture their bodies, but their hearts are not rent.
  What is this but the carkass of Repentance?" (:187). *[OCR/transcription flag: this file is
  sourced from an Oxford Text Archive/EEBO-TCP transcription and contains visible gap markers
  (`[...]`) inside quoted sentences — the surrounding sense is clear and the quotes above are drawn
  from intact text, but the file is not a clean modern edition.]*
- **John Bunyan, *Grace Abounding to the Chief of Sinners*** (1666; `tradition/john-bunyan/reading/grace-abounding.md`)
  is the corpus's most sustained first-person account of *worldly* sorrow tipping into despair —
  Cassian's "penal despair" narrated from inside. Bunyan's despair is explicitly measured against
  the Cain/Judas/Esau precedents Cassian names: "hearing always the sound of *Esau's* fall in mine
  ears, and the dreadful consequences thereof" (:1820) and comparing his state to Judas's
  unpardonable sin, hair's-breadth by hair's-breadth (:1812–1829). Earlier: "these things did sink
  me into very deep despair; for I concluded..." (:1227), and later the diagnosis is compounded —
  "all these thoughts added sorrow, grief, and horror to me, as whatever I now thought on, it was
  killing to me" (:1804). Bunyan's despair, like Cassian's "other sort of dejection," produces "no
  amendment of life... but the most destructive despair" until grace intervenes.
- **Charles Spurgeon, *Morning and Evening*** (`tradition/charles-spurgeon/reading/morning-and-evening.md`):
  applies the Puritan pastoral answer directly to despondency as a recurring, nameable spiritual
  hazard — "You may have sunk low in **despondency, and even despair**; but if your soul has any
  longing towards Christ... " (:417); "O backslider, be **cast down**, but do not despair" (:1846);
  God as the one "that comforteth those that are **cast down**" (2 Cor 7:6, :3978); and the
  therapeutic reframe that recurs throughout Cassian's own cure: "the thought of the coming weight
  of glory will greatly lighten the present **heaviness** of trouble" (:4271).
- **Barnes, *Notes on the New Testament*** on 2 Cor 7:10 (`reference/barnes/reading/barnes-nt-notes.md:169655–169749`)
  gives the clearest post-Reformation dogmatic gloss on the Pauline fork itself: godly sorrow "is
  such as arises from a view of the evil of sin as committed against a holy and just God... That
  which leads to God. It leads to God to obtain forgiveness" (:169655, :169664), producing
  "permanent and abiding changes" (:169675); worldly sorrow is "sorrow which arises from worldly
  causes, and which does not lead to God for consolation," including "sorrow for sin or vice when
  it overwhelms the mind with the consciousness of guilt... but it arises wholly from worldly
  considerations, and does not lead to God" (:169698–169718) — and can, in its extremity, produce
  literal self-destruction: "under its influence men often lay violent hands on themselves, and
  take their lives" (:169741–169744), an explicit gloss connecting Paul's "worketh death" to
  suicide, the same endpoint Cassian illustrated with Judas.
- **Matthew Henry** on 2 Cor 7:10 gives the same fork in miniature: "godly sorrow worketh
  repentance and that repentance will be to salvation," distinguished from sorrow that merely
  "lament[s] the miseries that are coming upon them" without producing "universal reformation"
  (`reference/matthew-henry/reading/mhc2-joshua-esther.md:86818–86822`) — note this commentary
  entry sits, tellingly, in Matthew Henry's Old Testament volume commenting typologically on a
  penitential text, showing how thoroughly 2 Cor 7:10 had become the Bible's standard cross-
  reference for sorrow anywhere it appeared.

### 5. The drift — from a moral fork to a clinical continuum
The pivot point in this corpus is **William James, *The Varieties of Religious Experience*
(1902)** — published the same year as James Allen's *As a Man Thinketh*, the hinge text of the
"guarding the heart" brief's own drift narrative. James's lectures on "The Sick Soul"
(`self-improvement/william-james/reading/varieties-of-religious-experience.md`) re-describe the
exact phenomenon Cassian called dejection, using an entirely different taxonomy: not sin/grace but
*health/pathology*.

- James renames Cassian's "penal despair" and "gloom" as **"pathological melancholy"**: "there is
  a pitch of unhappiness so great that the goods of nature may be entirely forgotten... For this
  extremity of pessimism to be reached, something more is needed than observation of life and
  reflection upon death. The individual must in his own person become the prey of a **pathological
  melancholy**" (:4425–4427). Where Cassian located the cause in "the instigation of our crafty
  enemy" plus buried personal sin, James locates it in "**the neurotic constitution**" (:4432) —
  the same causeless, sourceless gloom Cassian described in Institutes IX ch. 4 is now a nervous
  condition, not a spiritual attack to be discerned and resisted.
- He coins/borrows clinical vocabulary absent from the entire patristic-Puritan tradition:
  "Professor Ribot has proposed the name **anhedonia** to designate this condition" of "passive
  joylessness and dreariness, discouragement, **dejection**, lack of taste and zest" (:4443–4446) —
  note "dejection" surviving as a *symptom label inside a medical taxonomy* rather than as the name
  of the whole moral-spiritual struggle.
- James explicitly frames the whole condition as a *type of person* rather than a *contest of the
  will*: "the sanguine and healthy-minded live habitually on the sunny side of their misery-line,
  **the depressed and melancholy** live beyond it, in darkness and apprehension" (:4165–4166), and
  invites the reader to "hear what the **sick souls**, as we may call them in contrast to the
  healthy-minded, have to say of the secrets of their prison-house" (:4177–4179). This is the
  precise inversion of Cassian's frame: Cassian's dejected monk is a combatant who can (and must)
  fight and expel the passion; James's "sick soul" is a natural kind, a constitution, something one
  *is* rather than something one *does* or resists.

**The drift, stated plainly:** Evagrius → Cassian → Watson → Barnes all agree that sorrow is
*bivalent by intention* — one kind saves, one kind kills, and the discipline is learning to tell
them apart and expel the lethal one. Cassian's whole ninth book is a triage manual for exactly
this. James's 1902 "Sick Soul" lectures quietly retire the fork altogether: sorrow/dejection stops
being a moral-spiritual object with two possible outcomes (repentance or death) and becomes a
*third* thing — a psychological state ("melancholy," "anhedonia," "the neurotic constitution")
that is diagnosed rather than discerned, and treated rather than repented of. The demon is
replaced by the nervous system; the desert's fifth combat becomes psychiatry's presenting
complaint. Both the Puritans and James use the word "melancholy," but Watson and Baxter-adjacent
writers (Bunyan, Spurgeon) still ask "is this sorrow leading you to God or away from Him?" while
James asks only "how far below the misery-line does this person's constitution place them?" — the
theological question is not answered differently, it disappears from the frame.

## Why this matters for ChainBreaker

1. **The chapter has a built-in, textually-grounded fork rather than a flat condemnation.** Unlike
   most of the eight thoughts, sorrow's own proof-text (2 Cor 7:10) hands the book a ready-made
   two-question diagnostic: *does this sadness drive me toward God or away from Him? Is it abiding
   in a way that produces change, or corrosive in a way that produces despair?* Cassian, Watson,
   and Barnes all supply ready primary-source language for both poles.
2. **Cassian's causal chain (gluttony→fornication→covetousness→anger→dejection→accidie) gives the
   book a structural argument, not just a topical one**: dejection is presented, in the desert's own
   logic, as the direct parent of acedia (the "noonday demon" / modern "burnout" chapter). A reader
   who has just read the acedia chapter can be shown its root.
3. **The modern "depression" framing is not a hostile invention to be debunked — it is a genuine,
   textually documented narrowing.** James (1902) does not lie about the phenomenon; he simply
   drops the fork and the agency. ChainBreaker's payoff line writes itself: the ancients never
   denied that some sorrow is a nervous/bodily matter (Cassian allows for dejection "without any
   apparent reason"), but they never let go of the second question — *and where is this leading
   you?* — that modern clinical vocabulary alone cannot answer and was never designed to.
4. **Bunyan's *Grace Abounding* is the book's ready-made worked example**: a real person, narrating
   in real time, testing his own despair against Cain/Esau/Judas exactly as Cassian's ninth book
   prescribes — primary-source proof that the ancient triage method was still operative and
   effective in a 17th-century layman centuries after the desert.

## Gaps flagged for this theme
- **No sharp medieval-era treatise on *tristitia* comparable to Institutes IX.** Aquinas's *Summa*
  almost certainly treats it formally (sorrow/*tristitia* as a passion, ST I-II qq. 35–39) but
  pulling a precise, well-cited passage would require a dedicated pass through a 1,500-page file
  organized by disputed question, not by keyword density; not done here to avoid a weak citation.
- **Watson's *Doctrine of Repentance* file has visible OCR/transcription gaps** (`[...]` markers
  inside sentences) — flagged above; the quotes used sit in intact spans, but the file as a whole
  should not be treated as a clean critical edition.
- **Burton's *Anatomy of Melancholy*** (the obvious 17th-century bridge text between Puritan
  "dejection" and modern "melancholy," and the single most natural missing witness for this exact
  theme) **is not in the vault** — a real gap, since it sits chronologically and thematically
  between Watson/Bunyan and James and would likely show the drift already starting in 1621.

## — Update 2026-07-24 — §6. The Philokalia, Climacus, and Evagrius's own rebuttals: the fork confirmed in its home vocabulary

Newly available source material closes the Philokalia gap noted above and adds two more primary
witnesses (Climacus's *Ladder* and Evagrius's *Talking Back*) plus one modern scholarly foil
(DeYoung). All four keep the same fork this brief has traced from 2 Cor 7:10 onward: the **demon of
dejection** (a passion to be resisted, fought, and expelled) is never confused, in any of these
texts, with **godly grief / joy-bearing mourning** (*penthos*, compunction — a gift to be
cultivated). The vocabulary differs from Cassian's Latin *tristitia* but the diagnostic is
identical.

### 6a. The passion of dejection, in the Philokalia's own words
- **St John Cassian, "On the Eight Vices: On Dejection"** (Philokalia English translation,
  `patristics/philokalia/reading/philokalia-english-complete.md:816`): "Our fifth struggle is
  against **the demon of dejection**, who obscures the soul's capacity for spiritual contemplation
  and keeps it from all good works... he leaves it senseless and paralyzed, tied and bound by its
  despairing thoughts." The same text supplies the fork explicitly, in language close to but
  independent of the Institutes IX rendering already quoted in §2: "It was this demon that did not
  allow Cain to repent after he had killed his brother, or Judas after he had betrayed his Master.
  **The only form of dejection we should cultivate is the sorrow which goes with repentance for sin
  and is accompanied by hope in God**... this 'godly sorrow' nourishes the soul through the hope
  engendered by repentance, and it is **mingled with joy**... But from the other kind of dejection
  we come to know the fruits of the evil spirit: listlessness, impatience, anger, hatred,
  contentiousness, despair, sluggishness in praying" (`philokalia-english-complete.md:826`). Note
  the Philokalia's version already anticipates the joy/gladness language Climacus develops fully
  below — godly sorrow is not merely tolerable, it is "mingled with joy."
- **St Nikitas Stithatos, *On the Practice of the Virtues: One Hundred Texts*, §60**
  (`philokalia-english-complete.md:12496`): the sharpest single-sentence statement of the fork in
  the entire corpus so far: "**Dejection is a passion that corrupts soul and body, affecting even
  the marrow of one's bones** — I mean that cosmic dejection induced by the transitoriness of
  things and often resulting in death. **The sorrow prompted by God, however, is extremely
  salutary**." One sentence, two clauses, the whole brief in miniature.
- **Glossary, "COMPUNCTION" (*katanyxis*)** (`philokalia-english-complete.md:16087`): the
  Philokalia's editors define the positive term formally: "The state of one who is 'pricked to the
  heart,' becoming conscious both of his own sinfulness and of the forgiveness extended to him by
  God; **a mingled feeling of sorrow, tenderness and joy**, springing from sincere repentance." This
  is the closest thing in the corpus to a technical Greek-vocabulary confirmation that the
  tradition's positive term for grief is definitionally joy-bearing, not merely joy-adjacent.
- **St Nikitas Stithatos §70** (`philokalia-english-complete.md:12523`) draws a further internal
  distinction worth preserving for nuance: "**Tears of repentance are one thing, tears that flow
  because of divine compunction another.** The first are like a river in spate that sweeps away all
  the bastions of sin; the second are to the soul like rain or snow to a field, making it yield a
  bountiful crop of spiritual knowledge" — i.e., even within the "good" side of the fork, the
  tradition further distinguishes penitential grief (cleansing) from compunction proper (fructifying).

### 6b. Climacus, *Ladder of Divine Ascent*, Step 7, "On Mourning Which Causes Joy" — the fork made into a whole step
Climacus (7th c.) devotes an entire step of the *Ladder* to *penthos*, and its title alone —
"mourning which causes joy" — states the paradox the whole brief has been tracing since 2 Cor 7:10.
(`patristics/john-climacus/reading/ladder-of-divine-ascent-english.md:349`, heading; text runs to
line 381).

- **Opening definition**: "**Godly mourning** is the somberness of the soul and the disposition of
  a heart in pain, always fervently seeking that which it thirsts for and, in failing to attain it,
  relentlessly pursuing it with effort, while sorrowfully lamenting its loss" (`:351`). Mourning here
  is framed from the first sentence as desire-for-God frustrated, not free-floating low mood.
- **The fork made explicit, in Climacus's own terms — dejection and despair named as counterfeits
  of true mourning, not the same thing wearing a different name**: "it is not given to some, who
  torment and condemn themselves in seeking it, with **sighs, dejection, sorrow of the soul, deep
  somberness, and despair**, all of which naturally fill the place of tears without risk, even
  though they consider it to be of no benefit to them... I am astonished even when considering the
  nature of contrition, how sorrow and so-called grief contain within them **joy and gladness,
  interwoven like honeycomb**. What do we learn from this? That such contrition is truly **a gift
  from the Lord**. There is no joyless pleasure in the soul when God secretly comforts those who are
  broken hearted" (`:371`). This is the passage that most sharply keeps Climacus's *penthos* apart
  from Cassian/Evagrius's *lypē*: dejection, sorrow of soul, and despair are explicitly the
  fruitless, joyless counterfeit that "fills the place of tears" without being tears — real
  compunction is recognized precisely by the joy woven through it.
- **The positive fruit, stated as a rule**: "**Whoever continues in godly mourning will never cease
  to celebrate daily. But whoever continually celebrates in the flesh will be succeeded by eternal
  sorrow**" (`:367`) — Climacus's own version of the "two sorrows, two harvests" logic already
  documented in Cassian, Watson, and Barnes.

### 6c. Evagrius, *Talking Back (Antirrhetikos)* — verse-rebuttals against the demon of sadness, capped by 2 Cor 7:10 itself
Book IV, "Concerning the Thoughts of the Demon of Sadness"
(`patristics/talking-back-antirrhetikos/reading/talking-back-antirrhetikos.md:1498`), is Evagrius's
own prescribed combat-technique against this thought: a scripture verse to speak back at the demon
for each specific lie it tells.

- **Against despair of being heard**: "1. Against the soul that, due to the sadness that comes upon
  it, thinks that the Lord has not heard its groaning: *The children of Israel groaned because of
  their tasks, and cried, and their cry because of their tasks went up to God. And God heard their
  groanings* (Exod 2:23–24)" (`:1502–1504`).
- **The final rebuttal of the whole book is 2 Cor 7:10 itself**: "74. Against the thoughts of
  sadness that come to us concerning transitory affairs, sinking the intellect into great affliction
  and killing it: *For godly grief produces a repentance that leads to salvation and brings no
  regret, but worldly grief produces death* (2 Cor 7:10)" (`:1840–1842`), closing with the doxology
  "Blessed is our Lord God and Savior Jesus Christ, who has given us the victory over the demon of
  sadness!" (`:1856`). Evagrius's own translator's footnote confirms the point directly: "Evagrius
  discusses these two types of sadness ('godly grief' and 'worldly grief') in *To Eulogios* 7; see
  also *Eight Thoughts* 5.19" (`:1878`, n. 21). This closes a loop the brief only inferred before:
  Evagrius — the source of the whole eight-*logismoi* scheme — used the *identical* Pauline
  proof-text as his own weapon of choice against the very thought he named first among the eight,
  a full generation before Cassian systematized it for the Latin West.

### 6d. DeYoung, *Glittering Vices* — modern scholarly framing, offered as peer/foil
DeYoung folds this theme under her sloth/acedia chapter rather than treating it as a separate
vice (Aquinas's list absorbed *tristitia* into *acedia*/sloth; Gregory the Great's list did the
reverse and folded sloth into sadness) — she narrates the swap explicitly: "Aquinas's list includes
sloth, not sadness; however, he defines sloth as a kind of 'sorrow'"
(`owned-library/glittering-vices/reading/glittering-vices.md:2275`, n. 47). Her own definition of
the ascetic term, given as a footnote gloss on Gregory's *Moralia*, independently corroborates this
brief's central claim that the patristic passion is not the modern clinical category: "**Sadness or
dejection (*tristitia* in Gregory's Moralia) was a technical term for the sorrow that followed
incomplete interior relinquishment of a monk's or nun's former worldly life and all its pleasures
and treasures. It should not be confused with what is now called clinical depression, or with grief
or sadness in general**" (`glittering-vices.md:2244`, n. 19). A contemporary philosopher, writing
for a general Christian audience and with no stake in the ascetic tradition's own apologetic,
reaches the same conclusion as §5's reading of James: the ancient category and the modern clinical
one are not the same thing wearing different names, and collapsing them is a scholarly error, not
just a rhetorical one.

### What this update changes in the brief's argument
Nothing in §§0–5 is overturned; this update **confirms and deepens** point 1 in "Why this matters
for ChainBreaker." The fork Paul states in 2 Cor 7:10 is now attested as load-bearing at every
level of the tradition this brief has vaulted access to: Evagrius uses it as his own final weapon
against the thought he named (§6c); the Philokalia's compilers preserve Cassian's version of it and
add Nikitas Stithatos's one-sentence summary and a formal glossary definition of the positive term
(§6a); Climacus builds an entire step of the *Ladder* around it, adding the sharpest available
statement that dejection/despair are *counterfeits* of mourning rather than the same thing under
another name (§6b); and a 21st-century philosopher restates the clinical-category caution
independently (§6d). The chapter now has, in addition to the Institutes IX/Watson/Barnes material
already documented, a **Greek-vocabulary anchor** (*penthos*/*katanyxis* vs. *lypē*) to put next to
the Latin one (*tristitia* godly vs. worldly) — useful if the book wants a moment of "the church
has never had just one word for this, and it never confused the two things either."
