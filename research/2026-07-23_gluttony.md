# Gluttony / gastrimargia — a diachronic trace

**Research question.** How has *gastrimargia* — "belly-madness," the first of the eight thoughts
in the Evagrian/Cassianic scheme — been framed across the Christian centuries, and where did the
framing drift? Gluttony is the traditional **head of the chain**: Cassian and Evagrius place it
first not because it is the gravest sin but because it is the *foundation* the others are built on
(starve the body's discipline and fornication, avarice, and the rest follow). Tracing it matters
for ChainBreaker's own chain-logic.

**Method.** Corpus-wide search of `D:\repos\books` (323 public-domain works) under
`**/reading/**/*.md`, case-insensitive, per term:

Raw total across the nine clean terms: **8,894 occurrences**. The tight, unambiguous core —

diagnostic terms (*appetite*, *fasting*, *temperance*, *abstinence*) run much higher because those
words carry other theological work too (temperance as the whole cardinal virtue, fasting as a

## The arc

### 0. The biblical anchor — and a startling detail
> "He is a glutton, and a drunkard." — **Deuteronomy 21:20** (the law of the rebellious son),
> KJV (`scripture/kjv/reading/kjv.md:17644`)

> "For the drunkard and the glutton shall come to poverty." — **Proverbs 23:21**, KJV
> (`scripture/kjv/reading/kjv.md:54981`)

> "Take heed to yourselves, lest at any time your hearts be overcharged with surfeiting, and
> drunkenness, and cares of this life." — **Luke 21:34**, KJV (`scripture/kjv/reading/kjv.md:84065`)

And the detail that every era below has to reckon with: the accusation leveled at Jesus himself —
> "The Son of man is come eating and drinking; and they say, Behold a man gluttonous, and a
> winebibber, a friend of publicans and sinners." — **Matthew 11:19**, KJV
> (`scripture/kjv/reading/kjv.md:77171`; parallel at **Luke 7:34**,
> `scripture/kjv/reading/kjv.md:82211`)

That last verse becomes a live theological problem for the whole tradition: if the discipline is
"guard against gluttony," how is Christ's own freedom with food and wine not gluttony? Every
witness below has to thread that needle (Tertullian does it by insisting Christ was *not* "such a
glutton and toper" as the Marcionites imagined; Ryle and the evangelicals lean on it to distinguish
liberty from license).

### 1. Desert & patristic root — the first thought, the whole foundation
This is where gluttony is not just *a* sin but structurally *first*.
- **Clement of Alexandria, *The Instructor (Paedagogus)*, Book II, ch. 1 "On Eating"** (c. 198;
  `patristics/clement-of-alexandria/reading/the-instructor-paedagogus-book-2.md:15-25`): Clement
  builds the entire chapter on an etymological unpacking of the Greek vocabulary of gluttony —
  ὀψοφαγία (*opsophagia*, dainty-eating), λαιμαργία (*laimargia*, "gullet-madness"), and
  γαστριμαργία (*gastrimargia*, "belly-madness") itself — before cataloguing the delicacies of
  the wealthy table and naming the "**belly-demon**" that drives it. This is the source of the
  very word the research question uses.
- **Tertullian, *On Fasting*** (c. 210–220; `patristics/tertullian/reading/on-fasting.md`):
  a Montanist polemic against the lax "Psychics." Tertullian ties gluttony to the Fall itself —
  Adam's sin **was** a sin of the belly (`on-fasting.md:19`) — and to lust anatomically, arguing
  the two appetites share the same bodily seat (`on-fasting.md:9`). He insists Christ, contra his
  opponents' caricature, was not "such a glutton and toper" as accused (`on-fasting.md:41`,
  answering Matt. 11:19 directly).
- **Evagrius Ponticus, *Praktikos*, ch. 7** (c. 375–399, tr. Luke Dysinger 1990;
  `patristics/evagrius-ponticus/reading/praktikos-dysinger-english.md:83`): the distinctively
  Evagrian, almost clinical framing — the gluttony-thought works by suggesting to the ascetic that
  *illness* will follow from too little food, weaponizing legitimate bodily fear rather than mere
  appetite. This is a psychological analysis, not (yet) a moral catalogue.
- **Cassian, *Conference V* (Abbot Serapion, "On the Eight Principal Faults")** (c. 426–428;
  `patristics/john-cassian/reading/conference-05.md`): names *gastrimargia* explicitly as the
  Greek term (`conference-05.md:21`), gives the **causal chain** — gluttony feeds fornication,
  fornication feeds avarice, and so on up through pride (`conference-05.md:69`) — distinguishes
  **three sorts of gluttony** (eating before the set hour, eating any food to excess, seeking only
  dainty food) (`conference-05.md:75`), and closes with the parable of unpaid **creditors**
  besetting the soul that never mastered the belly first (`conference-05.md:133`).
- **Cassian, *Institutes*, Book V** (c. 420s; `patristics/john-cassian/reading/institutes-05.md`):
  the practical monastic rule-book companion to Conference V — chapters 1–18 lay out concrete
  fasting measures, the danger of pride *in* fasting (a fault of its own), and why gluttony is
  treated first "as the first weapon" of ascetic combat (`institutes-05.md:1,6-14`).

The desert logic: gluttony is first in the list not because it is worst but because it is
**foundational** — an unmastered belly makes every other thought easier to fall to. This chain
argument (Conference V) is the direct ancestor of ChainBreaker's own "eight thoughts as a
sequence" structure.

### 2. Medieval systematization — Scholastic anatomy and poetic exempla

**— Update 2026-07-23 —** Gregory the Great now has clean English reading copies in the vault
(`patristics/gregory-the-great/reading/moralia-book-30.md`,
`patristics/gregory-the-great/reading/moralia-book-31.md`, Library of the Fathers translation,
Oxford 1844–50), closing the gap flagged at the foot of this brief. Gregory can now be quoted in
his **own words** rather than only at second hand through Aquinas's citation of him — and Book
XXXI turns out to be the actual pivot text from Cassian/Evagrius's eight thoughts to the medieval
seven-capital-vices scheme.

- **Gregory the Great, *Moralia in Job*, Book XXX** (c. 595–600;
  `patristics/gregory-the-great/reading/moralia-book-30.md`): commenting on Job 39:7 (the wild ass
  who "heareth not the cry of the exactor"), Gregory names the belly itself as "the exactor" and
  insists gluttony must be conquered **before** any other spiritual battle is even attempted: "we
  cannot stand up to the conflict of the spiritual contest, unless the enemy who is posted within,
  that is to say, the appetite of gluttony, is first conquered; because if we overthrow not those
  evils which are nearer to us, we doubtless proceed in vain to attack those which are further off"
  (`moralia-book-30.md:171`) — Cassian's "first of eight" logic, restated. He then gives, in his
  own words, the exact five-ways passage Aquinas quotes as *Moralia* XXX.18 inside the Summa (this
  edition numbers it paragraph 60, not chapter-and-verse): "the vice of gluttony tempts us in five
  ways. For it sometimes anticipates the seasons of want: but sometimes does not anticipate them,
  but seeks for daintier food. Sometimes it looks for those things, which must be taken, to be
  prepared more carefully; but sometimes it agrees with both the quality of, and the season for,
  its food, but exceeds, in the quantity of what is to be taken, the measure of moderate
  refreshment. But sometimes that which it longs for is even of a baser kind, and yet it sins more
  fatally through the heat of unbounded desire" (`moralia-book-30.md:175`) — followed immediately
  by Gregory's own gloss that "it is not the food, but the desire that is in fault" (same
  paragraph, `moralia-book-30.md:175`).
- **Gregory the Great, *Moralia in Job*, Book XXXI** (c. 595–600;
  `patristics/gregory-the-great/reading/moralia-book-31.md`): this is where the eight Evagrian/
  Cassianic thoughts give way, in Gregory's own text, to the seven-capital-vices scheme that
  dominates the rest of the Western tradition. Pride is named the root, and "seven principal
  vices, as its first progeny, spring doubtless from this poisonous root, namely, vain glory,
  envy, anger, melancholy, avarice, **gluttony**, lust" (`moralia-book-31.md:281`). Gregory then
  gives gluttony's own daughters in his own words — "From gluttony are propagated foolish mirth,
  scurrility, uncleanness, babbling, dulness of sense in understanding" (`moralia-book-31.md:283`)
  — matching the list Aquinas later formalizes as the "six daughters." Gregory explicitly pairs
  gluttony with lust as the two carnal vices closing the chain, and grounds the pairing
  anatomically exactly as Tertullian had: "it is plain to all that lust springs from gluttony, when
  in the very distribution of the members, the genitals appear placed beneath the belly. And hence
  when the one is inordinately pampered, the other is doubtless excited to wantonness"
  (`moralia-book-31.md:285`). He even voices gluttony's own self-justifying rationalization, put in
  the vice's mouth as it "exhorts" the conquered heart: "God has created all things clean, in
  order to be eaten, and he who refuses to fill himself with food, what else does he do but
  gainsay the gift that has been granted him" (`moralia-book-31.md:287`). This is the direct
  textual bridge between Cassian's *chain* (gluttony first because foundational) and the later
  medieval *tree* (gluttony as one of seven sprung from a single root, pride) — both agree gluttony
  anchors the carnal half of the list.

- **Thomas Aquinas, *Summa Theologica* II-II Q148, "Of Gluttony" (six articles)** (c. 1265–1273;
  `patristics/thomas-aquinas/reading/summa-theologica-complete.md:159245`): the fullest systematic
  treatment in the corpus (the file carries 158 "glutton" hits, 1189 "appetite" hits, 573
  "temperance" hits overall — Aquinas's *Summa* alone accounts for a large share of the corpus
  totals above). Key moves:
  - **Definition:** "Gluttony denotes, not any desire of eating and drinking, but an **inordinate**
    desire... it is evident that gluttony is a sin" (`summa-theologica-complete.md:159287-159291`).
  - **The five species**, quoting Gregory the Great's *Moralia* XXX.18 verbatim inside the Summa
    (now confirmed against Gregory's own text above, `moralia-book-30.md:175`):
    "The vice of gluttony tempts us in five ways. Sometimes it forestalls the hour of need;
    sometimes it seeks costly meats; sometimes it requires the food to be daintily cooked;
    sometimes it exceeds the measure of refreshment by taking too much; sometimes we sin by the
    very heat of an immoderate appetite" — summarized in the mnemonic verse *"Hastily,
    sumptuously, too much, greedily, daintily"* (`summa-theologica-complete.md:159447-159454`).
  - **Capital-vice status:** gluttony is capital because pleasure itself (not utility) is one of
    two things desirable for their own sake — hence exactly **two** pleasure-based capital vices,
    gluttony and lust (`summa-theologica-complete.md:159560-159566`).
  - **Six daughters** — unseemly joy, scurrility, uncleanness, loquaciousness, and dullness of
    mind — each traced to a specific faculty gluttony corrupts: reason dulled ("abstinence
    conduces to the penetrating power of wisdom," quoting Eccles. 2:3), appetite disordered into
    "unseemly joy," speech loosened into "loquaciousness," action loosened into "scurrility"
    (`summa-theologica-complete.md:159595-159619`).
- **Dante, *Divine Comedy*** (c. 1308–1320, tr. Longfellow;
  `tradition/dante/reading/divine-comedy-longfellow-complete.md`): gluttony gets its own circle
  and its own terrace. **Ciacco** lies in the freezing mire of Inferno's third circle
  (Canto VI, `divine-comedy-longfellow-complete.md:1273-1275`) — gluttony literally reduced to
  wallowing appetite with nothing left to distinguish one glutton from another. On the Purgatorio
  gluttony terrace, voices call out exempla of temperance and its opposite from a laden tree,
  invoking the drunken Centaurs at Pirithous's wedding and Gideon's disciplined soldiers of
  Judges 7 as the negative and positive poles (`divine-comedy-longfellow-complete.md:~11443`).

The Scholastic move is to turn Cassian's practical list into a **fully reasoned moral taxonomy**
(species, causes, daughters) — gluttony stops being just a desert-monk's occupational hazard and
becomes a general human vice classified alongside all others, systematized rather than narrated.

### 3. The Puritan peak — gluttony as slavery of the will, fasting re-grounded
- **William Gurnall, *The Christian in Complete Armour*, vol. 2** (1662;
  `tradition/william-gurnall/reading/christian-in-complete-armour-vol2.md:22629`): warns against
  a **"gluttonous fast"** — a memorable inversion where even the discipline meant to correct
  gluttony can itself become excessive and "devour the worship of God in her family for all the
  year after." The Puritan concern is proportion and heart-motive, not just the plate.
- **John Owen, *Of the Mortification of Sin*** (1656;
  `tradition/john-owen/reading/mortification-of-sin.md:2801-2818`): explicitly re-grounds fasting
  against the medieval-ascetic reading. "Cutting short the natural appetite, by fasting, watching,
  and the like, is doubtless acceptable to God" — **but only** with two limits: (1) bodily
  weakening is never good *in itself*, only instrumental; (2) fasting has **no inherent power** to
  mortify sin — "for if they would, sin might be mortified without any help of the Spirit in any
  unregenerate person in the world." This is a live Reformed course-correction *of* the ascetic
  tradition, not a repetition of it.

The Puritans keep gluttony as a real sin needing real discipline, but relocate the danger: the
desert/Scholastic era worried about excess of *food*; the Puritans equally worry about excess or
misplaced confidence *in the discipline itself* (the "gluttonous fast," fasting treated as
meritorious). Same vocabulary, sharper theological guardrails.

### 4. Evangelical continuation
- **J. C. Ryle, *Practical Religion*** (1878; `tradition/jc-ryle/reading/practical-religion.md:7298`):
  "the gambler and the drunkard, the covetous and the passionate, **the glutton and the
  sensualist**, are not free, but slaves. They are bound hand and foot by the devil." This is
  Cassian's "creditors" logic in Victorian dress — gluttony as bondage, not just indulgence.
- Ryle also repeatedly returns to the Matt. 11:19 problem noted in §0 — that Jesus himself "was
  called a glutton and a wine-bibber" (`tradition/jc-ryle/reading/holiness.md:10032`;
  `tradition/jc-ryle/reading/expository-thoughts-matthew.md:2864,9464`) — using it to separate
  Christian liberty in eating/drinking from the vice itself, exactly the move Tertullian made
  seventeen centuries earlier.

### 5. The drift — from sin against God to obstacle to vitality
- **James Allen, *As a Man Thinketh*** (1902;
  `tradition/james-allen/reading/as-a-man-thinketh-allen.md:282-286`): "Here is a rich man who is
  the victim of a painful and persistent disease **as the result of gluttony**. He is willing to
  give large sums of money to get rid of it, but he will not sacrifice his gluttonous desires."
  Gluttony survives as a named vice, but the *frame* has moved from offense against God to a
  self-defeating failure of self-mastery that blocks health and prosperity — consequence, not sin.
- **Wattles, *The Science of Being Well*** (1910;
  `self-improvement/wattles/reading/science-of-being-well.md:1370-1418,1715-1779`): performs the
  same move Aquinas made theologically — distinguishing **natural appetite (hunger)** from
  **disordered appetite** — but entirely secularized: "you cannot hunger for tea, coffee...; the
  desire for these things is an appetite," eaten "to gratify appetite, never to satisfy hunger."
  The Scholastic natural-appetite/sensitive-appetite distinction survives almost structurally
  intact, but its payload is now physical vitality, not virtue before God.

**The drift, stated plainly:** in Deut. 21:20 → Clement → Cassian → Aquinas → the Puritans,
gluttony is disordered desire that **offends God and enslaves the will** — the corrective is
fasting/temperance *as spiritual discipline*, aimed at the soul's freedom for God. In Allen →
Wattles, the same vocabulary (appetite vs. hunger, gluttony as a named failing) survives almost
intact, but the corrective is now aimed at **health, vitality, and self-mastery for their own
sake** — sin against the body's economy, not sin against God. The chain-logic itself is even
partly preserved (Cassian: gluttony opens the door to the other seven; Allen: gluttony as a
"disease" that blocks the mind's mastery of circumstance) — it is the *object* of the discipline
that has been swapped out, not the structure of the argument.

### 6. Modern edge — Update 2026-07-23: appetite reframed as wellness and willpower, not sin
Two contemporary owned-library sources let the drift traced in §5 (Allen, Wattles) be followed all
the way to the present day, into a fully secular wellness/behavioral-psychology idiom.

- **Jabez el Israel, *Life Matters, So Let's Eat Like It*** (2022;
  `owned-library/life-matters-so-lets-eat-like-it/reading/life-matters-so-lets-eat-like-it.md`) —
  a contemporary popular-wellness book, quoted directly (this is the author's own text, not a
  summary). Where Wattles (1910) still borrowed the Scholastic natural-appetite/disordered-appetite
  distinction, this book replaces it with an entirely different, non-moral binary: "The amazing
  thing is you could sum this entire book up in a couple of statements: Natural versus unnatural
  and high vibration versus low vibration" (`life-matters-so-lets-eat-like-it.md:68`). The stakes
  are stated purely in terms of vitality and mood, with zero sin-vocabulary: the book's own
  subtitle promises "A Priceless Guide to Attain Your Natural Body Size," and the back-cover
  framing names the goal as "our natural body weight, as well as our energy and clarity of mind and
  spirit" (`life-matters-so-lets-eat-like-it.md:15`), while the negative pole the book is written
  against is simply being "overweight, unhealthy, lacking energy, and/or unhappy"
  (`life-matters-so-lets-eat-like-it.md:58`) — a health/mood problem, not an offense.
- **Shortform summary of Glenn Livingston, *Never Binge Again*** (third-party summary; owned-library
  copy at
  `owned-library/summaries/shortform-summary-never-binge-again/reading/shortform-summary-never-binge-again.md`).
  **This file is explicitly flagged in its own header as a summary, not the book's own words** —
  "Summary of ... third-party book summary (getAbstract/ExecSummaries) ... NOT the book's own
  words — use to locate themes/positions, never quote as the author" (line 3). Everything below is
  therefore paraphrase of the summary's account of Livingston's position, cited to the summary, not
  quoted as Livingston. The summary describes the book's central device: uncontrolled appetite is
  personified as "the Pig," a "fat-thinking self" that "hijacks your survival drive" — but the
  summary is explicit that this is a deliberate cognitive fiction, not a claim about the soul: "the
  Pig doesn't literally exist as a portion of your brain — it's just a conceptual framework to
  separate your primal feelings that have derailed your most rational self"
  (`shortform-summary-never-binge-again.md:70`). Gluttony-as-vice survives only as a behavioral-
  control technique. Discipline is reframed instrumentally, toward autonomy and body-image goals
  rather than holiness — per the summary, the position is that "by having discipline on one part of
  my life, I'm giving myself the freedom to eat without guilt, live in the body I want, have the
  energy to pursue my goals" (`shortform-summary-never-binge-again.md:169`). Even guilt itself is
  medicalized rather than treated as conviction of sin: the summary states the book's position that
  "guilt is a natural healthy response to bad behavior, so you can correct it. You should not
  eradicate it entirely" (`shortform-summary-never-binge-again.md:230`) — guilt as a regulatory
  signal for behavior-correction, with no reference to God at all.

**What the modern edge shows:** by the 21st century, gastrimargia's entire inherited vocabulary —
disordered appetite, the discipline needed to master it, even guilt as a felt response to failure
— has been re-hosted in a wellness/behavioral-psychology frame where the referent is health, body
image, and personal goal-achievement. Wattles (1910) still kept the *shape* of the Scholastic
appetite distinction; by *Life Matters* (2022) the categories have drifted to "natural body size"
and "vibration," and by the *Never Binge Again* summary the vice itself has been reduced to a named
mental subroutine to be managed, not a moral reality to repent of — sin's grammar (a hostile
internal power to be named, resisted, and disciplined) surviving with sin's referent (offense
against God) fully removed.

## Why this matters for ChainBreaker

1. **Gluttony's "first of eight" position is not arbitrary — it's structural.** Conference V's
   chain argument (`conference-05.md:69`) is the load-bearing text: an unmastered belly is framed
   as the foundation the other seven thoughts build on. This gives the book a ready-made
   justification for *why* the gluttony chapter comes first, straight from the primary source.
2. **The Matt. 11:19 problem is a gift, not a liability.** Every era in this arc has had to explain
   why Jesus eating and drinking freely isn't gluttony. That tension (liberty vs. license) is
   exactly the nuance a modern reader needs — ChainBreaker doesn't have to invent the distinction;
   Tertullian and Ryle already did the work, centuries apart, arriving at the same answer.
   Aquinas's own definition — "not any desire... but an **inordinate** desire" — is the cleanest
   one-line resolution available (`summa-theologica-complete.md:159287`).
   3. **The Puritan self-correction (Owen, Gurnall) heads off a real pitfall.** A book teaching
   fasting/temperance as a weapon against gluttony risks re-creating the "gluttonous fast" —
   discipline that becomes its own disordered excess, or fasting treated as automatically
   meritorious. Owen's two limits (`mortification-of-sin.md:2806-2818`) are a ready-made guardrail
   to state explicitly.
4. **The drift is unusually clean and citable here.** Allen's and Wattles's texts preserve the
   *vocabulary* and even the *categories* (appetite vs. hunger) of the Scholastic tradition while
   completely re-aiming the purpose toward health/vitality — a compact, well-documented case study
   in how a moral-theological category survives its secularization.
5. **— Update 2026-07-23 — Gregory's *Moralia* XXXI is the missing link in the chain-to-tree
   story.** ChainBreaker's own framing leans on Cassian's *chain* (gluttony first because
   foundational, `conference-05.md:69`); Gregory's Book XXXI shows, in his own words, the moment
   that chain became the medieval *tree* of seven capital vices rooted in pride
   (`moralia-book-31.md:281`), with gluttony still doing the same foundational, appetite-first work
   at the base of the carnal half of the list (`moralia-book-31.md:285`). The book can now cite the
   actual hinge text, not just Cassian on one side and Aquinas's inherited mnemonic on the other.
6. **— Update 2026-07-23 — the modern-edge material (§6) gives the book its "so what for today"
   close.** The *Life Matters* / *Never Binge Again* pairing shows the drift traced in §5 is still
   live and ongoing in 2022-era popular wellness writing, not just a Victorian-era relic (Allen,
   Wattles) — useful if ChainBreaker wants a present-tense illustration of gastrimargia's vocabulary
   persisting after its theological referent has been stripped out.
