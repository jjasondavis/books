# Sin as sickness — the physician of souls, and the medicalization of the moral life

**Research question.** The tradition described the soul's disorders on a *medical model*: the
passions/vices are **diseases**, Christ (and, derivatively, the priest, abbot, or elder) is the
**physician**, repentance/ascesis/the Word is the **medicine**, and the goal is **healing** — Greek
*therapeia*, the very word "therapy" descends from. Modernity completed a drift in which that frame
went **literal**: psychiatry, the DSM, and cognitive therapy inherited the physician-and-patient
vocabulary wholesale but set its moral-theological content aside. What was once *sin* (an offense
against God, calling for repentance) is now *symptom* (of a disorder, calling for treatment); "the
physician of souls" narrows to the psychiatrist; the metaphorical "disease of the soul" becomes the
literal, codified "mental disorder." This brief traces that arc and asks what, precisely, survived
the trip and what did not.

**A caveat that governs everything below, stated once, plainly, so it need not be repeated at every
turn.** This is *not* the claim that mental illness is "just sin," that psychiatric treatment or
medication is unnecessary or suspect, or that any named disorder is a euphemism for a vice. Real
biological and psychiatric illness exists; treatment relieves real suffering; none of what follows
maps a diagnosis onto a person's culpability. The argument is about a **category shift** — moral to
medical — and about naming honestly both what the shift **gained** (compassion, de-stigmatization,
symptom relief, recognition that some suffering is not chosen) and what it **lost** (the
moral-spiritual register: culpability-and-grace, repentance, God, the *telos* of union with God).
The fairest single formulation, and the one this brief argues for: the ancients medicalized the
moral life only *metaphorically* — they had the frame, and fasting/bodily ascesis besides, but
essentially no clinical medicine for the mind; the moderns medicalize it *literally and completely*
— they have real medicine but, inside the clinical encounter itself, no theology. ChainBreaker's
position is *both/and*, not a rejection of psychiatry: the tradition treated the deepest human
disorders as spiritual *as well as* psychological, and a recovery of that "as well as" is the
argument, not a case against modern treatment.

## The arc

### 0. The dominical anchor — Christ names Himself the physician, and names the disease
Two verses carry the whole tradition's medical vocabulary in embryo, and both are worth reading in
the plain KJV before any patristic gloss is laid over them.
> "They that be whole need not a physician, but they that are sick." — **Matthew 9:12**
> (`scripture/kjv/reading/kjv.md:76917-76918`)

Christ's own self-designation as physician is addressed *specifically to the objection that He
eats with sinners* (9:11) — the medical model, from its first appearance, is inseparable from the
scandal of mercy toward the guilty, not a neutral clinical metaphor. The Old Testament supplies the
tradition's other load-bearing image, later mined by the Puritans (§4):
> "Is there no balm in Gilead; is there no physician there? why then is not the health of the
> daughter of my people recovered?" — **Jeremiah 8:22** (`scripture/kjv/reading/kjv.md:62001-62002`)

Note the logic in both: the disease is real (sin, national apostasy) and the remedy is *available*
(a physician exists, a balm exists) — the tragedy Jeremiah laments is not the absence of medicine
but the patient's refusal of it. That logic — real disease, available cure, the crisis is uptake not
supply — is the thread this whole brief follows into its modern mutation.

### 1. Desert & patristic root — the passions as disease, the ascetic life as therapeia
The desert tradition builds its entire vocabulary on the *literal* Greek sense of "therapy"
(*therapeia*, service/healing, especially of God) applied metaphorically to the passions.

**Evagrius Ponticus**, *Praktikos* (c. 380), names the eight generic thoughts as a diagnostic
catalogue at the outset (`patristics/evagrius-ponticus/reading/praktikos-dysinger-english.md:77`:
"There are eight generic [tempting-]thoughts (*logismoi*), that contain within themselves every
[tempting-]thought") and, strikingly, reaches for exactly the modern clinical word to describe how
the passions manifest outwardly — a translator's choice worth flagging precisely because it shows
how naturally the medical register fits this material even in a 20th-century rendering:
> "It is only God who made us who knows our mind directly and does not need **symptoms** to know
> what is hidden in our heart." (`praktikos-dysinger-english.md:269`)

**Evagrius**, *Talking Back (Antirrhetikos)*, treats each thought as a named ailment with a
prescribed textual remedy; tears themselves are pharmacology — "the shedding of tears is a great
**remedy** for nocturnal visions that are born from listlessness"
(`patristics/talking-back-antirrhetikos/reading/talking-back-antirrhetikos.md:2266`) — and the book
closes its logic with Job's rebuke of false comforters, "ye are all **unjust physicians and healers
of diseases**" (Job 13:4, quoted at `talking-back-antirrhetikos.md:2932`), i.e., even the desert's
own vocabulary knows the physician-image can be abused by a counselor who diagnoses badly.

**John Cassian**, *Conference* 19 (c. 426), gives the model's single most complete one-sentence
statement in the entire corpus — medicine and repentance named *in the same breath*, not as
alternatives:
> "To those who are really seeking relief, **healing remedies from the true Physician of souls**
> will certainly not be wanting... but with an humble and yet careful heart flee to the **heavenly
> Physician** for the diseases they have contracted... nor in their wanton heart reject the **remedy
> of penitence**." (`patristics/john-cassian/reading/conference-19.md:83`)

This is the ancient integration the rest of this brief measures every later witness against: *one*
physician, treating *one* disease, by *one* remedy that is simultaneously clinical metaphor
("healing") and moral-spiritual act ("penitence"). Cassian's *Institutes*, organized precisely as a
disease-by-disease manual of the eight faults (Books V, VII–XI in the vault; Book VI, on
fornication, is a numbering gap — see Gaps), even titles its chapter on gluttony's cure "Of the
measure of the chastisement to be undertaken, and the **remedy of fasting**"
(`patristics/john-cassian/reading/institutes-05.md:57`).

**John Climacus**, *The Ladder of Divine Ascent* (7th c.), turns the metaphor into the monastery's
own architecture. The community *is* a hospital:
> "I have seen someone come to a **hospital** for one need, and, being held there by **the
> physician's kindness**, they were cleansed and restored... Where the infection is great, there is
> need for greater treatment to remove the filth. For the healthy do not go to the hospital where
> healing is sought." (`patristics/john-climacus/reading/ladder-of-divine-ascent-english.md:197`)

The elder is repeatedly "a **healer of hidden bruises**" (`ladder-of-divine-ascent-english.md:121`)
and "**spiritual physicians**" perform "the cleansing of wounds" that "a skilled and knowledgeable
physician" alone can do (`ladder-of-divine-ascent-english.md:189`); confession itself is medical
disclosure, a monk "held onto the serpent's tail and **exposed it to the healer**," who then
pronounces the cure (`ladder-of-divine-ascent-english.md:275`).

The **Philokalia** compilation states the model at its most explicit and most theologically loaded —
God Himself practicing differential diagnosis:
> "By means of His prescriptions the **Physician of souls** administers the remedy according to the
> cause of the passions lying hidden in the soul." (`patristics/philokalia/reading/philokalia-english-complete.md:4474`)
> "Neither does God, when treating the illnesses of the soul, regard a single kind of **therapy** as
> suitable for all conditions but He allots to each soul what is suitable for it and effects its
> cure." (`philokalia-english-complete.md:5706`)

— the second passage is the one place in the vault where the English word "therapy" itself surfaces
inside a patristic text, naming precisely the concept ("a single kind of therapy... suitable for all
conditions") this brief's title trades on. Evagrius, quoted within the same volume commenting on the
Sermon on the Mount, names Christ outright as the agent performing the cure through the ordinary
ascetic disciplines: "Observe how the **Physician of souls** here corrects our incensive power
through acts of compassion, purifies the intellect through prayer, and through fasting withers
desire" (`philokalia-english-complete.md:238`). And the compilers extend the same diagnostic logic
to the ascetic himself, once cured: "The truly **physician-like intellect** is one that first heals
itself and then heals others of the diseases of which it has been cured" (`philokalia-english-complete.md:7687`).

### 2. Chrysostom — repentance explicitly named "the medicine of consolation"
**John Chrysostom** supplies the corpus's most sustained working-out of the physician image applied
to an entire congregation in real historical crisis (the *Statues* riots, Antioch, 387). Preaching to
a terrified city awaiting imperial reprisal, he names his own homiletic function as pharmacological:
> "As long as the sore of despondency remains, we will apply to it the **medicine of consolation**.
> For if in the case of bodily wounds, physicians do not give over their fomentations... Despondency
> is **a sore of the soul**." (`patristics/john-chrysostom/reading/statues-homily-06.md:9`)

Elsewhere in the same series he narrates Genesis 3 as a house call — God visiting the fallen Adam not
as judge-only but as physician-first: "He comes to him **as a physician comes to a sick man**"
(`patristics/john-chrysostom/reading/statues-homily-07.md:21`), concluding a few lines later with the
triple office that this whole brief's thesis rests on: "God is at the same time **a Judge, a
Physician, and a Teacher**; for as a Judge He examines, and as a Physician He amends, and as a
Teacher He instructs those who have sinned" (`statues-homily-07.md:25`) — judgment, healing, and
instruction held together, not the healing alone.

Chrysostom names Christ "the skillful **Physician of Souls**" managing even human folly for good
(`patristics/john-chrysostom/reading/complete-works-grapevine.md:3513`) and turns the image into an
explicit pastoral job description: "having become a **physician of souls**, put in practice every
mode of cure according to Christ's laws" (`complete-works-grapevine.md:5899`) — before, elsewhere,
quoting Christ's own self-designation from Matthew 9:12 verbatim as the ground of it all: "They that
be whole need not a physician... but they that are sick" (`complete-works-grapevine.md:5943`).

### 3. The scholastic bridge — Aquinas keeps the phrase intact
**Thomas Aquinas**, *Summa Theologica* (13th c.), shows the phrase surviving the move from homily to
systematic theology completely undamaged — it appears as a matter-of-fact gloss inside a technical
objection-and-reply, evidence the identification needed no defense by this point:
> "The **physician of souls**, i.e. Christ, works in two ways. First, inwardly, by Himself: and thus
> He prepares man's will so that it wills good and hates evil."
> (`patristics/thomas-aquinas/reading/summa-theologica-complete.md:212753`)

### 4. Reformation / Puritan peak — the Great Physician and the balm of Gilead
**Richard Baxter** is, in his own century's idiom, a working physician of souls, and *The Reformed
Pastor* argues the pastoral office should be organized exactly like a medical practice confronting
an epidemic:
> "If you will undertake to be the **physician of an hospital**, or to a whole town that is
> **infected with the plague**... there is no bearing with your remissness."
> (`tradition/richard-baxter/reading/reformed-pastor.md:256`)
> "A minister is not to be merely a public preacher, but to be known as **a counsellor for their
> souls, as the physician is for their bodies**." (`reformed-pastor.md:1995`)
> "He is unworthy to be a physician, who will be driven away from a[patient's bedside, and neglect
> his] cure." (`reformed-pastor.md:2790`)

*Call to the Unconverted* applies the same image directly to Christ, twice, in language that folds
the clinical and the salvific into one sentence exactly as Cassian did thirteen centuries earlier:
> "The entertainment of Christ by Faith, who is **the physician of the soul**."
> (`tradition/richard-baxter/reading/call-to-the-unconverted.md:1192`)
> "Because he is become **the physician of souls**, and is able to save to the utmost all that come
> to God by him." (`call-to-the-unconverted.md:3878`)

**Thomas Brooks**, *Precious Remedies Against Satan's Devices* (1652; OCR-flagged, verified against
surrounding text), organizes an entire book on the literal device-and-remedy structure Evagrius
pioneered — each of Satan's temptation-"devices" is met with a numbered, prescription-style
"Remedy": e.g. "**Remedy (1)**. First, Keep at the greatest distance from sin"
(`tradition/thomas-brooks/reading/precious-remedies-against-satans-devices.md:655`), a formula
repeated (with the identical "Remedy (1). The first remedy..." header) at least ten more times across
the book. This is the Puritan era's own closest structural echo of Evagrius's antirrhetic handbook
(see the companion antirrhetic-method brief), run through the vocabulary of clinical prescription
rather than scriptural citation alone.

### 5. The drift begins — Beck's title performs the reclassification
**Aaron T. Beck, M.D.**, *Cognitive Therapy and the Emotional Disorders* (1976), is where the medical
model stops being a metaphor borrowed from bodily medicine and becomes literal clinical practice
conducted by an actual physician. The book's own title does the essential work before a single page
is read: `Cognitive Therapy and the Emotional Disorders`
(`self-improvement/cognitive-therapy-and-the-emotional-disorders/reading/cognitive-therapy-and-the-emotional-disorders.md:9`).
A corpus-wide search of just four words — *disorder, patient, symptom, treatment* — returns **698
hits in this single volume**, a density unmatched anywhere else in the vault outside the DSM itself.
Beck's opening pages state the reclassification as a simple matter of clinical consensus, not
argument: the neuroses are now, as a matter of course, "**the emotionally disturbed person**"
(`cognitive-therapy-and-the-emotional-disorders.md:97`) whose "**emotional disorders**" are treated
by "traditional neuropsychiatry, psychoanalysis, and behavior therapy" alike
(`cognitive-therapy-and-the-emotional-disorders.md:95`), and that cognitive therapy's own innovation
is simply a fourth *treatment* for the same category of *disorder* — "the therapist helps a
**patient** to unravel his distortions in thinking" (`cognitive-therapy-and-the-emotional-disorders.md:103`).
Note precisely what has *not* changed here from Evagrius's antirrhesis (catch the thought, answer it,
repeat — see the companion antirrhetic brief) and what *has*: the mechanism survives; the patient has
replaced the penitent, the disorder has replaced the passion, and the therapist has replaced the
elder — with no theological remainder anywhere in the frame.

### 6. The DSM-5 — the catalogue, with the moral-spiritual content measurably absent
**The DSM-5** (American Psychiatric Association, 2013) is the drift's completed terminus: a
1,000-page taxonomy of "disorders" that maps, thought by thought, onto the eight *logismoi* — not as
clinical equivalences (a diagnosis is not a vice, and this brief does not claim otherwise) but as
*analogies of category*, the same human phenomena re-sorted under a different master vocabulary.

- **Acedia ↔ depressive/anhedonic criteria.** The Major Depressive Episode criteria require
  "**depressed mood**" or "**markedly diminished interest or pleasure in all, or almost all,
  activities**" (`reference/dsm-5/reading/dsm-5.md:671`) — read this against Cassian's monk who
  "frequently goes in and out of his cell" and "gazes up at the sun" (the acedia brief,
  `patristics/john-cassian/reading/institutes-10.md`), and the phenomenology is continuous even as
  the explanatory frame (demon with a clock vs. neurochemical episode) is not.
- **The intrusive *logismos* ↔ OCD's "intrusive thoughts."** "**Recurrent and persistent thoughts,
  urges, or images that are experienced... as intrusive and unwanted**"
  (`reference/dsm-5/reading/dsm-5.md:1115`) is, verbatim, the desert's own definition of a *logismos*
  the monk did not invite and must talk back to (Evagrius, `talking-back-antirrhetikos.md:478`,
  quoted in full in the antirrhetic-method brief) — with the received scriptural counter-word (the
  desert's cure) simply absent from the DSM's own criteria, which name only distress and
  time-consumption, not a remedy.
- **Gluttony ↔ Binge-Eating Disorder.** Criteria include "eating... an amount of food that is
  definitely larger than what most people would eat" and, tellingly, "**feeling disgusted with
  oneself, depressed, or very guilty afterward**" (`reference/dsm-5/reading/dsm-5.md:1559`) — guilt
  is *observed as a symptom*, not addressed as a moral-spiritual reality calling for confession and
  absolution.
- **Anger ↔ Intermittent Explosive Disorder.** "Recurrent behavioral outbursts representing **a
  failure to control aggressive impulses**" (`reference/dsm-5/reading/dsm-5.md:2019`) — Cassian's and
  Evagrius's "wrath" thought, entirely stripped of its status as one of the eight and re-founded as a
  discrete impulse-control diagnosis with a prevalence rate (2.7%) rather than a spiritual etiology.
- **Vainglory/pride ↔ Narcissistic Personality Disorder.** "A pervasive pattern of **grandiosity**...
  **need for admiration**, and **lack of empathy**" (`reference/dsm-5/reading/dsm-5.md:2823`) reads
  as a clinical inventory of exactly the behaviors the vainglory and pride briefs document from
  Evagrius and Cassian onward — with the difference that the DSM names a *personality structure* to
  be managed, not a *passion* to be repented of and healed.

What the DSM's own vocabulary conspicuously lacks is the most load-bearing fact this brief can offer,
and it is directly countable rather than merely asserted: across this entire volume, the word
**"sin" occurs zero times, "repent"/"repentance" occurs zero times**, "God" occurs exactly **once**
— and that once is not the DSM speaking in its own diagnostic voice but *describing, as a normal
cultural variant, a patient's report of a religious hallucination* ("hearing God's voice,"
`reference/dsm-5/reading/dsm-5.md:583`) — and "soul" occurs exactly **twice**, both instances
likewise anthropological, cataloguing *other cultures'* folk-illness categories ("'soul loss' among
Cambodians," `reference/dsm-5/reading/dsm-5.md:1015`; an illness "attributed to a frightening event
that causes the soul to leave the body," `dsm-5.md:3451`) rather than the manual's own working
concepts. Contrast this with "disorder," which by simple count appears **over 12,800 times** in the
same file. This is not an accident of translation or genre; it is the category shift itself,
measured in one book: the medical vocabulary is total, and the moral-spiritual vocabulary survives
only as an object of clinical description in someone *else's* belief system, never as the DSM's own
operative frame.

## The balance — what is gained, what is lost, stated carefully
**What the medicalization gained, and gained genuinely.** Real psychiatric and biological illness is
real; the DSM's descriptive rigor lets sufferers and clinicians name a condition precisely, secure
treatment, and — this matters enormously and should not be minimized — escape the older, crueler
verdict that all suffering of the mind is simply unconfessed sin or insufficient faith. De-stigmatization,
symptom relief, and the recognition that some disorders of mood and thought are not chosen are
authentic goods the ancient vocabulary, taken alone and without qualification, did not reliably
protect (a monk in the grip of what we would now call clinical depression, told only that his
"acedia" was a demon to be resisted by staying in the cell, could be badly served by that counsel
alone). Modern psychiatry's medicine is real medicine, and this brief does not ask ChainBreaker's
reader to distrust it.

**What the medicalization lost, measured directly above rather than merely asserted.** The DSM's own
word-counts make the loss precise rather than rhetorical: a text that discusses human disorder more
than twelve thousand times contains not one operative instance of sin, repentance, or God, and only
anthropological, third-person instances of "soul." The category that vanishes is not any particular
fact about the mind but the entire register in which Cassian could write, in a single sentence, of
"healing remedies from the true Physician of souls" *and* "the remedy of penitence"
(`patristics/john-cassian/reading/conference-19.md:83`) — culpability-and-grace, a *telos* beyond
symptom-relief (union with God, not merely functioning), and repentance as itself part of the cure,
not a separate and optional add-on for those who happen to be religious.

**The honest asymmetry, stated once.** The ancients medicalized the moral life only *metaphorically*
— they had the physician-image and, at most, fasting and bodily ascesis as actual medicine, but no
clinical psychiatry, no antidepressants, no controlled trials. The moderns medicalize it *literally
and completely* — they have real, effective medicine, but, inside the clinical frame itself, no
theology: no category for sin as distinct from symptom, no repentance as distinct from treatment
compliance, no *telos* beyond the relief of distress and the restoration of function. ChainBreaker's
argument is not that the ancients were right and the moderns wrong, nor the reverse; it is that the
two halves — real medicine and real theology — were never meant to be separated, and that the
tradition's own witnesses (Cassian, Chrysostom, Baxter) show what it looks like when they are not:
the Physician who both amends the body's chemistry *and* leads the soul to God, treatment and
repentance named in the same breath, neither displacing the other.

## The drift, stated plainly

1. **The phrase survives sixteen centuries almost unchanged, then the content underneath it is
   quietly emptied out.** "Physician of souls" — the identical three words — runs from the
   Philokalia and Chrysostom through Aquinas, Baxter, Wesley, Spurgeon, and Edwards
   (24 hits, 17 files, see Method) without ever losing its meaning: Christ, healing sin, by grace,
   toward repentance. Beck and the DSM keep the *physician* and the *patient* and drop everything
   else the phrase used to carry.
2. **The unit of analysis narrows from "soul" to "disorder."** Cassian, Climacus, and the Philokalia
   diagnose a *soul* in relation to God (`conference-19.md:83`; `ladder-of-divine-ascent-english.md:197`;
   `philokalia-english-complete.md:4474`). The DSM diagnoses a *disorder* in relation to a symptom
   checklist and a prevalence rate (`dsm-5.md:671,1115,1559,2019,2823`) — the patient's relation to
   God is, by the numbers above, simply not part of the file.
3. **Repentance and treatment stop being named in the same sentence.** Cassian's "remedy of
   penitence" sits inside the same clause as "healing remedies... from the true Physician of souls"
   (`conference-19.md:83`); Beck's "the therapist helps a patient... to learn alternative, more
   realistic ways to formulate his experiences" (`cognitive-therapy-and-the-emotional-disorders.md:103`)
   has no moral or penitential clause left to sit beside it — the cure is entirely cognitive-behavioral.
4. **Guilt becomes a symptom to be assessed rather than a fact to be adjudicated before God.** The
   DSM records "feeling... very guilty afterward" as diagnostic criterion B5 for binge-eating disorder
   (`dsm-5.md:1559`) — guilt is *data about the patient's affect*, not itself a claim that could be
   true (rightly incurred, calling for confession) or false (excessive, calling for reassurance). The
   category that could adjudicate which is simply not available inside the DSM's own vocabulary.
5. **The word count itself is the drift, not just evidence of it.** A single 2013 clinical manual
   uses "disorder" upward of 12,800 times and "sin"/"repent" not once (`dsm-5.md`, corpus grep). That
   is not a subtle interpretive claim; it is the terminus of the arc that began with Matthew 9:12,
   made countable.

## Why this matters for ChainBreaker

1. **A ready quotable spine for the whole chapter.** Cassian's `conference-19.md:83` sentence —
   physician *and* penitence, in one clause — is the single best primary-source epigraph for a
   chapter arguing the ancients never separated what modernity has separated. It states
   ChainBreaker's thesis before ChainBreaker does.
2. **The DSM word-count argument is the book's strongest, most defensible evidence, because it is a
   fact about a text, not an interpretation of one.** "Zero 'sin,' zero 'repent,' one 'God' (used to
   describe someone else's hallucination), two 'soul' (used to describe someone else's folk illness),
   12,800+ 'disorder'" is a claim any reader can verify by opening the DSM-5 themselves. It lets the
   book make its hardest point — the category shift is total — without needing to argue that
   psychiatry is wrong about anything it actually claims.
3. **This brief is the antirrhetic brief's necessary companion at the level of the whole model, not
   just the technique.** The antirrhetic-method brief (`research/2026-07-24_antirrhetic-method.md`)
   already argues CBT's self-talk is antirrhesis with God quietly removed from the *procedure*; this
   brief makes the parallel claim about the entire *frame* the procedure sits inside — the physician
   is real, the patient is real, but the two are no longer standing before God together.
4. **Acedia is still the sharpest single case, and it is already sourced elsewhere — this brief adds
   the DSM's own criteria text to strengthen it.** The acedia brief
   (`research/2026-07-23_acedia.md`) documents the demon-with-a-clock becoming Aquinas's category
   becoming modern burnout; this brief supplies the DSM's actual Major Depressive Episode wording
   (`dsm-5.md:671`) as the literal endpoint diagnostic criteria, closing that argument with a primary
   clinical source rather than a synthesis.
5. **The balance section gives the book its safety rail.** Any chapter built on this material risks
   sounding like an attack on psychiatry or on readers who take medication or see a therapist. The
   explicit gained/lost accounting above — real illness, real medicine, real gains in
   de-stigmatization, *and* a real vanished register of culpability-grace-and-telos — is the paragraph
   ChainBreaker should lift near-verbatim to keep the chapter honest and pastorally safe.
6. **A genealogy, not a diagnosis, for the reader in front of a psychiatrist's door.** The point the
   book can actually make to a struggling reader is not "your disorder is really a sin" but "the
   physician you are (rightly) about to see inherited his whole vocabulary — physician, patient,
   healing — from a tradition that also insisted the deepest healing was of the soul before God; you
   are allowed to want, and to seek, both."

## Gaps flagged

  removes one plausible disease/remedy chapter from the set searched here.

  positions itself explicitly against "traditional neuropsychiatry, psychoanalysis, and behavior
  therapy" as the three prior schools (`cognitive-therapy-and-the-emotional-disorders.md:97`), but

  book wants the "disease model" traced back one more generation before Beck.
- **No DSM-III or earlier edition is in the vault**, so the brief cannot show the word-count drift

- **William James's *Varieties of Religious Experience*** (already flagged in the acedia brief as the

  acknowledgment that *other* cultures still name spiritual causes for affliction it declines to name
