# Avarice / Greed (philargyria — love of money) — a diachronic trace, with an inversion

**Research question.** How did the Christian tradition frame *philargyria*, "the love of money"
— one of the eight thoughts — and where, if anywhere, did that framing invert? Of all eight
thoughts this is the one where the answer is starkest: a whole modern literature (business /
self-improvement) doesn't just soften avarice, it **canonizes the desire for wealth as virtue and
duty**, in language that echoes the very texts it inverts.

**Method.** Corpus-wide, case-insensitive search of `**/reading/**/*.md` (323 works) for
`avaric|covetous|philargyria|love of money|mammon` returns **3,122 occurrences across 201
files** — one of the densest themes in the library. A second pass targeted the business/ and
self-improvement/ divisions specifically for "getting rich," "duty to get rich," and "science of
getting rich" framing. Every quotation below cites a local file and line; the one OCR witness
(Carnegie, *Gospel of Wealth*) is flagged.

---

## The arc

### 0. The biblical anchor
> "For the love of money is a root of all kinds of evil, which some coveting have erred from the
> faith, and have entangled themselves in many sorrows." — **1 Timothy 6:10** (quoted constantly
> below; also Matt 6:24, "Ye cannot serve God and mammon," and Luke 12:20, the rich fool).

The biblical logic: love of money is not "having," it's a *disordered desire* — a root-condition
that generates other sins. Every patristic writer below works from this root/fruit structure.

### 1. Desert root — Evagrius names it "the mother of idolatry"
- **Evagrius Ponticus, *Praktikos*** (c. 380; `patristics/evagrius-ponticus/reading/praktikos-dysinger-english.md:31`):
  the monk is committed to "**loving poverty but fleeing from avarice as the mother of
  idolatry**."
- (`...praktikos-dysinger-english.md:91`): Evagrius's psychological anatomy of the thought —
  "**Love of money suggests:** a long old age; hands powerless to work; hunger and disease yet to
  come; the bitterness of poverty; and the disgrace of receiving the necessities [of life] from
  others." Note the mechanism: avarice sells itself as *prudence against future want*, not as
  greed. That exact mechanism is what Cassian expands into a full case study next.

### 2. Cassian — the fullest patristic treatment in the corpus
Cassian devotes an entire *Institutes* book (VII) and a third of *Conference* 5 to this thought,
naming it by its Greek term.
- **Cassian, *Conference 5*** (c. 426; `patristics/john-cassian/reading/conference-05.md:21`):
  the canonical list — "thirdly **philargyria, i.e., avarice or the love of money**" — third of
  the eight, and (`:69`) placed in the causal chain: "from fornication **covetousness**, from
  **covetousness anger**" — avarice is not free-floating; it is caused by unchecked appetite and
  itself causes rage (the wallet threatened is the anger trigger).
- **Cassian, *Institutes VII*** ("Of the Spirit of Covetousness"; `patristics/john-cassian/reading/institutes-07.md:11`):
  opens by naming it **"a foreign warfare... outside of our nature"** — unlike gluttony or lust,
  no one is born wanting money; it is *acquired*, which is why it is "harder to drive away when
  once admitted" (`:39`).
- The set-piece case study (`:47`) is the single richest passage in the corpus on this thought: a
  monk rationalizes keeping "a small sum of money" against future illness, poverty, old age —
  *precisely* Evagrius's list above — and the passage ends: **"gold and the love of gain become to
  him his god, as the belly does to others."** Cassian then cites Col 3:5, that covetousness
  (φιλαργυρία) "**is the worship of idols**."
- Cassian's diagnosis of *why* it can never be satisfied (`:149`): "the madness of this avarice is
  **not satisfied with any amount of riches**" (of Judas, who kept the bag "that he might at least
  satisfy himself with plenty of money... yet his plentiful supply only broke out into a still
  greedier incitement of desire"). And the cure (`:173`): **"not to allow a gleam from the very
  smallest scrap of it to remain in our heart."** The only remedy is total stripping, not a
  ceiling on wealth — avarice can survive at zero net worth as a disposition (`:135`, "one who
  actually has no money may still be deemed covetous").

### 3. Cyprian, Chrysostom — the pastoral register
- **Cyprian, *On Works and Alms*** (c. 253; `patristics/cyprian/reading/on-works-and-alms-treatise-8.md:35`):
  direct address to a rich hoarder — "**the deep and profound darkness of avarice has blinded
  your carnal heart. You are the captive and slave of your money; you are bound with the chains
  and bonds of covetousness.**" The rhetorical move: the "owner" is in fact owned.
- **Chrysostom, *Homily 2 on the Statues*** (c. 387; `patristics/john-chrysostom/reading/statues-homily-02.md:37`):
  the definitional distinction the whole tradition depends on — **"A covetous man is one thing,
  and a rich man is another thing. The covetous man is not rich; he is in want of many things...
  The covetous man is a keeper, not a master, of wealth; a slave, not a lord."** Wealth itself is
  not condemned (Abraham was rich but not covetous, `:39`); the sin is the *grip*, not the *gold*.
- **Chrysostom, *No One Can Harm the Man Who Does Not Harm Himself*** (c. 407;
  `patristics/john-chrysostom/reading/no-one-can-harm.md:23`): the most vivid image in the corpus
  — greed for wealth as a literal wildfire: **"the flame has ascended to the very clouds... Nor is
  there any one to quench this fire: but all people are engaged in stirring it up."** And on Judas
  (`:37`): he was ruined not by his office but "because he was **covetous**, and destined to
  perish on account of his **love of money**."

### 4. Medieval and Reformed systematization
- **Westminster Larger Catechism** (1647, Q.142;
  `tradition/reformed-confessions/reading/westminster-larger-catechism.md:1318`): under the
  eighth commandment, "covetousness; **inordinate prizing and affecting worldly goods**" is
  formally enumerated as sin — the desert thought is now catechetical doctrine.
- **Thomas Watson, *The Art of Divine Contentment*** (1653;
  `tradition/thomas-watson/reading/art-of-divine-contentment.md:2195` and `:526`): the sharpest
  one-liners in the whole search — **"Avarice is an helluo [a glutton], that is never
  satisfied"** and **"covetousness is a dry dropsy"** (dropsy = edema; the more fluid the sicker
  body drinks, the thirstier it gets — a disease that *feeds on being fed*). Watson makes explicit
  what Cassian showed by narrative: covetousness is structurally unsatisfiable, and its opposite
  virtue is not wealth-limitation but *contentment*.
- **John Owen, *Indwelling Sin*** (1667; `tradition/john-owen/reading/indwelling-sin.md:2429`):
  covetousness as "**inflamed, enraged lust**," citing the same 1 Tim 6 text — "men 'pierce
  themselves through with many sorrows.'" The Puritans keep Cassian's root/fruit logic and his
  totalizing remedy (contentment, not a spending cap) intact 1,200 years later.

**Summary of the patristic-through-Puritan consensus:** love of money is (a) unnatural/acquired,
not innate; (b) structurally insatiable — it grows by being fed, never by being satisfied; (c) a
form of idolatry/slavery, inverting master and possession; (d) cured only by contentment /
total renunciation of the *desire*, not by any ceiling on the *amount* held. Riches themselves are
morally neutral (Chrysostom's Abraham); the disposition is the whole battlefield.

## 5. The inversion — business and self-improvement make getting rich a duty

This is the one thought where the modern corpus does not just relax the older judgment — it
**reverses the polarity** while re-using the tradition's own vocabulary of "duty," "sin," and
"the gospel."

- **Adam Smith, *Wealth of Nations*** (1776; `business/adam-smith/reading/wealth-of-nations.md:552`):
  the mechanism-level pivot. Self-love, which every Father from Evagrius to Owen treated as the
  thing to be mortified, becomes the engine of the whole system: **"It is not from the
  benevolence of the butcher, the brewer, or the baker that we expect our dinner, but from their
  regard to their own interest. We address ourselves, not to their humanity, but to their
  self-love."** Self-interested acquisitiveness is redescribed as a *coordinating* virtue, not a
  *corrupting* one.
- **P. T. Barnum, *The Art of Money Getting; or, Golden Rules for Making Money*** (c. 1880;
  `business/barnum/reading/art-of-money-getting.md`): the title alone signals the reversal — what
  the desert called a disease to be starved out now has "golden rules." Barnum treats charity as
  "a duty and a pleasure" (`:1289`) but the book's entire structure is a catechism for *acquiring*
  money methodically, the closest thing the corpus has to an "ars philargyriae" written without
  irony.
- **Andrew Carnegie, *The Gospel of Wealth*** (1889; `business/carnegie/reading/gospel-of-wealth.md`
  — **OCR text, archive.org scan, flagged in the file header as "Uncorrected OCR: verify before
  verbatim quotation"**): the most explicit reversal in the corpus. Of the wealthy man's pursuit
  of more, Carnegie writes (`:4705`, `:4707`): **"it be[comes] the duty of the millionaire to
  increase his revenues. The struggle for more is completely freed from selfish or ambitious
  taint and becomes a noble pursuit... His daily labor is a daily virtue."** The very phrase
  structure — "duty," "freed from... taint," "virtue" — answers Cassian's "gold and the love of
  gain become to him his god" almost word for word, but assigns the opposite value. Carnegie also
  names the mechanism explicitly as **"the law of competition"** (`:1494`), a providential
  replacement for the desert's providence-of-contentment.
- **Wallace D. Wattles, *The Science of Getting Rich*** (1910;
  `self-improvement/wattles/reading/science-of-getting-rich.md:166`, `:178-182`, `:249`): the
  starkest single inversion sentence in the whole search. Watson wrote "covetousness is a dry
  dropsy"; Wattles writes the mirror image — **"No man ought to be satisfied with a little if he
  is capable of using and enjoying more... to be content with less is sinful."** Contentment,
  the Puritan cure for avarice, is now itself the sin. Wattles goes on: "the basis of all
  advancement for man must be **the science of getting rich**" (`:166`), and neglecting to study
  it makes you "**derelict in your duty to yourself, to God, and to humanity**" (`:249`) — the
  desert's ascetic vocabulary of duty-to-God is transferred wholesale onto wealth acquisition.
- **Russell H. Conwell, *Acres of Diamonds*** (1890;
  `self-improvement/conwell/reading/acres-of-diamonds.md:466`, `:502-503`): delivered as a
  revival-style address, and explicit about the reversal it is performing — **"I say that you
  ought to get rich, and it is your duty to get rich."** Conwell then names the very doctrine he
  is overturning: **"We preach against covetousness, and you know we do, in the pulpit... [yet]
  Money is power, and you ought to be reasonably ambitious to have it."** This is the inversion
  caught in the act of happening — a preacher consciously arguing his congregation out of the
  Watson/Owen consensus and into the Wattles/Carnegie one, using the same pulpit vocabulary.

**The inversion, stated plainly:** Evagrius → Cassian → Watson → Owen treat the desire for money
as *structurally insatiable and therefore to be starved*, cured only by contentment; wealth itself
is neutral, the grip is the sin. Smith → Barnum → Carnegie → Wattles → Conwell treat the same
desire as *providentially productive and therefore to be cultivated*, recast as duty, virtue,
even gospel; contentment with less becomes the thing condemned. The two traditions share almost
every keyword — duty, virtue, gospel, sin, root — and assign them to opposite objects. This is a
sharper, more explicit inversion than the guarding-the-heart drift (which took three centuries and
a change of vocabulary); here Conwell states the reversal from the pulpit in one paragraph.

## Why this matters for ChainBreaker

1. **The two literatures cite the same verse and split.** 1 Tim 6:10 ("love of money is a root of
   all evil") is quoted or clearly presupposed by Cassian, Cyprian, Owen — and is the exact
   doctrine Conwell names and argues against by name, in the same homiletic register. A single
   page can show the reader Cassian's case study (`institutes-07.md:47`) beside Conwell's rebuttal
   (`acres-of-diamonds.md:466`) with no editorializing needed — the contrast makes the argument.
2. **The insatiability diagnosis is independently falsifiable and hard to refute.** Watson's "dry
   dropsy" and Cassian's Judas ("not satisfied with any amount of riches") describe a
   psychological mechanism (desire scales with supply) that modern behavioral-economics readers
   will recognize as hedonic adaptation — giving the desert case scientific-sounding backup
   without needing outside sources.
3. **Chrysostom supplies the needed nuance so the chapter doesn't read as "money is evil."** His
   rich-man/covetous-man distinction (`statues-homily-02.md:37`) and Abraham example let
   ChainBreaker condemn the *grip*, not wealth itself — avoiding a strawman the self-improvement
   camp could easily bat down.

## Gaps flagged for this theme
- Napoleon Hill (*Law of Success*) and James Allen (*From Poverty to Power*) were checked but
  yielded weaker/mixed material (Allen in particular partly critiques external wealth-seeking in
  favor of "true riches" of character, so he doesn't cleanly join the Carnegie/Wattles inversion
  camp — worth a closer read if the chapter wants a third New Thought voice).
- Carnegie's *Gospel of Wealth* is the only OCR-flagged source quoted here; the file header
  explicitly warns "Uncorrected OCR: verify before verbatim quotation" — the quoted lines were
  checked against surrounding context for sense but should be verified against a second edition
  before print use.
- No Basil or Chrysostom homily *specifically* "on avarice" (as opposed to touching it in a
  Statues homily) has a public-domain English witness in the vault — a possible gap parallel to
  the hesychast gap noted in the guarding-the-heart brief.
