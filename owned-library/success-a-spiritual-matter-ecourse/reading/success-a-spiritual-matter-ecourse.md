THE FAILURE QUOTIENT: The Gift of Proportion
When we get a rejection or have a setback, it's very hard not to feel at least momentarily thrown off course.
In fact, a major benefit people receive who have gone through difficult life challenges is that no matter what other daily challenge they face, they can measure it against the larger life adversity they've overcome, and put it into its proper perspective.
For example, as I portrayed in "The 9 Insights of the Wealthy Soul," I grew up with a sister who had a terminal illness my entire childhood. Sure, I still get knocked down temporarily by any number of setbacks, probably on a weekly to daily basis, but I can tell you that there are few things that life has been able to throw at me that has set me back for long.


I call this "The Gift of Proportion." Whether you've been through a serious illness, loss of a loved one, divorce, or have been a victim of violence, the rest of your life you can benefit by using that experience to weigh everything else you face against it.
I have often joked that I could wallpaper my entire home if I had kept all the rejection slips I've received since my teens when I became involved in magazine writing, then television writing, then book writing.
Like any writer, each rejection slip "stung," but how meaningless this setback -- this tiny "adversity" -- was when put to the test against the extraordinary strength I had gained by living through six years slowly losing my teenaged sister.
I thus have always been able put each rejection into its proper perspective (mainly the garbage can), and set out to make my next step.
This is how I developed quite a high F.Q. As a result, I had my first magazine articles published when I was 16 years old. And even though it's taken me more than 20 years since, I now have 3 books published and have been featured in some of the country's top publications such as The Wall Street Journal and The Atlanta Journal and Constitution.
It ain't been easy, friends. But ask me if it's been worth it...

So the first three steps of developing a high F.Q. after you receive a setback are:
1. FEEL SORRY FOR YOURSELF. Why not? You're disappointed. Why try to deny it? Okay, so after you've spent enough time doing that (roughly 30 seconds to 2 minutes is the recommended amount of time), move on to:
2. PUT THE SETBACK INTO PERSPECTIVE. Think how meaningless this one thing is compared to whatever else you've been through. (Another 30 seconds to 2 minutes is about right for this step.)
3. TAKE YOUR NEXT STEP. For me, that previously meant sending my writing out to another magazine or publisher. Now it means having my publicist call two more radio or TV shows for the


one which won't air me. As a doctor, it means trying a different procedure when I don't get the results I expect with a patient.
If you're Thomas Edison, it means trying another experiment of the 50,000 it will take to create the light bulb. If you're George Bush or Al Gore and it's the year 2000, it means making one more call to one more party to try to sway one more block of votes in your direction.
If you're in sales, it means picking up the phone, and making one more contact. If you're in love and he or she isn't, it means either sending one more bouquet of roses, or else putting that person behind you and opening yourself up to one more person.
