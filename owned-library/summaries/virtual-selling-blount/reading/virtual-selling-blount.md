He discusses how to "blend" your virtual selling activities with traditional, faceto-face selling to maximize sales, commissions and customer service.

When circumstances limit face-to-face selling, virtual selling becomes a vital skill.
These are tough times for salespeople. Reps are grappling with a number of negative effects from the coronavirus pandemic. With the inception of social distancing, buyers are working from home and their needs have shifted. Face-to-face selling is no longer practical for many salespeople, in particular, those in B2B sale. Virtual selling, a viable substitute, has assumed a new prominence in the time of coronavirus.
"If you fail to rapidly adopt and assimilate omnichannel virtual selling into your business development, sales and account management processes, you will either become extinct or be replaced by a robot. That's a brutal and absolute fact."
Virtual selling is necessary, as smart salespeople with an "evolve or die mentality" understand, but it is not new. [Though the term "virtual" technically refers to computer-based communication,] common usage deploys two terms Ł "virtual" and "remote" Ł to cover various forms of distant communication, which has existed, practically, forever. Smoke signals, drum beats, carrier pigeons, letters, the telegraph, the telephone, the fax, email, social media and instant messaging are all distant Ł as opposed to face to face Ł communication.
Since face-to-face sales have long been the norm, many salespeople are uncomfortable with distant selling.
Despite the ubiquity of virtual communications, many sales professionals believe the only way to sell is face-to-face. These reps may be uncomfortable with the technology of virtual selling, including videoconferencing, instant messaging, social media and the like.
"On virtual sales calls, closed-ended questions give you the illusion of control. Yet, they create poor emotional experiences for stakeholders who are subjected to these interrogations."
Everything virtual moves quickly; increased speed can push salespeople to take what they see as shortcuts. That tactic seldom goes well. Virtual sales calls don't offer the same easy-to-read cues that you can garner when you talk to someone in person, face-to-face. These cues include "buying signals" that salespeople can decipher to help them close. In face-to-face meetings, body language and other reactions tell salespeople where they stand. These signals are rare on virtual sales calls. Salespeople who work virtually have to figure out their next best step without having these messages to decode.

Success is possible: One truck-reselling firm makes more than 90% of its sales Ł $20,000 to $200,000 deals Ł virtually.
Many traditional salespeople have a hard time believing that a very successful commercial truck reselling firms works primarily on a remote basis. It doesn't sell inexpensive pick-ups or panel trucks. These vehicles cost between $20,000 to $200,000. Customers receive photos. They don't do test drives; they don't kick the tires. Nevertheless, this firm successfully closes "tens of thousands" of used-truck deals, annually.
"The more people you talk with, the more you will sell."
Customers don't regard buying used trucks virtually as a problem. Many have learned to prefer virtual sales over the time and trouble of conventional sales. Saving buyers time and trouble is one advantage of virtual selling. Because it's relatively easy for prospects, most are repeat virtual clients. The company saves money selling trucks virtually. This model keeps their costs low, allowing them to offer customers better deals while still earning sizable profits.
The company's salespeople became so expert at selling used trucks virtually that they began to sell trucks that were still on the road, actively hauling loads. In a sense, these vehicles became "invisible trucks," sold, sight unseen, to buyers. Today, this firms sells more than 90% of its used trucks virtually. In a recent year, it sold more than 25,000 used trucks. Virtual sales are the secret of its success.
Rules that apply to face-to-face sales apply to remote sales.
Even companies that operate virtually must stick to the sales basics. Even this used truck firm adheres to tried-and-true sales fundamentals: Operate fast and smart. Recruit the best sales professionals. Train and coach them well. Execute a formal sales process.
Smart salespeople adapt their selling techniques to the needs of individual customers. They treat customers like royalty. And they make superior performance a defining characteristic of their corporate culture. To emulate them, prioritize "human-to-human connections."
Prospective clients are adapting to a virtual sales approach.
Because virtual selling is relatively new, it frightens many salespeople. Often reps worry that their customers will not respond to virtual selling. Given the option of face-to-face or virtual, most prospects would still choose face-to-face. Many prospects want to speak with you in person before they sign off on a major or potentially risky purchase.
"No matter what you sell, how you sell, your sales cycle or the complexity of the sales process, emotions play a crucial role in the outcomes of your sales conversations, interactions and deals."

If clients understand that a virtual contact is the only way they can communicate, most will accept it. Most reservations about virtual selling come from salespeople, not prospects.
Successful selling requires confidence and control.
Making a sale is tough if you're not comfortable with your selling situation. To become confident and gain a sense of control, practice, practice and practice some more. Remember that, however advanced or high-tech it seems, virtual selling is still selling. Your sales will go well as long as you "leverage, execute and move deals through the sales process" as you always have. Stick to the sales basics.
Faithful adherence to a formal selling process enables you to combine the puzzle pieces of any successful sale: prospecting, targeting and qualifying buyers; engaging them; and conducting discovery with each one.
"Stalled deals plague the sales profession, clogging pipelines, ruining forecasts and causing untold frustration...it's always the same root cause: The salesperson failed to ask for and get a commitment to a next step."
Additional pieces of the sales puzzle include: developing solution maps, creating a compelling business case for each buyer, drafting proposals, making presentations, handling objections, negotiating deals and closing. The most important step still is asking for the sale you've worked so hard to achieve.
Blend traditional, face-to-face selling tactics with remote-selling tactics.
Even salespeople who like virtual selling don't abandon face-to-face selling; they add virtualselling activities. Often, salespeople will dedicate the time and money they invest in face-to-face selling to their biggest deals, and usually at the end of those giant sales. To maximize your virtual selling results, blend virtual and face-to-face sales.
"Prospects are so crazy busy that as soon your call ends, they will have already forgotten about you and moved on to the next pressing issue on their priority list. If you don't have a firm next step on their calendar, you'll spend the next month chasing them down."
Blending involves using various communication channels. Handle it well to increase your odds of getting prospects on your side, setting up appointments, moving to the "next step" in the sales process, securing sales and retaining customers.

Video calls display people's faces, so you can read their expressions. Video enables salespeople to establish "emotional connections" with prospects Ł something you can't do well with text or email. You can make video calls from a variety of devices, connecting instantaneously with your prospects. You can record evergreen messages to promote yourself and your offerings.
However, many businesspeople are uncomfortable being "on camera." To get over this fear, constantly record yourself on video. The more you do, the more comfortable you will feel.
"In the back of your mind, there is always that little voice warning you that you'll butcher your words, look foolish on camera, come across the wrong way on the phone, that people will laugh at you, that you'll blow it."
Focus on the important elements of an effective video sales call. Control your tone of voice and emphasis. Remain mindful of your body language. Consider how you move, gesture and use facial expressions. For better eye contact, make your video frame tiny and the frame of your contacts as large as possible. Pay attention, listen carefully and dress professionally.
Consider these popular virtual sales channels:
Ę Virtual presentations and demos Ł Keep your videos short. Use pictures and images, but not a lot of words. Limit yourself to five or fewer bullet points for each slide, and seven or fewer words for each bullet point.
Ę Video messaging Ł According to SalesLoft, videos helped rack up sales in 75% of the cases studied. Hippovideo.io determined that video messaging reduces the typical sales-cycle time up to 40%. Personalize the videos you create for specific prospects.
Ę The telephone Ł Many salespeople hate the phone. They claim prospects don't answer their phones, so why waste time calling? Research indicates that, depending on the industry, up to 80% of telephone contacts with prospects succeed.
Ę Voicemails Ł Should you leave voicemails for prospects? Usually, yes. Without a voicemail, you won't get a callback. Persistence is essential. If you don't get a callback, leave another voicemail. Research indicates salespeople must go through "20 to 50 touches" with "cold prospects" before making a connection.
Ę Texting Ł Use text messaging for managing accounts, helping clients, building relationships and moving prospects through your pipeline. Don't use texts for cold calling. Prospects need some familiarity with you for texting to be effective.
Ę Email Ł People send 300 billion emails each day. Email your prospects, but don't overdo it. Be judicious with emails, as with all virtual communication.

Social selling calls on the "five Cs": "conversion, consistency, connection, curation and creation."
Make yourself a familiar presence online. Familiarity lubricates virtual sales. Becoming a regular presence on social media requires regularly posting compelling content.
"The incoming data on the influence of video on buying behaviors is incontrovertible. Three quarters of buyers state that they made a buying decision after watching a video."
Attend to your personal brand. Make sure all your online activity is consistent with your personal brand, which must support your company's brand. Vest in the five Cs of social selling:
1. "Conversion" Ł Convert your social media activities into "leads, appointments, qualifying information, sales intelligence, closed deals, and account expansion and renewal."
2. "Consistency" Ł Be active online every day. 3. "Connection" Ł Reach out to your online audience members, so they become loyal followers. 4. "Curation" Ł Success online requires great, potent content. Provide instructive information
on your website and social media channels. 5. "Creation" Ł Generate your own original content.
