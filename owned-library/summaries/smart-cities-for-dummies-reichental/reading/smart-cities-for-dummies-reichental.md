Qualities Overview For Beginners Hot Topic

Smart Cities For Dummies
Jonathan Reichental | Wiley © 2020

Rapid change means today's cities demand new, creative responses to the needs of the billions of people living and working in them. But most cities still apply 20th-century solutions to 21stcentury problems. Learn how the smart city movement aims to change that using people-focused technology to improve city-dwellers' quality of life. This practical, well-organized guide is a great resource for leaders, stakeholders and anyone interested in the urban future.

Summary
Cities play a central role in the history of human development.
On a 200,000-year historical timeline of Homo sapiens, city dwelling is a very recent phenomenon. Rapid innovation in cities began around 1770, thanks to the industrial revolution. From that time onward, transformative technologies such as steam, electricity, semiconductors, digital technology and the internet accelerated urban development. City living contributed to the explosive growth in population that started in the early 20th century. By 2050, an additional 2 billion people could live in cities.

"A smart city is an approach to urbanization that uses innovative technologies to enhance community services and economic opportunities, improves city infrastructure, reduces costs and resource consumption, and increases civic engagement."
There is no official definition for a smart city. Many use the term to refer to the use of technology to deliver programs and services to city residents. A better definition of smart cities is broader in scope and focuses on improving the quality of life for residents. It encompasses economic, social, environmental, cultural and other factors. Smart cities are not a short-term trend focused on implementing specific technologies, nor does the term apply only to large well-known cities.
The needs and challenges of cities are changing rapidly in the era of globalization.
In the modern era, the economic base of many cities is changing from manufacturing to services and technology. Infrastructure and built form may no longer suit the current economy. These transformations pose challenges for decision makers regarding investing for the future. Smart cities must consider interdependencies between physical and digital infrastructure and the economy. Demographics, rates of growth, quality of life, the environment and housing availability also drive the need to remodel today's cities.
The digital age amplifies the changing expectations of urban citizens. Social media allows citizens and activists to reach a wider audience and is a means for increased community involvement in public planning.
"Expanded community engagement is a good news story. More voices mean a stronger, more informed, more engaged and more vibrant democracy."
Cities struggle with traffic congestion, long commutes and increased costs for goods movement. New and emerging technologies such as ride-sharing services, e-bikes, autonomous vehicles (AVs), light rail transit (LRT) and Hyperloop can resolve transportation inefficiencies and address environmental issues. Cities can also deploy new technologies to ensure renewable energy, for example, more efficient building standards or free Wi-Fi.
Incorporating sustainability into all aspects of urban development is an important strategy for cities to improve the health and quality of life of its citizens and meet climate change targets.
Building a smart city requires a strong vision and broad stakeholder input.
A smart city strategy starts with a vision. A vision statement describes the desired future state of the city. It should be long-range, aspirational but fairly general. The subsequent strategic document will guide planning and turn the vision into specific goals and objectives. Elected and appointed city leadership should be part of the exercise to create the smart city vision. The process should be inclusive, involve brainstorming and convey the basic values and culture of the city.

"A vision written correctly and agreed on by relevant stakeholders holds the initiative accountable and provides essential guidance in times of uncertainty."
Following approval of the vision, the next step is to create a "smart city team." The smart city team needs an executive sponsor ­ such as the city manager ­ to ensure local government buyin and availability of resources. The team will have different tiers and should include a steering committee of senior city leadership including heads of departments. An operational group will conduct the day-to-day work, possibly via a project management office. There may be "working groups" of subject-area experts for specific projects. Visual accountability tools such as RACI charts will ensure clarity over who does what. The executive sponsor should hold a kickoff meeting to describe the rationale and vision of the smart city initiative, and ensure everyone is in agreement.
A "smart city strategy" converts the vision into an action plan.
Developing the strategic smart city plan should be an iterative and inclusive process that involves many stakeholders. Many smart city initiatives suffer from a lack of focus and buy-in that leads to an absence of results. A worthy smart city strategic plan is a powerful communications tool to be shared widely.
The strategic plan's goals drive the creation of specific and actionable objectives as determined by the smart city team and stakeholders. For example, a transportation goal might be to reduce single occupancy car use; the objective would be to define specific reduction as the percentage of all trips. These objectives determine the selection criteria for the most doable projects that will best achieve these results given finite resources.
"The smart city movement [is]...the future of almost every person on the planet."
Each objective should develop metrics for success known as key performance indicators (KPIs). These KPIs should be quantitative, like the financial results of for-profit companies, but reflect the goals of the smart city plan. Examples could include the number of electric car charging stations or city services available online. You can use third party KPI metrics to help you, such as CITYkeys, a project collaboration of a number of EU cities.
To implement the strategy successfully, cities need supportive policies, regulations and funding.
A smart city strategy needs to be responsive to new innovations and technologies, which can happen quickly. Developing policies to react to these rapid changes should occur via a series of defined stages. For example, a policy may be required to ensure that data collection technology respects privacy concerns.

"Smart city work is also about having smart regulations in place, improving training for city staff and creating policies for improved environmental management."
Smart city projects require a funding source. Public, private or public-private partnerships are options, as is financing through bonds or loans. There are many alternative procurement methods that can enhance a smart city strategy.
A business analyst will assess a smart city project to ensure it aligns with organizational needs and offers a positive return. Successfully executing smart city projects will require the deployment of project management expertise. Effective project management employs best practices to ensure that projects stay on track and meet clearly defined objectives. It provides metrics of success and reporting protocols for stakeholders.
A culture of innovation enables smart cities to improve the urban quality of life.
Innovation networks among government, private sector, higher education and other stakeholders strive to solve urban challenges. Some smart cities establish "urban innovation labs" focused on solving specific urban challenges with dedicated staff and resources.
"The art and science of applying new ideas to solve urban problems has existed since the first human settlements."
Other initiatives include innovation districts to test new ideas, and "hackathons," during which experts focus on solving specific challenges. Cities must share the results and data from these innovation efforts to gain feedback.
The latest technological advancements and a sound data strategy can transform today's cities.
Digital services transform how cities deliver public services at lower cost and higher efficiency. They allow for greater citizen engagement. The Internet of Things (IoT) ­ which inserts sensors into objects and directly connects them to the internet ­ will further drive innovation in cities. Data are collected throughout the network of urban infrastructure and analyzed to enable realtime response to changing conditions. In Los Angeles, California, for example, IoT and artificial intelligence (AI) combine to form a traffic management system that examines data from sensors on the road and cameras. This ultimately assists drivers to respond to busy traffic conditions or signal problems in real time.
"In Barcelona, Spain as well as in many other cities around the world, smart waste management systems use AI and IoT technology that is fitted on trash cans and can notify waste collectors when full."

Social media facilitate easier communications and interaction between officials and stakeholders with respect to planning, marketing, emergency management and other areas. Other new and emerging technologies such as blockchain offer the potential to secure critical data such as licensing and property deeds online. Autonomous vehicles (AVs) and drones could revolutionize urban transportation and goods delivery while reducing private car ownership. Technology will enhance efforts to make cities more sustainable through the use of smart energy grids and more efficient use of local water resources.
Smart cities should make better use of data to drive decision-making and enhance performance. This includes creating a data strategy, and carefully managing and securing data. This strategy should delineate roles and responsibilities in the management of city data and clarify legal, regulatory and privacy requirements. A transparent data policy engenders trust and aids collaboration.
Smart cities should leverage technology to protect against threats.
Resiliency requires planning strategically for unexpected events such as cyberattacks, natural disasters and pandemics. The absence of planning for such events makes it likely that the impact will be more serious, as many cities have discovered with the recent COVID-19 pandemic.
"The major threats that urban environments face include the effects of climate change, cyberattacks, pandemics, decaying infrastructure, natural disasters and terrorism."
As smart cities increasingly rely on technology and digital networks, investing time and resources to protect data and systems is essential. Larger cities should consider hiring a chief information officer to oversee these efforts and provide input to smart city projects.
Many cities struggle to meet their citizens' needs with "old ways of doing things." In leveraging new ideas, technology and digital infrastructure, the smart city movement can feasibly deliver an inclusive, sustainable and prosperous future for all cities.
