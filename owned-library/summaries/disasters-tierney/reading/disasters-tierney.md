Disasters
A Sociological Approach
Kathleen Tierney | Polity Press © 2019
Wildfires, hurricanes and other calamities aren't areas of inquiry only for climate scientists, writes sociologist Kathleen Tierney. Natural disasters fall under the purview of social scientists like her, as she proves in this eye-opening examination of catastrophes. While it's no surprise that poor people are more vulnerable to hurricanes and floods, Tierney applies demographic data to climatological events and details further risks to vulnerable populations. Her analysis of climate events and their societal risks is insightful and illuminating.
Take-Aways

Summary
Disasters are increasing in severity and social significance. From 1996 to 2005, some 1.5 million people died in disasters worldwide, while many more suffered injuries, illnesses and impoverishment. Disasters can drive economically vulnerable people into permanent poverty and cause lasting mental health issues for survivors.
Climate change promises to amplify the economic toll and societal costs of disasters. Extreme events are growing more common and more costly.
"Rapid and uncontrolled urbanization and intensified development in hazardous areas put ever larger populations at risk."
Large economies can recover from the financial setbacks of disaster, but struggling economies aren't as resilient. While media coverage of calamities focuses on immediate damage, the effects are often long-lasting. For example, the 2017 wildfires in California denuded hillsides, leaving them vulnerable to landslides in subsequent heavy rains. Disasters can disrupt communities by destroying treasured landmarks or displacing longtime businesses. Botched responses can lead to political upheaval. For example, the Chinese Communist Party lost credibility when it tried to cover up a 2003 outbreak of severe acute respiratory syndrome (SARS).
Several catastrophic events in recent history captured the attention of sociologists.
The September 11, 2001, terrorist attacks brought an immediate loss of life and a lasting bureaucratic response to a new threat. The United States created the Department of Homeland Security and formalized its response to the threat of disasters with a focus on disruptive terrorist assaults. In 2004, a 9.0 earthquake in the Indian Ocean generated a tsunami that killed 230,000 people across 14 nations, including Indonesia, Sri Lanka, India and Thailand. The following year, Hurricane Katrina struck New Orleans, laying bare the racial and economic disparities that leave some populations more vulnerable to death or displacement.
"What perhaps did come as a shock was the sheer incompetence of the responding agencies and the viciousness of the racially motivated attacks against Katrina survivors."
New Orleans is a low-lying city, with levees and wetlands that were not strong enough to withstand a direct blow from a major storm. Katrina's impact wasn't especially surprising. But the chaos that ensued afterward became a crucial area of inquiry for sociologists.
Katrina underscored the harsh reality that societies can marginalize people by class, race and gender, thereby creating starkly unequal results in how different social groups come through a disaster. This impact was reinforced in 2011 when an earthquake that devastated Japan produced a tsunami that killed 20,000 people and caused a nuclear plant to go into full meltdown.

The social costs of disasters are not equally distributed.
People living in certain areas and members of certain sociological groups prove more vulnerable to disaster. Coastal zones near warm waters are prone to hurricanes, while parts of California, China, Chile, Japan and New Zealand lie in active earthquake zones. Such vulnerabilities deeply affect the built environment. For example, disasters can damage power grids and fires can wipe out infrastructure.
Sociologists study "social vulnerability" ­ the concept that disasters will do greater harm to certain populations made up of those who are less able or less equipped to recover. In general, poorer people are more vulnerable, as are children and the elderly. The poor are more likely to suffer interruptions in employment and social support, as well as temporary homelessness.
"Disaster-related burdens fall most heavily on the have-nots."
In the United States, the wealth gap manifests along geographic and racial lines. Mississippi has the highest poverty rate among American states, with nearly 21% of its residents living below the poverty line. New Hampshire has the lowest rate, at just 7%. In Puerto Rico, nearly 44% of residents were living in poverty as of 2016, making the territory susceptible to lingering damage when Hurricane Maria struck in 2017.
As for racial and ethnic gaps, as of 2016, white Americans had a median net worth of $171,000, compared to just $20,700 for Hispanics and $17,600 for Black Americans. The poor are less able to prepare for catastrophes and less able to support themselves afterward.
The poor typically can't "stockpile" emergency supplies. They might not own cars, thus complicating efforts to flee an impending hurricane or wildfire. Following South Florida's devastation in Hurricane Andrew in 1992, for example, the mostly white town of Homestead received more public assistance than the predominantly Black community of Florida City. A similar pattern played out after Superstorm Sandy struck New Jersey in 2012. Whites were more likely to win resettlement grants and repair assistance than were Hispanics and Black Americans.
A stark gender gap leaves women more vulnerable during disasters.
Wealth inequality in the United States places women at a disadvantage. Women are far more likely than men to earn incomes below the poverty line, and more than half of American children in poverty live in female-headed households. While white women earn 80% as much as men, Black women earn only 63% as much as men, and Hispanic women barely half as much as men. Women of color are more likely to live in poverty, a level the US government officially set in 2016 as $24,339 a year for a family of four.

"Adding further to their burdens, women in the United States lag behind their counterparts in other affluent countries with respect to paid family leave and child care opportunities."
Men seem to be at greater risk of injury or death in disasters, perhaps because men are less likely to heed evacuation orders.
The risk factors of disasters disproportionately affect children and the elderly.
Children are more susceptible than adults to post-disaster maladies, such as malnutrition, toxic mold, and extreme heat or cold. Children also are more at risk for anxiety and other mental health and lifestyle issues. After Hurricane Katrina, for example, poor children were more likely to move frequently and to lose touch with their extended families, a condition sociologists refer to as "permanent temporariness." This rootlessness can harm children's long-term development. A child growing up in poverty and unstable home circumstances can be more susceptible to the stress of a disaster ­ one more awful, jarring experience in his or her already-difficult life.
"Children are vulnerable to disasters in a variety of ways."
Older people are more at risk after disasters. They tend to be less resilient to extreme temperatures, which can complicate pre-existing cardiovascular and respiratory problems. After Hurricane Katrina, people 60 and older accounted for 75% of the deaths, even though that age cohort made up only 15% of the New Orleans population. The pattern of higher mortality rates among older people held steady in other disasters, including the 2011 earthquake and tsunami in Japan and the 2017 wildfires in Northern California. When a heat wave hit Chicago in 1995, nearly three-quarters of those who died were elderly. Social isolation can leave older people with health issues, especially during disasters.
People with disabilities are more likely to die during and after disasters.
Many disabled people have mobility impairments or sensory deficits. These disadvantages make it harder for them to respond quickly to surprise disasters such as earthquakes. While the Americans with Disabilities Act mandates accommodations for the disabled in everyday life, the public response to disasters does not sufficiently protect them. Often shelters are not set up adequately to take care of them.
"Mass shelters operated by the Red Cross were not equipped to serve persons with disabilities."
During Hurricanes Katrina and Rita, for example, Louisiana residents with sensory disabilities didn't receive warnings in formats they could understand. In one unfortunate example, televised storm warnings lacked closed captions or sign language interpretation. Efforts to create disaster resilience have gained momentum in recent years.

With more people and property in harm's way, developed nations turned their attention to disaster mitigation ­ tougher building codes and other policies designed to harden the built environment. To understand how effective these measures can be, consider two earthquakes that struck the Americas in early 2010. The first, which hit Haiti on January 12, claimed at least thousands of lives; reports say that hundreds of thousands of Haitians may have perished. By contrast, about 500 people died after an 8.8 earthquake hit Chile on February 27. Chile had invested in buildings that architects designed to withstand earthquakes. Haiti had taken no such steps toward resilience.
"If hazards cannot be avoided entirely, a second line of defense is to ensure that the built environment can resist the forces unleashed by disasters."
The sociological concept of "social capital" illuminates understanding how societies invest in disaster resilience. It explains why some societies can build robust systems to protect against and respond to disasters, and others cannot. In the absence of social capital, individual members of society are on their own. With social capital as a motivating factor, societies combine forces.
While social capital can bring a society together and strengthen its response, in some cases, social capital can work against the broader interests of society. In instances of disaster mitigation and response, social capital can exacerbate wealth inequality. For example, in the aftermath of Hurricane Katrina, New Orleans officials steered supplies of FEMA trailers for displaced people away from neighborhoods in which residents frequently voted or showed other signs of civic participation. Instead, they sent trailers to neighborhoods where residents were less likely to mobilize in opposition to them.
Migration trends to prime societies for more severe disasters.
The recent spike in devastating wildfires results from more people living in fire-prone areas. As homeowners built in fire country, the US government changed its policy. The US Forest Service now focuses on suppressing fires. That means forests have even more fuel when uncontainable fires do break out.
Hurricane losses grow as people build in storm-prone regions. In Houston, where Hurricane Harvey struck in 2017, development meant more roads and rooftops, and therefore more impermeable surfaces and fewer places for water to drain. Development replaced ecological features that had offered natural protection from floods. "Amenity migration" ­ affluent people flocking to areas of natural beauty ­ pushed more people into areas vulnerable to catastrophes.
"Induced earthquakes present significant societal challenges."
The hydraulic fracturing (fracking) boom of recent years has caused a spike in earthquakes in places that had previously experienced little seismic activity. Oklahoma, Arkansas, Kansas, New Mexico, Texas and Colorado all saw sharp increases in earthquake activity in recent years.

Deep injection of water as part of the fracking progress destabilizes areas of previous seismic stability. The creation of such unexpected disasters places new stresses on societies: How do areas that never experienced earthquakes respond? Who's responsible for the damage? In Ohio, a series of earthquakes led to new regulations on fracking. Climate change is generating more catastrophic natural disasters. Shifting weather patterns raise the stakes for societies worldwide. Climate change drives more frequent and more catastrophic natural disasters, such as 2013's Typhoon Haiyan, the strongest storm ever to make landfall. Droughts, fires, floods and other extreme ecological events are becoming more common.
"It is not difficult to see how social change is contributing to risk buildup and setting the stage for disasters." Rising sea levels leave low-lying cities such as New York, Miami and Mumbai, and countries such as Bangladesh, at risk of major floods. Warming temperatures change ecosystems in ways that could prove harmful to humans. Rising temperatures leave people increasingly at risk of heat stroke and cardiovascular disease, while warmer weather enables the vectors for such diseases as malaria, dengue fever and Lyme disease. Changing weather patterns and rising ocean acidification imperil food supplies and raise the risk of food insecurity.
About the Author
Kathleen Tierney is a professor of sociology at the University of Colorado Boulder.
