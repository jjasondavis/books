How to Sell in Place
Closing Deals in the New Normal
Tom Searcy and Carajane Moore | Tom Searcy, Carajane Moore ® 2020
The COVID-19 pandemic reshaped B2B selling. However, B2B sales expert Carajane Moore, president of Big Hunt Sales, has managed her sales on a "sell in place" basis for years, as has her colleague, the company's founder, Tom Searcy. They prefer the efficiency and lower costs of selling in place compared to selling face-to-face. Moore and Searcy reveal their selling-in-place practices, protocols and secrets. They provide valuable advice on hardware, software and selling techniques for connecting with clients and closing sales.

During the coronavirus pandemic, B2B salespeople have had to adapt to selling in place.
The COVID-19 pandemic calls on everyone to shelter in place and take other measures to stay safe and keep those around them safe, as well. B2B salespeople now must arrange online meetings with prospects.
"Early on the development of our business, we made a guiding principle decision: Traveling to sell was expensive and wasteful."
This unnerves many salespeople. They believe meeting face-to-face is the only way to develop profitable relationships with prospects. Today, thanks to email, instant messaging, videoconferencing, internet document sharing, and other high-tech communication and connection advances, B2B salespeople can close huge deals working from home.
To sell in place you need to get the attention of your prospects' executive decisionmakers.
To get the attention of top-level decision-makers at a prospective client firm, demonstrate how you can help fix their most pressing problems within three months.
"Offer a unique solution that is unassailable to a problem requiring resolution within 90 days, or you will be ignored."
Executives will assume you already know what their main problems are. If you can solve one of the three primary problems ¡ time, money or risk ¡ executives will listen. Can you reduce wasted time and effort? Can you increase revenues or cut costs? Can you help manage risk and guarantee the firm's future viability and success? Selling in place requires control of the "sales process." To sell remotely, you want to close deals within 30 to 60 days, and no longer. Otherwise, they'll blow away.
For B2B sales, follow a five-stage, scheduled and efficient process: "The Path."
Segment your overall sales process into five distinct, sequential stages. Then move your sales prospects through your process more quickly than you would in the slower, face-to-face days. The Path, a process of intelligent segmentation, demands a firm timeline.
"Selling in place is not about just selling, it is about connecting with those companies who need your services now."

Communicate this timeline ¡ and everything else ¡ to prospects so they understand the important milestones in the process of working together on a sale, including the timetable for making decisions. The Path has four basic requirements:
1. Prep all contacts with pre-work ¡ advance planning ¡ to prepare for the upcoming sales stages. 2. Your pre-work includes planned questions for follow-up connections with prospects. 3. Each stage in this process has various "follow-up requirements." 4. Each stage of the sell-in-place process requires specific actions from your sale's executive
sponsor, your internal "point person." He or she will relay your solution and evidence or data to client decision-makers.
"Selling in place works because you are able to communicate with the right people who are involved in the decision along the way."
The executive sponsor should play a central role. Besides being close to the executive decisionmaker, the executive sponsor can put you in touch with other influential people who may help the main decision-maker shape and plan his or her approach to your offerings. The executive sponsor also can help you break through logjams that could prevent you from closing your deal quickly. Plan on speaking with this person at least once a week until you close. Insist on a formal "executive sponsorship agreement."
The Path is intentional and efficient. It requires a detailed timeline and quickly eliminates frivolous prospects. The Path's five stages, which put you in control of the sales process, are:
1. "Initial contact" is when you determine if clients qualify and decide if you'll work with them.
The ideal prospect is a previous customer. The next best prospect is someone a previous customer referred to your firm. After this, perhaps you have a prospect who contacts you due to your publicity, be it due to a webinar, magazine article or podcast. The least attractive prospect is the one who contacts you after an "internet keyword search."
"Interruptions change; they don't go away. If you are looking for advice and simple rules for making your time at home more productive, go online and there are top 10 lists to help you."
Before you begin Stage 2, do a comprehensive digital investigation. Review relevant company websites, social media and Google mentions. Ask to speak with crucial stakeholders and promise them a detailed business solution. If the prospect's interest in your firm derives from a bylined article, blog post or other personal communication, review what attracted the prospect's attention. Develop an agenda for your upcoming "Discovery" stage and send it to the prospect in advance of your meeting.

2. "Discovery" is when you learn all you can about the prospect.
During this stage, answer these questions about the prospect: How does your firm make its decisions? What are your firm's objectives? What are your firm's success metrics? Who in your firm suffers from the problem(s) it faces? According to your senior leaders, what causes these problems? What do you personally regard as the causes?
"When you want to hunt and land big sales, the key to your success lies in the information you can get."
During this contact with the prospect, detail your understanding of the problem(s). Review your firm's expertise ¡ but not its history ¡ in solving problems similar to the one confronting your prospect. Don't explain just yet how you reach successful outcomes. During Discovery, focus on the prospect, not yourself.Most major ¡ $100,000 or better ¡ B2B purchases require from seven to 11 top executives to agree: They make up the "Buyer's Table." You don't want these people to say no. During this phase, publicly identify the members of the Buyer's Table whose involvement you require.
To get ready for Stage 3, send your Executive Sponsor an agenda for the next meeting. Create an overview that shows how you have worked for similar clients. Include testimonials. Ask the prospect for an organizational chart and for information about his or her firm's approach to the relevant problem.
3. "Assessment" is when you gather information about executives at the "Buyer's Table."
Collect information on the prospect's personnel and corporate culture. You want to know its history and the names of its leaders, especially those who make purchasing decisions. Target your prospect's specific challenges and how your firm would solve those problems. Answer questions from the prospect about the work overview you supplied earlier. Request all the data available about the members of the Buyer's Table, including contact information. Determine the range of specific profits, from lowest to highest, you could earn from the prospect. Use this information to your advantage in your upcoming proposal, but don't discuss specific pricing or specific solutions just yet.
"The unpredictability of the world is now a vivid reality rather than a punchline."
The pre-work before Stage 4 includes speaking with your executive sponsor and members of the Buyer's Table to clarify how each one sees the origin, size and scope of their firm's problem. Send an agenda for the upcoming sell-in-place meeting to the executive sponsor. Ask all Buyer's Table members to attend. Recruit senior leaders from your firm to participate. These people should

have executive positions in your firm parallel to the positions Buyer's Table members hold in their company.
4. "Scope definition" is a collaborative process for developing a solution to a prospect's problems.
"Collaboratively design" a specific solution for the prospect. Your scope definition includes a "program definition," "outcome declaration," "milestones and expectations," a list of the participating or affected people from your and the prospect's company, and a list of possible project variances, such as your fees if the client cancels a working meeting on short notice. Plan to pre-qualify the price of your proposed solution.
"Thin slicing the market does something magical; it makes you the undeniable expert for that defined group of buyers."
The pre-work before Stage 5 includes developing the proposal and sending it to the prospect with an agenda for the upcoming sell-in-place meeting. The scope definition forms the basis of your future proposal.
5. "Proposal review" is when you close the sale. Price your proposal on your timetable ¡ not the prospect's.
This sell-in-place meeting includes speaking via video with your executive sponsor. Before the proposal review, discuss the prospect's expected results. Review the proposal with the prospect. Adjust or amend the proposal as required. This crucial closing phase involves contract terms, logistical plans, and dates for your first invoice and the client's first payment.
"Frequently, a prospect pushes immediately to a proposal with pricing. It is our experience that this does not end well. The reason is that there is not a clear and shared agreement on what the challenge truly is."
Many prospects will ask immediately for a fully priced proposal, even before your analysis of their requirements and your suggestions as to how your offerings can help. Don't agree. Such a request usually crops up because you don't mutually agree about the prospect's challenges. Without that, your proposal devolves to a "pricing exercise," which turns your offerings into replaceable commodities.
Set guidelines for selling in place and assemble the right tools.
Create and follow a set of rules for selling in place. You want to control your schedule, your sales environment and your sales process.
"Selling in place requires you to be the expert about the executive's big problem, not a vendor comparison but a rapid solution to their business problem."

Accept that remote selling is the new normal, and set out to establish your own levels of discipline based on these rules:
À Make every step an appointment ¡ To manage your time, set appointments for each call. À Pay attention ¡ To build trust, focus on your conversations with prospects. À Don't do business while you drive ¡ It's rude, inefficient, unsafe and unengaging. À Mind the noise ¡ Noise might include children yelling, dogs barking or music blaring it. Keep
your environment quiet and business-like. À Agendas are everything ¡ Never waste prospects' time. Follow a plan and a schedule. À Be on time ¡ Start and end meetings punctually. À Plan your questions ¡ Ask your prospect specific questions about his or her organization's
history or current behavior. À Keep good notes on everything ¡ You will refer to these often as you build your
prospect's business solution. À Stay in close touch with your prospects ¡ Connect with prospects once every three days
after your initial contact. Stay accessible. À Forget about "when things get back to normal" ¡ They will never get back to normal.
For B2B salespeople, selling-in-place is normal now.
Make sure you have the tools you need for remote selling:
À Headset ¡ Get the best headset you can afford with "noise-canceling, volume controls" and Bluetooth. Good models include Apple AirPods Pro and Bose. You may need a video gamer headset for webinar communications.
À High-speed internet ¡ Buy the highest speed you can get for communication, research and handling large files.
À "Video package" ¡ Because demand is high, you don't have to spend a lot of money to get the quality you require.
À Quality lighting ¡ Make sure your lighting is neither too bright nor too dim. Avoid glare. À Quality video camera ¡ Logitech Brio's cameras will do the job, as will cameras built into
computers released since 2015. À Video platform ¡ Use Zoom, Skype, Adobe or Marco Polo. Choose the latest version of the
one you find easiest to use. À The right workspace ¡ Curate your setting. What the world sees behind you must enhance
your credibility.
