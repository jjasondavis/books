Think Outside the Building
How Advanced Leaders Can Change the World One Smart Innovation at a Time
Rosabeth Moss Kanter | Public Affairs ® 2020

Harvard Business School professor Rosabeth Moss Kanter argues that solving today's complex social problems requires a new kind of leadership: "advanced leadership." An advanced leader understands that established social institutions, such as big companies, nonprofits or governmental agencies, can become barriers to progress if they focus on defending their turf rather than embracing innovative reforms. Advanced leaders prioritize seeking new ideas, soliciting input from people who usually don't have a voice in social issues, and drawing on expertise from a range of business, scientific and cultural fields

Over time, these institutions erect constraints and boundaries by generating conventional wisdom, enshrining procedures and insulating themselves in silos. The institutional mind-set is a variation on thinking inside the box: thinking "inside the building."
"As every change-seeking leader and entrepreneur knows, innovation rarely comes from deep inside the establishment."
Addressing climate change, health care access, refugee crises, and racial and gender inequality requires a different mode of leadership: advanced leadership. Today's "systems problems" often interconnect and cut across institutional sectors and national boundaries. They require leaders with an entrepreneurial attitude, a predilection for innovative thinking, a willingness to improvise and a knack for building coalitions among diverse stakeholders.
One potential source of advanced leadership is the cohort of successful people who retain the idealism of youth. Such achievers usually build up the management prowess they need to spearhead change and accumulate the necessary social and financial capital. Yet, the skills they polish at the top of their field are not the only ones they must master to drive change. They may need to forget practices that worked well for them in hierarchical organizations. They can no longer lead by fiat. They must master the art of persuasion.
"Big problems" are complex and multifaceted.
Institutions are ineffective at addressing complex systems problems because such issues don't limit themselves to one area: They sprawl across communities and national borders, and they ignore the divisions among industries, organizations and professional fields.
"Advanced leaders see and tackle problems that are intractable precisely because no one really knows exactly what to do or what the direction is."
Such problems generally have five qualities in common:
1. Enormous range ¡ The problems encompass a wide web of contributing factors. 2. "Ambiguity" ¡ Problems like racism or climate change are amorphous. They don't lend
themselves to magic-bullet solutions. Difficulties arise reaching consensus on priorities or approaches. 3. Diffuse responsibility ¡ No one is in charge of these problems. For example, no central authority governs cancer care, gender pay gaps or the health of the oceans. 4. Diverse stakeholders ¡ The problems involve groups with disparate interests, and their views about what to do or not do may conflict. 5. Missing connections ¡ The problems can't match available resources with needs. For example, the UN World Food Programme estimates that 30% of the world's food goes to waste, but no one has found a practical way to bring this surplus food to hungry people.

Advanced leaders are disruptive.
Solving big problems requires people with an innovative mind-set, idealism and entrepreneurial energy, like the motivation behind disruptive start-ups that remake whole industry sectors. For example, St. Louis banker John Dubinsky founded the Contractor Loan Fund by combining the outrage he felt about injustice when he was in college with the capital he accumulated as a banker. The fund aims to help minority contractors acquire the necessary capital to compete for large construction projects.
"When problems can't be solved using conventional approaches, motivated leaders can use innovative thinking to find new routes to change."
Richard Fahey, a corporate lawyer in Ohio, merged his professional capital with his passion for environmentalism to found the Liberian Energy Network, which works to bring affordable green energy to village homes. Former Trader Joe's CEO Doug Rauch created a grocery store to connect surplus food supplies with communities of people who have nutrition deficits, thus reducing the environmental impact of waste.
Change starts with a dream.
Before an advanced leader can make a difference, he or she must have a dream, a personal vision of what making a difference means. Such a dream usually grows out of noticing a gap ¡ something a community lacks or something that needs improvement. The gap is often something the leader emotionally invests in, perhaps from personal experience. His or her dream highlights the human consequences of an otherwise abstract issue.
For Gilberto Dimenstein, the dream was using digital media to reduce inequality in Brazil, his home country. He created a multimedia project, Catraca Livre. On its various platforms, Catraca Livre aims to open access to cultural, educational and career opportunities to everyone. It has promoted free or low-priced concerts, hosted free online courses, mobilized campaigns against sexual harassment, and offered advice on health, education and jobs.
"You don't start the innovation process by consulting a guidebook, reading a cookbook or choosing from a menu. First comes the dream."
With the dream in place, the leader needs to generate ideas for how to bring it to life. This can involve a period of studying, exploring and probing for gaps in the current environment that might suggest new approaches. The goal of the process is to think beyond well-worn paths. Innovators strive to "shake up" their thoughts, as you would shake a kaleidoscope, to connect elements of a situation in new and unexpected ways.

Originally, Rauch thought of addressing the problem of hunger by forming a venture that would gather unsold bread to deliver to food banks. But that idea was only a variation of something food banks already did. Focusing on bread wouldn't achieve the nutritional or the environmental components of his dream. Rauch juggled elements of his idea. He spoke with dietitians, environmentalists and community members. Eventually, recognizing that affordable nutrition was the keystone of his quest, he came up with the Daily Table: Instead of the charity food-bank model, this would be a nonprofit grocery store that served people who live in "food deserts" by selling them nutritious "surplus" ¡ but not expired ¡ food at very low prices.
Sell a change initiative with a compelling story.
For an idea to move forward, an advanced leader needs to inspire support by engaging people's hearts and minds. The leader can't provoke passionate support with dry facts and statistics. That requires telling a story that brings the facts to life and reveals their human implications. Presenting statistics about childhood poverty inspires charitable giving, but telling the story of one child living in poverty spurs even higher levels of philanthropy.
"The advanced leadership quest begins with a belief that the world could be better than it is, accompanied by a feeling of deep discontent that it isn't."
If you want to bring about change by acting as an advanced leader, you can use narrative effectively to:
À Change perceptions of the past ¡ Attempt to reframe the past, especially if people have a distorted picture of history. New Orleans mayor Mitch Landrieu wanted to remove statues of Confederate figures from city land because they offended the black community. Citizens who felt the statues represented history opposed him. Landrieu's research showed that their perception wasn't accurate. Advocacy groups had erected the statues long after the Civil War to promote a revisionist history, probably in support of continued racial oppression. By reframing the statues' history accurately, Landrieu won support for removing them.
À Welcome "diverse stakeholders" ¡ The narrative should appeal to and embrace a wide range of potential participants. An inclusive story attracts supporters and inspires them to reach out to even more people. Forge ties with organizations working in the same field to show that you want to join their work, not supplant it.
À Demonstrate how an innovation will work ¡ Show how your idea would work in practice. Create prototypes or pilot programs that demonstrate its feasibility. A pilot program can provide early "wins" you can build on for further success. Such wins defuse skepticism and soften the potential resentment community members might feel toward what they view as outsiders swooping in to instruct them.

Enlist allies and build coalitions.
To create successful social change, an advanced leader must reach beyond his or her connections to build support across multiple sectors. And once you build this network, managing relationships among stakeholders who may have competing interests can become a full-time job.
"Big successes come when allies are united, opponents are weakened and those on the fence are gradually converted."
A project usually has three types of stakeholders:
1. "Allies" ¡ These people believe in your idea and find it inspiring. They can contribute valuable resources such as funding or supplies, share their knowledge and experience, and lend legitimacy. The Daily Table's Doug Rauch gained support from a community health center's founder whose participation gained the organization its first retail space and muted its possible critics.
2. "Undecideds" ¡ Fence-sitting groups of undecideds want different things. Rauch faced potential suppliers who were reticent to donate food unless they gained a tax deduction. The IRS was skeptical about the nonprofit status of the enterprise and withheld the needed tax status.
3. "Opponents" ¡ Rauch's allies and connections helped resolve issues with opponents, including the IRS and an uncooperative health inspector.
Prepare for the "miserable middle."
Between the excitement of inaugurating a project and the satisfaction of reaching your goal, you may face a long middle period during which surprises and reversals can derail your plans. Facing such setbacks, the advanced leader must determine whether the project is dead or can move forward. Setbacks can include conflict among members of the coalition, a disparity between projected and real progress, and resistance from established institutions.
"The work of advanced leadership is more like improvisational theater, requiring intense listening to the audience, savvy interpretation of the context, give and take with partners, and flexibility to adjust rapidly."
Leaders sometimes give up in the middle without a fight. Surprisingly, this happens frequently with leaders who achieved success in their prior endeavors. Such leaders may be accustomed to bending circumstances to their will and unable to adapt to an environment of roadblocks, dissension and detours. Others may experience disenchantment because they misunderstood or underestimated the challenges of a social-change project. Some successful people stretch themselves too thin by chasing too many opportunities, such as requests to serve as a mentor, invitations to sit on boards or offers of speaking engagements.

Plan for growth to ensure the greatest impact. A successful social innovation does more than meet its benchmarks. Bringing electric light to a Liberian village or opening your first surplus-food retail location may be cause for celebration, but innovation projects can't rest on their laurels.
"For anyone who wishes that the world could be a better place, there is no shortage of pressing problems to consider." A successful project must aim for "impact." Having an impact means generating a clear, significant influence on the wider world and its institutions, laws and cultural attitudes.To ensure impact, a leader must plan on scaling his or her social venture by multiplying the number of its customers or users, serving new locations, enlarging the scope of its activities, inspiring others to launch similar initiatives or finding partners who can fill in the venture's shortcomings. A growth plan should include three phases: 1. Demonstrate ¡ Form a pilot program or prototype that shows how the idea works and proves that it is practical. 2. Replicate ¡ Once the project succeeds in its initial environment, show that you can repeat that success in a second environment. 3. Expand ¡ Help more and more people adopt your idea until it becomes the new "institutional pathway."
About the Author
Rosabeth Moss Kanter is the Ernest L. Arbuckle professor at the Harvard Business School and co-founder of the Harvard University Advanced Leadership Initiative. She is the author or coauthor of 20 books, including Confidence: How Winning Streaks and Losing Streaks Begin and End; Men and Women of the Corporation, and SuperCorp: How Vanguard Companies Create Innovation, Profits, Growth, and Social Good.
