Scaling Leadership
Building Organizational Capability and Capacity to Create Outcomes that Matter Most
Robert J. Anderson and William A. Adams | Wiley ® 2019
To deal with today's unsettled business environment, companies must scale up their leadership. Robert J. Anderson and Bill Adams explain the best methods, drawing from research asking 50,000 leaders and employees worldwide, "What kind of leadership, if it existed, would enable the organization to thrive in its current marketplace and into the future?" The study's goal was to identify the nature of leadership "stripped of all the models, theories, frameworks and pontifications" Ł "unplugged" and "unvarnished." This comprehensive, technically impressive leadership book will serve leaders everywhere.

Scale equals growth, life's most essential dynamic.
"Life scales, or it dies." For example, a giant locust tree sheds millions of miniscule seeds. Each seed could scale up into another mammoth locust tree and produce millions more seeds. Each tiny locust seed represents a potential future forest of giant locust trees Ł because of scale.
"Rapid growth solves a lot of problems and brings with it a whole lot more."
Businesses are like locust trees. The basic business imperative is toward growth. Without growth and scale, businesses die. Business growth goes beyond developing quality products and services to include securing financing and recruiting employees. For sustainable growth, business leaders must scale up with the growth of their organizations.
In business, scaling up leadership calls for the intelligent, effective development of new leaders.
Scaling up corporate leadership calls on mature, high-quality, effective leaders to develop additional mature, high-quality, effective leaders. Reliable business growth demands regular development of new leaders to manage expansion. Leadership scaling calls for "innovation, adaptability, sustainability, agility and engagement."
"Leaders learn out loud, and the best leaders do so openly, vulnerably and in deep relationship."
Leadership scaling enables manageable growth. Enlightened leadership depends on each person exercising genuine, inspired and goal-oriented "leadership of self." However, this takes training and development. Leaders who thrive directing a firm with $30 million in annual revenues could be out of their depth running the same firm with $100 million in annual revenues.
Leaders who strive to increase their leadership skills engage in a bold spiritual journey.
Noble leadership requires leaders to be the best people they can be. Leaders who engage in this journey partake in a "spiritual boot camp" to become better people and better leaders. Because of the stakes involved, leaders who undergo a spiritual boot camp become more willing to ask for help. They understand and value humility.
Leaders should take three steps to "scale their leadership" and positively transform their companies.

1. "Start with yourself" Ł Examine your leadership strengths and weaknesses using an industry-standard self-evaluation tool. In their book Immunity to Change, Robert Kegan and Lisa Lahey suggest that leaders who want to change should focus on "one big thing" Ł the single change that will unlock their leadership potential.
2. "Develop leadership teams" Ł Think of your team as a team of leaders. Create a leadership "development agenda" for your team members. Assess their individual effectiveness and their effectiveness as a group.
3. "Build leadership systems" Ł Build a "developmental organization" by institutionalizing your "development agenda."
Leaders range across a "full spectrum" of three types of leadership.
Three levels make up the "full spectrum of leadership." Ideally, leaders should advance from the unenlightened "reactive" level to the more innovative, mid-point "creative" level. After that Ł following a big personal jump Ł they can achieve the inspired "integral" level of leadership.
"Scale can only be achieved by developing capacity and capability in others. Effective leaders develop other leaders."
Consider a bell curve that identifies the proportion of leadership qualities that appear at its two ends and in the center. The curve shows unenlightened "high-reactive, low-creative" leaders (28%) on one end of the spectrum and the more innovative "high-creative, low-reactive" leaders (also 28%) at the other. The larger midsection (44%) is evenly split between "mid-reactive" and "midcreative." Each group has strengths and weaknesses. The integral level is the third tier, after the reactive and creative levels.
"We build our teams, improve our collective effectiveness, and scale our leadership by doing this work together Ł transparently and vulnerably Ł and at every level in the organization."
Reactive leaders are always on the defensive. They micromanage. They stifle their creative impulses and those of their team members. Their retrograde orientation reduces their effectiveness. Reactive leaders prefer hierarchical, bureaucratic environments and can drive the people they lead to burnout. Reactive leaders diminish their employees' productivity and returns. Sadly, most leaders are reactive.
"Most of us are underwater, over our heads. We have no more time to give, yet the organization wants to grow, pushes us to grow, pulls us internationally."
Conventional leaders conform to common wisdom; creative leaders move beyond it. Creative leadership is the opposite of reactive leadership. Creative leaders are imaginative and openminded; they entertain new ideas. Creative leaders focuses on achieving significant goals. Creative leaders aren't creative only for themselves. "By its nature, leadership will push us to our limits Ł and maybe beyond them. It will call on the highest and best of who we are and what we offer to meet the daily challenges we face."
Integral leaders make a giant leap by being effective, mature, wise visionaries Ł yet they represent only 5% of current leaders. They understand they can always improve, so they exemplify the positive concept of "forever unfinished-ness." Integral leaders understand that they don't know everything. They willingly "ask for help," detach from ego and embrace lofty motivations. As they relinquish power to a "higher purpose," they become knowledgeable and motivated. They have grace and faith that "unseen forces" move in their favor.
"It is hard to change deeply grooved patterns in how we show up every day in our lives and our leadership."
To make an accurate judgment about whether you are reactive, creative or integral, turn to your subordinates, who always know your qualities and deficiencies. Those working for you see your strengths, weaknesses and liabilities on a daily basis. Your "intuitive moments" can advance the way you lead. Albert Einstein believed that trusting intuition opens a person to wisdom, while seeking only the facts, without a larger context, crushes the imagination. He regarded intuition as the fuel of creativity.
Integral leaders are servant leaders.
Integral leaders dedicate themselves to the welfare and betterment of their companies and the people they lead. They regard leadership as an opportunity to be of service. Integral leaders are reflective and believe that thinking things through delivers benefits. Reflection helps leaders operate in an organized way, makes them more intentional, increases their alertness, enables them to operate with an enhanced perspective, and puts them in touch with their emotional and physical states.
"Ineffective leadership caps the ability of an organization to grow, and this liability is exposed the minute you start to scale."
Effective leaders know how to communicate with their people. Integral leaders nourish teams and hear what their people have to say. They demonstrate integrity, vision, passion and drive. They are personable and positive, and they develop and empower their employees. Integral leaders get the best from those they lead. They are architects of their companies' renewal and positive future. They make their employees feel like genuine partners. This approach needs to be the future of leadership.

Effective scaling to integral leadership requires specific conditions.
At spiritual boot camp, leaders retire their "socialized, dependent" and "reactive" selves and turbocharge their "self-authored, independent" and "creative" selves. Some even achieve integrated leadership. Leaders should pursue six conditions as they scale up their abilities:
1. "Creative leadership" Ł Reactive leadership, which is retrogressive, will never scale properly. Reactive leaders must upgrade to creative leadership on the way to integral leadership. Creative leaders inspire the people they lead. They make those around them better. They know when to get in front and lead and when to step back and function as just another team member. Creative leaders promote an "open, honest, authentic, optimistic, generative and innovative culture."
2. "Deep relationship" Ł Think of this as relating well to the people you lead. Integral leaders have affection for the people they lead. On high-performing teams, this affection Ł or love Ł is a common characteristic of all team members. Great leaders exemplify love. In doing so, they scale up the "capacity and capability" of the people on their teams. The stronger the relationships between leaders and the people they lead, the greater will be the opportunity to scale leadership within the organization. Strong relationships unlock the potential of human imagination and enable team members to persevere together.
3. "Radically human" Ł With humility, vulnerability and courage, integral leaders learn from everyone around them. Radically human leaders understand that disruption and significant change in their organizations begins with them. These astute, mature leaders see knowing and controlling as illusions. They substitute learning for knowing and empowering for controlling.
4. "Systems awareness" Ł How leaders design and organize their companies Ł the systems approach they utilize Ł will determine how these companies fare in the future. Feedback enables leaders to operate intelligently. Creative and integral leaders rely on, value, respect and leverage feedback. They institutionalize robust "feedback systems" as part of developing their organizations.
5. "Purposeful achievement" Ł Inspiring leaders motivate others to strive to achieve a higher purpose. Such leaders channel their people's collective hopes and dreams to accomplish lofty goals. Having an elevated purpose prompts people and systems to evolve. Purposefulness is the "beating heart of creative leadership."
6. "Generative tension" Ł Gaps can loom between employees' aspirations and the reality of their organizational situation. Generative tension accompanies these gaps. Great leaders work to close the gaps between goals and reality. This involves ambitious leadership scaling within organizations. To direct this scaling, quality leaders must function as their firms' chief development officers.

The "Leadership Circle Profile Self-Assessment" provides valuable feedback on leaders' effectiveness.
The authors' Leadership Circle Profile Self-Assessment (LCP), based on the "Universal Model of Leadership," can provide valuable feedback on leaders' effectiveness and can analyze "the creative and reactive elements" in a leader's approach to leadership. Encapsulating 360-degree feedback results, the LCP enables quantitative and qualitative leadership assessments of individual and collective leadership.
"The higher we go in organizations, the less feedback we get because it feels risky to speak truth to power."
Analysts use a circle to portray LCP results graphically. The upper hemisphere Ł the "Creative" half Ł addresses positive leadership skills. In the outer ring of the circle lie specific leadership competencies, like integrity, strategic focus and balance. The lower hemisphere of the LCP circle is the "Reactive" half and identifies reactive tendencies that limit a leader's effectiveness. Having profiles for each leader and leadership team member gives you a "collective leadership profile."
Strong leaders value objective feedback.
Comprehensive feedback is essential for sound leadership and quality leadership scaling. Feedback energizes leadership development. People who provide unfiltered feedback will describe a leader's positive and negative traits. All leaders operate in a "feedback-rich environment" and should take advantage of it. Doing so isn't complicated. Simply ask your people questions and listen to their answers. Objective feedback can often be tough to take, but it's necessary.
Organizations today operate in a VUCA world.
Futurist Bob Johansen says that modern business leaders work in a VUCA business climate that's characterized by volatility, uncertainty, complexity and ambiguity. In this environment, "disruptive change" is a giant monkey wrench that makes maintaining the standard level of business leadership a huge challenge. According to Johansen, as difficult as things are now for business leaders, they're going to get worse.
"Over the past decade, the term `VUCA' has become part of the business lexicon as executives, managers, and others grapple with its effects."
The VUCA world overloads the "internal operating system" of many leaders, who are often in over their heads. Leaders who can't deal effectively with the topsy-turvy business world will reap a
