Clockwork
Design Your Business to Run Itself
Mike Michalowicz | Portfolio ® 2018
Entrepreneur Mike Michalowicz provides a simple, well-structured, easy-to-follow guide to building a small business. He urges fellow entrepreneurs to stop doing all the work and making all the decisions. In seven steps, he shows you how to hire and develop a team, so you gradually can delegate your tasks and decisions and leave your staff to run the business day by day while you design it for efficiency and impact. For any stressed-out entrepreneur working crushing hours for no apparent reward, he offers an organized, realistic path to success that doesn't require sacrificing your friends and family.

Summary
Most entrepreneurs never break free of the chains that enslave them and their businesses.
Entrepreneurs in the United States work longer hours than their employees, and commonly take no vacation. Instead, entrepreneurs should approach work systematically, prioritize more effectively and spend more time doing what they love.
"Time is everything. Every. Single. Thing. Time is the only thing in the universe...that is not renewable. Either you use it wisely or you don't."
As an entrepreneur, you probably believe you must work harder and suffer more than anyone else to succeed. Habits form and, in an instant, you've worked five years with little to show. You've chased opportunities and put out fires, but failed to organize your business. This approach can consume you and keep you from ever getting ahead.
Stop doing things. Spend more time thinking about and designing your business.
Determine where you are now against the "4D Mix":
1. "Doing" Ł In this phase, you do everything because you have no employees. About 80% of US businesses never break free from this.
2. "Deciding" Ł When you hire staff, you still make all the decisions, making yourself indispensable to the point of exhaustion.
3. "Delegating" Ł You share the workload and the decisions. 4. "Designing" Ł You earn the time to Design. Your business doesn't need you to survive day to
day.
"A business can only experience extraordinary progress with extraordinary design."
When you hire employees, you probably want to guide them and answer their questions. Don't. That creates learned helplessness. Let them learn by doing and make their own decisions. Delegate. Your firm should spend 80% of its efforts doing, 2% deciding, 8% delegating, and 10% designing Ł this is the "Four D 80/2/8/10" pattern. As CEO, you should spend 99% of your effort designing, and no time doing. Assess your current situation, and then take six steps to "make your business run itself":
Step one: "Analyze your company's time."
Record every task for five days Ł how much time you spend on it, and what category or phase of the 4D Mix it falls into. Add up your Doing, Deciding, Delegating and Designing time. Now you have a benchmark to rebalance toward the ideal mix.

Step two: "Declare your company's Queen Bee Role (QBR)."
Bees build their colonies systematically and efficiently. All activity revolves around protecting the queen as long as she is producing eggs. If she doesn't produce, the hive replaces her. The role matters most, not the person or bee who performs it. Discover your QBR and make it everyone's most important concern. To determine your QBR, record the six most important things each person does, such as sales or coding.
"Everyone else on your team must prioritize protecting the QBR so that the role can be fulfilled...only then can they focus on their primary job."
Note the time each person spends on each item. Then, drop two of your six tasks and identify things other people can do. Remove another job, and another, until you have only two left. Which must succeed for your business to succeed? That task is your primary job. Each employee should have one primary job. It comes first, unless the QBR needs attention. Take each employee's primary task and repeat the process of elimination for the company. Keep reducing until you have one overriding priority for the firm. That's your QBR.
Step three: "Protect and serve the QBR."
Hospital emergency rooms have one QBR: Get patients in front of doctors for diagnosis and appropriate action. Every nurse, receptionist, physician's assistant, technician and janitor obsesses about freeing doctors to see incoming patients. Then should they focus on their primary tasks.
"The real holy grail is organizational efficiency. Productivity gets you in the ballpark. Organizational efficiency gets you hitting home runs."
How can you eliminate, outsource or streamline the activities furthest from your QBR? Can you cut a weekly meeting? Can you eliminate a time-consuming monthly report no one reads? Can you post an FAQ for customers so you don't have to answer the same questions repeatedly? Streamline what you can, trash what you can and outsource where possible. Mark things you have to keep doing now but can cut later. "Trash, transfer or trim" everything possible to maximize time for serving your QBR. Do this for everyone in your firm. Keep your most skilled, important tasks inhouse; outsource everything you can't trash. If you can't decide whether to stop doing something, stop temporarily to see if you miss it.
Step four: "Capture systems."
Don't stress about creating or documenting your systems. You can't hope to document and maintain descriptions of them in manuals. Record the way you do things Ł your systems Ł in voice recordings or narrated videos you make with a smartphone or screen capture software. Save your recordings in four directories corresponding to "Attracting, Converting, Delivering for" and

"Collecting from" customers. For example, marketing videos go into your Attract directory. Any processes dealing with money Ł paying bills or invoicing Ł go into your Collect folder. Before you know it, you'll have a training library anyone can access. When employees find something missing or outdated, encourage them to find the answers and update the content. This makes them trainers and learners, and keeps your library current.
"Productivity doesn't get you out of the doing; it just gets you doing more."
By documenting your processes and systems, you'll gradually find you can delegate fully and completely. Unless you believe a decision might harm the firm, insist that employees make their own Ł even if you don't agree. Afterward, talk to them to see what they learned. To wean employees off wanting you to decide everything, let them make mistakes they learn from. Encourage and reward people who take risks, fail and learn.
Step five: "Balance the team."
Before you build your team, know your strengths and passions so you hire people with complementary traits. Knowing your QBR and primary roles, align people to the right roles for their strengths, and connect them to your learning library. Align their work so they do what they love and excel.
"Have a few expensive skilled team members at your business, focused almost exclusively on doing the most skilled work, and transferring all the other necessary, but easy, repeating tasks down the skill chain."
Choose diverse people, not clones of yourself. Select them for their eagerness to learn and work, not for experience and skills. You can teach skills, not character. Extend responsibility and trust to your new hires slowly. Communicate your firm's mission and purpose frequently. List all the important things that must get done Ł not jobs Ł and match the best people to them. This tailors jobs to each person's strengths. By the end of this process, you should get close to the Four D 80/2/8/10 balance.
Step six: "Know who you're serving."
Determine who your best customers are and the best ways to serve them. Commit to them. Write: "Our commitment is to serve [whom] by [how]." Don't target more than one kind of client. Pick a niche and serve it. Don't let your niche choose you; do what you do because you want to do it. To figure out your ideal clientele, take your QBR and think about whom it serves best. Then, assuming you have an ideal client, seek more like that one. The group you want to sell to Ł lawyers, cooks, vineyard owners, or anyone else Ł should have a community or association where you can access them, a "congregation point."

"The more services you provide to a wider mix of customers, the more variability you have, and the harder it becomes to provide extraordinary and consistent services."
If you don't know your best niche, create a four-column table. In the first column, rank your existing customers from the biggest spenders to the least. In the next column, score them against how much you like working with them. In the third, record the niche they occupy and, in the fourth, their congregation points. Identify the narrowest niche possible from this list.
Step seven: "Keep an eye on your business."
You now should know your QBR, who your niche is and how you serve them. Now, put it all together. You have people in your business with various skills and capabilities. Put them in the right places at the right time to leverage them fully. Monitor and measure your business to spot and fix problems.
"Cash is the most important yet most overlooked part of every business."
Keep your metrics simple; stick with things you observe directly. For example, the gauges in your car alert you to driving too fast or not having enough gas. Knowing these things, you slow down and get gas. In your business, similar simple metrics can tell you where you need to take action. When your gauges read as they should, work on designing and organizing your business for more success. Choose one or more metric for each of your four key activities. You want a metric that tracks how many new leads you Attract per day, how many you Convert within a space of time, and what percentage of customers you retain Ł a measure of Delivering Ł plus a metric or two for Collecting, such as monitoring accounts receivables.
Identify a way to measure your QBR directly. Track between five and eight simple but informative metrics that reveal issues for you to spot and adjust. Set goals for each one. When things go wrong, check your metrics and address one problem at a time. Experiment, change things and observe the results. If sales fall, for example, but your metric on leads looks normal, investigate what changed in sales. Start making small adjustments, one at a time, to see their effects until you isolate the cause. If you panic and try several things at once, you might make the problem worse and have no way of knowing the cause. Your metrics give you an honest snapshot of the critical components of your business, including your QBR.
Resist pressure from yourself and others to jump back into Doing rather than Designing.
Once you implement the seven steps and your teams run your business without you, you may not feel necessary. Resist the temptation to reinsert yourself to feed your ego. Now you have time to think about your business and design it for optimal effectiveness. Show your partners, staff and investors why you had to remove yourself from operations for the business to grow.

To know whether your company can run without you, take a vacation from your business with no contact.
Take a long break from the business Ł at least four weeks during which you have no contact. That gives your team enough time to handle Attracting, Converting, Delivering and Collecting without you. In four weeks they will face at least one problem or crisis in your absence. Afterward, you'll know you built a "Clockwork" business. You may have tweaks to adjust, but you'll have more confidence in your team, and they will be more confident in themselves.
"The designing work is about creating the best way to do things your clients value, and have your company do those things on automatic."
Plan your four-week break 18 months out. Choose your dates first, then tell your family and close friends. Do the Clockwork process Ł the seven steps Ł to prepare. Announce your plans to your team and get their support. Align and assign responsibilities. Institute a brief "daily huddle" to share progress and key tasks.
Keep using the seven steps to reduce your Doing time. About one year out, book and pay for your vacation to deepen your commitment. Keep cutting back on your Doing time. At six months out, take a full week off without contact as a trial run. Meet afterward to debrief and address issues. By two months out, 95% of your time should go toward Designing and nothing toward Doing. At four months out, repeat your one-week vacation. Return for a week, debrief and do it again. Before you leave, make sure you've achieved 99% Design time. Arrange an emergency contact. Take a final week off, but this time, spend it at the office observing. Make any final changes and then go. Debrief when you get back and schedule your next break Ł one a year.
You can systematize any business, no matter how unique.
First ask "who will get the work done," not how. This puts you in the mind-set to design and streamline your business. Consider the things you would do with the rest of your life if you had all the money in the world. Then consider what you would most want to do if you had no money and had to support yourself. When you come to the same answer for both questions, you've found your calling. Knowing this, design your business so you can do more of it, while taking time for yourself and your family and friends.
