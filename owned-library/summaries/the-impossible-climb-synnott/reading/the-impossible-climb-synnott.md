The Impossible Climb
Alex Honnold, El Capitan, and the Climbing Life
Mark Synnott | © 2019 From THE IMPOSSIBLE CLIMB by Mark Synnott Summarized by arrangement with Dutton, an imprint of Penguin Publishing Group, a division of Penguin Random House LLC.

Alex Honnold free solos, climbing with no ropes or protection. A fall from near the top of the 3,000-foot El Capitan, Yosemite's biggest cliff, would last 14 seconds and prove fatal. But Honnold did not fall. He became the first person to free solo El Cap, as author Mark Synnott watched. He details big-wall climbing history and marvels at Honnold's strength and grace. The author pulls no punches as he describes feuds, drug use and rivalries among climbing's most fearless adventurers. Honnold's feat will inspire climbers and non-climbers alike.

Summary
El Capitan towers 3,000 feet over the Yosemite Valley, attracting rock climbers from all over the world.
El Capitan is Yosemite's largest rock wall. Native Americans called the towering rock face "To-tokon oo-lah," or "rock chief," because from one angle it looks like a man's head. Physician Lafayette Bunnell translated that name into Spanish in his 1880 book, Discovery of the Yosemite and the India War of 1851. Since then, the wall's name has been El Capitan. "El Cap" draws rock climbers from around the world.
Rock climbing in Yosemite Valley progressed from big-wall "aid climbing" ascents in the 1950s to "free soloing" by elite climbers today.
By the 1950s, Yosemite climbers launched a "golden age" when Royal Robbins and Warren Harding scaled big walls in the valley using ropes and steel pitons. Their use of aid climbing depended on a great deal of equipment, which allowed them to rest on ropes and drill holes in the rock to place their protective gear. Later generations, notably the Stonemasters ­ a freewheeling group that took over the Yosemite climbing scene in the mid-1970s ­ emphasized climbing without embedded protection, using ropes for rest or relying on stirrups that attached to the ropes.
"I can depart in peace, for I have here seen the power and glory of a Supreme being: the majesty of His handy-work is in that `Testimony of the Rocks'." (author Lafayette Bunnell on seeing El Capitan, 1851)
Climbers regarded this style as far more challenging and more aesthetically beautiful. Then climbers John Bachar and John Long had a revelation. The two were training by climbing boulders ­ "bouldering" ­ in California's Joshua Tree National Park. Bouldering involves climbing without ropes or safety gear, but remaining only a few body lengths off the ground, so a fall does not result in serious injury.
"To put that much on the line, to work so hard for something that doesn't have any quantifiable value, it's just this wonderful, crazy, uniquely human thing." (climber Peter Croft)
Long suggested climbing a higher route without ropes. When Bachar balked, Long asked, "If you climbed this [bouldering] route 100 times, how many times would you fall?" Bachar answered, "None." And free soloing was born.

Free soloing is climbing an entire pitch ­ 150 to 230 vertical feet ­ or many pitches, without ropes or other protective equipment. Bachar said climbing higher routes without the burden of gear was "better than doing drugs." Bachar, Long and the rest of the Stonemasters would know, because they did a lot of drugs. In 1977, a plane carrying 6,000 pounds of marijuana crashed in a lake somewhere in the Yosemite bush. Climbers found the crash site and stole, smoked or sold the cache before the DEA could find it.
"Climbing was his one salvation, and he hardly ever missed a day."
Years later, young free-solo climber Alex Honnold echoed Bachar's sentiment ­ but not the one about drugs, because Honnold abstains from all drugs and alcohol. Just as Bachar realized he wouldn't fall if he climbed a bouldering route 100 times, Honnold reminds critics that his probability of falling doesn't increase simply because a rope-free fall would be certain death. He likens free soloing to driving a car: It's terrifying at first, but the more you do it, the easier it becomes.
In 2004, rescue personnel airlifted Alex Honnold off Mount Tallac in California after he fell hundreds of feet down a gully.
Honnold castigated himself in his climbing diary on December 27, 2004, the day after he slid hundreds of feet down a steep, icy gully into a jumble of granite boulders on California's Mount Tallac. He'd climbed alone through the snow covering the steep side of the gully. If he hadn't had his phone, he might have died there alone. His mom had given him the phone for Christmas less than 24 hours before his plummet. Honnold called her before losing consciousness. Hours later, a helicopter fought through high winds to make a dangerous landing and evacuate him. Later, he was mad at himself that he hadn't just walked out, though he suffered a concussion, sinus and tooth damage, a fractured right hand, and cuts needing stitches on his hands and face.
"An adrenaline bath might help you run faster than you ever have, but if you're trying to find the precise body position to unlock an enigmatic sequence of climbing moves, it would be like using a chainsaw to perform surgery."
He always preferred to climb alone. When Honnold climbed with more seasoned mountaineers, they were taken aback when he didn't wear a helmet or chose to free solo unthinkable routes on which a fall meant certain disaster. Later in his career, the photographers and writers following Honnold's exploits spent sleepless nights debating the ethics of documenting his possibly fatal climbs. Honnold's blasé attitude about his near-death experiences, as his journal entry shows, was typical. The Tallac fall was not. Later in his career, he built a reputation for one outstanding skill: not falling when it mattered. Until that point, falling hadn't been much of an issue, because Honnold had done most of his early climbing indoors.

Honnold began indoor rock climbing in 2000 to cope with his parents' divorce, deaths in the family and a lonely year at UC Berkeley.
A week after his accident on Mount Tallac, Honnold punished his battered body by biking to the climbing gym in Sacramento, California, where he'd been a member since it opened in 2000. A cast on his right arm covered his hand except for his pinky and ring finger, but he hefted himself up the walls, aggravating his injury and slowing his recovery. The accident was one of several difficult events in his life over the previous couple of years. He'd lost his grandfather, with whom he'd been close. His parents divorced, and his dad died of a heart attack.
"I've actually spent more time climbing than driving. Imagine New York City cabbies and all the outrageous things they do. That's kind of like me with my climbing." (Honnold)
Honnold dropped out of UC Berkeley after a lonely first year. Climbing mollified his angst, and he was good at it. During the chaos that marked those years, Honnold placed second at 2004's National Climbing Championships. After his father's death, Honnold became the beneficiary of a small life insurance policy that provided him about $300 every month. With this money and his mom's old Chevy minivan, he set out to pursue the "dirtbag" climbing lifestyle. Starting in April 2005, he wandered from Joshua Tree to the Mojave Desert to the Sierra Nevadas.
"What it all came down to was that for Alex Honnold, a life lived less than fully is a fate worse than dying young."
Honnold lived in the van until it broke down. He switched to a tent, and bummed rides or rode his bike to various climbing spots. He set a goal to free climb 25 pitches a day, each pitch being around 200 feet. Honnold became a free soloist almost out of necessity. He was too shy to make friends with other climbers who could have become climbing partners.
Rock climbers in Yosemite Valley formed a wild counterculture often at odds with the park service.
Visitors can stay in Yosemite Park for only 30 days at a time, but climbers usually want to take advantage of the six-month climbing season. Climbers try to evade the park service by camping in secret spots or living in caves or, as climber "Chongo Chuck" preferred, camping out on El Cap. Chongo would climb ropes fixed by a group that preceded him up the wall, set up a portaledge ­ a small, hanging tent ­ and live on the cliff.
"The Stone Monkeys were known for their antics both on and off the rock."
Chuck was the mascot of "Chongo Nation," the generation of Yosemite Valley climbers that emerged between the Stonemasters of the '70s and the Stone Monkeys of the '90s. The Stone Monkeys free climbed new routes all over Yosemite Valley, had wild, all-night parties, slept illegally in hidden campsites and survived off leftover tourist food they snatched from picnic tables. This was the climbing scene Honnold discovered when he first made his way into Yosemite Valley.
Honnold didn't like to self-promote, but by 2007 his incredible feats became common knowledge in the Yosemite climbing community.
When Honnold arrived in Yosemite, he continued climbing alone, slowly improving, and free soloing harder and harder routes. He was unknown in the climbing world and didn't make friends among the Stone Monkeys. Few knew who he was until fellow climber James Lucas "sprayed" for him on SuperTopo.com, an online climbers' forum. "Spraying" is climber jargon for selfpromotion. For high-level climbers like Honnold, spraying can lead to sponsorships from major companies.
"Alex hates rest days."
Nobody knew Honnold, and he wouldn't promote himself, so Lucas promoted his elite-level climbing feats: free soloing The Pipeline, the Lightning Bolt Cracks, Chud, Astroman and the Regular North Face of Rostrum. These climbs all rate between "5.10 and 5.13a" on the Yosemite Decimal System (YDS), a scale that denotes the difficulty of a climb. Free soloing climbs of that difficulty is extraordinary and out of reach for almost all climbers. Eventually, Honnold's skill spoke for itself. He gained the attention of sponsors like The North Face, along with writer David Roberts, who would help Honnold write his autobiography and Oscar-winning filmmakers Elizabeth Chai Vasarhelyi and her husband Jimmy Chin.
Honnold wanted to free solo the Freerider, a notoriously difficult route up El Capitan. Word of his project started spreading in early 2016.
When Honnold told Chai Vasarhelyi he wanted to free solo El Capitan, she discussed it with her husband. The climb would be an exciting focus for the documentary film they were making about Honnold, but the couple had grave reservations. A single wrong move would mean Honnold's immediate death. If the couple filmed the climb, his final moments would be on camera if he fell. Chin confided his concerns to author Mark Synnott, who wanted to write about the climbing project. Synnott shared Chin's ethical worries. An accomplished mountaineer, he had climbed Freerider, but with safety gear and, ultimately, aid. After long deliberation, the documentarians realized Honnold was going to attempt the free solo whether they filmed it or not. Tommy Caldwell, a professional climber who helped Honnold prepare for the climb, came to the same conclusion. Caldwell realized he could not dissuade Honnold, and so he became dedicated to helping him succeed.

On June 3, 2017, Alex Honnold successfully free soloed Freerider on El Cap in only three hours and 56 minutes. After an aborted attempt in November 2016, Honnold climbed the entire height of El Capitan with no rope or protective gear in June 2017. An elite team of climber-cinematographers hung from the wall and filmed from the ground. Honnold made it to the top with little fanfare because few people knew he was going to attempt the climb that day.
"Oh my god. You're hiking barefoot. You're so tough." (A tourist on seeing Honnold atop Half Dome, not knowing he'd just completed a grueling free solo) Later that morning, after making the climb, Honnold told Synnott he wanted to do some "hangboarding." A hangboard is climber's training tool. Honnold's hangboard was an eight-inchhigh, two-foot-long piece of wood with hollows of various sizes drilled into the surface. He hangs from the board to strengthen the muscles in his fingers, arms, back and shoulders. Synnott pointed out that any other person who'd completed a feat like free soloing El Cap would probably take the afternoon off. Honnold replied that his schedule called for hangboarding that day, and he was not going to miss a session.
