Live From Cupertino
How Apple Used Words, Music, and Performance to Build the World's Best Sales Machine
Michael Hageloh | Post Hill ę 2019
Michael Hageloh, who worked in sales at Apple for 22 years, details how Apple prioritized customer relationships over revenue and put humanity, not hardware, at the heart of its marketing. His narrative pays homage to Steve Jobs, who energized Apple's account executives by encouraging them to market themselves and to make mistakes fearlessly. Salespeople have the most to gain from this insider's look at how Apple thrived by focusing on people.

Summary
Apple co-founder Steve Jobs helped the company turn storytelling into a potent selling tool. Music played a key role in transforming Apple from a computer company to a globally influential innovator. It started with the company's first blockbuster product for mobile digital devices: the iPod, which it introduced in 2001 as an upgraded alternative to dysfunctional MP3 digital music players. Apple's foray into mobile music demonstrated that it understood its products as creations, like songs, which people may want but no one needs. Each member of the Apple team brought

Sony could have introduced a product similar to the iPod because it owned the music publishing and licensing giant BMI. But infighting over content and licensing terms scuttled efforts to develop a digital music device as popular as the Sony Walkman cassette player. Sony lacked an internal "common vision," which Apple had. A common vision allows a company to minimize friction and convey its identity to the market.
"Great salespeople are artists and great listeners. That's what we had at Apple, and it helped that so many of us were musicians."
Apple fired Steve Jobs in 1988, and he returned to the company in 1997. Even before he came back to Apple, Jobs was obsessed with preparation, and under his leadership, that came to characterize the company as well. He spent days ş sometimes a week or longer ş rehearsing and refining each of his major speeches to the Apple sales team. Other technology company leaders were better than Jobs at design, engineering and technology, but he was a superb salesman and storyteller. He triumphed over recency bias ş the tendency to believe that current conditions will persist forever ş by describing his vision of the future, which engaged audiences inside and outside the company. Storytelling, the essence of music, was central to the Apple sales approach.
Salespeople who share personal stories build trust by revealing their character through the sacrifices they make and the values they hold ş not just through what they do, but why they do it. Customers have a greater appreciation for why someone works in sales than in retail.
A good story has a setup, a conflict and a resolution of the conflict. And it is brief. Seasoned Apple sales professionals wrap up their interactions with prospects promptly and leave them wanting to hear more. That allows the selling to happen later, when the client recalls the conversation. People see themselves at the center of stories. Effective storytellers understand this dynamic, and avoid the tendency to turn a personal story into a sales pitch.
Under Jobs, Apple managers discouraged salespeople from memorizing presentation pitches. Relying on a pitch reduces salespeople's flexibility. Word-for-word recitation is like following GPS turn-by-turn instructions instead of traveling with a map that provides a comprehensive overview of the likely customer.
Apple excelled at listening to the market and finding "unspoken clues."
In 2007, four years before he died, Jobs announced the launch of the iPhone. At the time, the Palm Pilot mobile digital device was near the peak of its popularity. It featured a stylus, but Jobs resisted pressure to add one to the iPhone. He acted on his correct belief that fingers provide greater dexterity. This illustrates Jobs's ability to hear the subtext of what people want, not what they say.

"God gave us 10 styluses. Let's not invent another one!" (Jobs)
Members of the Apple sales team were excellent listeners, in part because many were musicians and music lovers. The sales team's job is to manage the actions and emotional responses of customers ş and to control the interaction while making sure that the customer feels in control.
Hearing is a mechanical exercise. Listening is the act of hearing in a respectful, purposeful and empathetic way. All innovation starts with listening. Salespeople who act on credible information that they draw from carefully observing people's actions, statements and subtext utilize listening as a tool. Good listeners ask questions and, in sales situations, they are aware that customers' answers often reveal personal motives before the conversation turns to the details of a possible transaction. Dealing only with questions of quantities and prices reduces a sales professional to "an order taker."
Jobs believed the market might not articulate what it wants, but it "knows what it needs," and provides guidance in the form of "unspoken clues." For example, he perceived consumers' frustration with MP3 players ş due to bad control functions and limited storage, among other flaws ş as a broad need for an innovative alternative. The market did not ask for the iPod, but it "clearly needed it."
Rhythm provides structure to sales activity and keeps it on a productive path. Sales happen when the selling process unfolds in a particular sequence with an intensity and a pace that varies to suit each customer. Rhythm has three parts: the initial interaction with a customer (the "attack"), the sequence of an encounter (the "meter") and the speed of a presentation (the "tempo"). Allowing a long silence in a presentation is a powerful, underused selling tool.
The pivotal "Think Different" ad campaign celebrated the human quality of Apple, not the features of its products.
Apple applied Jobs's skepticism about mainstream thinking to its products and to the words that it used to sell them.
In 1997, the advertising agency Chiat\Day created a two-word title for a pivotal Apple advertising campaign: "Think Different." These two words "saved the company." The ad campaign distinguished owners of Apple products from all other computer users. To this day, Apple advertising avoids technical descriptions of its products and focuses on human beings instead. Under Jobs's leadership, employees used carefully chosen words in their internal communication because he discouraged clichÚs, acronyms and jargon.
"Here's to the crazy ones. The misfits. The rebels. The troublemakers. The round pegs in square holes...They change things. They push the human race forward." (Apple TV ad script)

Jobs said his greatest accomplishments were decisions not to do lackluster things because then he could spend more time doing what he truly wanted. For example, he had an affinity for colleges and universities. Apple has a longstanding major presence in the education market, its only robust source of revenue in 1996. Apple tested its early blockbuster consumer products on college campuses.
From the early to mid-1990s, Apple had poorly coordinated operations and sold 41 different products. When Jobs returned, he immediately eliminated all 41 products and focused on two markets, professionals and consumers. Though his management style was more dictatorial than democratic, Jobs replaced the chaos at Apple with calculation and creativity. By 1999, the depopulation of the product line was complete, and Apple had introduced the iMac for consumers and the PowerBook for professional users.
During Jobs's absence, Apple management penalized risk taking in sales initiatives. When he returned, salespeople who embraced his messaging could take chances without fear of reprisal, even if they produced little or no revenue. Companies typically provide sales training programs that discourage creativity and drain energy. More companies should train account executives to achieve "flow," the effortless, engrossing focus that psychologist Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi described. This sublime state of mind eludes salespeople who recite preprogrammed sales pitches.
Selling with a sense of humanity set Apple apart from its competitors.
After Jobs returned in 1997, one of his first actions was replacing rectangular tables in the cafeteria at corporate headquarters with round tables. His purpose was to encourage employees from different parts of the company to meet each other and engage in informal, interdepartmental conversations. Sales benefitted from this and other steps that Apple took to keep its departments from becoming defensive silos that resisted collaboration and coordination. Establishing events and environments to bring Apple employees from sales, operations, engineering and marketing together proved extremely productive for sales.
The problem with sales training programs is the flawed premise that salespeople can structure human interaction. Apple rebuilt its sales training program and renamed it Apple University in 2008. The company designed the program not to train employees, but to teach them how to "harmonize" with customers. Apple also assigned a system engineer to each sales account executive to assist with technical demonstrations for customers.
"Canned sales approaches actually disrespect the salesperson, because they implicitly say, `We don't trust you when you're out of our sight'."

Apple puts a higher priority on relationships with customers than on closing transactions at all costs. Instead of overselling, salespeople take customers on mental journeys to explore how Apple products can empower them. They offer hope and, then, the tools customers need to turn their hopes into success with a perpetually expanding set of tools from the App Store. Jobs required members of the sales team to discuss their sales triumphs and defeats, a learning exercise that strengthened the company.
Apple is likely to evolve from a being leader in digital devices to being a service provider.
Tim Cook, who succeeded Jobs as Apple's chief executive officer, announced in 2019 that Apple would terminate iTunes. The online music business it launched in 2001 became obsolete when streaming music emerged. iTunes was the first major Apple product available on computers with a Windows operating system, and it was impetus for a shift within the company toward "building a business around the platform, not the hardware."
"The focus of the organization was to improve people's lives through technology. That was the soul of the company."
Apple's technological advances in the foreseeable future probably will be product upgrades that are more incremental than incredible. Apple never based its success on being the first company to produce a particular type of product. Instead, it sold improved versions of existing products. It is likely to evolve into a provider of services that relate to smart homes, cloud-based storage and mobile software applications. As the Internet of Things expands, expect Apple to deliver software that allows its devices to operate seamlessly from one platform to another.
