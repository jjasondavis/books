The Good Drone
How Social Movements Democratize Surveillance
Austin Choi-Fitzpatrick | MIT Press © 2020
Do drones make you think of missiles and war? If so, think again, says social scientist Austin ChoiFitzpatrick. Drones democratize airspace. The author surveys civil society's use of drones, which range from sampling the mucus of whales to putting graffiti in previously inaccessible places to surveilling the rich and powerful. If you follow Choi-Fitzpatrick's gracious advice to skip his overly academic sections, you will encounter compelling examples that will lead you to ponder the consequences of affordable "unmanned aircraft."

Summary
Material technologies are worthy of social scientists' attention. In 2014, activists ­ Austin Choi-Fitzpatrick among them ­ flew a camera-equipped drone over a protest in Budapest, Hungary. Members of the crowd pointed their phones' lights at the drone. An iconic photograph of this moment made the front page of The New York Times. The drone recorded the image and made it possible.

"Emboldened crowds saw in our videos something they didn't see in the local newspapers."
Social scientists often regard new devices as weapons or threats to civil liberties. But social movements and activists use tools and technologies, from barricades and the printing press to mobile devices and the internet. The function of these tools is to collect and disseminate information. At other times, tools make it too costly for those in power to maintain the status quo. The social scientist Charles Tilly has called the strategies and tactics that people use to influence society a "repertoire." You can also apply this term to material objects and their associated techniques.
Activists must choose and use repertoires to be effective and establish trust. According to political scientist Langdon Winner, decisions about the adoption of technology are political. They demonstrate what the adopter values, and what interests he or she will serve.
Drones began as nonviolent tools.
Engineer Abraham Karem built the first American drone, the Amber, as a surveillance vehicle. The US military was not interested. A simpler version, the Gnat 750, didn't find a market. But the armed Predator drone revolutionized the way people fought wars.
"I believe the Bosnian conflict ended four to six times faster because the Gnat was deployed." (drone developer Abraham Karem)
Drones in industry inspect wind farms and railroads. And drones have entered the repertoire of social activists.
Drones became practical and affordable when manufacturers made them easy to control. Quadcopters are the most popular. Drone use by intergovernmental organizations, governments, businesses, scientists and civil society groups took off in 2012.
"Places for politics include public spaces (coffeehouses and piazzas), cyberspaces (social media and virtual reality) and aero spaces."
The air is no longer a place where governments and corporations rule and surveil the population. The air is becoming a "public sphere."
Some drone use is "disruptive," some isn't.
A drone above the Kruger National Park in South Africa can spot endangered rhinos and the poachers who hunt them. This use of drones is not "emergent," as, for example, helicopters could do this same job. Nor is it "disruptive," because it doesn't break any norms.

Drones over protests have documented the size of the crowds in Moscow, Kyiv, Bangkok and Istanbul (where police shot down the drone). Thailand has banned camera-equipped personal drones.
"Guns weren't allowed, and I didn't have guns, but they saw the laptops, which is the worst gun for them." (Syrian citizen journalist Monther Etaky)
In Aleppo, Syria, revolutionaries' drones soared through the streets in 2016. They showed the effects of a brutal siege by Syrian government forces. These images competed with much more favorable ones that Syrian government drones had shot.
Australian biologists investigated the health of humpback whales by flying drones over them to sample exhaled mucus. This use is emergent ­ no other method was available ­ and nondisruptive.
The Slavery from Space project searched for brick kilns in India in satellite images. The resulting kiln register provides a map of places where exploitation, or "bonded labor" (slavery), is rampant. The project helps human rights organizations pressure governments, so it is disruptive.
Drones can turn the air into a political space.
A drone helped a Sinti-Roma settlement in Hungary show the world their plight. The local mayor wanted to use their living area as a parking lot for a football stadium. Inhabitants placed rocks in a large field nearby. From the air, the rocks formed a giant P, like a "large parking sign," to indicate that an alternative, suitable location outside their village was available.
"The slide lantern show was a new way of seeing in both senses: visually with the naked eye, but also viscerally with a pricked conscience."
Social scientists pay much attention to images, but too little to the camera itself. Cameras once shared technology with guns. Now they are part of a plethora of sensor-equipped devices. Photography used to require training. Now, everyone can take a picture.
Cultural critics have written more about photographs than about the camera. Essayist Susan Sontag, for example, wrote that the camera defines reality "as a spectacle (for the masses) and surveillance (for their rulers)." Writer and filmmaker Ariella Azoulay takes into account all participants in the production of an image: camera, photographer, subject and spectator.
According to social theory scholar and professor Sharon Sliwinski, images of human rights violations do not illustrate the concept of human rights. Rather, they illustrate its absence. An example is the "shaky vertical video" that a mobile phone takes. This format signals that the subject is worthy of attention by social movements. The same happens with aerial images that drones make.

Drones disrupt this mode of photography by taking flight. The next phase of drone use may be "near-instantaneous monitoring" of places or events. On-demand satellite coverage is expensive, and drones and balloons can take their place.
"The present technological moment is producing a new wave of image-making devices that have limbs and lives of their own."
From the ground, American embassies seem impregnable to terrorist attacks. But they look accessible from the air. Drones provide unfettered views of skyscrapers, prisons and factory farms, and thus provide democratic surveillance.
New forms of data gathering may enable drones to document the actions of police officers. Maybe citizens should have the right to fly over military installations that their tax dollars support.
Drones will change our concept of private spaces. Is observation of backyards and rooftop gardens from the air an invasion of privacy? Or, would drones flying over the penthouses of the privileged subject them to the same surveillance that less-privileged people have to endure?
Malevolent drone use has been rare.
Large-scale deployment of commercial drones forces the development of air routes and collision avoidance systems. Privacy remains an issue. In 2015, about a third of regional and national drone regulations had to do with police use. Nearly a quarter dealt with surveillance without permission. Fully 15% addressed hunting by drone. Another 10% outlawed using drones to harm people or to fly over important infrastructure. A rogue drone near Gatwick Airport, for example, affected 140,000 passengers in a day. The airport had ordered a "Drone Dome" that would have prevented this, but it had not yet arrived.
Countermeasures against drones exist.
The start-up DroneShield, for example, offers drone detection by recognizing their sound. SkySafe promises equipment that can take command of a drone. A GPS transmitter can disrupt drone navigation. Energy beams will bring a drone down, as will nets, shotguns or, as the Dutch National Police showed, trained eagles.
"The process of giving drones the slip is also in beta."
Drones can't fly in strong winds, heavy fog or dense rain. Shards of glass or a mirror on the ground confuse their cameras. Warm surroundings can render a human body undetectable by infrared sensors.

Tools that accomplish social change must be "visible, accessible, affordable, useful and appropriate." Social activists need to understand events, communicate them to the public and make the status quo they disapprove of more costly. As tools of resistance, drones have found use in estimating the size of protesting crowds. They may one day monitor police officers on probation. The graffiti artist KATSU used a drone to draw red lines on a Calvin Klein billboard in Manhattan. Thus, drones carry rebellious activity like graffiti to spaces that were previously unreachable. Activists of the Shark Online organization, for example, use drones and hidden cameras to document hunting they disapprove of.
"As the wall was going up, however, drone prices were coming down." Those using drones, says Langdon Winner, must be mindful of the question: "What kind of world are we making?" As people embrace new technology, they must consider its "psychological, economic, political and social" consequences. The crucial question people must consider is: Does this technology increase or diminish human freedom?
About the Author
Austin Choi-Fitzpatrick is associate professor at the University of San Diego and the University of Nottingham. His other books include What Slaveholders Think.
