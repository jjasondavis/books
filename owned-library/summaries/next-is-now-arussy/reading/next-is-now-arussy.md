5 Steps for Embracing Change ¡ Building a Business that Thrives into the Future
Lior Arussy | From the book NEXT IS NOW: 5 Steps for Embracing Change Building a Business that Thrives into the Future by Lior Arussy. Copyright ® 2018 by Lior Arussy. Reprinted by permission of Simon & Schuster, Inc.

Companies that drop the status quo and embrace change will rocket past organizations that fear it and end up being irrelevant. His insightful, commonsense guidance will inspire executives to foster and grow comfortable with change.

Change is the one irrefutable constant today. You must become "change-resilient."
"The change" is the change you encounter daily ¡ change that never goes away. It alters lives and transforms organizations. This is the kind of change that many people and organizations try to ignore. But it's impossible to avoid. You want to learn to adapt to change and to shape it so it works for you, not against you.
"This is not a book about change. It is a book about THE change. The change you live through every day."
Constant change can be unsettling, so you and your organization must become changeresilient. Try to become excited about change, not afraid of it, though this is a difficult transition for most people. Fear of change is primal, if illogical. People and organizations must summon the courage to accept change as a positive, and that requires understanding change resilience as a way of life ¡ not just a goal. It's how you must exist, day in and day out, in the modern world.
Becoming change-resilient in the short-term is not enough. People and organizations must strive for long-term change resilience, which involves training for and adapting to circumstances that shift constantly. Use four strategies to achieve long-term change resilience:
1. "Never stop exploring the Next" ¡ Your job as a leader is to manage current change and to scope out significant future trends. Watch for upcoming challenges and promising opportunities. Leaders must choose "the Next" over "the Now" by pursuing what's around the bend.
2. "Stop living in fear" ¡ Everyone must deal with stressful and frightening circumstances. Manage these situations with grace. Move beyond fight-or-flight, and choose rationality over illogical fears. Rise above fear to overcome the difficulties of change. Demonstrate fortitude in challenging times.
3. "Keep it simple and flexible" ¡ Live by the rule "always do the right thing for all parties involved," including your customer, your organization and yourself.
4. "Remember that change can be liberating" ¡ In 1961, Pampers began to sell disposable diapers. Initially, consumers weren't enthusiastic about this revolutionary product. The public felt that responsible mothers should wash their babies' diapers, not discard them. As disposable diapers became more popular, the attitude changed. Mothers who tried disposables liked not spending time washing cloth diapers. Often, this type of liberation is one of the most notable benefits of change.

Organizations create initiatives to try to deal with change, but few succeed.
Knowing how to adapt to and manage change is an essential skill within organizations and among executives, managers and employees. Change management requires people who are change agents and organizations that undertake change initiatives.
"We must change the way we change."
Risk accompanies any transformation, and only 9% of change initiatives fully succeed. Every change initiative represents new actions and new territory. "Change veterans" use several techniques to help their change initiatives succeed:
À "Don't start with a complete plan" ¡ No one can predict the future. Never create a fullblown plan for a change initiative. What looks like something that might work today might not work tomorrow due to further change that no one can anticipate.
À "Fix the trust gap" ¡ Leading a transformation effort is more difficult if there's a gap between what leaders promise and what they deliver. Trust can vanish. Demonstrate to your employees that your change initiative is real and that you will do everything possible to ensure its success.
À "Don't let change blockers derail you" ¡ Once you announce your change initiative, count on negative employees to oppose whatever you're proposing. Getting employees to support your change initiative will become even tougher if naysayers openly criticize it. Get rid of them. You don't need them poisoning everyone else.
À "Be authentic" ¡ Leaders must become the public standard-bearers of change to inspire their employees.
À "Own the conversation" ¡ To promote a change initiative, leverage your stature to focus discussions within the organization on the change program.
À "Quit seeking out a perfect 10" ¡ Assess your organization's change resilience by considering your customer-satisfaction surveys. Do they request validation with questions that seek scores of perfect 10s? Include survey questions such as, "What else could we have done to delight you?" Your goal should be internal improvement, not external endorsement.
The success of a change program depends on engaged employees.
While executives institute change initiatives, employees make the initiatives work. Without employee support, no change initiative can succeed. Furnish the tools your employees need to move ahead and accomplish their goals
"The world is always changing. Are you?"

You don't want employees to think of themselves as cogs in a machine, so empower them to feel like superheroes who can do anything. Establish inspirational goals. Provide a safety net to ease their fears of trying new things.
Managing organizational change requires a change-resilient culture.
Organizations need a change-resilient culture to introduce successful change initiatives and to manage external change.
"When employees are inspired and empowered, change will be accelerated."
Follow these specific guidelines to develop a change-resilient culture:
À Highlight your foundational mission ¡ Your core cause is whatever drives and inspires your company. Put your core cause first.
À Show that you're personally dedicated to change ¡ If you don't commit to your change initiative, your employees won't either.
À Keep emphasizing the question, "What's next?" ¡ Adopt an attitude of maximum curiosity and experimentation.
À Help people anchor themselves on things that aren't changing, like your corporate values ¡ Change is unsettling, but people accept it more readily if important aspects of their lives remain constant, like your company's core causes. An organization's values should never change.
À Present doable goals ¡ The future is unpredictable. If things go as you hope and plan, you can be certain of the outcome ¡ but little else. Don't burden employees with overly ambitious expectations. Set realistic goals.
À Acknowledge that each employee is different ¡ Every person has his or her own way of doing things. Don't insist that employees adhere to a formulaic approach to their work. Affirm their individual styles.
À Reward innovative thinking ¡ Institute a recognition program that honors your most innovative employees and their achievements. Recognize people who tried new approaches that didn't succeed.
Follow five steps to be "future ready" for change.
Don't ignore change. It will take place no matter how you feel about it. Using a five-step strategy can enable you to prepare for the future.
Step one: "Face it," so you can determine what to do.
To begin, face change rationally and honestly, without denial. Put aside passivity and establish the objective reasons for change. Once you've identified your reasoning, figure out your next steps.

Step two: "Analyze it," so you don't fear it.
Assess and understand the burdensome emotions you'll feel as you confront change. One of these emotions will be fear. Examine your apprehension about change and the pain that will accompany it.
"Our job as human beings is not to just accept the world the way it is ¡ but to build it as we want it to be."
Candidly assess and come to terms with any unreasonable opposition you may feel about the change you face. Be watchful for lame excuses that may surface as to why you shouldn't pay attention to change. Such excuses indicate that you don't want to change.
Step three: "Redefine it," so you understand it.
Reframe change to clarify how this alteration of traditional circumstances supports your core cause. Reshape your understanding of change so you lead it and aren't led by it. Appreciate how the change you face isn't really something to fear, but is, instead, the gateway to a bright future. A new understanding will help you develop the confidence to move ahead.
"Life is full of unexpected turns and tribulations. We can't control those changes, but we can control our response to them."
Develop a positive mind-set about change. One manager described the steps of change as a fighting through a "12-round match." Don't think in such negative terms. See change as promising and good ¡ not ominous and bad.
Step four: "Grow it; "build up your organization so you can use change effectively.
You and your organization must grow to take advantage of upcoming change and leverage it to your benefit. Growth will require professional and personal development as it also opens opportunities.
"The millions of small decisions employees make every day...activate the desired change."
Work to achieve a mind-set in which you "live the change effortlessly." Build your skills to fulfill the operational demands and promises of change. As you move away from the status quo, aim high.

Step five: "Own it" to impel your future.
You must own the change and make it a vital dynamo that powers your future practices and actions. Change shifts everything, but you can put your signature spin on it. To make it real, think about which old practices you need to cancel and which fresh practices you must begin.
"Change is no longer the exception; it's the rule. And it is going to rule you, unless you take charge."
Develop and implement a new tactical plan that will work most effectively with the change you're undergoing.
Change means new opportunities. If you don't seize them, your competitors will.
The John F. Kennedy Center for the Performing Arts in Washington, DC, has undergone a major change. In the past, the Kennedy Center featured ballet and classical music. It then began to showcase the novelty of "street art and participatory theatrical experiences" along with traditional fine arts. CEO Deborah Rutter has become a genuine change agent in her efforts to find balance between the venue's strong heritage and its continued relevance.
People commonly put off change and all that it involves ¡ the shifts in strategy, personnel, budgets and culture ¡ until the perfect time. Unfortunately, there's no perfect time for change. As a leader and change agent, you are responsible to the employees you lead. If you see that change is going to displace employees from their current jobs, make sure your organization has a plan ready for retraining and redeploying them.
"Transformation requires the confidence to operate with incomplete information and deduce from trends what the future will bring."
Change ultimately represents opportunity, which will knock lightly, wait only a little while and then quickly move on to someone else's door. Your organization must accept its opportunities, even if you will never get perfect validation about what you're facing and how you address it. Don't run from change. Move ahead by enthusiastically accepting all it entails. Make change your impetus and your environment.
