Billion Dollar Whale
The Man Who Fooled Wall Street, Hollywood, and the World
Tom Wright and Bradley Hope | Hachette Book Group USA ę 2018
Jho Low is a mysterious Malaysian who burst onto the party scenes in Las Vegas and Manhattan in late 2009. Surrounded by celebrities and thinking nothing of losing $2 million at the baccarat tables, Low was impressive and confounding: Where did his money come from? In this rollicking account, Wall Street Journal reporters Tom Wright and Bradley Hope lay out how Low stole billions from his home country's sovereign wealth fund. With vivid writing, they detail not just Low's debauchery but also the complicity of international banks, which didn't look too closely at all that suspicious money moving around the world. And the story is not over yet: At this book's publication, Low remained at large, and the prime minister who helped him in his swindles faces public corruption charges.

Summary
Jho Low, a Malaysian national, is alleged to have stolen billions from a sovereign wealth fund.
At the peak of his fame, Malaysian money man Jho Low projected power and oozed riches. A chubby, soft-spoken man, Low was a "whale" ş Las Vegas jargon for a free-spending highroller. He surrounded himself with celebrities, many of whom collected six-figure appearance fees to show up at his events. In the run-up to his 31st birthday celebrations in November 2012, Low rented a $25,000-a-night suite at the Palazzo hotel in Las Vegas. Guests there included the actors Leonardo DiCaprio and Benicio Del Toro, rap artist Pras MichÚl and hip-hop producer Swizz Beatz. Attending his main Vegas birthday bash were Kanye West, Kim Kardashian, Paris Hilton, Martin Scorsese and Bradley Cooper. Jamie Foxx was the emcee for the evening, which featured performances by musicians Busta Rhymes and Pharrell Williams. A scantily clad Britney Spears jumped out of a cake and sang "Happy Birthday" to Low. Enticing all those household names to the party likely cost millions.
"His was a 21st century global scheme. His conspirators came from the world's wealthiest 0.1%, the richest of the rich, or people who aspired to enter its ranks."
Even as Low brought attention to his success, the source of his fortune remained murky. Rumors swirled that he was an Asian arms dealer or that he had inherited billions from his grandfather. In truth, Low swindled the money from Malaysian taxpayers. As his take rose into the billions, the scope of his scam made Bernie Madoff's fraud pale in comparison.
After attending business school, Low moved to Kuala Lumpur and fashioned himself as a dealmaker.
Even as a boy, Low had a taste for luxury and a desire to impress. His father had built a garment business and sold it for a profit of perhaps $15 million. The elder Low sent Jho to an exclusive English boarding school, where the youngster set about ingratiating himself with the children of Middle Eastern royalty. He persuaded a few of his friends to visit Malaysia during summer vacations. To dazzle his classmates, Low borrowed a 160-foot yacht and a colonial home owned by a billionaire who was a family friend. Then Low replaced his host's family photos with his own so that he could pretend that his family owned the yacht and mansion.
"If Low had started cultivating famous friends to quell some kind of lingering insecurity ş or even for the sheer hell of it ş he also came to realize it was good business."
At the University of Pennsylvania's Wharton School, Low continued the self-aggrandizement. To impress his Ivy League pals, Low booked a swanky bar and provided free drinks and food; the tab was $40,000. He drove a Lexus convertible around campus and often led gambling trips to Atlantic City, NJ. An indifferent student, Low didn't seem cut out for working long hours at an

investment bank or a consulting firm. After college, he went home to Malaysia with a vague plan of using his network of connections from Wharton and boarding school to do big deals. Low leased space in the most prestigious office building in Kuala Lumpur and began hiring a staff.
After his first big score, Low targeted 1MDB, a sovereign wealth fund.
Low dreamed up a scheme that involved buying two construction firms with hopes of winning contracts for a large development in Malaysia. To make himself seem well-connected in the Middle East, Low created a company that he called Abu Dhabi-Kuwait-Malaysia Investment Co. and based it in the British Virgin Islands. He implied that deep-pocketed Middle Easterners were bankrolling his firm. For the next step of his caper, Low needed to find a rich but gullible buyer for his construction venture. His mark was Taib Mahmud, the chief minister of a remote part of Malaysia. After selling at a premium, Low pocketed $110 million.
Jho Low's "money came from a series of events that are so unlikely, they appear made up."
The deal meant that Low, not yet 30, was hugely wealthy ş richer than his father and better off than his Wharton classmates. But Low wanted more, so he targeted a sovereign wealth fund, 1Malaysia Development Berhad, or 1MDB. The pool of cash was to create job opportunities for Malaysians by investing in green energy and tourism. To sway Malaysian prime minister Najib Razak, Low introduced him to Prince Turki Bin Abdullah, a Saudi royal who, despite his lineage, wasn't especially wealthy. He was one of 20 heirs of the Saudi leader King Abdullah, and Prince Turki's main asset was PetroSaudi, an impressive-sounding concern that was really just a shell company. Low again worked his magic: Aboard a yacht rented at a cost of $500,000 per week, Prince Turki and Najib agreed to a deal that called for 1MDB to invest $1 billion in cash in a joint venture, along with PetroSaudi's alleged rights to extract oil in Turkmenistan and Argentina, supposedly valued at $2.5 billion. Next, the new joint venture made an unusual disbursement: It spent $700 million to repay a phantom loan to PetroSaudi.
Low spent tens of millions on gambling, planes and yachts, and paid celebrities to appear at his parties in Las Vegas, New York and Cannes.
These deals, consummated in September and October 2009, meant Low now was truly wealthy. And the party started. Within weeks, Low hosted a mammoth bash at the Palazzo. He hired 20 models to attend and also brought DiCaprio in for one of his first Vegas appearances with Low. The guests had no idea that a loosely regulated state fund that Low had fleeced was paying for the party.
"At that moment, in late 2009, Low had access to more liquid cash than almost anyone on Earth ş and he wasn't shy about spending it."

The Vegas blowout kicked off an $85-million-round of debauchery that lasted for eight months. Low and his entourage drank, gambled, flew on private jets, rented megayachts, and paid Playboy Playmates and Hollywood figures to party with them. In one bacchanal, Low ran up a bar tab of $160,000 in a single evening in New York. He also began to court Paris Hilton, paying the celebrity $100,000 each time she attended one of his parties. In February 2010, Low was in Las Vegas to celebrate Hilton's birthday. Baccarat was Low's game of choice, and as celebrities and hangers-on watched, he bet hundreds of thousands of dollars at a time. Low lost $2 million in just 10 minutes, but he was unperturbed, and his reaction raised rumors about the wealthy Malaysian who didn't mind losing millions in a few minutes. Low did try to do some legitimate business. He paid $45 million in cash for the luxury hotel L'Ermitage in Beverly Hills. He also began to snap up real estate in London and Los Angeles. In New York, Low used a shell company to pay $36 million for a condo near Central Park, and he dropped $35 million on a private jet.
"Low believed government funds were limitless and he could just keep on spending."
But hiding $700 million required work. Low began a convoluted string of wire transfers, sending money from Switzerland to New York. When 1MDB board members questioned him about the missing money, Low warned that raising too many queries risked upsetting Malaysia's new partners in Saudi Arabia. Low also needed to grease the skids with Najib, who supported Low's relationship with 1MDB with the understanding that Low would back the prime minister's reelection efforts. Low hoped to cover up his chicanery by making a profit with the stolen $700 million. For Low, the more prominent that Najib seemed internationally, the better. So when Najib and his wife visited the United States in 2010 for a meeting with President Barack Obama, Low worked behind the scenes to make certain the visit achieved maximum impact.
Keen to become a Hollywood player, Low produced The Wolf of Wall Street.
Low also hoped to break into Hollywood film production, and DiCaprio was an ideal target because he was a huge star. DiCaprio wanted to make The Wolf of Wall Street, a movie about the financial scammer Jordan Belfort, but DiCaprio found himself thwarted by the Hollywood power structure. Low represented the sort of producer DiCaprio dreamed of ş one with deep pockets and a willingness to hand over artistic freedom. DiCaprio and Scorsese stayed close to Low because of the money he offered to finance their film. Low's co-conspirators wished he would tone it down, but a subdued approach wasn't Low's style. At the Cannes Film Festival in 2011, Low made a splash. Pharrell Williams, Kanye West and Jamie Foxx performed at a party that he hosted. Among the guests were not just DiCaprio but also Belfort, the Wolf of Wall Street himself. Watching the festivities as Low's guest, Belfort concluded that Low was a fraudster. "You wouldn't spend money you worked for like that," Belfort told his girlfriend. Low later offered Belfort $500,000 simply to attend an event in Las Vegas. Belfort, however, was trying to put his criminal past behind him, so he turned down the easy payday.

"There are lots of wannabe producers out there, but none threw money around like Low."
During the film festival, both Low and his new movie production company anchored massive yachts in the Mediterranean. Low also hoped to become a music impresario, so he set up a recording studio on his ship. Low invested $12 million in the musical career of Elva Hsiao, a Taiwanese singer he briefly dated, although she never managed to break into the US market. Low hired the musician Alicia Keys and her husband, Swizz Beatz, for his record label. Beatz would prove to be a loyal member of Low's entourage, perhaps because his delinquent tax debts to the US Internal Revenue Service reached six figures. Low budgeted more than $100 million for The Wolf of Wall Street. By mid-2011, he had purloined nearly $2 billion from 1MDB. To replace the missing money, Low frantically searched for deals that might turn a quick profit. He entered the bidding for Coroin Limited, a company that owned a luxury hotel in London. The seller had never heard of Low or 1MDB, but Low smoothed over his obscurity by signing a check for ú50 million [$65 million]. Low didn't win the bidding, but he did make other contacts. Meanwhile, his expensive friendships with DiCaprio, Hilton and other celebrities served a purpose: They set Low apart from the rest of the Asian and Middle Eastern money looking to make deals.
Low's money "could provide DiCaprio and Scorsese with the Hollywood holy grail: boundless financing coupled with unfettered artistic control."
Low needed a broad circle of accomplices, which complicated his ability to keep the scam quiet. Xavier Justo, the head of PetroSaudi's London office, became upset when his promised bonuses never materialized and he had to pay out-of-pocket for some company expenses. In 2011, Justo demanded a severance payout of 6.5 million Swiss francs [$6.5 million] from PetroSaudi. One of Low's lieutenants agreed to pay him four million Swiss francs. Feeling cheated and suspecting something was amiss with 1MDB, Justo began digging for the truth.
The scam unravels when the US Department of Justice files a suit seeking the more than $1 billion stolen from 1MDB.
Meanwhile, Low had developed a keen sense for navigating banks' compliance departments. Technically, moving hundreds of millions in stolen money around the globe is impossible. But Low sensed that banks made profits not by catching criminals but by allowing suspicious transactions to go through. One of Low's tactics was known as layering, in which he created a web of transactions that made it seem as if his money was inherited and therefore legitimate. Looking for a bank that wouldn't ask too many questions, Low in 2009 approached his father's private banker, Yak Yew Chee, with a half-baked tale about opening an account on behalf of 1MDB. The story was ridiculous ş why would a sovereign wealth fund use an unknown man in his 20s to put its money in a small bank? But Yak didn't ask many questions, and when he moved to BSI, a struggling Swiss institution, Low followed. The small bank soon had a burgeoning business in

Asia, and Yak was pulling down $5 million per year in salary and bonuses, based on his association with Low.
"Low's genius was he sensed that the world's largest banks, its auditors and its lawyers would not throw up obstacles to his scheme if they smelled profits."
Low and Najib also had an interdependent relationship. Low needed Najib's protection, and Najib relied on kickbacks from Low. In April 2013, with Najib facing a tight reelection campaign, Low paid for Busta Rhymes, Swizz Beatz and others to put on a concert in support of Najib. The Malaysian first lady used the money from Low to live lavishly, spending $6 million on shopping sprees on Rodeo Drive, on Fifth Avenue and at Harrods. Low's scam was particularly galling, given the poverty of his home country. More than half of Malaysians scrape by on less than $1,600 a month. Low blithely blew their money on art, champagne and parties.
"The 1MDB affair was shaping up as the single largest financial scam of all time."
In 2014, Justo leaked 140 gigabytes of PetroSaudi data to a Malaysian blogger. In February 2015, the blogger posted documents and emails that revealed how Low and his accomplices had looted 1MDB. In July 2015, The Wall Street Journal published a front-page story asserting that Najib had received more than $680 million from 1MDB. The scam fully unraveled in July 2016, when the US Justice Department filed suit to recover more than $1 billion in money stolen from 1MDB. DiCaprio, for his part, turned over the gifts Low had given him. Ultimately, authorities estimated Low's fraud to have totaled $4.5 billion.
Low remains at large, apparently living either in China or aboard a yacht.
Low's scam proved a disaster for Malaysia. Moody's calculated that the 1MDB scam had created a liability of $7.5 billion, about 2.5% of Malaysia's output. The scandal spooked international investors and devalued the country's currency. Najib tightened his grip and turned away from Washington, DC, and toward Beijing, thwarting hopes that Malaysia would become a democracy or a Western ally. In 2018, Malaysian police raided Najib's home, seizing jewelry and other items worth hundreds of millions of dollars, along with $28 million in cash. Najib went on trial in early 2019. Low remains at large, apparently living either in China or aboard a yacht.
