The Rumble Zone
Leadership Strategies in the Rough & Tumble of Change
Jim Boneau
Most people associate drum circles with the counterculture and rarely think of them in relation to how their corporations function. However, drum circles can build emotional and energetic connections among people, says drummer and organizational change expert Jim Boneau. The energy of the drum circle can provide a powerful transformational metaphor for organizational change. All of that happens in the "rumble," a time of chaos and change.

In a drum circle and an organization, a "rumble" is a time of chaos and transformation.
Drum circles blend the energy of many participants and can incorporate a range of instruments. Rhythms rise and clash, and can overwhelm the senses and the mind. When people play near one another, they find a shared rhythm. At the same time, each participant brings something special to the circle. This is a metaphor and a catalyst for organizational interaction. At times, the rhythms in a drum circle "wobble." Participants feel the wobble as it grows, and the groove falls apart. They feel helpless to fight it. Some people stop drumming. Others follow their personal rhythms, and they don't recognize that the group rhythm is breaking down.
Organizations work similarly. A leader might have the instinct to save a group, and impose order when it starts to wobble so it doesn't dissolve into chaos. If the leader lets the wobble continue, participants find their way out and create a new rhythm. The facilitator can guide this transition from rhythm to rhythm, in drum circles and in organizations. A facilitator can quiet or stop the group, speed up the rhythm, and so on. Another option is the rumble.
"In the drum circle world, a rumble is a time when everyone plays as many notes as they possibly can on their instrument. It sounds like joyful, celebratory and loud chaos."
In a rumble, raucous chaos is a celebration. Rumbles can build energy to escape a rhythmic trap, stop a groove, or guide a group from groove to groove. Leaders may not know where a rumble will take them, so they must trust their instincts and their teams. This isn't easy. Groups need to practice being open to the rumble.
Drumming, especially during a rumble, can have its own powerful energy. People feel a connecting rhythm, and they want to feel it again. What is true of drum circles is true of organizations. As they pass through times of change that bring struggle and ambiguity, a rumble is a "bridge." A rumble blends excitement and sadness: One thing is ending as another begins. The rumble is the feeling of a new paradigm emerging. Even the word rumble sounds like change and suggests movement.
Facing either physical or social risk takes courage.
Mention courage, and people think of the military or of patients bravely facing medical treatment. That is not the only kind of courage. Daily life requires courage. People need courage to ask for help, to voice concerns in the workplace or to ask someone on a date. The second kind of courage entails not physical risk, but emotional risk, like the risk of humiliation or loss. These two types of courage may seem different, but numerous studies show that your brain treats both kinds of risk a similar way.

Courage means facing risk and danger. A major part of learning is figuring out what types of danger are worth the risk. The relationship between payoff and risk isn't always clear. That's when people need "moral strength." They have to be ready to rumble, to walk into the unknown, an uncomfortable and unfamiliar zone where things are changing.
Change surrounds you all the time. Denying change means denying reality. By contrast, being willing to take one step forward can generate growth. Running away from pain is human, while moving toward it requires strength. To pass through times of transformation, people have to know what they believe. Then, they must be brave enough to live and articulate their beliefs, and to listen to others.
You must be present to navigate change.
When you get difficult news, or when you're trying to find your way through a challenging situation, you must be fully present. Mindfulness has become increasingly mainstream, though in many cases, it seems like a cliché ­ like the idea that you should eat well or get plenty of rest.
"Being present is ultimately the act of stepping out of your stories about the future and the past, and, instead, seeing and feeling what is happening here and now."
To be mindful, heed your own thoughts and feelings. Realize that people often must escape the present moment by looking to the future or constructing stories. Slow down, and witness what is happening right now. Accept the threatening feelings, like fear, that come with change. Witnessing them is the first step to dealing with them.
Clashing or changing points of view can produce stress, but they fuel growth.
In his article "Being a Grown Up," Anthony B. Robinson, a pastor, articulates three principles about adulthood: You must understand that what's going on isn't about you. You must be able to "say something unpopular." And you must recognize that life is both tragic and complex. Understanding that life is not about you allows you to see people's differing viewpoints. Sharing differing points of view might lead to frustration, clashes, feelings of being threatened and even a rumble.
Robinson's second principle refers to the fact that challenging other people's perspectives may be unpopular and unpleasant. Many people only go as far as holding a point of view. If they live more profoundly, they have to live that point of view. This means using a sense of perspective as a "compass" when facing great change.
"To thrive in the rumble, we must take action."
Robinson's third principle applies to group interaction. Guiding groups is complex, even paradoxical. But often, people need someone to listen to them. Let them feel heard. Help them

believe in themselves. Point of view also involves paradox. In the excitement of the rumble, your point of view guides you. As you go through the rumble and navigate a new path, your point of view may change.
"Fact-based" curiosity shows you the tip of the iceberg. "Heart-based" curiosity shows you the depths.
When you interact with people in a serious situation, you must practice "true curiosity." To rumble, you have to be ready "to wrestle with different points of view." These clashing viewpoints can be inside your head, or between you and others. Any time you lead a group through change, you have to be curious about the members' different viewpoints.
People experience two kinds of curiosity: fact-based and heart-based. Imagine a human as an iceberg. Fact-based curiosity focuses on the visible part of the iceberg: It isn't wrong, but it has limits. More exists to the iceberg ­ and to people. Much of the iceberg rides below the water. To see that larger iceberg, you must practice heart-based curiosity. Heart-based curiosity takes people deeper, lets leaders access depth, and is more likely to lead to genuine transformation. It lets you see factors that might block or influence a larger situation.
"Confirmation bias" ­ the human tendency to look for information that confirms existing beliefs ­ is a major obstacle to curiosity. Even when you ask questions, confirmation bias shapes what you do with the information you get.
Practicing empathy makes change easier.
People are "inherently social." They want to relate to other people. People observe other people around them, notice how they act, and how those actions form patterns. The patterns let us know what to expect. However, change is inevitable. Being willing to "do something different" can start a rumble. No change happens in a vacuum. Change yourself, and you change the "unwritten social contract" you share with other people. To flourish in a rumble, consider how change will affect the people around you.
"If we cannot become aware of our limiting judgments, then today's path to success will be undermined by yesterday's perception of failure."
Empathy makes change go more smoothly. You can empathize with others without agreeing with them or feeling as they do. Empathy means accepting how another person feels. Three main obstacles impede empathy. The first is mistaking the nature of empathy. It is not sympathy, agreement or an effort to change others. Empathy involves witnessing and listening. The second obstacle arises when your priorities interfere with empathizing with someone else. The third obstacle ­ old pain ­ can reinforce this. Pain from the past, especially repeated pain, can cause emotional "scar tissue." It's hard to empathize with others if your past created emotional scars.

Such scar tissue can cause you to get stuck in a rumble rather than enabling you to use the rumble as a path to change.
You need an open mind to work through change.
When people don't know how a situation will play out, their minds can fabricate dark stories, leading to fear and limitation. People use their judgment of events in the past to inform their evaluation of the present. However, if those judgments are hasty or incorrect, they lead people astray.
"Human beings are inherently judgmental."
Judgments themselves aren't bad, but "limiting judgments" can cloud people's view of a situation and lead them to bad decisions. In a rumble, you have to "cultivate an open mind." The first step is becoming aware of past judgments and how they might limit you. The most limiting judgments are the ones people make about themselves and other people.
Being fully nonjudgmental or keeping an open mind on demand aren't possible. Instead, actively cultivate "a stance of open-mindedness." Consider that you might be judgmental. Pay attention to your feelings. This can help you continue to welcome new information, rather than shutting it out.
Even in a drum circle, some people feel they aren't musical. Perceptions that lead you to be less open-minded about yourself and what you can do with the drum can come from many sources. To reach a state of open-mindedness, reframe these limiting judgments and move beyond your comfort zone, past the known to the uncomfortable unknown.
When evaluating a judgment, ask: Do you have evidence that this judgment is accurate? Is your evidence factual? When did you make this judgment? Has anything changed since that time? Reworking that judgment may be possible if you can follow the factual, logical steps. Certain emotional actions can also propel you toward an open mind. Examine your judgments to see if they carry frustration or anger. Are pride or resentment keeping you from being openminded? If so, what has to happen to "transform these emotions?"
To deal with change successfully, you must take action.
It is not enough to share perspective or even to be aware. At some point, you must act. Your actions must be purposeful, and they should move you closer to your goals. Otherwise, you can get stuck. You don't make it to a new state, and then fall "back into your old habits."
"To thrive in the rumble and emerge wiser, more in sync with your surroundings, and more deeply connected to yourself and others, you have to, whether it's easy or difficult, take that next step."

In the rumble, taking action moves you from an inner focus to an outer one. The rumble is "inbetween." This ambiguity confuses and frightens people. They have to leave the old, and don't know how to find the new. Acting purposefully can be reassuring. Successfully negotiating change often requires you to adopt new perspectives, talk with someone else and take action to achieve a goal. In the rumble, acting changes you and empowers others. Sometimes, though, people need support or reassurance. When you feel that need, ask for support, and never feel shame when you do. Taking one action might take you to a stopping point. You might be breaking an existing pattern. You won't know that until you act.
Building resilience will help you manage with life's challenges. When life is overwhelming, you need resilience, the capacity "to recover quickly from difficulties." Resilience calls on your "physical, emotional and spiritual" reserves. To build resilience, get rest and exercise. Pay attention to what you're feeling, and connect to something bigger than yourself.
"Tapping into the well of resilience is a choice toward the heartbeat ­ it's a choice for life." The rumble tests people. It takes them to the brink of their limits. When you want to create change, you will meet boundaries, ambiguity and threats to your ego. In those moments, choose how you respond.
The different strategies for dealing with change all work together. You can use many tools to deal with the rumble. However, these mechanisms don't occur "in isolation." Once your mind is open, you have made room for curiosity, which may affect your point of view. You need to practice empathy with yourself and others, and eventually, you must act. All of these strategies require resilience. All of them can generate hope.
