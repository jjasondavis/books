S

Specificity is the secret

Writing is like painting a picture in your reader's mind. The vaguer you are, the blurrier the image--and blurry is bad. It makes people feel uncertain, and uncertain people are like deers in headlights: they don't act. Two ways to get specific:
The Rule of One
If you write to everyone, you write to no one. So before any piece, set the scene. Pick: One person (your One True Fan) One problem One solution One story
You'll be tempted to do more. Don't. People are busy--write like it.

Quantify
Unspecific writers are like those shady salesmen you wouldn't let in your grans house. Example:
I help people lose weight.
No one's gonna believe you, let alone pay you. But specificity creates authority and appeal. Use numbers wherever you can. And not round numbers either--unless that's the truth.
I've helped 56 clients lose an average of 21lbs in under
4 months.
Now that's a `shut up and take my money' kinda statement. Let's talk about A-grade writing.

The good news is being unique isn't as hard as people think. First, build frameworks--or what they call in the copywriting world, unique mechanisms. This SUCKS framework is an example: I've taken something old and created something new. People are always looking for new solutions to old problems. But this next tip is the killer:
Write from personal perspective.
My rule: everything you write must help your audience get to know you a little better. I'm not suggesting write a diary entry. That's where people go wrong--you're not the hero of your content, your reader is. The key is dropping daily breadcrumbs about who you are and what you do so they can relate. That's fan-building material.
1. Turn your How To's into How I's
2. Start every piece with a tidbit of your story.
Re-read the first page of this guide to see this in action. Tens of thousands of people will learn a little bit more about me.

U Be undeniable
Gurus will burn me at the stake for saying this, but the most important marketing question is not, `What's in it for me?" It's, `Why the hell should I believe you?" Because traffic without trust is like bread without butter. No bueno. Being undeniable is about proof, and you have three levels to tap into:

Persuasion

Authority by Association

Your Results

Customer Results

Proof

You know those adverts that say 86% of dentists recommend this? They give that stuff to us for free. Shit, I haven't bought a toothbrush in 10 years. They know how powerful authority by association is.
So talk about who influences you. Use studies and stats to support your arguments. Get endorsements from authority figures.

The next level is your results.
Many people talk the talk online but few people walk the walk. Share your story-- don't tell people what to do, show them what you're doing.
But your results will never be as powerful for perception as the results you've helped others achieve.
Save every piece of feedback. Collect testimonials. Write case studies.

C

Clarity is King

Let me lay down the truth:
Your job as a writer is not to say smart things. It's to learn smart things and then make them easy to understand.
Too many people try to be clever instead of clear. Remember, uncertainty's a killer. The smarter you sound, the more stupid your audience feels. Nobody enjoys feeling dumb.
My simple fix:
During editing (please don't edit and write at the same time... give it 24 hours), go through line by line and ask:
What the hell do I mean?
Say that instead.

X:

You might believe your role as a writer is to present complex information that impresses your audience. But it's the distillation of information that surprises and delights. Transform complexity into simplicity and watch your writing soar.

Y:

Your job as a writer is not to say smart things. It's to learn smart things and then make them easy to understand.

C Curiosity is key

The area of the brain responsible for hunger sits snuggly next door to the area responsible for curiosity. This explains innovation--we can't help but pursue what fascinates us. You have four simple ways to make people curious:
Talk about what they want
Talk about what they didn't know
Open loops
Tell stories

If this guide were about MyLittlePony, you wouldn't still be reading (unless that's your thing, then more power to you). This is why you gotta niche down. People will always be intrigued by the writer giving them what they want.
But they also love to be surprised, so start a surprise document.
Save everything that catches your eye.
You want people to go, `huh, I did not know that'. For example, I found out about that curiosity fact in a book on how the brain works. I thought it was cool, hopefully you did too.
Now, storytelling's a whole guide in itself. But take my word on it: the best storyteller wins. We're hardwired to pay attention because our ancestors who ignored stories about lions behind bushes became dinner.

C Be conversational
Something weird happens when we sit at our computers. We write on robots so we sound like them.
But the point of content is connection. You don't do that by making speeches at people. You do it by speaking with people. So when you write, pretend you're writing to your best friend... or your Gran (if they're the same person, again, more power to you).
You won't nail it at first, but writing to a familiar person will help you develop style-- your voice.
Before you hit publish, check:
Would I say this in person?
If not, rewrite it until it sounds like you.
Oh, and by the way:
You should slather one word onto your writing like thick cream on a good scone. Your audience loves to see it. And it's by far the best editing trick I can give you-- leading to strong relationships and much more revenue.

K

Keep it simple (stupid)

Da Vinci once said simplicity is the ultimate sophistication. It's never been more true. With so much noise online, the simplest writer wins. Note: this does not mean being the most braindead or lacking depth (like 90% of content). It means you explain your shit the best. Check out Paul Graham or Richard Feynman as examples. Two rules:
1. Never use a big word when you can use a small
2. Never use two words when you can use one
This is why you must edit your work. First drafts always suck. It's only once your thoughts are on paper can you tidy them the hell up. Make a habit of simplicity and your audience will love you for it.

Three editing tips:

1. Use Hemingway. Never publish anything over grade 4.

2. Change your screen, change your state. If you write on your laptop, edit on your phone. If you write in the morning, edit after the gym.

3. Use the 1/3rd rule. Before you publish anything, delete a third of it. This forces a higher word: wisdom ratio.

Before people read, they skim. To be easy on the eye, use:
Bullets
White space
Short paragraphs (even one sentence/word paragraphs!)
Headings and subheadings
This turns your writing into a slippery slope, which is important because the magic happens at the end. You gotta get people to finish if you want them to buy (your products, services, or ideas). Oh, and heads up: We don't see what we read. We hear it. Seriously. So the fastest `structure hack' I can give you is to vary your sentence length. Sometimes use short. Sometimes use long. Always read your stuff out loud before you publish. And if you really want to be an effective editor, record yourself reading --it's torture but it works.
