Various Biographies of Saint John of the Ladder

Brief Life of Abba John, the abbot of the Holy Mount Sinai, truly among the saints, written by the monk Daniel of Raithu, a man of honour and virtue

As for which city bore and nurtured him before his athletic struggle, so to speak, I am not able to say precisely or with certainty. Nor do I know which place now hosts and feasts the most admirable one with ambrosia. For he himself now belongs to that place concerning which the loud cicada somehow exclaims thus: 'Their citizenship is in heaven,' being nourished by the incorporeal perception with the insatiable, and rejoicing with the mind in the formless beauty, having received the rewards worthy of his labours and the reward for his sufferings without suffering, having gained the inheritance there, being one and forever with those whose foot stands henceforth in righteousness. How he now communes with the incorporeal while being corporeal, I shall explain very clearly.

At the age of sixteen, although blessed in visible youth, yet possessing a mental acuity that reached a thousand years, he offered himself as if something unblemished and willingly to the great priest as a sacrifice. His body he brought to Sinai, while his soul, I think, he carried to the heavenly mountain, considering that even from the visible place he could reap the greatest guidance toward the invisible. Embracing humility, which is the protection of our intellectual maidens, he cut off with vigorous purpose the boastful and deceitful tyrant within him at the very entrance. Bending his neck and trusting in the excellent tamer, with safe guidance, he aimed to cross the stormy sea with a good purpose. Thus he established himself as one who had an irrational and unwilling soul, entirely freed from natural inclination. This is indeed more marvellous: though learning worldly wisdom, he became a pupil of heavenly simplicity, utterly humbling the arrogance of philosophy, and after nineteen years, like an elder and supporter, he sent himself forth as an envoy to the king, the master of exercises, carrying as powerful weapons the prayers of the great one for the destruction of fortresses, to the arena of stillness.

He received the solitude of the place (the name of the place being Tholas), and for forty years he passed there tirelessly, always burning with fervent love and fire. But who is capable of triumphing in words over the struggles he endured there or of celebrating them with his narratives? How can something that was sown secretly in toil be spoken of openly? From some initial offerings, let us hear the remaining sacred substance of the thrice-holy one. He possessed all things that were blamelessly appropriate for his profession, but very briefly. In this, I think, he wisely broke the horn of pride, extinguishing it with brevity, and quelled it, crying out to it, 'Be silent, be muzzled,' quenching its flame in the desert and the absence of people, until it was entirely consumed and calmed. Bravely he avoided the worship of idols, even in the scarcity of oil and other necessities. The hourly death of the soul, which he pricked with the sting of death itself, he overcame. And the longing for the island, or perhaps for the rest and the sensible things, he untied with an incorporeal bond of grief. Tyranny, having perished before him through the sword of obedience, died from his untroubled body and even more untroubled speech, killing the spider-like leech that was already weakened.

What is the reward of the eighth maiden for this noble mystic? And what, then, is the highest purification? The purification that Bezalel of obedience began, and which the Lord of the heavenly Jerusalem completed with His presence, visiting in person: without which, neither the devil nor his conformed company shall be vanquished. Where shall I place in the present weaving of our crown the fountain of his tears, a matter that is not common among many, whose secret remains until now, a workshop in some remote and obscure cave, a very small cave, situated far enough from his own cell and all other cells to block out the ears of vainglory? Yet close enough to heaven, where with their cries and supplications, as is fitting, those wounded by swords and cauterizations, and deprived of their sight, are inclined to do.

He took just enough sleep to prevent his mind from being harmed by constant vigilance. Most often before sleep, he would pray and arrange small notes; for this was his only defense against sloth. His entire course was an unceasing prayer and an incomparable love for God. For, seeing Him in the purity of chastity, as in a mirror both by night and by day, he did not wish to find satisfaction; rather, I would say more fittingly, he could not. Inspired by his zeal, a certain Moses, one of those pursuing the solitary life, earnestly pleaded to become a disciple and to be initiated into true philosophy. He moved envoys to the great one, and through their intercessions, he was compelled to accept him as his disciple.

So, on one of the days, the great one instructed Moses to carry some earth from one place to water the plants. And indeed, having reached the designated spot, he carried out the command without hesitation. But when the midday sun was at its highest and most intense (for it was the last day of the month), Moses, having rested beneath a very large stone, fell asleep. The Lord, not wishing to let His servant suffer distress, intervened as usual. While the elder was in his cell, meditating and speaking with God, he fell into a light sleep. He saw a vision of someone waking him up in a reverent manner, mocking his sleep, and saying to him, 'John, how can you sleep so carelessly, while Moses is in danger?' Quickly, he jumped up and armed himself with prayer for his disciple.

Later, when evening came, he asked Moses if anything unfortunate or unexpected had happened to him. Moses replied, 'A huge stone was coming down on me during the heat of the day while I was asleep underneath it, and I would have been crushed had I not heard your voice calling me, which made me leap out of that place.' The humble elder, however, said nothing of what he had seen, but in secret cries and fervent prayers, he praised the good God.

He was indeed both an example and a healer of hidden bruises, just as once Isaac, who was heavily oppressed by the burden of the fleshly demon, fell into deep despondency and hastened quickly to this great man. He revealed his struggle mixed with laments, and the all-good one, admiring his faith, said, 'Let us both stand in prayer, dear one.' As they completed the words of supplication, while the suffering one was still lying face down, God, the healer, fulfilled His will so as not to show David to be a liar. The serpent fled, being tamed by the vivid prayers, and the one who had been sick, seeing himself now healthy, was profoundly astonished and offered thanks to the one who had glorified him together with the one who had been glorified. Yet, some, driven by envy, called him mute and a babbler; but he educated them through his deeds, demonstrating to all that he was strong in the Christ who empowers all. He remained silent for a full year, so that those who mocked him became supplicants, saying that a fountain of eternal benefit had been blocked, and the salvation of all had been harmed. The unyielding one relented, and returned to his former state. Then, all, admiring his accomplishments in every aspect, raised him by force to leadership among the brothers, placing the lamp on the high lampstand. The good examiners were lifted up and were not deceived; for he, too, ascended the mountain and entered the hidden darkness, receiving the God-given law and vision, being elevated on spiritual steps. By the word of God, he opened his mouth, drew in the spirit, and uttered speech, words from the good treasure of his heart. He completed the course of the visible life, leading the Israelites, the monks, having differed from Moses in only one respect: Moses entered the lower Jerusalem, but he, the upper Jerusalem. And the good and merciful God will not disregard our prayer.

Many who were saved by the spiritual influence through him testify, as well as those who are still being saved even now; an excellent witness is David the Younger, of wise wisdom and salvation. And our holy shepherd John, who prayed earnestly for his flock, descended from the mountain to us, this new seer of Sinai, and showed us the tablets written by God, containing outwardly practical teachings, and inwardly, theoretical principles, saying, 'I have found it possible to confirm many things briefly, for in brevity, there is beauty for those who speak.

Concerning Abba John, the abbot of Mount Sinai, also known as the author of the Ladder.

Once, when Abba Martyrios and Abba John came to the great Anastasios, he looked at them and said to Abba Martyrios, 'Tell me, Abba Martyrios, where is this young man from, and who tonsured him?' Martyrios replied, 'He is yours, Father, and I tonsured him.' And Anastasios said to him, 'Alas, Abba Martyrios! Who would have thought that you tonsured the future abbot of Sinai?' And the saint did not lie, for forty years later, he became our abbot.

Another time, Abba Martyrios, his supervisor, took John to the great John the Sabbaite, who was then living in the desert of Goudda. When the elder saw them, he rose, poured water, and washed the feet of Abba John, and kissed his hand. But he did not wash the feet of Abba Martyrios. When his disciple, Stephen, asked why he had done this, he replied, 'Believe me, my child, I do not know who this young man is, but I have received the abbot of Sinai and washed the feet of the abbot.'

Moreover, Abba Strategios, on the day when Abba John was tonsured, being twenty years old, prophesied about him, saying that he would become a great star. Indeed, when he became our abbot, about six hundred pilgrims came here. As they were seated and eating, he saw a man with a shaven head, dressed in a tunic, running around, and authoritatively giving orders to the cooks, stewards, cellarers, and other servants. After the people had left and the servants sat down to eat, that man who had been running around giving orders was sought but could not be found. Then our holy father, the servant of God, Abba John, said to us, 'Let him be; Moses did nothing unusual by serving in his own place.'

Once, during a drought in parts of Palestine, he was asked by the locals to pray, and a rich rain was brought down. And it is not unbelievable, for the Lord will do the will of those who fear Him, and He will hear their supplications."

It should be noted that John of the Ladder had a true brother, the remarkable Abba George, whom he appointed as abbot of Sinai while he himself embraced the life of quietude, which he had chosen as his bride from the beginning, in his wisdom. When our new Moses, the most holy Abba John, the abbot, was about to depart to the Lord, his brother Abba George stood by him weeping, saying, "Behold, you are leaving me and going away? I prayed that you would send me forth first, for I am not capable of shepherding the community without you, my lord. Rather, I now send you forth."

Abba John said to him, "Do not grieve, nor be anxious; for if I find favour with the Lord, I will not allow you to complete a year after me." And so it happened: within ten months, he too departed to the Lord.

Our Holy Father John, the author of the Ladder.

At the age of sixteen, being already sharp-minded, he offered himself as a most holy sacrifice to God, ascending Mount Sinai, where he became a monk and lived in obedience. After nineteen years, he rose and went to the arena of stillness, settling in the divine monastery of the wrestling ground (the place being called Tholas), which was five miles from the main church. He spent forty years there, consumed by a burning love and inflamed by the fire of divine love. He partook of all things that were blamelessly permitted for his profession, but very sparingly and without excess. In this, he wisely broke the horn of pride. But who can express in words the fountain of his tears? He slept just enough to prevent his mind from being harmed by constant vigilance. His whole journey was an unceasing prayer and an incomparable love for God.

Thus, having practiced every virtue and lived a good life, being filled with the greatest visions, he once saved his disciple from death. The disciple was sleeping in a place under a very large rock, which was about to fall and crush him to pieces. John, who was in his cell, perceived this through the Holy Spirit, and appearing to his disciple in a dream, woke him up just before the rock was to fall.

Having reached such heights of virtue and having served as the abbot of the holy Mount Sinai, he left this temporary life and was transferred to eternal rest. Before his departure, he composed the all-wise book of divine and spiritual ascents, which is called the Ladder."

Letter from Abba Isaiah of Raithu to John, the esteemed abbot of Mount Sinai

To the most exalted and angel-like Father of Fathers and superior teacher, the sinner and abbot of Raithu in the Lord sends greetings.

We, who are insignificant, recognize your unwavering dedication to the Lord in all things, adorned with virtues, especially in your obedience where there is the opportunity to increase the talent given to you by God. Truly humble as we are, and relying on your compassionate support, we take to heart what is written: 'Ask your father, and he will tell you; your elders, and they will inform you.' Therefore, as to a father common to all, elder above all in asceticism and wisdom, and as an excellent teacher, through these letters of ours, we beseech you, the pinnacle of virtues, to write to us, the ignorant, the things which you beheld in the divine vision, like the ancient Moses on the same mountain, and to inscribe for us on a precious tablet, like God-inscribed tablets, for the instruction of the new Israel, who have now emerged from the spiritual Egyptians and the sea of life.

Just as you once performed miracles with your divinely speaking tongue in the sea after God, we now implore you not to refuse to clearly and diligently engrave the necessary and fitting instructions for the monastic life for our salvation, as the great guide of all those who have chosen such an angelic way of life, not considering our words as flattery or praise. For you know, truly sacred leader, that such things are foreign and unspoken by all. Therefore, we trust in the Lord that you will quickly receive and embrace what we hope to gain from you—precious inscriptions on the tablets, truly guiding those who follow without error, and as a ladder set up to the gates of heaven, leading those who choose to ascend, safely and without harm, and without hindrance from the spiritual powers of wickedness, the rulers of darkness, and the princes of the air. For if Jacob, a shepherd of sheep, was granted such a fearful vision on the ladder, how much more will you, the leader of rational beings, not only reveal the vision but also demonstrate by deed and unerring truth the ascent to God for all?

Be well in the Lord, most honoured Father.

To John, from John, Greetings.

I received with due respect the honourable letter you sent to us, the poor and destitute in virtues, as befits your dignified and dispassionate life and your pure and humble heart. It was more of a command and an instruction than a simple message, one that exceeds our abilities. For it was indeed fitting for you, and characteristic of your holy soul, to seek from us, the uneducated and ignorant both in word and deed, words of teaching and admonition. You have always shown us a model of humility through your actions.

However, I now confess that, if it were not for fear and the great danger of casting off the yoke of holy obedience, which is the mother of all virtues, we would not have dared to undertake something beyond our strength. You, O wondrous Father, should have learned these things from those who know well, for we are but learners. But since our divinely inspired teachers and true mystics of knowledge have prescribed that we should obey without hesitation even in matters beyond our strength, behold, disregarding our own inadequacy, we have humbly attempted to undertake what is beyond us, not because it would be of any use to you, nor to reveal what you already know better than us, O holy leader (for I am convinced, as is every right-minded person, that your mind's eye is cleansed from every earthly and dark influence and is continuously illumined by the divine light), but fearing the death that comes from disobedience and being driven by it into obedience, I have come with fear and longing to your most holy command, as a grateful servant and unworthy child of a great master artist, attempting to portray with our poor and faint knowledge and meagre offering, the living words with the mere shadow of ink.

And so, I leave it to you, the leader and chief of teachers, to adorn, clarify, and complete what is lacking, so that the spiritual law and tablets may be fulfilled. I have not sent this to you, nor have I put forward our attempt as anything of value (God forbid! For that would be a sign of the utmost foolishness; for in the Lord, not only others but we ourselves are to be grounded in divine virtues and teachings), but to the God-called community that learns from you along with us, O greatest of teachers, with your prayers lifting the burden of ignorance from us like a gentle breeze filling the sails, handing over the rudder of our words to the good pilot, I now go with all supplication through you to them, asking that anyone who finds anything beneficial in this work may consider its fruit as a gift to our excellent master, with gratitude. And may they pray to God to grant the only true reward for this undertaking, not judging by the words themselves (which are indeed poor and full of ignorance), but accepting the Lord's intention of the one who offers them. For God does not reward according to the abundance of gifts and labours, but according to the abundance of intention.

Be well.

Step 1 On Renunciation of the World

It is fitting to begin with the good, supremely good, and all-good God, our King (for it is good to start from God when addressing the servants of God), who has honoured all rational beings that He created with the gift of free will. Among these beings, some are His friends, others His true servants, others are unworthy servants, some are entirely estranged, and still others, though weak, are nevertheless His adversaries.

We, the simple and unlearned, ascribe to God, our holy Head, the spiritual and incorporeal beings around Him as His friends. Truly, His servants are all those who tirelessly and faithfully do and have done His will. The unworthy servants are those who think themselves worthy of baptism but have not faithfully kept their covenant with Him. As for those who are strangers to God and His enemies, they are those who are either unbelievers or hold false beliefs. The adversaries are those who not only disregard the Lord's command and cast it off from themselves but also fiercely oppose those who carry it out.

Since each of the aforementioned categories has its own particular and appropriate description, and it is not beneficial for us, the ignorant, to expound on these matters at this time, let us now address those who have piously and faithfully constrained us with their commands, the true servants of God. Stretching out our unworthy hand in undivided obedience and taking up the pen of speech through their wisdom, let us dip it in their humble yet radiant humility and inscribe upon their smooth and white hearts—as if on some sheets, or rather, spiritual tablets—the divine words, or rather the seeds, and let us speak thus:

God, who is the life of all who choose Him, is the salvation of all—both believers and unbelievers, the righteous and the unrighteous, the pious and the impious, the dispassionate and the passionate, monks and laypeople, the wise and the simple, the healthy and the sick, the young and the old. Just as the sun's light shines on everyone, as air changes without distinction, so too is there no favouritism with God.

It is a mark of impiety for a rational and mortal nature to willfully avoid life and consider its own Creator, the ever-existing One, as non-existent. It is unlawful for one to hold the law of God with a wicked intent and to believe in opposition to God. A Christian is one who imitates Christ to the best of their ability, in words, deeds, and thoughts, and who rightly and blamelessly believes in the Holy Trinity. A lover of God is someone who exists in union with all that is natural and sinless and does not neglect doing good to the best of their ability.

A temperate person is one who, amidst temptations, traps, and distractions, strives with all their might to imitate the ways of those who have been freed from them. A monk is someone who lives according to the order and condition of the bodiless beings, while still in a physical and corruptible body. A monk is one who holds firmly to the commands and words of God at all times, in every place, and in every action. A monk is one who constantly exercises control over their nature and unceasingly guards their senses. A monk is a purified body, a cleansed mouth, and an enlightened nous. A monk is a soul that is deeply pained and constantly meditates on death, whether awake or asleep.

Renunciation of the world is the voluntary rejection of praiseworthy material things and the denial of natural inclinations in pursuit of what is beyond nature. All those who eagerly abandon the things of this life do so either for the sake of the coming kingdom, because of the multitude of their sins, or out of love for God. If none of these motives precedes their departure, their renunciation is irrational.

However, the outcome of this journey remains to be seen, and our good Judge awaits it. Let those who have left the world to scatter the burden of their sins imitate those who, before the grave, sit outside the city. Let them not cease from fervent and burning tears, and from the unspoken cries of the heart, until they too see Jesus coming and rolling away the stone of fire from their hearts, and until their nous, like Lazarus, is freed from the bonds of sin, as the Lord commands His angels to "Unbind him, and let him go," to the blessed state of dispassion. If this does not happen, there will be no benefit.

All who wish to leave Egypt and escape from Pharaoh need a mediator like Moses to stand before God and with God, stretching out their hands to God, so that those being led may cross the sea of sins and overcome the Amalek of passions. Those who give themselves to this task without a guide are deceived. Those who left Egypt had Moses; those who fled from Sodom had an angel to guide them.

Those who are healed of their spiritual passions through the care of their spiritual physicians resemble those leaving Egypt. Those who long to shed the uncleanness of their wretched body need an angel or, as it were, someone like an angel to assist them. For the cleansing of wounds, a skilled and knowledgeable physician is required.

Those who attempt to ascend to heaven with their body require true struggle and unceasing pain, especially in the beginnings of renunciation, until our pleasure-loving nature and unfeeling heart are transformed into love for God and purity through evident mourning. Indeed, the bitterness of this struggle is invisible and great, especially for the careless, until we turn our combative and defiled nous, through simplicity and deep gentleness and diligence, into one that is loving and vigilant.

Let us, the passionate and weak, take courage, offering our weakness and spiritual incapacity with unwavering faith to the right hand of Christ, confessing our sins and always seeking His help. We shall receive His assistance far beyond our worth, continually humbling ourselves in the depth of humility. Let everyone who enters into this difficult and narrow but light path understand that they are stepping into fire, awaiting the divine and unquenchable fire to dwell within them.

Let each person examine themselves, and only then partake of the bread of this renunciation, which is mixed with bitterness, and drink from the cup that is filled with tears, so that they do not bring judgment upon themselves. If not everyone who is baptized is saved, I shall remain silent about the rest, for those who come to lay a good foundation will abandon all, despise all, mock all, and shake off all. A good foundation is a threefold and tripartite foundation: innocence, fasting, and chastity. All who are infants in Christ should begin with these, taking the example of visible infants.

Let us not despise or judge circumstantial renunciations. I have seen some who fled and unwillingly came to the King, encountering Him and, though unwilling, they eventually served in His palace and shared in His feast. I have seen seed fall to the ground unwillingly, and yet it produced much fruit and flourished. Just as I have also seen the opposite. I have seen someone come to a hospital for one need, and, being held there by the physician's kindness, they were cleansed and restored, casting off the cloud that darkened their light. Sometimes, what begins unwillingly becomes more steadfast and stronger than those that are voluntary.

Let no one excuse themselves by the weight and multitude of their sins, declaring themselves unworthy of the monastic profession, nor think to degrade themselves due to indulgence, making excuses in sin. Where the infection is great, there is need for greater treatment to remove the filth. For the healthy do not go to the hospital where healing is sought.

If the King Himself wishes to enlist us in His service, we do not delay, nor do we make excuses. But leaving everything behind, we eagerly hasten to Him. Let us be watchful, lest we, being called by the King of kings and Lord of lords to this heavenly rank, make excuses out of sloth and laziness, and find ourselves without excuse before the great judgment seat. For it is possible, though bound by the cares of life and iron chains of concern, to walk, though with difficulty. Even those who have iron around their feet often still walk, but they continually stumble and receive wounds from it.

The unmarried person in the world, bound only by worldly matters, is like one with shackles on their hands; therefore, when they wish to run to the solitary life, they are not hindered. But the one who has married is like one who has shackles on both their hands and feet.

I heard some people in the world, who live carelessly, say to me: "How can we, living with a spouse and burdened with public concerns, lead a monastic life?" To which I replied:

Do all the good you can; do not revile anyone; do not steal from anyone; do not lie to anyone; do not dishonour anyone; do not hate anyone; do not separate yourself from the assemblies; show compassion to those in need; do not scandalize anyone; do not approach what belongs to another; and be content with the wages of your wives. If you do these things, you will not be far from the kingdom of heaven. Let us run with joy and fear in the good fight, not fearing our enemies, even if they are unseen. If they see us retreat out of fear, they will arm themselves more bitterly against us, perceiving that we were afraid. Therefore, let us arm ourselves bravely against them, for no one fights willingly against someone who is eager to fight. The Lord wisely lightened the battles for beginners so that they would not immediately return to the world after just starting. Therefore, rejoice always in the Lord, all you servants of God, recognizing this first sign of the Lord's love for us, as He has called you.

God often reveals Himself in this way: seeing brave souls, He allows battles to come upon them from the beginning, wanting to crown them quickly. The Lord has hidden from those in the world the difficulty of the path, or rather, its ease. For if they knew it, no flesh would ever renounce the world. Give the labours of your youth willingly to Christ, and you will rejoice in old age upon the wealth of goodness; what you gather in youth will feed and comfort those who have grown old. Let us labour especially while we are young; let us run with sobriety, for death is uncertain. We truly have wicked, fierce, deceitful, cunning enemies, who hold fire in their hands and desire to burn the temple of God through the flame within it. They are powerful, tireless, immaterial, invisible enemies. Let no young person accept the words of their demons saying: "Do not wear out your flesh, so that you do not fall into sickness and weakness"; for hardly anyone in the present generation will be found who is willing to destroy this flesh, even if they deprive themselves of many and pleasurable foods. The purpose of this demon is to make our entrance into the struggle weak and sluggish, and thus align the end with the beginning.

Above all, those who truly want to serve Christ should seek out places, ways, seats, and occupations that are suitable for them through spiritual Fathers and personal discernment. For not all are suited for communal life due to gluttony, nor for hermitages due to wrathfulness; each should examine where they fit best. The entire monastic way of life can be found in three main forms of dwelling: in solitary retreat and monasticism; in quietude with one or two others; or in a communal life with patient endurance. The Ecclesiastes says, "Do not turn to the right or to the left; walk in the royal way," for the middle path of those mentioned above is suitable for many. Woe, it says, to the one who is alone, for if he falls into despondency, sleep, laziness, or despair, there is no one among humans to raise him up. But where two or three are gathered in My name, there I am in the midst of them, the Lord has said. Who then is the faithful and wise monk who has kept his fervour unquenched, and until his last day has added fire to fire, fervour to fervour, zeal to zeal, and passion to passion without ceasing?

The one who has put his hand to the plow should not look back.

Step 2 On detachment

He who truly loves the Lord, who sincerely seeks to attain the future kingdom, who genuinely feels sorrow for his own sins, who truly holds the memory of punishment and eternal judgment, who sincerely fears his own departure, will no longer love, care for, or worry about money, possessions, parents, worldly glory, friends, or brothers, nor anything earthly at all. Instead, having cast off every attachment and concern for these things, and having come to hate them, he will follow Christ naked and free from worry, looking always towards heaven and awaiting help from there, as the saint said: "My soul cleaves to you," and as the other, of blessed memory, said: "I did not grow weary following you, and I did not desire the day or rest of man, O Lord." It is the greatest shame to abandon everything previously mentioned after our calling, which the Lord—not a man—has given us, and to care about someone who cannot help us in our time of need, that is, at the time of our departure. For this is what the Lord said: "Turning back is to be found unfit for the kingdom of heaven." Our Lord, knowing our tendency to fall back into worldly things and how easily we turn again to the world while living or associating with the world, said to the one who told Him, "Let me go and bury my father," "Let the dead bury their own dead." The demons, after our renunciation, suggest that those who are merciful to us and sympathetic to the world should be praised, and that they have deprived themselves. The goal of our enemies, through false humility or towards the world, is either to turn us back to the world or to plunge us into despair if we remain alone. It is possible to despise those in the world through pride, and it is possible to ignore them in their absence, to avoid despair and acquire hope. Let us then listen to the Lord who said to the young man who had kept almost all the commandments, "One thing you lack: sell all you have, give to the poor, and make yourself poor, accepting alms."

Let us carefully consider how the Lord condemned all those who live in the world and are spiritually dead, saying to someone, "Leave the dead to bury their own dead," referring to those who are dead in body. The wealth of that young man did not prevent him from approaching baptism. Therefore, it is vain for some to say that the Lord commanded him to sell his wealth for the sake of baptism. Let it suffice for us as the greatest confirmation of the supreme glory of our calling, that we have such knowledge.

We must inquire how those who live in the world and engage in vigils, fasting, toil, and suffering, and who, living a monastic life, as if in a trial or contest, withdraw from men, no longer practice their previous, corrupted and fabricated asceticism. I have seen many and varied plants of virtues cultivated by them in the world, and just as from an underground mire, they are watered by vainglory, driven by showmanship, and fertilized by praises. However, once transplanted into the barren, untraveled land of solitude, free from worldly influences and devoid of vainglorious, foul water, they immediately withered. For plants that thrive in water cannot bear fruit in harsh and arid training grounds.

If someone has hated the world, he has escaped sorrow; but if someone still clings to any of the visible things, he has not yet been freed from sorrow. For how could he not be grieved at the loss of what he loves? In all things, we need great wisdom, especially more than anything else. I have seen many in the world who, burdened by worries, cares, idle talk, and physical vigils, escaped the madness of their own bodies. But when they came to a life free from care, they were defiled by the constant stirring of the body. Let us be cautious, lest while saying we walk the narrow and difficult path, we actually stray onto the broad and easy one. The narrow path will reveal itself to you through the affliction of the stomach, all-night standing, limited water, deprivation of sleep, humiliations, scorn, mockery, cutting off of one's own will, patience in afflictions, enduring contempt without complaint, and not getting angry when wronged. Blessed are those who walk the path of the aforementioned roads, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven. No one will enter the heavenly bridal chamber wearing a crown unless he has renounced the first, second, and third renunciations—namely, the renunciation of all things, people, and parents, the cutting off of one's own will, and the third renunciation of vainglory that follows obedience. "Come out from among them, and be separate, and do not touch the unclean thing of the world," says the Lord.

Who among them has ever performed miracles? Who has raised the dead? Who has cast out demons? No one. For all these are the rewards of the monks, which the world cannot accommodate. If it could, what need would there be for asceticism, or withdrawal from the world? When the demons, after our renunciation, inflame our memory towards our parents, brothers, and warm our hearts towards them, then we must arm ourselves against them with prayer and kindle our hearts with the memory of eternal fire, so that by this remembrance we may extinguish the untimely fire in our hearts.

If someone believes he is unattached to anything, yet his heart is grieved at its loss, he has deceived himself completely. Those young people who are passionately inclined towards bodily desires and food and who wish to join the monastic life should train themselves in all sobriety and prayer, and refrain from all luxury and wickedness, lest their last state becomes worse than the first. The harbor is both a place of safety and a source of danger, as those who sail the spiritual sea know. It is a pitiful sight to see those who were saved in the open sea shipwrecked in the harbor.

Second Ascent: Let the one who runs not imitate his wife, but flee like Lot.

Step 3 On Exile

Exile is the irrevocable abandonment of all things in one’s homeland that oppose our purpose of piety. Exile is an unhesitating character, an unknown life, an unseen aim, and an invisible thought. It is a longing for humility, a desire for hardship, a premise of divine yearning, a great passion, a renunciation of vanity, and the depth of silence. This thought, too, seems to arise naturally and persistently in the beginnings of those who love the Lord, like a divine fire: by this, I mean the separation from one’s own, in pursuit of humility and affliction, urging lovers of such good things to act. Yet, as it is great and praiseworthy, so it also requires great discernment. Not all exile, when undertaken to the extreme, is good; if every prophet is honoured in his own country, as the Lord says, we must be cautious lest our exile becomes a premise for vanity. For exile is the separation from all things in order to make the mind inseparable from God. Exile is the lover and labourer of insatiable mourning. The stranger is one who flees all ties, whether his own or others. Do not expect those with worldly souls to hasten towards solitude or exile, for the thief comes unnoticed. Many who have attempted solitude became lazy and were lost together when the fire was extinguished over time. Once you have received the flame, run, for you do not know when it will be extinguished and leave you in darkness.

Others are saved, which we are all called to do. For the divine Apostle says, "So then, each of us will give an account of ourselves to God." And again, he says, "You who teach another, do you not teach yourself?" As he said, concerning others, I do not know, but we must all certainly examine ourselves. In exile, secure the seeker and the pleasure-loving demon. For exile gives him an opportunity. Detachment is good, and exile is the mother of it; the one who has exiled himself for the Lord no longer has any relations, lest he be seen as being driven by passions. When you exile yourself from the world, no longer touch the world. For passions tend to return. Eve was involuntarily exiled from paradise, and the monk voluntarily from his own homeland. If one desired again the tree of disobedience, he would face certain danger from his carnal kin. Avoid the places of the dead like a scourge. For when the fruit is not present, we do not constantly crave it. And do not let this method and trick of the thieves escape you; they suggest to us that we should not separate from worldly things, saying that we would receive a great reward if we hold on to our worldly ties, which we must not believe but rather do the opposite. When, after withdrawing from our loved ones for a time or for years, we achieve a little piety, or contrition, or self-control, then the thoughts of vanity arise and permit us to return to our homeland, under the pretext of edifying others and providing an example and benefit to those observing our unlawful actions. But if we are prosperous in words and cherished knowledge, then they set us up as saviours of souls and teachers in the world, so that the good things we gathered in the harbor may be scattered in the sea. Let us strive to imitate not Lot's wife, but Lot himself: for a soul that turns back to where it came from will become like salt that loses its flavour and remains immovable. Flee Egypt without turning back. For those who turned back in heart did not see the land of dispassion, Jerusalem.

It sometimes happens that those who, out of infantile simplicity, have left their homes and have been fully purified, may beneficially return to them, perhaps with the aim of saving others after securing their own salvation. Although Moses, that God-seer, was sent by God for the salvation of his kin, he faced many dangers in Egypt, or rather in the world, such as the obscuration’s of darkness. It is better to grieve one's parents than to grieve the Lord; for He both created and saved, whereas they often lost and condemned to punishment those whom they loved. The stranger is one who sits in knowledge. We do not depart from our loved ones or our places because we hate them—God forbid!—but to escape the harm they bring us. As in all things, Christ is our teacher even in these matters. For it appears that He also often left His parents according to the flesh and heard from some, "Your mother and your brothers are seeking you," whereupon our good Lord and teacher displayed dispassionate hatred by saying, "My mother and my brothers are those who do the will of my Father in heaven." Let your father be the one who can share the burden of your sins with you and who wills to do so; let your mother be contrition, which can wash away your filth; let your brother be the one who shares your upward journey and struggles with you. Let your inseparable companion be the memory of death; let your beloved children be the sighs of your heart. Make your body your servant. Let your friends be the holy powers that can assist you at the hour of death if they become your friends. "This is the generation of those who seek the Lord." The love of God extinguishes the love of parents. The one who says he has both is deceiving himself, listening to the words of the one who said, "No one can serve two masters," and what follows. "I did not come," says the Lord, "to bring peace on earth" (meaning among parents and children and brothers who choose to serve Me), "but a sword and division," to separate those who love God from those who love the world: the material from the immaterial, the proud from the humble. The Lord rejoices in ambiguity and separation, when it happens out of love for Him. Be careful, lest everything appears full of water to you because of your attachment to your dear ones, and you join the flood of conflict. Do not pity the tears of parents or friends; otherwise, you will weep eternally. When you are surrounded like bees, or rather like wasps, by those who mourn over you, quickly turn your gaze towards death and keep your soul's eye fixed unchangingly on your deeds, so that you may drive away pain with pain. Our own people, and those who are not ours, deceitfully promise to fulfill all our desires, but their goal is to hinder us in our best course, and thus lead us back to our own aims. Let our withdrawal be to more desolate, unpretentious, and humble places; otherwise, we will fly with passion. Hide your noble birth, and do not parade your good reputation, lest you be found different in body from what you are in reality. No one has given himself so completely to exile as that great man who heard, "Get out of your country and from your kindred and from your father's house," even though he was called to a foreign and barbarous land. There are times when the Lord glorifies someone who has gone into exile for a long time even more. Yet even if the glory is God-given, it is good to turn it away with the shield of humility. Whenever demons, like those of great achievement, praise our exile, or even people do, we should recall the one who, for our sake, exiled Himself from heaven to earth, and we will find ourselves unable to repay that debt for eternity. It is a grievous thing to attach oneself to some relative or even stranger, which can gradually draw us back to the world and completely extinguish our fire of contrition. Just as it is impossible to look with one eye toward heaven and with the other toward the earth, so it is impossible for the mind and body that have gone into exile not to endanger the soul. A good and orderly character is built in us with much labour and toil, and what is achieved with much toil can be lost in a single moment. "Bad company corrupts good character," as do worldly and disorderly associations. He who associates with worldly people after renouncing the world, or who finds himself near them, will inevitably fall into their snares, or he will defile his heart with thoughts of them, or if not defiled, he will condemn those who are, and will be defiled together with them.

Step 3 Continued: On Dreams Following Introductory Remarks

It is evident that our understanding is entirely incomplete, filled with ignorance, and cannot be hidden. Just as the throat discerns food, so too does hearing discern meanings; the weakness of the eyes is revealed by the sun, and the ignorance of the soul is revealed by words. Yet the law of love compels even beyond our strength. I do not presume, nor do I define it, as a continuation after the words on exile, but rather, within them, to include a small section on dreams, so that we might not remain uninitiated into even this deception of the deceivers. A dream is a movement of the mind while the body is at rest. A fantasy is a deception of the eyes in a sleeping mind; a fantasy is an ecstasy of the mind in an awake body; a fantasy is an insubstantial vision. The reason why we desired to speak of dreams after the previous order is obvious: when, for the sake of the Lord, we leave behind our homes and loved ones and sell ourselves into exile for the love of God, then the demons will try to disturb us through dreams, showing us our loved ones either in distress, dying, or suffering on our behalf. Therefore, whoever believes in dreams is like one who chases after his own shadow, thinking he can grasp it. Demons of vanity prophesy in dreams, cunningly predicting the future, and announcing it to us in advance. When these visions are fulfilled, we are astonished and, believing that we are close to the gift of prophecy, we elevate our thoughts. Among those who believe the demon, a prophet often arose; among those who despised him, he frequently lied. A spirit that has seen what is within this air and, recognizing one who is dying, prophesied it to the frivolous in a dream. They know nothing of the future through foreknowledge since even sorcerers could predict death for us. They have shown themselves to us in dreams as angels of light; and when we awoke, they overwhelmed us with joy and vanity. This will be the sign of delusion for you: angels show punishment, judgments, and separations; when we awake, they leave us fearful and gloomy. Whenever we begin to obey the demons in our dreams, then they will mock us even while we are awake. Whoever trusts in dreams is altogether unproven. But believe only in those who proclaim the judgment for all; if despair troubles you, know that this too comes from demons.

The third path of the Trinity: whoever has embarked on it should not look to the right or to the left.

Step 4 On Blessed and Ever-Memorable Obedience

The discourse has now appropriately turned to us, the boxers and athletes of Christ. For every fruit is preceded by a flower, and every act of obedience is preceded by exile, whether of the body or of the will. In these two virtues, like golden wings, the saint ascends tirelessly to heaven. Perhaps someone inspired by the Spirit once sang about it: "Who will give me wings like a dove, so that I may fly through practice and find rest through contemplation and humility?" Let us not overlook even the form of these courageous warriors in our discourse, making it evident how they hold the shield of faith towards God and their trainer, in it repelling every thought of unbelief or change, continuously raising the sword of the Spirit, and killing every will of their own that comes close to them. They are also clothed in iron breastplates of meekness and endurance, repelling every insult, prick, and arrow directed at them. They also have the helmet of salvation, the protection of the leader through prayer. They stand not with both feet together, but with one foot extending for service and the other firmly set in prayer.

Obedience is the complete renunciation of one's own soul, demonstrated effectively through the body. Or perhaps, obedience is the mortification of the limbs within a living mind. Obedience is an unexamined movement, a voluntary death, a life free of meddling, a carefree danger, an unprepared defense before God; fearlessness of death, a risk-free voyage, a sleeping journey. Obedience is the burial of the will and the resurrection of humility; the dead do not argue or dispute, whether in good things or what seems evil. For the one who has piously killed his own soul will be justified above all. Obedience is the laying aside of judgment in the abundance of discernment, the beginning of the mortification of the soul's will and the toil of a bodily member; sometimes toil, sometimes painlessness in the middle; finally, all insensitivity and immobility of pain: then it is seen to be toiling, and this blessed dead one, this living dead one, is in pain when he sees himself doing his own will, fearing the burden of his own judgment.

All those who have undertaken to strip for the stadium of the spiritual confession, all those who wish to bear the yoke of Christ on their own neck, all those who strive to place their own burden on the neck of another, all those who hasten to write their deeds willingly, and instead wish to write freedom for themselves: all those who are lifted up by the hands of others and swim across this great sea, know that you have undertaken to walk a short and rough road, possessing only one delusion, which is called self-will. For the one who renounces this in all things, even in those that seem good and spiritual and pleasing to God, has already passed before beginning to walk. For obedience is distrust of oneself in all good things until the end of life.

Those who are about to bow their necks in the Lord with a purpose and reason of humility, and truly entrust their salvation to another in the Lord: before entering, if any wickedness and prudence happen to be with us, let us examine and test the captain, so that we may not fall upon the sailor as captain, the sick as a doctor, and the passionate as dispassionate, and finding ourselves shipwrecked instead of reaching the harbor. After the entrance into the stadium of piety and submission, let us no longer perhaps still view our good judge and trainer in it as a human being, overlooking even small errors. If not, we will not benefit at all from submission if we examine. It is absolutely necessary for those who wish to have unwavering faith in those who oversee them, to keep their achievements indelibly and always in their hearts, so that when the demons sow doubt in us towards them, we can silence them with what is remembered in us. For as much as faith flourishes in the heart, so much the body hastens in service. But when it stumbles into disbelief, it has fallen. For whatever is not from faith is sin.

If your mind suggests examining or condemning the one who leads you, flee as if from fornication; do not give any room, entry, or beginning to this serpent. Speak to the dragon: "Oh deceiver, it is not I who judge, but He has taken up my judgment." The Fathers consider the psalmody as a weapon, prayer as a wall, and the spotless tear as a bath. They have judged the blessed obedience to be a confession, without which no one passionate will see the Lord. The one who is obedient judges himself; if he obeys completely for the Lord, even if it seems not completely, he has removed his own judgment. But if he fulfills his own will in some things, or seems to obey, he bears his own burden. But if the leader does not stop reproaching him, he is well off; but if he remains silent, I do not know what to say. Those who submit in simplicity in the Lord complete a good course, not having disturbed themselves with scrupulousness the craftiness of the demons. Before all, let us confess to our good and only judge, and if he commands, to all; for those who are beaten will not progress to worse, but will be healed.

I have seen a frightening judgment in a monastery, where a good judge and shepherd was present. For it happened that a certain one from a band of robbers came to the monastic life, whom that excellent shepherd and doctor commanded to enjoy seven days of complete rest for contemplation and staying in one place. After the seventh day, the shepherd privately called him and asked if it pleased him to live with them; and seeing him agree with all sincerity, he asked again what unlawful thing he had done in the world. When he knew him to have confessed everything eagerly with words, he said again, testing him: "I want you," he said, "to triumph over all the brotherhood with these things." And he, who truly hated his own sin, despised all shame, and promised unhesitatingly: "And if you wish," he said, "in the middle of the city of Alexandria." Then the shepherd gathered all the sheep, numbering three hundred and thirty, in the church, and when the divine assembly was held—it was once a Sunday among them—he lived without remembrance of idle talk or jesting; and if any of them began to doubt towards their neighbor, another would come by and, having made repentance, would dispel the anger. And if he noticed them holding grudges, he reported it to the one who managed the second of the order, and prepared them to reconcile before the sun set. If they hardened and remained hardened, they were either commanded not to partake of food until reconciliation or were expelled from the monastery. This praiseworthy and careful practice was not in vain among them, but produced much fruit and was evident. For many were shown among them to be practical, visionary, discerning, and humble. And it was terrifying to see them, and angelic in appearance. Revered elders, holy in appearance, were running in obedience like children and boasting of their own humility as their greatest pride. I have seen there men in obedience for fifty years, who implored to know if they had received any consolation from such toil. Some said that they had reached an abyss of humility, through which they deflected every war for eternity. Others said that they had reached complete insensitivity and indifference to insults and abuses. I have seen others among those blessed ones, with their angelic grey hair, who had advanced into the deepest innocence, a wise simplicity, a voluntary conscience, and a God-directed spirit. For just as the evil man is twofold, one thing outwardly, and another hidden; so the simple man is not twofold, but one thing. Not some foolish or unwise by the worldly standards of the aged, whom they also like to call delirious; but outwardly gentle, approachable, cheerful, having unpretentious, unaffected, and unadulterated speech and character, something not found in many; and inwardly having God Himself and their leader as they breathe like innocent children. And they possessed a bold and steadfast mind’s eye towards the demons and the passions.

My time, holy summit and beloved gathering, will run short in recounting the virtues of those blessed ones and their life that imitates heaven. However, it is better to embellish our discourse with the sweat of their labours and to stir up a zeal that is pleasing to God, or to draw from our own exhortations. For without any contradiction, the lesser is adorned by the greater. But I ask, and I ask again, that you do not suspect us of writing anything misleading; for it is customary for disbelief to spoil what is beneficial. Let us hold fast to what was previously said.

There was a man named Isidore, of noble lineage from the city of Alexandria, who renounced the world and joined the aforementioned monastery some time ago, and I also encountered him there. The all-holy shepherd, seeing that this man was very malicious, fierce, dreadful, and arrogant, wisely devised a plan to counter the devil's cunning through human ingenuity, and he said to Isidore: "If you have indeed chosen to bear the yoke of Christ, I wish for you to practice obedience above all." And he replied, "As iron submits to the blacksmith, so, most holy one, I have given myself to submission." The great one, seeing his resolve, immediately assigned him the task of standing at the gate of the monastery and said, "I want you, brother, to stand at the gate of the monastery and to make a prostration before every soul entering or leaving, saying, 'Pray for me, Father, for I am an epileptic.'" He obeyed like an angel to the Lord. After serving there for seven years, and advancing into profound humility and contrition, the blessed one desired, after the legal seven years and the man’s unparalleled patience, to count him among the brothers and ordain him. But he made many supplications through others and through me, the weak one, asking the shepherd to allow him to complete his course in that same state, hinting at and subtly alluding to his approaching end, which indeed came to pass. For after the teacher permitted him to remain in that role, he gloriously departed to the Lord on the tenth day after his repose, taking the gatekeeper of the monastery with him to the Lord. For the blessed one had said to him, "If I gain any confidence before the Lord, you will quickly be inseparable from me even there." And this came to pass, as a great confirmation of his unshakable obedience and God-like humility.

I asked the great Isidore, while he was still with us, what work his mind was engaged in while serving at the gate. And the ever-memorable one, wishing to benefit me, did not hide anything. "At first," he said, "I thought that I had been sold for my sins. Therefore, with all bitterness, violence, and blood, I cast myself into repentance. After one year, I remained calm in heart, waiting for the reward of patience from God. After another year passed, I began to think myself unworthy of the life in the monastery, the sight of the Fathers, the participation in the mysteries, and even the face of anyone. So, lowering my eyes and humbling my mind, I began to earnestly beg those entering and leaving for their prayers."

Once, while sitting at the table with the great abbot, he inclined his holy mouth to my ear and said, "Do you want me to show you a divine mind in the deepest old age?" I earnestly asked, and the righteous one called a certain Laurentius, who had been in the monastery for forty-eight years and was second in the priesthood. When he came and made a prostration before the abbot, he was blessed by him. But after he rose, he said nothing at all, but stood before the table without eating. His entrance was very noble, so much so that I was ashamed even to look directly at the worker’s face. For he was completely grey-haired, eighty years old. Without responding, he remained standing until the meal was finished. After we had risen, the holy one sent me to ask the great Isidore what he thought while standing at the table. He replied, "Placing the image of Christ upon the shepherd, I never thought to judge him, but rather saw it as coming from God. Therefore, Father John, I stood not as before the table of men, but as before the altar of God, praying to God. Never did I think an evil thought against the shepherd, out of my faith and love for him." For someone has said, "Love does not think evil." But know this, Father, that when one gives themselves over to simplicity and voluntary innocence, the evil one no longer finds place or time to act. The Lord, who is just, as He was the Savior of rational creatures, sent another to manage the affairs of the monastery. For if anyone was prudent there, they were also gentle, which was very rare.

This elder was brought for the benefit of others when he ordered him to be driven out of the church for no apparent reason. But knowing him to be blameless in the matter for which the shepherd reproved him, I made a defense for the steward privately to the great one. The wise one said, "I also know, Father, but just as it is not right, but pitiful, to snatch bread from the mouth of a starving child, so too should the one in charge of souls not harm himself or the worker by causing him to lose the crown that he earns by enduring insults, dishonour, humiliation, and mockery at every hour." For he is wronged in three great ways: first, by being deprived of the reward that comes from reproof; second, because he could have benefited others by the virtue of another, but did not do so; and third, which is the most grievous, because even those who seem to be patient and enduring, when neglected for a time and not reproved or rebuked by the leader, lose their rightful meekness and patience. For even if the earth is good and fruitful, it knows that a lack of water can cause it to produce thorns of pride, fornication, and fearlessness. Knowing this, the great Apostle wrote to Timothy: "Be present; reprove them in season, out of season." While I was arguing with that true leader, and presenting the weakness of our generation, saying that many perhaps fall away from the flock due to the reproofs, whether deserved or not, the house of wisdom again said, "A soul bound to the shepherd with love and faith in Christ will not depart, even unto bloodshed, especially if it has ever received kindness from him during its wounds, remembering the one who said, 'Neither angels, nor rulers, nor powers, nor anything in creation will be able to separate us from the love of Christ.' But if a soul is not bound and fused and united, I wonder if it will not complete its sojourn in that place in vain, tied to a feigned and deceptive obedience. But the great one has not deceived himself; rather, he has restrained, guided, completed, and offered blameless sacrifices to Christ. Let us listen to the wisdom of God and marvel at finding it in earthen vessels."

I was amazed while there at the faith, patience, and indomitable endurance of some of the newly professed, but also of those much advanced. For the sake of edification, I asked one of the brothers, Abba Kyros, who had been in the monastery for fifteen years, and who was often wronged by nearly everyone, sometimes even driven away from the table by the servants, as he was by nature a brother very much lacking in control over his tongue. I said to him, "Brother Abba Kyros, why do I see you driven away from the table every day, and often sleeping without supper?" And he said, "Believe me, Father, my Fathers are testing me to see if I am a monk, though they do not do this in truth. So, knowing the purpose of the great one and theirs, I endure everything without complaint. And behold, for fifteen years I have had this in mind, just as they told me upon my entry: that they test those who renounce for thirty years; and rightly so, Father John. For gold is not perfected without testing." This brave Abba Kyros, having remained in the monastery for two more years after my arrival, departed to the Lord, saying to the Fathers before his end, "I thank the Lord and you that I remained untroubled by the devil for seventeen years." And the righteous shepherd ordered him to be laid among the confessors with the saints who rested there.

I would wrong all those who are zealous for what is good if I were to bury the achievement and struggle of Macedonius, the first of his rank, in the grave of silence. He, who was ever devoted to the Lord, once, during the feast of the Holy Theophany, pleaded with the shepherd (bishop) two days before in the city of Alexandria to enter for a certain personal need, promising to leave the city quickly for the sake of the feast's observance and preparation. But the evil devil, placing an obstacle before the archdeacon, caused the holy feast to be observed in the monastery according to the rule which he had received from the superior. After the day had passed, the shepherd removed him from his ministry and placed him among the lowest ranks of novices. The good deacon, however, accepted this judgment of the Father with patience and endurance, without any distress, as if another had been reproved and not himself. After forty days of remaining in this lowly state, the wise superior restored him to his former rank. Yet, on the very next day, the archdeacon again pleaded with him to be put back into his former state of penance, saying that he had sinned grievously in the city. Recognizing that the holy man was not speaking the truth, but was seeking this out of humility, the superior granted his good desire. And it was a sight to see this venerable elder, living in the rank of the novices, and earnestly begging all to pray sincerely for him. 'Because,' he said, 'I have fallen into the sin of disobedience, but I, the humble one, have found courage through this great Macedonius, and why should I not voluntarily adopt this humble way of life? Never before have I experienced such relief from all inner turmoil, nor such sweetness of divine light within myself as I do now.'

It is the nature of angels, as some say, not to fall, and perhaps even not to be able to fall, but for humans to fall and to rise again quickly whenever this happens; but for demons alone, it is characteristic that having fallen, they do not rise again.

The one who was entrusted with the ministry of the monastery found courage. 'When I was young,' he said, 'and living under the care of irrational concerns, I happened to fall into a very heavy sin of the soul; but since I had made it my custom never to conceal a serpent in the den of my heart, I held onto the serpent's tail and exposed it to the healer. By the serpent's tail, I mean the end of the deed; and the healer, smiling, said to me, "Go, my child, and continue your ministry as before, without any fear whatsoever." And trusting in his fervent faith, I received the assurance of healing in a short time and ran my course, rejoicing and trembling at the same time.'

Every order of created beings, as some say, has its own differences of rank. Now, since there were also differences of opinion and advancement among the assembly of the brothers, whenever the healer noticed anyone seeking glory during the times of visitors from the world to the monastery, he subjected them to the harshest insults and the most humiliating services in the presence of those visitors, so that they would henceforth quickly withdraw whenever they saw visitors arriving from the world. And what was extraordinary to see was the vanity itself driving itself away and hiding from human eyes. 'May the Lord not deprive me of the prayer of my holy Father,' I prayed. About a week before my departure from the holy place, he summoned a man, who was admirable in his direction of the second rank under the shepherd, by the name of Menas, who had lived in the community for fifty-nine years, having served in every ministry. On the third day, while we were performing the usual ritual for the repose of the saint, suddenly the entire place where the saint was laid became filled with fragrance. The great one then permitted us to uncover the grave in which the saint was laid, and when we did this, we all saw that from his honoured feet there flowed like two springs the fragrant oil of myrrh. Then, the teacher said to all, "Do you see? Behold, the sweat of his labours, which the feet of his labour brought forth, has been offered to God as myrrh." And indeed it was so. And many other achievements of this most holy Menas were reported to us by the fathers of that place, among which they also said this: 'It is said that the superior, wishing to test his God-given patience, had him ascend to the rank of the abbot and, when he placed repentance before the abbot in the evening, also received the customary portion, pleading to be allowed to stay prostrate on the ground until the hour of the canon.' Then, having blessed and reproached him as a lover of glory and impatience, he raised him up. The saint knew well that he would bear this trial nobly, and so he acted for the edification of all. And the disciple of this holy Menas reported to us about the steward: "Therefore," he said, "when I inquired of him repeatedly whether he had perhaps fallen asleep in that prostration before the abbot, he assured me that he had recited the entire Psalter while lying prostrate on the ground." I will not fail to adorn the crown of this discourse with this emerald that follows.

Once, I raised a conversation with some of the most honourable elders concerning quietness, and they, with a smiling face and a cheerful demeanour, humorously replied to me: 'We, Father John, being of a material nature, also adopt a more material way of life, choosing, according to the measure of our weakness, to endure the warfare against men, who are sometimes fierce and sometimes repentant, rather than to constantly engage with demons who are always raging and arming themselves against us.' Another one of the blessed, who had much love for me and boldness towards God, said to me with kindness: 'If indeed you possess the energy of the soul's perception, as the one who said, "I can do all things through Christ who strengthens me," if the Holy Spirit of purity like dew upon the Virgin has come upon you; if the power of the Most High of endurance has overshadowed you; then live like a man (Christ our God), girding your loins with the towel of obedience, and after rising from the banquet of quietness, wash the feet of the brothers in a spirit of humility. Rather, crawl under the feet of the assembly with a humble mind. Set strict and sleepless gatekeepers at the gate of your heart to control your mind that is easily led astray by the distractions of the body; practice spiritual quietness in the midst of moving and shaking limbs. The most paradoxical thing of all, be undisturbed in your soul amidst the noise: restrain your tongue from jumping to rash words in arguments; fight with this mistress seventy times seven times in the day; nail to the wood of the soul's cross a mind that is struck like an anvil with repeated hammers and blows, mocked, reproached, ridiculed, wronged, and not at all shaken, nor enduring any break, but remaining wholly smooth and unmoved. Strip off the will as a garment of shame, and enter naked into the arena. Clothe yourself with the rare and hard-to-find breastplate of faith, not dissolving nor being wounded by disbelief in the Judge. Restrain the hand that rashly reaches for the reins of temperance; tie your eye to the meditation of death, not wanting to inspect the size and beauty of bodies. Silence the curious mind that in idle carelessness wants to judge your brother; and always display true love and sympathy towards your neighbor. Everyone will truly know by this, dearest father, that you are a disciple of Christ, if we have love for one another in the assembly.' "Come, come," said the good friend again, "come and dwell with us." Reject every hour's mockery like living water, since after searching out all that is pleasant under the sun, David finally said in wonder: "Behold, how good and pleasant it is for brothers to dwell together in unity." If we have not yet been deemed worthy of the blessing of such patience and obedience, it is still good to acknowledge our own weakness and, standing far from the athletic arena, to bless those who compete and pray for their endurance. I was defeated by a good father and the best teacher who fought for us evangelically and prophetically, and I have definitely wished to give the prizes to blessed obedience. Having mentioned one more of these blessed virtues, I will now, as if departing from paradise, place before you the worthless and unprofitable thorns.

In prayer, while we often stand together, the blessed shepherd noticed some who had made certain encounters and interactions, and standing for a week's time before the church, he commanded everyone entering and leaving to make prostrations, and the most remarkable thing, even though they were clerics, that is, presbyters, he included this among them. Seeing one of the brothers standing with more heart-felt attention than many others during the chanting, and especially at the beginning of the hymns, conversing with some according to his own habits and face, I asked to understand the meaning of the blessed man's demeanour. He, knowing that hiding would not be helpful, said: "I am accustomed, Father John, to gather my thoughts and mind together with my soul from the very beginning of the service and to call out to them: 'Come, let us worship and fall down before Christ, our King and God.' I once caught the cook doing this; for when I saw a small scroll hanging from his belt, I learned that he was recording his thoughts every day and reporting them all to the shepherd. Not only this, but I also observed many others doing the same in that place; this was a command of the great one as I had heard. Once, one of the brothers was driven away by him after slandering his neighbor as talkative and garrulous. He persisted at the gate of the monastery for a week, pleading for entrance and forgiveness; and when the compassionate one learned that he had not eaten anything at all during the six days, he informed him: 'If you wish to make your dwelling in the monastery, I will place you in the rank of the penitent.' And when the penitent received this with joy, the shepherd commanded that he be placed in the solitary part of the monastery among those who mourn over their sins, which was done."

Since we have mentioned this monastery, let us say a few words about it. It was a place called the 'Prison,' a part of the large monastery, utterly desolate: no smoke was ever seen there, no wine, no oil for food, nor anything else, but only bread and thin vegetables. In this place, those bound together by the call were shut in without provision, each one alone and separate, or at most two, until the superior was informed about each one. He appointed for them also a great topos-maker named Isaac, who demanded of those handed over to him unceasing prayer; they had many branches to hinder despondency.

This life, this state, this way of living belongs to those who truly seek the face of the God of Jacob: to admire the holy labours is good; to aspire to them brings salvation; but to desire to imitate their way of life exactly is irrational and impossible. Let us remember the rebukes for our sins until the Lord, seeing our violence, the violence of those who strive with Him, erases these sins and transforms the pain that bites us in our hearts into joy. For in the multitude of my sorrows within my heart, as it is said, Your comforts have delighted my soul. Let us not forget the words spoken to the Lord: You who have shown me many and grievous troubles, and turned again and revived me, and brought me up again from the depths of the earth after my fall. Blessed is he who, being reviled for God daily, has overcome himself. He will dance with the martyrs and will speak boldly with the angels. Blessed is the monk who considers himself worthy of all dishonour and rejection at every hour; blessed is he who has completely mortified his own will and handed over his care to the Lord, for he will stand on the right hand of the Crucified One. If someone has rejected both just and unjust rebuke from himself, he has rejected his own salvation. If someone has accepted it, whether with pain or without pain, he will quickly obtain forgiveness for his sins. Show to God, before your Father, faith and love sincerely, and he will unknowingly add you to his own, according to your disposition towards him. The one who always exposes the serpent clearly has shown faith. The one who conceals it has yet been deceived into hopelessness. Then someone will truly know his brotherly love and genuine affection when he sees himself mourning for his brother's faults and rejoicing for his progress and gifts. The one who uses his own words, even if he speaks the truth, to establish and promote himself, should know that he is afflicted with the sickness of the devil. And if he does this in conversation with equals, there may be hope that he could be cured by the rebuke of superiors. But if he behaves this way towards his superiors or those wiser than himself, it is an incurable condition from men. The one who has not submitted to words has not submitted to deeds either. The one who is faithless in small things will not be steadfast in deeds, labouring in vain and receiving nothing of the holy submission if he brings judgment upon himself. If someone has acquired a clear conscience to the utmost in submission to his Father, he will therefore wait for death as for sleep, or rather as for life, daily without fear, knowing with certainty that not he but the superior will be held accountable at the time of separation. If someone has accepted a ministry from the Father in the Lord without compulsion and has experienced an unexpected stumbling in it, let him not blame the one who gave him the weapon but rather the one who received it. For he received a weapon for fighting the enemy but directed it against his own heart. Yet, if he has compelled himself for the Lord and has told the giver about his weakness, let him be encouraged: even if he has fallen, he has not died. I have forgotten, my friends, to add this sweet bread of virtue, that I have seen in the Lord those who, obediently to insults, have been grinding themselves down and dishonouring themselves for the sake of God, so that when they are later confronted with external dishonour, they may be prepared and not be troubled by it. For a soul that acknowledges its confession is held by it as by a bridle, keeping it from sinning. For we do fearlessly practice what is concealed in darkness. When the superior is absent, and we imagine that he is present with us in his image, and we reject any meeting, word, food, or sleep, or anything else for which we have perceived that he has a distaste, then we have truly known uncorrupted obedience. The illegitimate children rejoice at the absence of the teacher, but the legitimate ones consider it a loss.

Once I asked one of the novice, begging to know how obedience acquires humility, and he said: 'Even if the obedient servant raises the dead or acquires tears or is delivered from wars, he always considers that it is by the prayer of the spiritual father that he has done this and remains himself a stranger and foreigner to the vain dwelling. For how can one be exalted by that which another has helped him accomplish and not by his own effort?' The hesychast does not know this work. For, to him, the idea that he has accomplished anything by his own effort or diligence is considered folly. The one who has conquered the deceitful devices of the devil in obedience remains forever a servant of Christ, an eternal obedient one. The devil strikes at those who are obedient, sometimes by defiling them and making them hard-hearted, causing them to become troublesome and restless contrary to their habit, sometimes by making them dry, fruitless, and sluggish in prayer, sleepy and darkened, so that having received no benefit from their obedience, but even lagging behind, they may be torn away from the contest. For he does not allow them to understand that often the removal of the apparent good things within us is a means of deep humility. The previously mentioned deceiver has often been repelled by patience in some. Then, while still speaking, another angel standing nearby attempts to deceive us in another way after a little while. I have seen obedient ones who were very devout, kind, self-controlled, diligent, unyielding, and fervent in the protection of their father, with whom the demons, seeing that they were likely to reach perfection and dispassion through quietness, sowed suggestions of restlessness, leading them away from the harbor into the open sea, where they encountered storms, and lacking navigators, they were miserably endangered by this foul and salty sea. It is necessary for the sea to be stirred up and become rough so that the matter which the rivers of the passions and the chaff and all the corruption in them have brought into it may be cast up again onto the land. Let us observe, and we will find that after the storm in the sea, there is a deep calm. The one who sometimes obeys and sometimes disobeys his father is like a man who sometimes applies collyrium, sometimes lime to his eye. 'For,' as it is said, 'he who builds and he who tears down, what has he profited but toil?' Do not deceive, my son, in pretending obedience to the Lord, by bringing your faults to the teacher as if from another person. There is no way to escape shame except by shame itself. Expose your bruise to the healer; speak and do not be ashamed. 'My wound, Father, my blow, is from my own negligence and not from anything else. No one else is to blame, not man, not spirit, not body, nor anything else, but my own negligence.' Be as one convicted in your confession, bowing to the ground, and if possible, wetting the feet of the judge and healer with your tears as if they were the feet of Christ. It is often the habit of the demons either to suggest that we do not confess at all or to do so as if from another person or to blame others for our own sin as if they were responsible.

If everything depends on habit and follows it, then much more so do good things, as they have God as their great helper. You will not toil, my son, for many years to find blessed rest in yourself if you fully give yourself up to dishonour from the beginning. Do not disdain to make your confession before God as your helper with a contrite spirit. For I have seen condemned men, in the most wretched state, with fervent confession and supplication, softening the severity of the judge and transforming his wrath into compassion. For this reason, John the Baptist also demanded confession before baptism from those who approached him, not because he needed it, but because he was concerned for the salvation of those who approached. Let us not be alarmed if we are still tempted after confession. For it is better to struggle with defilements than with conceit. Do not rush, nor be carried away by the stories of the hesychasts and hermits, for you are on the path of the first martyrdom. And do not flee from the arena when you fall. For at that time, we need a healer much more. The one who stumbles with aid is one who, without aid, would not only stumble but also die. When we are thrown down, the demons rush upon us quickly, seizing upon the pretext of a reasonable or rather unreasonable argument, offering us quietness. Their purpose is to add wound upon wound when our healer is powerless, so that we are forced to turn to another. For without a healer, few are healed. Who is he who thinks to contradict us when we set limits? 'For every ship that has an experienced pilot, if it falls into a shipwreck without a pilot, it will certainly perish.' From obedience comes humility; from humility comes dispassion. If 'in our humiliation the Lord remembered us, and redeemed us from our enemies,' there is no obstacle to saying that dispassion comes from obedience, through which the end of humility is achieved. It begins with the latter as Moses with the law. But the daughter perfects the mother, as Mary with the synagogue. Those who are sick after experiencing the healer and the benefit from him and choose another before complete healing are worthy of all punishment from God. Do not flee the hands of the one who has presented you to the Lord, for you will not find someone like him in your life. It is not safe for the inexperienced to separate himself from the multitude of soldiers and engage in a single combat, and it is not without danger for a monk to enter into quietness without prior experience and much training in spiritual warfare. For one is naturally inclined to physical danger, the other to spiritual danger. 'The Scripture says that two are better than one.' This means that it is good for a son with his father, through the energy of the Holy Spirit, to struggle against temptations. He who deprives a blind man of his guide, a flock of its shepherd, the one who is being led by his hand, the infant of his father, the sick of his healer, the ship of its pilot, brings danger to both. The one who tries to wrestle with spirits without assistance is killed by them. Those who have come to the hospital in the beginning should note their own sufferings. Those in obedience should note the humility that comes upon them. For the relief from sufferings for the former and the increase in the recognition of one's own fault for the latter is a sure sign of health, as nothing else will appear. Let your conscience be a mirror of your obedience, and that will be sufficient.

Those who are in quiet obedience to their father have only demons opposing them; those who are in the assembly struggle both with demons and men. The former keep the commandments of their teacher more strictly through the continuous sight of him; the latter often violate them a little because of his absence. However, if some are diligent and zealous, through their patience in stumbling, they compensate for the lack and receive double crowns. Let us keep ourselves with all vigilance. For a harbor full of ships is prone to shattering them, especially those that have been secretly eaten away by worms, though outwardly polished. Let us practice the utmost silence and ignorance towards the superior; for a silent man, the son of philosophy, always acquires much knowledge. I have seen an obedient one who, seizing the explanation from the mouth of the superior, rejected obedience, seeing in it pride rather than humility. Let us be sober in all things, watchful in all things, and vigilant in all things, as to when and how the ministry of prayer should be preferred. Do not always attend to yourself among your brothers, nor hasten to appear more righteous than any of them. For you will harm them with your false zeal and yourself by producing pride from every corner. Be zealous in soul, showing nothing of this in body, neither in appearance, word, nor gesture, and this only if you have ceased from despising your neighbor. If you are ready for this, be like your brothers and not unlike them in conceit. I have seen an untested disciple boasting about the achievements of his teacher among some men, and while he seemed to gain glory for himself from the other's grain, he rather brought shame upon himself, as everyone said to him: 'How did a good tree bring forth a barren branch?' We are not judged as patient by enduring the father's rebuke bravely, but when we are despised and struck by every man; for we bear the father out of respect and debt. Drink eagerly every mockery as living water from every man, as one who seeks to be cleansed of impurity; then deep purity will spring up in your soul, and the light of God will not fail from your heart. Let no one who sees the assembly of brothers and is satisfied with himself boast in his mind. For the thieves are all around. I remember the one who said, 'When you have done all that you were commanded, say, "We are unprofitable servants, we have done what we ought to have done."' The judgment of our labours will be known at the time of departure. The common life is an earthly heaven; therefore, let us set our hearts as angels ministering to the Lord. Sometimes those in this heaven are like stones in their hearts; other times they are comforted by tears, fleeing from conceit and comforting their labours with tears. A small fire melts much wax, and a small dishonour that comes often suddenly softens, sweetens, and erases all the hardness and insensitivity of the heart. I once saw two sitting quietly, observing and listening to the sighs and labours of the strugglers; one was doing so to be inspired, the other to triumphantly scorn them when the time was right, to hinder the workman of God in his good work. Do not be a silent fool, causing disturbance and bitterness to others, nor a slothful one in spirit, hastening to be diligent. Otherwise, you are among the hands of those who are mad and disturbed. I have seen such things, as Job says, often due to slothful behaviour, sometimes even due to malicious intent, souls in pain; and I marvelled at the variety of evil. The one in the midst can gain more from prayer than from chanting; for confusion is the dissolution of psalmody. Wrestle with your mind continuously, gathering it back to yourself when it wanders. For God does not require the mind of the obedient to be unstrained in prayer; do not be disheartened by being robbed, but be disheartened by always recalling the mind. The one who has convinced himself not to withdraw from the contest until the last breath, enduring a thousand deaths of both body and soul, will not easily fall into such things; for hesitation of heart and distrust of places always cause progress and disasters. Those who are easy to move are altogether untested; for nothing so produces barrenness as impatience. If you find yourself in an unknown healer and hospital, be like a passerby, silently observing the experience of those present. But when you sense the benefit from the craftsmen and ministers in your sicknesses, especially when you find the sought-after thing in the swelling of your soul, then approach and sell yourself to the humility of submission and obedience, and mark the transaction with golden letters, with angels as witnesses, tearing up the bond of your own will. For by wandering, you overturn the purchase; Christ has now purchased you. Let the place be for you a grave before a grave. For no one comes out of the grave until the common resurrection; and if some have come out, see that they have died, which we pray the Lord to spare us from experiencing.

When those who are more sluggish perceive that the commands are heavy, they then try to prefer prayer instead. But when the commands seem lighter, they flee from them as if from fire. There is one who undertakes a ministry and abandons it to provide rest for another brother when asked, and there is another who abandons it out of laziness. Still, there is one who abandons it out of vanity, and there is one who abandons it out of eagerness. If you are caught up in the conditions and see that the eye of the soul is unprofitable, do not abandon the order. Nevertheless, the one who is tested is proven everywhere, just as the reverse is true. Insults according to the world have caused many separations, but gluttony in gatherings causes all kinds of falls and breaches. If you begin to rule over the mistress, every situation will bring you apathy; but if she rules over you, you will be in danger everywhere, even outside of the grave. The Lord makes the eyes of the subjects blind to the virtues of the teacher but darkens them to their shortcomings. The evil one, however, does the opposite. Let our model of excellent obedience be like what is called liquid silver: rolling beneath everything and remaining uncontaminated by anything impure. Those who are diligent should pay more attention to themselves so that by judging the lazy, they may not be judged more severely than them. For this reason, I believe Lot was justified because, being in the midst of such people, he never appeared to judge them at all. Let us always, and especially in hymns, keep quiet and undisturbed. For the demons aim to destroy prayer through disturbances. The servant stands before men with his body, but his mind knocks on heaven through prayer. The insults and contempt and the like in the soul of the subject are likened to the bitterness of wormwood; the praises, honours, and acclamations, like honey, produce every sweetness in those who are fond of pleasure. Let us therefore examine how the nature of each is: one purges everything within that is material, and the other tends to increase bile. We must trust without worry in those who have taken up the care of us in the Lord, even if they command something contrary to our opinion and our salvation. For then our faith in them is tested in the crucible of humility: for this is the truest mark of faith—to see things contrary to our hopes and to obey those who command us without hesitation. From obedience comes humility, as we have already said; from humility comes discernment, as the great Cassian philosophized beautifully and loftily in his discourse on discernment. From discernment comes spiritual insight, and from this, foresight. And who would not run in the good race of obedience, seeing such goods prepared before him? About this great thing, the good Psalmist said: 'You prepared in your goodness for the poor servant, O God, your presence in his heart.' Do not forget in your whole life that great athlete who in all eighteen years did not hear with the ears of the outer man from the overseer but heard daily from the Lord, not 'You shall be saved' (for this is a wish and uncertain), but 'You are saved,' which is definitive and certain.

Some of the subjects have deceived themselves by perceiving the compliant and accommodating nature of the leader and seeking permissions for their own desires. But let them know that they have completely fallen from the crown of confession, for obedience is the disguise of hypocrisy and self-seeking. There is one who receives a command, and sensing that it is not gladly received by the one who gave it, abandons it, and there is one who, sensing this and obeying without doubt, follows through. It should be asked which of them acted more devoutly. It is impossible to resist the devil with one's own will; let those living in neglect persuade you, whether in a solitary cell or in a communal life. Let our withdrawal from places that are fought over be a demonstration of our pleasing service to God. If indeed being attacked is a sign of being on the offensive, I will not be a secretive, unjust, and greedy person looking out for you in matters I should not keep silent about. The very pious John of Sabas recounted to me worthy things. That the man was dispassionate and free from all lying and evil words and deeds, I learned from his own experience.

In my monastery in Asia (for that is where the righteous man started), there was a very negligent and unruly elder. I say this not to judge, but to reveal the truth. This elder somehow took a younger disciple named Akakios, a man of simple character and wise in thought. This disciple endured so much from this elder that it might seem unbelievable to many. Not only was he abused and dishonoured, but he was also daily beaten. His patience was not without reason. Seeing him every day being tormented as if he were a purchased slave, I often said to him in passing: 'What is it, Brother Akakios? How are you today?' And he would immediately show me either a bruised eye, or a swollen neck, or a head wound. Knowing that he was a worker, I would say to him: 'Well done, well done, endure, and you will benefit.' After serving under this merciless elder for nine years, he departed to the Lord. After he was buried in the cemetery of the Fathers, five days later, his overseer came to a certain great elder there and said to him: 'Father, Brother Akakios has died.' When the elder heard this, he said to the one who spoke: 'Believe me, elder, I do not believe it.' But the overseer insisted: 'Come and see.' The elder quickly got up, and together with the overseer of the blessed fighter, they went to the cemetery. And the elder called out as if to a living person to the one who was truly alive in his repose, and said: 'Brother Akakios, have you died?' And the obedient servant, showing obedience even after death, answered the great elder: 'How, Father, is it possible for a man who was a worker of obedience to die?' Then the elder, who was formerly his overseer, became fearful, and fell on his face with tears, and after asking the abbot of the lavra for a cell near the grave, he lived out the rest of his days in sobriety, always telling the Fathers that he had committed murder. To me, Father John, it seems that this great John was the one who spoke to the dead man. And he also recounted another thing to me as if about someone else.

He said, 'Another man in this monastery in Asia became a disciple of a certain gentle, lenient, and quiet monk. Seeing himself being honoured and protected by the elder, he wisely considered that which has caused the downfall of many and earnestly begged the elder to release him. For he also had another disciple, and it was not too distressing to let him go. So he left the elder and placed himself under the oversight of a certain cenobitic monastery in Pontus through a letter. On the first night that he entered the monastery, he saw in his sleep that he was being audited by some, and after the terrifying audit was finished, he was found to owe one hundred pounds of gold. Waking up, he interpreted the vision and said: "Humble Antiochos (for that was his name), we truly owe much of our debt." And so I remained, he said, in the monastery for three years in unreserved obedience, being humiliated and oppressed by all as a stranger (for there was no other stranger monk there). And then again, I saw in my sleep someone giving me a receipt for ten pounds of my debt. Waking up, I understood the vision and said: "Ten more, and when will I ever repay it?" Then I said to myself: "Humble Antiochos, you need more toil and dishonour." From then on, I began to endure all the work given to me without complaint, yet still not being idle in my service altogether. Seeing me in the same order and eagerness, the merciless Fathers placed all the heavy tasks of the monastery upon me. After enduring such a way of life for thirteen years, I saw those who had first appeared to me come again and write off my entire debt. Therefore, whenever I was oppressed, I bore it bravely, recalling the debt of my monastery.' This is what the all-wise John recounted to me, Father John, as if from the face of another. Hence, he renamed himself Antiochos. He truly was one who bravely tore up the handwritten debt through his endurance. And how he became discerning through his extreme obedience, let us hear.

While he was sitting in the monastery of St. Sabas, three young monks came to him wishing to be his disciples. He received them and immediately offered them hospitality, desiring to refresh them from the labours of their journey. After the third day, the elder said to them: 'Brothers, by nature, I am a man who is prone to fornication and cannot accept any of you.' But they were not scandalized, for they knew the elder's work. After much pleading with him, they were unable to persuade him at all. Then they threw themselves at his feet, beseeching him to at least be regulated by him, as to how and where they should settle. The elder, yielding and knowing that they received his counsel with humility and obedience, said to one of them: 'The Lord desires, my child, that you should dwell in a quiet place with a father in obedience.' To the second, he said: 'Go, sell your will, and give it to God; take up your cross, and endure in the company of brothers in a cenobitic monastery, and you will surely have a treasure in heaven.' Then he said to the third: 'Take up with your breath the saying: "He who endures to the end will be saved." And go, if possible, do not leave the most demanding and harsh trainer in the Lord; endure daily, drinking mockery and scorn as if they were milk and honey.' The brother said to the great John: 'But if such a one provides negligence, Father, what then?' The elder replied: 'Even if you see him fornicating, do not depart but say to yourself: "Friend, for what have you come?" Then you will see your vanity vanish and your ardour wither away. We all struggle with all our might, those who wish to fear the Lord, lest in the training of virtue, we acquire for ourselves more wickedness and malice, cunning, and deceit. It happens, and it is no wonder. For as long as a man is a private citizen, or a sailor, or a farmer, the king's enemies do not arm themselves so much against him. But when they see him having received the seal, and the shield, and the sword, and the spear, and the bow, and the garment of the soldier, then they gnash their teeth against him, and they all try to destroy him. Therefore, let us not sleep. I have seen untimely and very beautiful infants arriving in the school for wisdom, and play, and benefit, and yet learning nothing there but wickedness and malice from the company of others. He who has understanding will understand. It is impossible for those who learn an art with all their heart not to progress in it every day. But some recognize the progress, while others, by divine economy, remain unaware. The best banker counts his profit or loss for the day every evening without fail. But he cannot know it clearly unless he records it hourly on his tablet. For the hourly audits illuminate the daily account. When the fool is insulted or condemned, he bites back and tries to argue, or briefly gives repentance to the one reproaching him, not for the sake of humility but to stop the insults. When he is struck, he remains silent and accepts the burning pain of the soul, rather, the light of chastity. When the physician stops, then he repents. For in anger, perhaps he does not even accept repentance.

Let us struggle against all passions, especially these two in the company of others: against gluttony and sharp temper, for our own passions thrive in the crowd. The devil implants in those under obedience a desire for unattainable virtues, just as he suggests inappropriate things to those in solitude. Examine the mind of an unapproved novice, and you will find there a wandering thought, a desire for solitude, extreme fasting, unbroken prayer, absolute freedom from vainglory, an unerring memory of death, continuous compunction, complete lack of anger, deep silence, and exceeding chastity. These desires, being unreasonable in the beginning, have led many to leap foolishly and be deceived. For the enemy made them seek these things before the proper time so that they would not endure and achieve them in due time. The deceiver blesses the novices with hospitality, ministry, brotherly love, and companionship, and service to the sick, so that by these means he may make them as impatient as the elders. For it is indeed rare to genuinely pursue solitude according to its true purpose. Only those few who have acquired divine consolation to comfort their labours and the help of God's grace in their battles succeed. Let us distinguish and choose appropriately according to the quality of our passions and our obedience. Let the ascetic who is prone to lust and excess have a strict trainer, not a miracle worker, eager to welcome and feed everyone. Let the one who is proud have a sharp and unyielding trainer, not a gentle and compassionate one. Let us not seek seers or prophets. But above all, let us always choose one who is humble-minded and suited to our sickness both in character and in dwelling. Let this be according to Abba Kyros, the righteous one mentioned earlier, who said that the best way to obedience is always to think that your superior is testing you, and you will never fail. When, while being reproached constantly, you increase your faith and love for the one reproaching you, know that the Holy Spirit has invisibly dwelt in your soul, and the power of the Most High has overshadowed you. But do not boast, nor rejoice, in bravely enduring insults and dishonours. Instead, mourn that you have done something worthy of insult and have disturbed a soul against you. Do not be amazed at what I am about to say, for I have Moses as my advocate: it is better to sin against God than against our Father. For if God is angered, our leader can reconcile Him to us, but if we have provoked this one, we no longer have anyone to propitiate for us. To me, it seems that both run back to one glory. Let us observe, discern, and be vigilant. Sometimes, when we are accused before the shepherd, we must endure it joyfully and quietly. At other times, we should make a clear statement before him. To me, it seems that in all things that bring dishonour upon us, we should be silent, for it is a time of gain. But when it affects the face of another, we should make a defense for the sake of the bond of love and unbreakable peace. Those who have leapt from obedience will tell you of its benefit. For then they realized in what heaven they stood. Whoever runs toward dispassion and God counts it a great loss every day when he is not insulted, just as trees shaken by the winds send down deep roots. Likewise, those who dwell in obedience acquire strong and unshakeable souls. Whoever, sitting in solitude, recognizes his own weakness, and after entering into obedience, has acted upon it, though he was blind, he looked up to Christ without effort.

Stand firm, stand firm, I say again, stand firm, running, brothers and athletes, hearing about yourselves the wise one calling out: "The Lord tested them as gold in the furnace, even more so in a monastery, and accepted them as a whole burnt offering in His bosom." Oh, the glory and eternal power with the beginningless Father and the Holy and worshipful Spirit. Amen.

Step 5 Concerning Repentance, Thoughtfully Considered and Clearly Manifested, in Which is Included the Life of the Holy Penitents, and Concerning the Prison

Repentance is the recall of baptism. Repentance is a covenant with God for a second life. Repentance is the purchaser of humility. Repentance is the perpetual despair of bodily comfort. Repentance is a self-imposed judgment, a carefree self-concern. Repentance is the daughter of hope and the denial of despair. The penitent is a convict without shame. Repentance is the reconciliation with the Lord, through the labour of good deeds contrary to the faults committed. Repentance is the cleansing of the conscience. Repentance is the voluntary endurance of all tribulations. The penitent is the creator of their own punishments. Repentance is a strong affliction of the stomach and a mighty blow to the soul through keen awareness. Come, gather, and listen, and I will tell you. All who have angered God, gather together and see what things He has shown to my soul for edification. Let us prioritize and esteem the story of these despised yet honoured labourers. Let us hear, keep, and do whatever is necessary, especially those of us who have suffered a disgraceful fall. Arise and stand, you who are lying down in the ruins of your fall. Take heed, my brothers, to this word of mine. Incline your ear, those of you who wish to reconcile yourselves with God through true repentance.

I, the weak one, heard of the great and extraordinary condition and humility of those in that particular monastery called the prison, under the aforementioned light-bearer among the stars, and while I was still there, I begged the righteous one to allow me to go there. And indeed, the great one consented, never wishing to grieve a soul. When I arrived at the monastery of the penitents, truly a place of mourning, I saw with my own eyes things which, if I may dare say, no careless human eye has seen, and no sluggish ear has heard, and no lazy heart has perceived. Things that can compel God to change His mind, practices and forms that can quickly bend His mercy.

I saw those responsible standing under the open sky from night until morning, their feet immovable, pitifully succumbing to sleep due to the violent nature of their situation, not granting themselves any rest. But rather, they were reproaching themselves and rousing themselves from sleep with dishonour and insults. Others were gazing into the heavens, invoking help from there with lamentations and cries. Still others stood in prayer, binding their hands behind their backs like condemned criminals, bowing their darkened faces to the ground, condemning themselves as unworthy even to look up to heaven, unable to speak, pray, or make any plea to God due to the shame imposed by their thoughts and conscience, finding no way or means to offer a supplication, presenting their soul as mute and their mind as speechless before God, filled with darkness and utter despair.

Others sat on the ground in sackcloth and ashes, covering their faces with their knees and striking their foreheads against the ground. Others were constantly striking their chests and calling upon their soul and life. Some were soaking the ground with their tears. Others, lacking tears, were tearing at themselves. Some were wailing over their souls, unable to bear the anguish of their hearts. Others were groaning in their hearts and stifling the sound of their lamentations with their mouths. Sometimes, unable to restrain themselves, they suddenly cried out. I saw some who seemed as if they were out of their minds, made insensible by their overwhelming distress, and entirely disconnected from everything in life, their minds sinking into the abyss of humility, their tears dried up by the fire of despondency. Others sat in deep thought, their heads bowed to the ground, constantly shaking their heads, growling and moaning like lions in their hearts and with their claws.

Some were earnestly seeking and praying for complete forgiveness. Others condemned themselves as unworthy of forgiveness out of unspeakable humility, crying out that they could not offer any defense before God. Some implored the Lord to be punished here so that they might find mercy there. Others, crushed under the weight of their conscience, sincerely said they were neither worthy of punishment nor the kingdom. "It is enough for us," they said. I saw there souls that were humble and crushed, bent under the weight of their burden, so moving in their cries and words to God that they could even pierce the sense of stones. They bowed to the ground and said, "We know, we know that we are now worthy of all punishment and torment, and rightfully so. For we are no longer capable of offering a defense for the multitude of our debts, even if we were to call the whole world to mourn on our behalf. But this is the only thing we ask, this we implore, this we beg: 'Do not rebuke us in Your anger, nor chasten us in Your wrath.' But have mercy on us and deliver us from the threat of Your great wrath and the unnamed and hidden torments. For we dare not ask for complete forgiveness."

How could those who did not keep their pledge of innocence, but defiled it after the previous kindness and forgiveness, ask for complete remission? It was there that the words of David could be seen clearly, witnessing the wretched, bowed down to the very end of their lives, walking all day in sorrow, their bodies bruised and uncared for, forgetting even to eat their bread, mixing their drink with tears, eating ashes for bread, with their bones clinging to their flesh, and withering away like grass. There was nothing else to be heard from them but these words: "Woe, woe, alas, alas, justly, justly, have mercy, have mercy, Master." Some said, "Have mercy, have mercy." Others even more pitifully, "Forgive, Master, forgive if it is possible."

It was terrifying to see their tongues flickering like flames, and their mouths protruding like those of dogs. Some tormented themselves in the heat, others in the cold. Some, after tasting a little water, stopped just enough not to die of thirst. Others, after partaking of a little bread, threw it far from their hands, declaring themselves unworthy of rational food as they had acted like irrational creatures. Where among them was there any sign of laughter? Where was idle talk? Where was anger? Where was wrath? They no longer even knew if wrath still existed among humans. Their grief had completely extinguished their anger. Where was disputing? Where was celebration? Where was boldness? Where was the care of the body? Where was any trace of vainglory? Where was the hope of indulgence? Where was the thought of wine? Where was the taste of fruit? Where was the comfort of a stew? Where was the sweetness of the throat? For all these things, their hope in this present age had been extinguished. Where among them was there any concern for anything earthly? Where was the judgment of another person? Nowhere. These were the things constantly said and cried out to the Lord. Some struck their chests mightily, as if standing at the gate of heaven, and said to God: "Open to us, Judge, open to us, for we have closed ourselves off with our sins, open to us." Others said, "Let your face shine on us, and we shall be saved." Another: "Let your face shine on those who sit in darkness and the shadow of death, the humble ones." Another: "Let your mercies quickly overtake us, Lord, for we are lost, we have given up, we are fading away."

Some said, "If only the Lord would now appear to us." Others, "Has our soul passed through that insurmountable debt?" Another, "Will the Lord ever be reconciled with us again? Will we ever hear Him say to us, the unbreakable prisoners, 'Come out,' and to those in the hades of repentance, 'You are forgiven'? Has our cry reached the ears of the Lord?" All sat constantly seeing death with their eyes, saying, "What will be the outcome? What will be the verdict? What will be the end? Is there any recall? Is there any forgiveness for the dark, the lowly, the condemned? Has our prayer been strong enough to enter before the Lord, or has it been rightfully turned away, humiliated and dishonoured? And if it has entered, how much has it appeased? How much has it accomplished? How much has it benefited? How much has it acted? For it was sent up from unclean mouths and bodies, and it has little strength. Has it completely reconciled the Judge, or only partially? Or by half the bruises? For these are indeed great, requiring many sweat and toil. Have our guardians approached us, or are they still far from us? For if they do not approach us, all our labour is in vain and meaningless. For our prayer has no power of boldness, nor a wing of purity to enter before the Lord, unless our angels come close to us, take it, and present it to the Lord."

Often, they would wonder aloud to one another, saying, "Are we succeeding, brothers? Are we obtaining our request? Is He accepting us again? Is He opening to us?" To this, others would respond, "Who knows, as our brothers the Ninevites said. Perhaps the Lord will turn and deliver us from great punishment. Yet let us do what is in our power, and if He opens, it will be well and good. For blessed is the Lord our God, who has justly shut us out. But let us persist in knocking until the end of our lives. Perhaps by our great importunity, He will open to us." Therefore, encouraging one another, they said, "Let us run, brothers, let us run. For we are in need of a race, a race, since we have fallen behind our good company. Let us run, sparing not this filthy and wicked flesh of ours. But let us kill it, as it has killed us, just as those blessed penitents did." Among them could be seen knees hardened by many repentances. Eyes sunken and deep within, deprived of hair, cheeks bruised, and inflamed by the heat of many tears. Faces shrivelled and pale, differing in no way from the dead. Chests sore from blows, with blood spat from the beatings of their breasts. Where was the establishment of a bed among them? Where was the cleanliness or firmness of clothing? But they were torn and filthy, covered by lice.

What was the suffering of those enduring trials in comparison to them? What was the mourning of those grieving for the dead? What was the plight of those living in exile? What was the torment of those condemned for murder? Indeed, their suffering, and the involuntary punishment, was nothing compared to the voluntary suffering of these. And do not consider what I have said to be mere tales, I beg you, brothers. Many times they would implore the great judge, I mean the shepherd, the angel among men, and beg to have iron chains and collars placed on their hands and necks, and their feet secured in the stocks of those suffering, and not to be released from there until the grave took them, and not even the grave. I will not hide even this truly blessed humility of theirs, having been shown mercy by God, and their love for God and repentance. When they were about to depart for the Lord and stand before the impartial judgment seat, these good citizens of the land of repentance, whenever one of them saw himself near the end, he would implore the great one through their leader, with oaths, not to be deemed worthy of a human burial, but to be left like an irrational beast, either in the stream of a river or in a field, to be handed over to the beasts. Often the light of discretion complied, ordering them to be sent off without any psalmody or honour.

What a dreadful and pitiful sight it was at their last hour. For when their fellow condemned perceived that one of them was about to depart, while his mind was still strong, they would surround him, thirsting, and mourning with the most sorrowful demeanour and grim words, shaking their own heads, and they would ask the one departing, burning with compassion for him, "What is it, brother and fellow convict? What do you say? What do you hope for? What do you think? Have you obtained the desired outcome from your toil, or have you not succeeded? Have you opened the door, or are you still accountable? Have you reached your goal, or have you not achieved it? Have you received any assurance, or is your hope still uncertain? Have you found freedom, or is your mind still wavering and doubtful? Have you felt any illumination in your heart, or is it still darkened and dishonoured? Have you heard a voice within you saying, 'Behold, you are healed,' or, 'Your sins are forgiven,' or, 'Your faith has saved you'? Or do you still hear that voice saying, 'Let the sinners be turned back into hades,' and, 'Bind his hands and feet,' and, 'Let the wicked be taken away, so that he does not see the glory of the Lord'? What do you say simply, brother? Tell us, we beg you, so that we may also know in what condition we shall be. For your time is now closing, and you will find no other in this age."

To this, those of the sleepers replied, "Blessed be the Lord, who has not rejected my prayer or His mercy from me." Others, "Blessed be the Lord, who has not given us as prey to their teeth." Others said with great sorrow, "Will our soul pass through the water of the spirits of the air, that insurmountable obstacle?" Still not daring, but waiting to see what happens in that terrible examination. Others answered with even more sorrow and said, "Woe to the soul that did not keep its blameless promise; in that hour, it will know what is prepared for it."

Seeing and hearing these things among them, I was almost ready to despair of myself, seeing my own indifference and comparing it with their suffering. What, indeed, was the condition and dwelling of that place? All dark, all foul-smelling, all filthy and barren. For it was rightfully called a prison and a place of condemnation. The very sight of that place was a teacher of all repentance and mourning. But what is difficult and unacceptable for some, is easy and acceptable for those who have fallen from virtue and spiritual wealth. For a soul deprived of previous boldness and fallen from the hope of passionlessness, having broken the seal of purity and been stripped of the wealth of gifts, alienated from divine consolation and having violated the commandments of the Lord, quenched by the good fire of spiritual tears, and struck by its memory and painfully pricked, will not only willingly accept the aforementioned labours, but will also seek to destroy itself through pious asceticism, if indeed there remains in it any remnant of a spark of love or fear of the Lord.

These blessed ones indeed existed. Keeping this in mind and remembering the height from which they had fallen, they said, "We remembered the days of old, the fire of that zeal of ours." Others cried out to the Lord, "Where are Your ancient mercies, O Lord, which You showed to our soul in Your truth? Remember the reproach and the labours of Your servants." Another, "Who would put me back to the months of the days of old, when God watched over me, when the light of His lamp shone over the head of my heart?" And they recalled their former accomplishments, mourning over them like dead children, and said, "Where is the purity of prayer? Where is its boldness? Where is the sweet tear instead of the bitter one? Where is the hope of complete purity and cleansing? Where is the expectation of blessed passionlessness? Where is faith in the shepherd? Where is the beneficence of his prayer in us? All these have perished. And as if they had never appeared, they have vanished. And as if they had never existed, they have disappeared and gone." Saying and lamenting these things, some wished to be possessed by a demon. Others implored the Lord to fall into a holy disease. Some begged to lose their sight and become a wretched spectacle. Others asked to perish, just not to be tempted by the wickedness there.

As for me, how wonderful it was that I lost myself in that mourning, and my whole mind was completely seized, unable to control myself. But I must return to the subject. So after staying in the guardhouse for thirty days, I, the impatient one, returned to the great monastery to the great one. And when he saw me, he immediately recognized the change in me, as if I had been completely altered and out of my mind. And he said, "What is it, Father John? Have you seen the struggles of the suffering ones?" I said, "I have seen them, Father, and I have marvelled, and I have envied those who have fallen and are mourning, more than those who have not fallen and do not mourn. For by falling, they have risen to a secure resurrection." He replied, "It is so," and his truthful tongue recounted to me. "About ten years ago," he said, "I had here a brother, very zealous and diligent, such that when I saw him burning with zeal in the spirit, I trembled for him, fearing the devil's envy, lest in his great zeal he should stumble over a stone, as is the case with those who walk quickly. Which indeed happened. He then came to me late one evening, showing me his wound, uncovered. He sought a plaster, asked for a cautery, was terribly agitated. Then, since he was deserving of compassion, seeing that I did not wish to use a very severe cut as a doctor, he threw himself to the ground, grasped my feet, and sufficiently bathed them with tears. He asked to be sentenced to the guardhouse you saw, crying out that it was impossible not to go there. Then he compelled me to transfer the compassion of a doctor into amputation, which is rare among the sick and most astonishing. He quickly joined the penitents, became a sharer and fellow sufferer. Having been struck in the heart as if by a sword of sorrow for the love of God, on the eighth day he departed to the Lord, having asked not to be granted a burial. But I buried him here and laid him to rest with the fathers, as was fitting. Therefore, after seven servile days, on the eighth day, he was released as a free man. Someone clearly understood that he could not rise from my vile and filthy feet until he had appeased God. And it is no wonder. For having taken up the faith of that harlot in his heart, he, too, bathed my humble feet with such assurance. 'All things are possible to him who believes,' the Lord said.

I have seen unclean souls madly raging with the love of bodies. And indeed, having received a thought of repentance, from the experience of love, they transferred the same love to the Lord. And having leaped over every fear, they were deeply rooted in the love of God. Therefore, the Lord said to that chaste harlot, not that she feared, but 'that she loved much,' and she was able to easily exchange one love for another. I do not ignore, wonderful one, that what I have narrated seems unbelievable to some, hopeless to others, and utterly despaired of by others. But a courageous man takes from these things a spur and a fiery dart, and zeal in his heart, and departs carrying it. The one beneath him recognizes his own weakness, and having easily acquired humility through self-reproach, he ran at once to the goal, but I do not know if he reached it. But a careless man does not touch upon what has been said, lest he, too, should abandon what he is working on, out of despair, and scatter it. And that saying may also apply to him: 'From the one who does not have eagerness, even what he seems to have will be taken away from him.' It is not possible for us who have fallen into the pit of lawlessness, and who have not descended into the deep abyss of the humility of the penitents, to be drawn out from there. The gloomy humility of the penitents is one thing, and the condemnation of conscience of those still sinning is another. And the blessed wealth of humility that comes to the perfect through the energy of God is another. Let us not try to find the third by words, for we will run in vain. But the sign of the second is the complete endurance of dishonour by patience, which often tyrannizes the mourner. And it is no wonder, for the word concerning judgments and falls is obscure and altogether incomprehensible to the soul. What are those falls that happen through negligence, and what through an economic abandonment? What are those that occur through God's withdrawal? But someone told me that those that happen to us through economy have a quick reversal from them. For the one who delivers us does not allow us to be much held by the demon of sorrow. Let us fight above all against him who, when present at the time of our prayer, and reminding us of our former boldness, wants to nullify our prayer.

Do not be astonished if you fall every day, and do not run away, but stand bravely, and the angel who guards you will honour your patience. For a wound that is fresh and hot is naturally easy to heal. But old and neglected and barren wounds are difficult to heal, requiring much labour, and iron, and dryness, and fire for their treatment. Many things are incurable with time, but with God, all things are possible. Before the fall, they say, God is merciful, but after the fall, He is severe, according to the demons. Do not believe the one who says that after the fall, the small faults do not matter. For often small gifts have appeased the great anger of the judge. The one who gives true repentance, mourns every day, considering that he has lost it, even though he has done good in it. Let none of those who mourn expect assurance in their departure. For the uncertain is not certain. Let me have assurance, so that I may be refreshed before I depart hence unassured. Where the Spirit of the Lord is, the bond is loosed. Where there is unspeakable humility, the bond is loosed. For those without these two are deceived, for they are still bound. Those in the world and alone without these are strangers to assurances, and especially the former, but some run at the departure in hope of gaining their profit. The one who mourns will not recognize his own mourning, nor the fall of another, nor any reproach. A dog caught by a wild beast became more furious against it, raging unyieldingly in his pain from the wound. Let us take heed, lest we cease to reproach our conscience out of malice. The sign of the loosening in the fall is to always consider oneself a debtor. There is nothing equal to or greater than the mercies of God. Therefore, the one who despairs of himself has killed himself. The sign of thoughtful repentance is to consider oneself worthy of all the visible and invisible afflictions that occur, and even more than these.

Moses, after seeing God in the burning bush, returned to Egypt, that is, to the darkness, to the making of bricks, perhaps to the metaphorical Pharaoh. But he ascends again into the bush, and not only that, but also onto the mountain. The one who knows the vision does not ever despair of himself. The great Job became poor, but again he was enriched doubly. Among the lazy, the falls after the calling are grievous, striking the hope of passionlessness and persuading them to consider the resurrection from the pit as blessed. Behold, for not by the way we were led astray do we return, but by another, shorter path. I saw two people walking towards the Lord in the same manner and at the same time. And one of them was old and advanced in labours. The other was a disciple, and he ran faster than the elder and arrived first at the tomb of humility. Let us all take heed, especially those who have fallen, not to entertain in our hearts the disease of the atheist Origen. For the vile hope of God's mercy, when presented, becomes acceptable to the pleasure-loving. In my study, or rather in my repentance, a fire of prayer burning the wood will be kindled. Let the aforementioned holy penitents be your mountain, your type, your model, and your image for repentance, and you will not need a book at all in your whole life until Christ, the Son of God, and God, shines upon you in the resurrection of thoughtful repentance. Amen.

Step 6 On the Memory of Death

Every thought precedes words. The memory of death and of sins precedes weeping and mourning. Therefore, it is placed in its proper order within the discourse. The memory of death is a daily death, and the memory of departure is a sigh purified. The fear of death is a natural characteristic that has come upon us due to disobedience, while the terror of death is a sign of unrepentant sins. Christ fears death, but does not tremble, in order to clearly manifest the properties of both natures. Just as bread is more necessary than all other foods, so is the thought of death more necessary than all other labours. The memory of death generates labour and idleness among those in the midst, but even more, it fosters the sweetness of dishonour. Among those who are outside of turmoil, it brings the laying aside of cares, constant prayer, and the guarding of the mind. Its mothers and daughters are the same. Just as tin is obvious when compared to silver, even if it appears similar in appearance, so is natural and unnatural fear of death evident and distinct to those who are discerning. This is a true sign for those who remember death with heartfelt awareness: voluntary detachment from all creation and complete renunciation of one's own will. Worthy is the one who expects it daily. Holy is the one who longs for it hourly. Not every desire for death is good. There are those who, continuously sinning out of habit, pray for it with humility. And there are those who, unwilling to repent, call upon death out of despair. And there are those who, considering themselves passionless through arrogance, are not afraid of it. And there are, if there still are, those who, through the work of the Holy Spirit, seek their own departure. Some wonder and are perplexed as to why God, though the memory of death benefits us so much, has hidden from us the foreknowledge of it, not realizing that God has wonderfully worked out our salvation through it. For no one who had foreseen their own death long before would have hastened to baptism or to the monastic life. Rather, they would have spent all their days in lawlessness and would only come to baptism and repentance at the very moment of their departure. Never accept with mourning that dog who persuades you that God is compassionate. For its purpose is to drive out grief and fearless fear from you, unless you see yourself being dragged down into deep despair. The one who wishes to continually hold within themselves the memory of death and God's judgment but gives themselves over to material cares and distractions is like someone swimming who wants to hold onto their own hands. The vivid memory of death has cut down food, and with the humble reduction of food, passions have also been cut off. The insensitivity of the heart has hardened the mind, and an abundance of food has dried up the springs. Thirst and vigilance have pressed the heart. When the heart is pressed, waters have sprung forth. What is said is hard for the gluttonous, and unbelievable for the lazy. But the practical man will test it willingly. The one who has found this through experience will smile at these things. But the one who seeks will be more sorrowful. Just as the Fathers define perfect love as being without fear, so I declare that the perfect sense of death is without fear. The labours of the practical mind are many. I speak of the thought of love, towards God; of the memory of death, the memory of God, the memory of the kingdom, the zeal of the holy martyrs, the memory of God Himself present, according to the one who said, "I foresaw the Lord always." The memory of the holy and intelligent powers, the memory of departure, encounter, punishment, and decision. We have begun with great things but have ended with things beyond our reach. An Egyptian monk once told me how, after establishing the memory of death with heartfelt awareness, when I wanted to comfort myself a little with clay when a need arose, I was hindered by the memory, as if by a judge. And the wonder was that even if I had wanted to, I could not push it away. Another man, living here in the place called Tholos, often fell into a trance from such thoughts, and as if fainting or even having an epileptic fit, was carried nearly breathless by the brothers present. I will not keep silent about the story of the hermit from Choribos. He always lived in complete neglect, caring nothing at all for the salvation of his soul. But after his body became seriously ill and he had departed for about an hour, he returned to himself and immediately begged us all to leave. Having rebuilt the door of his cell, he remained inside for two years, meeting with no one at all, speaking not even a small word, not a great word, but only tasting bread and water. Sitting there and reflecting on what he had seen in the trance, he was so changed that he never altered his demeanour towards the Lord, but always remained silent, and warm tears constantly streamed forth without sound. When he was about to die, we broke open the door and entered. After much pleading, we heard this alone from him: "Forgive me, for no one who has truly known the memory of death can ever sin." We marvelled, seeing the one who had been so negligent before, now so suddenly transformed into a blessed change and transformation. After we buried him piously in the cemetery near the castle, the next day we sought his holy relic, but we did not find it. The Lord assured us even in this that his repentance had been diligent and worthy of praise, leading us all to believe that there are some who, after much negligence, can correct themselves, just as an abyss is deemed unfathomable. They call such a place bottomless. Thus, the thought of death preserves both purity and labour incorrupt. This is confirmed by the previously mentioned holy man. Those who continually add fear upon fear do not cease until this very power of the bones is spent. Let us be assured that this too is a gift from God among all good things. For how is it that even when we come to the very tombs, some of us remain tearless and hard? Yet outside of such a contemplation, we are often moved to compunction. The one who has died to all is the one who remembers death. But the one who is still attached to things cannot be at rest, always being in conflict with themselves. Do not seek to convince everyone through words of your love for them. Rather, ask God to manifest it to them in an ineffable way. Otherwise, the time will not suffice for relationships and compunction. Do not be deceived, foolish labourer, by trying to make up for time with time. For a single day is not sufficient to fulfill the debt we owe to the Lord without fail. Among men, it is said that it is impossible to piously pass the day at hand unless we consider it the last of all days. And it is truly a wonder how even the Greeks spoke something similar, as they define philosophy as a meditation on death.

Step 7 On Mourning Which Causes Joy

Godly mourning is the somberness of the soul and the disposition of a heart in pain, always fervently seeking that which it thirsts for and, in failing to attain it, relentlessly pursuing it with effort, while sorrowfully lamenting its loss. Alternatively, sorrow is the golden spur of the soul, stripped of all attachment and connection, and pierced in the watchful heart by holy grief. Contrition is the continual torment of a conscience, struggling through spiritual confession to cool the fire in the heart. Confession is the forgetting of one's nature, just as if someone forgot to eat their bread. Repentance is the sorrowful deprivation of all bodily comfort. For those who are still progressing in blessed sorrow, their characteristic is restraint and the silence of the lips. For those who have advanced further, their characteristic is the absence of anger and the lack of resentment. For those who have reached perfection, their characteristic is humility, a thirst for dishonour, voluntary affliction, voluntary hunger, the refusal to judge those who sin, and sympathy beyond their strength. The first are acceptable, the second praiseworthy. Blessed are those who hunger for affliction and thirst for dishonour, for they shall be filled with an insatiable food.

Cling to mourning with all your strength, for it is easy to lose before it is even done. It melts away from disturbances, bodily cares, and indulgence, especially from excessive talk and frivolity, just as wax melts before fire. The fountain of tears after baptism is greater than baptism itself, although this is a bold statement. For baptism cleanses us of the evils that have come before, while tears cleanse those that have come afterward. Baptism is received by infants, yet we all have polluted it. Through tears, we cleanse even baptism. If this gift were not given by the merciful God to mankind, indeed very few and difficult would be those who are saved. Sighs and sorrowfulness cry out to the Lord. Tears born of fear intercede for us. But tears of perfect love reveal to us that our prayers have been accepted. Nothing stands so much against mourning as laughter. Hold fast to the blessed mourning of holy compunction and do not cease working in it until you present yourself pure and elevated from this world to Christ. Never stop reflecting and examining the abyss of dark fire, the merciless servants, the uncompassionate judge, the pitiless chasm of the subterranean flame, and the dreadful places and narrow descents of such images. So that the innate lust in our soul, gripped with great fear, may be joined to incorrupt purity and receive the appearance of light, more brilliant than any fire.

Stand in prayer trembling, like a convict before the judge, so that by outward demeanour and inner conduct, you may extinguish the wrath of the righteous Judge. For the soul cannot bear to be overlooked when it stands in profound sorrow before Him, and presents labours to Him who needs no labours. If someone has obtained spiritual tears, then every place is fitting for mourning. But if someone works only externally, they will never cease discriminating between places and methods. Just as a hidden treasure is safer than one displayed in the marketplace, so let us understand the previously mentioned. Do not be like those who, having buried their dead, sometimes weep over them and at other times indulge themselves because of them. Rather, be like prisoners in mines, scourged every hour by executioners. The one who sometimes mourning, sometimes indulges, and laughs, is like someone who throws stones at the dog of pleasure while eating bread, pretending to drive it away but in reality encouraging it to stay. Be mindful and unaffected, wholly focused on your own heart, for demons fear mindfulness as thieves fear dogs.

There is no call to marriage here, none at all. The one who called us here has indeed called us to mourn for ourselves. Some, while weeping, force themselves to think that they should not be weeping at this blessed time, not considering that thoughtless tears belong to an irrational nature, and that tears are the offspring of thoughts, and the father of thoughts is reflection and the mind. Let your lying down on your bed remind you of lying in your grave, and you will sleep less. Let the enjoyment of your meal remind you of the painful table of worms, and you will indulge less. Do not drink water without remembering the thirst of that flame, and you will surely overcome nature. When reproached and insulted by our overseer, let us consider the fearful judgment of the Judge, and we will cut off with meekness and patience the unreasonable grief and bitterness that sprout within us, as if with a double-edged sword.

With time, the sea is depleted, as Job says, and with time and patience, the things spoken of within us gradually come to pass and are perfected. The memory of eternal fire should lie down with you in the evening and rise with you, and it will never allow negligence to overpower you during the time of psalmody. Let your very clothing persuade you to the work of mourning. For all who mourn the dead wear black. If you do not mourn, mourn for this. If you mourn, mourn even more, because through your sins, you have brought yourself from a state of ease into one of hardship.

It is judged, indeed, by our good and righteous Judge that the power of nature is in all things, even in tears. For I have seen small drops appearing like blood, and I have judged the sufferers more by their toil than by their tears, and I believe God does the same. Theology is not fitting for those who mourn, for it tends to dissolve their sorrow. One seems like a teacher sitting on a throne, the other like someone sitting on a dung heap and sackcloth. And this is, I believe, why David, though he was a teacher and wise, answered those who questioned him while he mourned, "How shall I sing the Lord's song in a strange land?"—that is, in the land of passion.

In mourning and in contrition, there is both self-motivation and external motivation. When the soul, without your haste or intention, becomes tearful, soaking, and gentle, let us run, for the Lord has come uninvited, giving us a sponge of godly mourning, and the refreshing water of pious tears to wipe away the sins written on the parchment of our hearts. Guard this as the apple of your eye until it departs. Its strength exceeds that of what comes from our own effort and intention. It is not always that the one who mourns outpaces others in mourning, but rather the one who mourns as God wills. Often, the tear of vanity is interwoven with godly mourning, and we will recognize this clearly and piously when we see ourselves mourning and doing evil. Contrition is the steadfast mourning of the soul, providing no comfort to itself, but continually contemplating its own dissolution and accepting the comfort of the God who consoles humble monks as cool water. Those who have acquired heartfelt mourning have come to hate even their own life and have turned away from their body as an enemy. When we see anger and pride in those who seem to mourn according to God, let us consider their tears to be the opposite. For what communion has light with darkness? The offspring of corrupted contrition is arrogance, but the offspring of praised contrition is comfort. Just as fire consumes straw, so does pure mourning burn away all apparent and perceived impurity.

The teaching on tears is considered obscure and hard to find by many of the Fathers, especially in the introductory stages, and it is understood to arise from many and various sources: from nature, from God, from opposing afflictions, from praised contrition, from vanity, from lust, from love, from the memory of death, and from many other things. Let us exercise these various sources in the fear of God, and acquire for ourselves pure and untainted tears for our salvation. There is no deception or arrogance in them. Rather, they are cleansing and a loving clinging to God, a washing away of sin and freedom from passions. It is not a wonder to start with good tears and end in evil, but it is praiseworthy to graft spiritual things onto natural or opposing sources. This is clearly understood by those who are inclined to vanity. Do not trust your fountains before complete purification. Just as wine drawn straight from the press is not trustworthy. No one denies that all tears according to God are beneficial, but what gain comes from them will be known at the time of departure.

Whoever continues in godly mourning will never cease to celebrate daily. But whoever continually celebrates in the flesh will be succeeded by eternal sorrow. There is no joy for the condemned in prison, and there is no true feast for real monks on earth. Perhaps that is why the one who mourns well once said, "Bring my soul out of prison," that it may rejoice in your inexpressible light. Be like a king in your heart, high in humility, sitting and commanding laughter, "Go," and it goes; and sweet weeping, "Come," and it comes; and your servant and tyrant body, "Do this," and it does.

If anyone has put on blessed and graceful mourning as a bridal garment, they have known the spiritual laughter of the soul. Who, then, is the one who has spent all their time in the monastic life so piously that they have never lost a day, an hour, or a moment, but have spent it entirely for the Lord, considering that the same day can never be seen twice in life? Blessed is the monk who can gaze with the eyes of the soul upon the spiritual powers. Truly untouchable is the one who continuously wets their cheeks with living tears from the memory of death and sins. I struggle to believe that the former condition could have existed without the latter. I have seen beggars and shameless poor people who, with a few cunning words, quickly moved the hearts of kings to compassion. And I have seen poor and needy souls, not with cunning, but with humble, dark, and desperate words, crying out shamelessly and persistently from the depths of their hearts to the heavenly King, and by their violence, they forced His compassionate and natural mercy. The one who is spiritually puffed up by their own tears and condemns those who do not weep is like someone who asks the king for a weapon against their enemy but then turns it against themselves. God does not need or desire a person to mourn out of a heart in pain, but rather to rejoice in the soul with laughter out of love for Him. Remove sin, and the painful tear of the bodily eyes is unnecessary. There was no tear in Adam before the fall, and neither will there be after the resurrection when sin is abolished. If pain and mourning have fled away, as it is said, "Then the ransomed of the Lord shall return, and sorrow and sighing shall flee away."

I have seen in some people mourning, and in others, confusion about mourning. Some have mourning but act as if they do not possess it, and through holy chastity remain free from it. These are they of whom it is said, "The Lord gives wisdom to the blind." Often, the lighter the soul, the more tears it sheds. Therefore, it is not given to some, who torment and condemn themselves in seeking it, with sighs, dejection, sorrow of the soul, deep somberness, and despair, all of which naturally fill the place of tears without risk, even though they consider it to be of no benefit to them. If we observe closely, we will often find a bitter laugh by the demons against us. For they prick us when we are full, and harden us when we fast so that, deceived by false tears, we may hand ourselves over to the mother of the passions for her nourishment. We must not obey them, but rather do the opposite. I am astonished even when considering the nature of contrition, how sorrow and so-called grief contain within them joy and gladness, interwoven like honeycomb. What do we learn from this? That such contrition is truly a gift from the Lord. There is no joyless pleasure in the soul when God secretly comforts those who are broken hearted.

To understand the premise of true mourning and beneficial grief, let us listen to a spiritually beneficial and pitiful story. A certain Stephan, who lived here, embracing the eremitic and quiet life, and having spent sufficient time in the monastic wrestling school, adorned with fasts and tears, and possessing other good qualities, had his cell at the descent of the holy and God-seeing sun on this holy mountain. This blessed man had undertaken a stricter and more painful repentance by taking a place among the recluses, in the place called Siddein, where he lived for some time under the most strict and demanding lifestyle, as the type was unyielding and inaccessible to any human passage, being about seventy miles from the fortress. The elder was nearing his end, and he ascended to his own cell on the holy summit. He had two very devout Palestinian disciples there who guarded the elder's cell. After spending a few days, the elder fell ill with an illness from which he eventually died. One day before his departure, he was in an altered state of mind, and with his eyes open, he looked to the right and to the left of his bed, as if speaking with someone, and said, "Yes, indeed, truly, I fasted for so many years for this." Then he would say, "No, indeed, you lie, I did not do this." Again, "Yes, truly, this, yes, but I wept and served." Then again, "No, you totally accuse me falsely." At one point, he said to someone, "Yes, truly, yes, but I do not know what to say to this. Mercy is with God." And it was indeed a dreadful and fearful sight, an unseen and unforgiving judgment. What was most terrifying was that he was accused even of things he had not done. Alas! The hermit and recluse was saying to some of his own sins, "I do not know what to say to these," having been a monk for about forty years and having the gift of tears. Alas! Where was then the voice of Ezekiel to say to them, "In whatever condition I find you, in that I will judge you," said the Lord. Truly, he could say nothing of the sort. Why? Glory to the only One who knows. Some, as they told me by the Lord, said that he wrestled with a leopard from his hand in the desert. And thus, while being judged, he was separated from the body without revealing the verdict, the outcome, the sentence, or the end of the judgment. Just as a widow who has lost her husband and has an only son, finds comfort in that son alone after the Lord, so there is no other comfort for the fallen soul at the time of departure than the labours of the throat and the tear. Such souls will never sing or rejoice in hymns. If you attempt to approach them by means of these, you are still far from the soul that is on fire. mourning has often been the forerunner of blessed passionlessness, preparing and consuming the material. A certain tested worker of this good once told me that often when I was inclined toward vanity, or anger, or gluttony, the thought of mourning within me testified, saying, "Do not seek vanity, for I am departing from you," and likewise for the other passions. And I would say to him, "I will never disobey you until you present me to Christ." mourning beheld consolation as an abyss and received the illumination of purity of heart. Illumination is an unspeakable energy, perceived unknowingly and seen invisibly. Consolation is the refreshment of a sorrowful soul, like a child weeping within itself while smiling cheerfully. Support is the renewal of the soul, which, having fallen into sorrow, wondrously transforms the tear of mourning into a tear of consolation.

Tears of departure give birth to fear. Fear gives birth to fearlessness, and as the flower of holy love blooms, unending joy arises. Push away the joy that comes to you as unworthy with the hand of humility, so that, being acceptable, you do not receive a wolf instead of a shepherd. Do not hasten to contemplation at a time not suited for contemplation, lest your beautiful humility is overtaken, and you are seized, and eternally joined to it in a most pure union. In the early stages, when the infant knows its father, it is filled with complete joy. But when the father departs for a time according to his economy, and then returns again, the child becomes mixed with joy and sorrow—joy for seeing what it desired, and sorrow for being deprived of the good and beautiful presence for such a long time. The mother hides herself from the infant, and seeing it desperately seeking her, delights in this, training it to cling to her continually and igniting its love for her more strongly. "He who has ears to hear, let him hear," says the Lord.

The condemned, who have received their sentence, will not concern themselves with the management of entertainments. Nor will the one who is truly weeping ever pay attention to luxury, glory, anger, or irritability. mourning is the painful distress of a repentant soul, adding distress upon distress daily, like a woman in labour. The Lord is righteous and holy, and He pierces the one who is silent with His word and refreshes the one who submits to it daily. The one who does not pursue mourning unadulterated by other means is freed from it. Push away the dog that approaches you in the deepest sorrow, suggesting that God is merciless and unsympathetic. Observe him, and you will find that before sin, he proclaims God to be compassionate and forgiving. Idle chatter gives birth to constant talk, and that ends in awareness. What is done with awareness is hard to remove, even if we engage in great activities, and if we do not have a sorrowful heart, such works are false and empty. It is indeed necessary, absolutely necessary, so to speak, for those who have defiled themselves again after baptism to anoint their hands with the unyielding fire of the heart and the oil of God.

I have seen in some the highest level of mourning. For I saw them visibly shedding blood from their sorrowful and wounded heart out of their mouth. And I remembered the one who said, "My heart was smitten like grass, and it withered." Tears born of fear keep within themselves both trembling and vigilance. But the tears of love, before perfect love, may be harmless to some. I do not know if it is not rather that the everlasting fire at the time of its operation will scorch the heart. It is remarkable to say how humility is more secure at the time of its operation. There are substances that dry up our springs, and others that give birth to filth and wild beasts in them. Through the former, Lot unlawfully engaged with his daughters. Through the latter, the devil fell from heaven. Great indeed is the malice of our enemies, who produce the mothers of virtues as the mothers of vices and turn the materials of humility into the creators of pride.

It often happens that our own dwellings and vistas summon our mind to contrition, and perhaps Jesus, Elijah, and John are persuaded to pray by themselves. I have seen tears often stirred in cities and tumult, believing that the noise does not harm us so that we might draw closer to the world. For this is the aim of the wicked demons. A single word has often dissolved mourning, and it is a wonder if a single word has ever gathered it again. We will not be reproached at the departure of our soul because we have not performed miracles. Nor because we have not engaged in theological discourse. Nor because we have not become contemplatives. But we will surely give an account to God for not having mourned unceasingly.

Step 8 On Gentleness and the Absence of Anger

Just as the gradual addition of water completely extinguishes a flame, so too does the tear of true sorrow quench all the flames of anger and irritability. That is why we place it appropriately here. Absence of anger is an insatiable desire for dishonour, just as endless praise is for the vainglorious. Absence of anger is a defeat of nature, gained through struggles and the sweat of enduring insults without sensitivity. Gentleness is an immovable state of the soul, remaining the same in both dishonour and praise. The beginning of absence of anger is silence of the lips when the heart is disturbed. The middle stage is silence of thoughts when the soul is lightly troubled. The end is perfect peace even in the midst of the winds of unclean spirits.

Anger is the recollection of hidden hatred, or in other words, resentment. Anger is the desire to harm the one who has provoked us. Irritability is a movement in the soul that brings no pleasure. Bitterness is a settled movement in the soul that remains without joy. Wrath is a quick, changing movement of the temperament and a disfigurement of the soul. Just as darkness retreats when light appears, so too does all bitterness and wrath vanish at the scent of humility. Some are quick to anger and careless about its treatment and management, failing to consider the saying: "The moment of anger is the moment of downfall." The movement of a millstone in one moment can crush and destroy the grain of the soul and its fruit more than in a whole day. Therefore, we must be especially vigilant.

The sudden flare-up of anger, quickly kindled by a strong wind, burns and destroys the field of the heart more thoroughly than a fire that lasts for a long time. Let us not be ignorant, dear friends, that the evil demons often withdraw quickly so that, by neglecting small passions, we may end up suffering from incurable diseases. Just as a sharp and jagged stone, when struck against others, gradually loses its rough edges and becomes smooth, so too does a sharp and abrupt soul, mingling and associating with harsh individuals, undergo one of two things: either it heals its own wound through endurance, or, by retreating, it recognizes its own weakness through the cowardice of flight, as if seeing itself in a mirror.

A hot-tempered person is like a voluntary epileptic, whose fits are triggered by an involuntary seizure, tearing them apart. Nothing is more inappropriate for those who are repenting than a disturbed anger, as repentance requires much humility. Anger is evidence of all arrogance. If the summit of gentleness is to remain calm and loving toward the one who provokes us, even while they are present, then the peak of anger is to argue and rage against the one who offends us, even when alone, in words and gestures.

If the Holy Spirit is defined as the peace of the soul, and indeed exists as such, then anger is the disturbance of the heart, both in existence and in name. Nothing, therefore, undermines the presence of the Holy Spirit within us like anger.

Knowing the many and difficult offspring of anger, we have recognized only one involuntary descendant, which, although illegitimate, is still useful. I have seen those who were furiously enraged and who vomited out their long-held and deeply-rooted resentment, and by means of this passion, they were freed from passion. From long-lasting grief or repentance, or receiving assurance from the one who caused the sorrow, they were freed. And I have seen those who appeared to have patiently endured without reason, and who inwardly harboured resentment through silence, suffering more than those who were enraged, like those who destroy the whiteness of a dove by covering it with black.

We need much care in dealing with this serpent, for it has in its nature, as with the body, an ally in itself. I have seen angry people who, in their bitterness, refused food, yet through irrational self-control, they took poison instead of nourishment. And I have seen others who seized upon anger for an allegedly just cause and indulged in gluttony, falling from one abyss into a chasm. But I have seen sensible people who, like good doctors, combined both methods and greatly benefited from moderate consolation.

Sometimes melody dissolves anger best, while at other times it aligns with pleasure, becoming immoderate and untimely. Therefore, we must regulate the times for its use accordingly. I once heard, while sitting outside for some need, the sounds of men living in silence, like partridges in a coop, arguing within themselves out of bitterness and anger, yet jumping in joy on their faces as if their offender were present. I advised them devoutly not to overlook this, lest they become demons among men. And I have seen others, licentious and impure in heart, who pretended to be gentle, flattering, brotherly, and affable, yet who hated silence like a razor that cuts lust and impurity, lest they be pitifully transformed from a rational to an irrational nature.

When some told me of their pitiful failure in both respects, I absolutely forbade them from acting on their own will. However, I suggested to their overseers to allow them to practice one thing at one time and another at another time, but always submitting to the one who holds the reins. The pleasure-seeker harms himself, and perhaps another pleasure-seeker who imitates him. But the hot-tempered person is like a wolf that often disturbs the whole flock and crushes many souls by humiliating them.

It is hard to disturb the eye of the heart with anger, as the saying goes, "My eye is troubled with anger." It is even harder to reveal the impulse of the soul through the lips. And using hands for this purpose is totally contrary to the monastic, angelic, and divine way of life. If you want to heal the splinter in another's eye, or rather, if you think you are not pulling out a beam instead of a splinter, then you must act accordingly. The beam is harsh speech, inappropriate gestures; the splinter is gentle teaching and patient rebuke. "Rebuke, reprove, exhort," it says, "but do not strike." If this must be done, let it be rarely and not by yourself.

Let us observe diligently, even among the many angry, the vigil, fasting, and silence they practice. For the demon's goal is to offer the materials that increase the passion under the pretext of repentance and sorrow. If one wolf, as we have said, can disturb a flock with the help of a demon, then surely one wise brother, like a good skin of oil, can calm the storm and make the ship peaceful, with an angel as his helper. According to the measure of the former judgment, he will receive his reward from God and become an example of benefit to all.

The beginning of blessed forbearance is to accept insults with bitterness and pain of soul. The middle stage is to endure them without pain. The final stage, if there is one, is to consider them as praise. Rejoice, the first one; be strong, the second; blessed in the Lord and exalt the third. I have observed a pitiful sight in angry people, occurring unbeknownst to them out of arrogance. They became angry because of their failure and then became angry again. Seeing one fall after another, I marvelled. And observing them avenging sin with sin, I pitied them and was astonished at the cunning of the demons, nearly giving up hope for my own life.

If anyone sees themselves easily overcome by arrogance, irritability, malice, and hypocrisy, and then decides to use the double-edged sword of gentleness and forbearance against them, they should go and dwell in a community of brothers, especially harsh ones, if they truly wish to be free from them. There, through the insults and dishonours, and the storms of the brothers, being mentally struck and perhaps sometimes physically hit, trampled on, and kicked, they will wash away the filth that resides within the sensitivity of their soul.

Let this popular saying convince you that reproach is the laundry of the passions of the soul. Some in the world, when they reproach someone to their face, boast to others that they have "washed them clean." And indeed, this is true. The absence of anger born from sorrow in beginners is different from the immobility of the perfect. The former is bound by tears like a bridle; the latter is slain like a serpent by impassibility, as if by a sword. I have seen three monks who were insulted together. One was pained but remained silent. Another rejoiced for his own sake but was saddened for the insulter. The third, considering the harm to his neighbor, wept warmly. It was a sight to behold: workers of fear, reward, and love.

Just as fever in the body, though one in kind, has many causes for its heat, so too does the heat of anger, the movement of our other passions, and the rest, have many and various causes. Therefore, it is impossible to define it in a single way. I advise instead that those who are sick seek treatment with careful attention to each one’s particular condition. The first step in treatment is to recognize the cause of one's pain so that, having found the cause, they may receive the remedy from the foresight of God and spiritual physicians.

Let those who wish to enter the heavenly and unseen judgment seat with us in the Lord and judge the passions and causes mentioned, come with us, even if dimly, and we will scrutinize them. Let anger be bound like a tyrant in the chains of gentleness, and being struck by forbearance and dragged by holy love, let it stand in this court of speech and have its case examined. Tell us, O mad and obscene one, the name of your father and the title of your evil mother, and the names of your vile sons and daughters. Not only that, but also the signs of those who fight against you and kill you. And the anger answered us, saying, "Many have given birth to me, and my father is not one. My mothers are vanity, greed, gluttony, and sometimes lust. My father who begot me is arrogance. My daughters are resentment, hatred, enmity, and self-justification. My adversaries, who now hold me captive, are absence of anger and gentleness. My betrayer is called humility. But who gave birth to her, ask her in due time."

On the eighth step lies the crown of absence of anger. And the one who naturally possesses these virtues will perhaps never possess another. But the one who has acquired them through effort has indeed conquered all the eight.

Step 9 On Resentment

The holy virtues resemble the ladder of Jacob, while the unholy vices are like the chain that fell from Peter's hands. The former, when connected one to another, lead the person who chooses them to heaven. The latter are born from and entangled with one another, binding the soul. Therefore, we now hear the foolish anger admitting that resentment is its natural offspring, and so, as the time calls for it, let us speak about it.

Resentment is the culmination of anger, the keeper of sins, the hatred of righteousness, the loss of virtues, the poison of the soul, the worm of the mind, the shame of prayer, the cutting off of supplication, the alienation from love, the nail driven into the soul, the painless perception of bitterness loved in its sweetness, a continuous sin, a sleepless transgression, an unclean evil.

This dark and unpleasant passion, I speak of resentment, has its origin in those who generate and those who do not generate it, or in those who generate it intermittently. For this reason, we do not wish to say much about it. He who has put an end to anger has also abolished resentment. For as long as the father lives, the offspring continue to be born. The one who has acquired love has expelled wrath. But the one who harbors enmity gathers unnecessary toil for himself. A careless table is the mother of boldness, and gluttony will enter through the window of love.

I have seen hatred break the long-standing bond of fornication, and then resentment maintained that bond without fetters for the rest of the time, paradoxically. It is a remarkable sight—a demon healing another demon. But perhaps this is a work of God, providentially arranged, and not truly of the demons.

Resentment is far from natural, solid love, but fornication is easily close to it, and you can unwittingly see lice in a dove. Hold resentment against demons, and enmity against the body at all times. The flesh is an ungrateful and deceitful friend; even when cared for, it wrongs you even more. Resentment is a cunning teacher, allegorizing the words of the Spirit according to its own understanding. Let prayer shame it, for if we cannot say the words of Jesus' prayer together with it, then let it be ashamed.

When you have fought long and hard but still cannot dissolve the thorn, reconcile with your enemy, even with your lips, so that, embarrassed by your hypocrisy towards him for a long time, you will finally accept it wholly, pricked by your conscience as if by fire. Then you will know that you have been freed from this festering wound—not when you pray for the one who wronged you, nor when you invite him to your table, but when you hear of his suffering, either in soul or body, and you feel for him as if it were your own pain, and you weep for him.

A resentful hermit is a snake hiding in a hole, carrying deadly poison within himself. The remembrance of the Passion of Jesus will heal resentment, making it deeply ashamed by His forbearance. Rotten wood breeds worms within itself, and even the meek and falsely gentle are often plagued by wrath. He who casts it off has found forgiveness; he who clings to it has been deprived of mercy.

Some people have given themselves over to toil, sweat, and forgiveness. But a person who bears no resentment is greater than these, having pre-empted them. For it is said, "Forgive quickly, and you will be abundantly forgiven." The proof of genuine absence of resentment lies in the person who holds it and yet believes they are repenting—they are like someone who dreams they are running.

I have seen people resentful of the absence of resentment. They advised others to be resentful and, ashamed of their own words, ceased the passion. Let no one think this dark passion is a trivial matter. For it often extends even to spiritual men.

The ninth step is for the one who has obtained boldness to ask the Savior Jesus for the forgiveness of sins.

Step 10 On Slander

No one with sound judgment, I believe, would deny that slander is born out of hatred and resentment. Therefore, it is naturally placed alongside its predecessors, as in a sequence. Slander is the offspring of hatred, a subtle yet severe disease, a hidden and lurking leech that drains and consumes the blood of love, a pretense of love, a source of defilement and burden to the heart, and the ruin of purity.

Just as there are young women who shamelessly commit evil acts, there are others who, more discreetly and modestly, commit even graver offenses. So it is with the passions of dishonour: some are openly malicious, while others hide their malice under the guise of virtue. The most deceitful of these young women are hypocrisy, malice, sorrow, resentment, and slander of the heart, which may appear to be concerned with one thing but aim at something else.

I have heard those who slander, and I was shocked. When I confronted them, they justified their actions by claiming that they were motivated by love and concern for the person they were slandering. But I told them, "Cease this kind of love, lest the words of the Psalmist be proven true: 'Whoever secretly slanders his neighbor, him I will destroy.' If you claim to love, pray secretly and do not mock the man. This is the way that is acceptable to the Lord."

Let this not escape your notice: you must definitely awaken from the habit of judging the one who sins. Judas was among the disciples, and the thief was among the murderers. And it is a wonder how, in a single moment, their roles were reversed.

If anyone wishes to conquer the spirit of slander, let him direct his blame not at the one who sins but at the demon who instigates the sin. For no one desires to sin against God, though we all do so without being compelled. I have seen someone who sinned openly but repented secretly, and the one I condemned as a fornicator later turned out to be chaste before God, having genuinely appeased Him through repentance.

Never listen to someone who slanders another before you. Rather, say, "Stop, brother; I sin more grievously every day, so how can I judge him?" In this way, you will heal both yourself and your neighbor, achieving two goals with one remedy. This is one of the quickest ways to attain forgiveness of sins: refraining from judgment. "Do not judge, and you will not be judged."

Just as fire is foreign to water, so is judgment to the one who wishes to repent. Even if you see someone sin at the very moment of their death, do not judge them, for God's judgment is hidden from men. Some have committed great sins openly but have done even greater good deeds in secret, deceiving those who love to judge, who hold onto smoke instead of sunlight.

Listen to me, listen, all you who are wicked judges of others. If it is true, as it is indeed true, that "with the judgment you pronounce, you will be judged," then in whatever way you judge your neighbor, you will fall into the same fault. And there is no other outcome, whether it be in bodily or spiritual matters.

Those who are sharp and precise in judging the faults of others do so because they have not yet made a complete and unwavering memory and reflection on their own sins. For if anyone were to see their own faults clearly, without the veil of self-love, they would no longer concern themselves with the sins of others, realizing that even a hundred years would not be enough time to grieve over their own shortcomings, even if they shed as many tears as the entire Jordan River.

I have observed that true mourning is free from any trace of slander or judgment. Demons either tempt us to sin or, if we have not sinned, to judge those who have, so that by the second fault, they may pollute us with the first. Know this: slanderers and those who harbor resentment and envy are known for gladly and easily criticizing the teachings, deeds, or successes of others, being submerged in a spirit of hatred.

I have seen some who secretly and discreetly commit all manner of sins, and yet, in the presumption of their own purity, harshly judge those who sin in lesser ways and more publicly. To judge is a brazen usurpation of God's authority, and to condemn another is the ruin of one's own soul.

Just as arrogance, even without any other passion, can destroy a person, so can judgment, by itself, completely ruin us. If that Pharisee was condemned because of this, the good harvester who eats the ripe grapes will not bother with the sour ones, and the grateful and discerning mind will note the virtues it observes in others. But the fool will seek out faults and failings, as it is written: "They search out iniquities; they have failed in their search."

Do not judge even with your own eyes, for they can often deceive you.

Step 11 On Talkativeness and Silence

It has been briefly stated in previous discussions that it is exceedingly dangerous, even for those who seem spiritual, to fall into the sin of judging others, or rather, to be judged and condemned by their own tongues. Now, therefore, it is fitting to explain both the cause and the door through which this sin enters, or rather, exits, in a more detailed manner through words.

Talkativeness is the throne of vanity, through which it reveals and expresses itself. Talkativeness is a sign of ignorance, the doorway to slander, the guide to frivolity, the servant of lying, the dissolution of compunction, the herald of sloth, the precursor of sleep, the disperser of thought, the destroyer of watchfulness, the cooling of fervour, the obscuring of prayer, the destroyer of silence, the mother of prayer, the recall from captivity, the guard of fire, the overseer of thoughts, the target of enemies, the prison of mourning, the friend of tears, the worker of the memory of death, the painter of judgment, the lover of inquisitiveness, the servant of agitation, the enemy of frankness, the consort of stillness, the adversary of teaching, the addition to knowledge, the creator of visions, the unseen progress, the hidden ascent.

The one who has recognized his transgressions has controlled his tongue. But the talkative person has not yet come to know himself as he ought. The friend of silence draws near to God, and in secret conversation, he is enlightened by God. Jesus' silence confounded Pilate, and the quietness of a man has often silenced vainglory.

Peter, after speaking, wept bitterly, remembering the words, "I said, 'I will guard my ways that I may not sin with my tongue.'" And another has said, "It is better to fall from a height to the ground than from the tongue." And though there are many things to say on this matter, I do not wish to write more, even though the cunning of passions urges me to do so.

I once heard someone who loved stillness say that talkativeness is certainly born from one of these causes: either from an evil and unrestrained association and habit, or from vainglory, especially in those who are striving. Sometimes it even comes from gluttony. Therefore, many who restrain their stomachs often, by some necessity or weakness, also restrain their tongues and their inclination to talk excessively.

The one who is mindful of his exit from this life has cut down his words. And the one who has acquired mourning in his soul has fled from talkativeness as from fire. The one who has loved stillness has shut his mouth. But the one who rejoices in his progress is often driven from his cell by this passion. The one who has recognized the scent of the highest fire flees the gathering of men like a bee flees smoke. For just as smoke drives away bees, so does the gathering of men disperse the spirit of stillness.

The eleventh step is when one moves away from the multitude of evils by eliminating this one thing: talkativeness.

Step 12 On Lying

Fire is born of iron and stone; lying is born of talkativeness and frivolity. Lying is the ruin of love; perjury is a denial of God. Let no one of sound mind think that the sin of lying is a trivial matter. For the Holy Spirit, with a fearful decree, condemned it above all things, as David said to God: "You will destroy all who speak lying" (Psalm 5:6). What then will be the fate of those who weave lies with oaths?

I have seen some who take pride in their lies, weaving humorous tales and idle chatter, and thereby extinguishing the mourning of those who listen with pity. When demons see us striving to withdraw from the infectious disease of idle talk after the initial instruction of our spiritual guide, they try to ensnare us with two thoughts: "Do not grieve the instructor," they whisper, "or you might seem more devout than those present, and flee the gathering prematurely." If you do not, in prayer, your mind will recall these idle thoughts. Not only should you flee, but also piously dissolve the sinful gathering, reminding them of death and judgment. For it is better for you to be lightly tainted with a small vanity from this than to be found a source of benefit to all.

Hypocrisy is often the mother and cause of lying, for some define hypocrisy as a studied practice of lying, a habit and skillful means of deception. Whoever has acquired the fear of the Lord has fled from lying, having as an impartial judge his own conscience.

Just as we recognize varying degrees of harm in all passions, so also in lying. The judgment of one who lies out of fear of punishment differs from one who lies without any danger at hand. Some lie out of indulgence, others out of sensuality. Some lie to amuse those present, while others do so to plot against a brother and harm him. Lying is erased under the pressure of rulers; through many tears, it is finally destroyed.

The one who fabricates lies often justifies it with claims of expediency, and sometimes considers it an act of righteousness, even though it leads to the loss of his soul. A liar may claim to imitate Rahab, who lied to save the spies, and justify his own lies as a means to save others while destroying himself.

When we have entirely cleansed ourselves from lying, then, with fear and at the appropriate time, we may encounter it. A child does not know lying, nor does a soul devoid of wickedness. He who rejoices in wine will involuntarily speak the truth in everything, and he who is intoxicated with compunction will not be able to lie.

The twelfth step is an ascent: he who has stepped upon it has attained the root of all good things.

Step 13 On Acedia (Spiritual Despondency)

As we have already mentioned, acedia is often one of the branches of talkativeness, and it is its first offspring. Therefore, we have placed it in the appropriate position within the evil chain. Acedia is the neglect of the soul, the dissolution of the mind, indifference to asceticism, hatred of the spiritual vocation, praise of worldly things, slander against God as being merciless and unloving, weakness in psalmody, infirmity in prayer, laziness in service, relentless in manual work, yet efficient in obedience.

A person subjected to acedia is unrecognizable, as they skillfully fulfill their duties by focusing on the tangible instead of the spiritual. Acedia is an adversary to communal life and an eternal companion to a hermit, from whom it will not depart before death. Every day, it will wrestle with him before his end. Seeing the cell of a hermit, acedia smiles and draws near, making its dwelling close by. A physician visits the sick early in the morning, but acedia comes to those who are idle around midday.

Acedia urges hospitality, using it as a pretext, and it encourages almsgiving through manual work. It eagerly suggests visiting the sick, recalling the words of Christ, "I was sick, and you visited me." It proposes to comfort the fainthearted, but in doing so, it makes the faintheartedness even worse. When one stands in prayer, acedia reminds him of necessary tasks and uses every trick to draw him away under the guise of reasonable concerns. A demon of acedia creates a three-hour anxiety, headache, and dizziness. When the ninth hour arrives, it slightly relaxes, allowing the monk to eat, but after the meal, it urges him to his bed. When prayer begins again, the body is weighed down. Standing in prayer, acedia baptizes the monk with sleep, and with untimely yawns, it tears apart the verses.

Other passions can be overcome by a single virtue, but acedia is a comprehensive death to a monk. Courage revives a dead mind, but acedia and sloth squander all wealth. Being one of the eight principal passions, and the heaviest of them all, we should deal with it in its proper order. Additionally, let us note that where there is no psalmody, acedia is not far away. Once the rule of prayer is completed, the eyes are opened. In times of acedia, the brave appear, for nothing brings crowns to a monk like acedia. Reflect and you will find it struggling in your standing, and in your sitting, it tries to incline you to recline. It prompts you to peek out from the cell’s window, making noises and causing footfalls.

He who mourns for himself does not know acedia. This tyrant is bound by the memory of sins and beaten by manual labour. It is dragged by the thought of future blessings and brought to trial in due course. Speak now, you despondent and listless one, who has begotten you so wickedly? Who are your offspring? Who are those who wage war against you, and who is your slayer?

Acedia responds, "I have no place to lay my head among those who are truly obedient. But among those where I do dwell, I live in peace. My children are many: at times, insensitivity of soul, at others, forgetfulness of heavenly things. Sometimes it is the excessive heat of the day. My offspring are wanderings from place to place, resulting from disobedience to the father, and forgetfulness of judgment. At times, it is the abandonment of the spiritual vocation. My adversaries, who now bind me, are psalmody combined with manual work. My enemy is the thought of death. The one who kills me is prayer with firm hope in good things. But if you want to know who gave birth to prayer, ask it yourself."

The thirteenth step to victory belongs to him who, indeed, has become in every way a tested and worthy servant of the good.

Step 14 Concerning the Gluttonous and Evil Mistress of the Stomach

As we are about to speak concerning the stomach, which affects all things, we now intend to philosophize more deeply about ourselves. For I marvel if anyone, having lived in a grave, has ever become free from it. Gluttony is the hypocrisy of the belly. Though it is full, it cries out in want, and even when it is stuffed and bursting, it still cries out that it is hungry. Gluttony is the creator of spices and the source of delights. You sever one vein, and another arises, and after you cut that one, another prevails. Gluttony is the deception of the eyes. Though it receives a moderate amount, it desires to consume everything it is presented with. An excess of food is the father of fornication, but the affliction of the belly leads to chastity. Many have tamed lions by flattering them, but those who serve the body make it wilder. The Jew rejoices in the Sabbath and the feast. The gluttonous monk rejoices in the Sabbath and Sunday, considering the Easter celebration ahead of time and preparing the feast days ahead of schedule. The belly servant considers which foods to feast on, while the God-servant considers which gifts he will be enriched with. When a stranger arrives for a meal, the glutton acts entirely out of greed, and he views the brother's invitation as a means of indulging his own appetite. In the presence of others, he thinks about breaking his fast with wine, and while he believes he is concealing his virtue, he becomes a slave to passion. Vanity often quarrels with gluttony, and these two struggle like slaves within the wretched monk. The one forces the breaking of the fast, while the other attempts to triumph in its own virtue. A wise monk will escape both, shaking off one vice by means of the other at the right time. When the flesh is strong, we will punish it at all times and places. But when it calms down, which I do not believe will happen before the grave, we will hide our labours. I have seen elderly priests mocked by demons, and the young, who are not subject to these demons, breaking their fast with wine and other foods at meals, even with a blessing. If they are bearing witness to the Lord, let us break our fast moderately. If they are neglectful, let us not concern ourselves with their blessings, especially if we are engaged in combat with fire.

The God-inspired Evagrius thought himself wiser than the wise in speech and understanding, but the wretched one was deceived, appearing more foolish than the fools, in many ways, including this: He says that whenever our soul desires various foods, it should be restricted to bread and water. But this is like telling a child to climb an entire ladder in one step. Therefore, let us oppose his teaching and say: Whenever our soul desires various foods, it seeks what is natural. So let us use the most cunning device for this cunning one. Otherwise, we face a severe battle, or the accountability for a fall. Let us cut off first what fattens us, then what inflames us, and finally what delights us. If possible, give your belly food that satisfies and is easy to digest so that by fullness, we may drive away its insatiable greed, and through quick digestion, we may be freed from the scorching as if by a whip. Let us observe most of those who practice spirituality, and we will find them in motion. Laugh at the demon who, after dinner, suggests to you that you are superior. For when the ninth hour arrives, the soul renounces the formula of the previous one. There is a different kind of restraint appropriate for those without responsibility and another for those with responsibility. Some have their bodily movements at a level, while others remain committed to this practice until death. The former always wish to preserve the balance of their mind, while the latter appease God through the sorrow and mourning of their soul. A time of joy and comfort is carefree for the perfect, but it is a time of struggle for the fighter, and for the passionate, it is the feast of feasts and the festival of festivals. The dreams of food and drink dwell in the heart of the gluttonous, but the dreams of punishment and judgment dwell in the heart of those who mourn. Control your belly before it controls you, and then you will strive for self-control with shame. Those who have fallen into the unspeakable pit know this, but eunuchs are ignorant of it. Let us cut the belly with the fire of thought. Some, having obeyed this, have cut off their own members and have died a double death. Let us investigate and surely find that most of the shipwrecks around us are caused by this alone. The mind of the fasting one prays with sobriety, but the mind of the incontinent is filled with unclean images. The fullness of the belly dries up the springs.

He who cares for his stomach and wishes to conquer the spirit of fornication is like one trying to extinguish a fire with oil. When the stomach is oppressed, the heart is humbled. But when it is nurtured, the mind becomes arrogant. Examine yourself in the middle of the day and at the last hour before eating, and you will learn from this the benefit of fasting. In the morning, the thoughts leap and draw near, but by the sixth hour, they have slightly diminished, and by sunset, they are utterly humbled. Oppress your stomach, and you will certainly close your mouth as well, for the tongue is stimulated by an abundance of food. Contend with your stomach, and if you labour just a little, the Lord will immediately assist you. The softened wineskins expand, but when disregarded, they do not hold as much. The one who forces his stomach enlarges his bowels, but the one who struggles against it tightens them. When these are tightened, they will not hold much, and then we naturally become those who fast. Thirst has often quenched thirst, but it is difficult and impossible to curb hunger with hunger. Whenever it overcomes you, subdue it with toil. But if this is impossible due to weakness, wrestle with it through vigilance. When the eyes become heavy, take up handiwork; and if sleep does not come, do not touch it. For it is impossible for the mind to serve both God and mammon, that is, both God and handiwork. Know that often the demon sits in the stomach and prepares the person not to be satisfied, even if they eat all of Egypt and drink the Nile River. After food, the unholy one retreats and sends us the spirit of fornication, having reported to it what has occurred. “Seize him,” it says, “and disturb him.” For when the stomach is burdened, you will be weary. It comes with a smile, and binding our hands and feet with sleep, it does whatever it desires. It defiles the soul and body with pollutions, fantasies, and emissions. It is a wonder to see a bodiless mind defiled and darkened by the body, and again purified and refined through clay. If you are determined to walk the narrow and difficult path with Christ, tighten your stomach. For if you nurture it and broaden it, you will violate your covenant. Observe, and you will hear the one saying, "Wide and broad is the way of the stomach, leading to the destruction of fornication, and many are those who enter through it." But narrow is the gate and difficult is the way of fasting, leading to the life of chastity, and few are those who enter through it. The ruler of demons is the fallen Lucifer, and the ruler of passions is the throat of the stomach. When reclining at a table of food, bring to mind the memory of death and judgment. For only then will you be able to hinder even a little of your passion. When drinking the cup, do not cease to remember the vinegar and gall of your Master. And you will certainly either practice self-control, or you will groan, or you will humble your spirit even more. Do not be deceived, you will not be freed from Pharaoh, nor will you see the heavenly Passover unless you continually eat bitter herbs and unleavened bread. Bitter herbs are the force and toil of fasting; unleavened bread is the uninflated spirit. Let your words cling to your breath as you say, "When the demons trouble me, I clothed myself in sackcloth and humbled my soul with fasting, and my prayer returned to the bosom of my soul." Fasting is the violence against nature, the circumcision of the sweetness of the throat, the cutting off of the fire, the removal of evil thoughts, freedom from dreams, purity of prayer, the light of the soul, the guardian of the mind, the release from hardness, the door of contrition, humble sighing, joyful contrition, the cessation of much speaking, the occasion for quiet, the guard of obedience, the lightening of sleep, the health of the body, the cause of dispassion, the forgiveness of sins, the door of paradise and delight. Let us demand from this enemy, and above all from the leader of our evils, the door of passions, the fall of Adam, the loss of Esau, the destruction of the Israelites, the disgrace of Noah, the destruction of the sons of Eli the priest, the leader of pollutions. Where does it originate, and who are its offspring? Who is the one who crushes it, and who utterly destroys it? Tell us, you tyrant over all mortals, who have bought everyone with the gold of greed: where did you gain entrance into us? And what do you naturally bring forth after entering? And what is your exit from us? The one who laboured in insolence, tyrannizing over us, answered fiercely and wildly: "Why do you insult me, who am accountable to you? How do you strive to separate from me, for I am bound by nature? The door for me is the nature of foods. The cause of my greed is familiarity. The foundation of my passion is habitual practice, insensitivity of the soul, and forgetfulness of death. How do you seek to learn the names of my offspring? I will number them, and they will outnumber the sand. Nevertheless, listen to the names of my firstborn and beloved: My firstborn son, the servant of fornication, and his second, hardness of heart."

Sleep is the third offspring, an ocean of thoughts, waves of impurities, and a depth of unknown and unspeakable defilements that issue forth from me. My daughters are laziness, idle talk, loquacity, jesting, frivolity, contradiction, stubbornness, disobedience, insensitivity, captivity, pride, arrogance, worldliness, followed by a defiled soul and wandering thoughts. Often, unexpected and unforeseen calamities also arise, accompanied by the most grievous of all, despair. Memory of sins battles against me but does not overcome me. The thought of death opposes me, but nothing fully destroys me among men. The one who has obtained a comforter intercedes against me. When that one is supplicated, he does not allow me to operate with impassioned intent. For those who have not tasted his sweetness seek to delight in mine.

Victory comes through manliness. The one who strives for dispassion and ultimate chastity prevails.

Step 15 On incorruptible purity and chastity to which the corruptible attain by toil and sweat

Chastity is the familiarization with the nature of the incorporeal. Chastity is an extraordinary denial of nature beyond nature, and indeed a paradoxical struggle of the mortal and corruptible body against the incorporeal. The chaste person is one who has repelled desire with desire and quenched fire with a spiritual fire. Temperance is the universal epitome of all virtues. A temperate person is one who, even in dreams, feels no movement from their present state. A temperate person is one who has acquired complete insensitivity to the distinction of bodies at all times. This is the measure and boundary of perfect and pure chastity: to be equally disposed toward both animate and inanimate, rational and irrational beings. No one who has practiced chastity should consider its acquisition their own; for to conquer nature itself is among the things impossible. Where there has been a defeat of nature, the presence of the supernatural has been recognized. For without contradiction, the lesser is always overcome by the greater. The beginning of chastity is an unyielding mind, and the rejection of images over time. The midpoint of chastity is natural movements upon a multitude of foods, occurring without images and free from any emission. The end is the mortification of the body before the thoughts have passed away. Blessed truly is the one who has attained complete insensitivity to every body, colour, and time. It is not the one who has kept their clay free from impurity who is chaste, but the one who has perfectly subjected the members of their body to their soul. Great indeed is the person who remains unaffected upon contact and has remained invulnerable, triumphing over the vision of fire with higher contemplation and other heavenly thoughts.

The one who repels the dog through prayer is like a lion fighting with a soldier. The one who overcomes it with reasoning is like one who pursues his enemy. But the one who, at once, despises the attack has, even though in the flesh, already risen from the tomb. If this is the true proof of chastity—being immovable even in the phantasms of sleep—then this must surely be the boundary of lust, to endure the flow while awake in one’s thoughts. The one who struggles and toils against this adversary, who has bound his enemy with ropes, resembles one who, by self-control and vigilance, has wrapped him in a noose. And the one who, through humility, lack of anger, and thirst, has killed the enemy and buried him in the sand. By “sand,” understand humility; for it does not nourish the pasture of passions, but is like earth and ashes. One person is sanctified through struggle, another through humility, and another through divine revelation, holding the tyrant bound. One is like the morning star, another like the radiant sun. Both have their citizenship in the heavens. From light arises light, and from light the sun rises. So also, these things may be understood and found. The fox pretends to sleep, and so does the demon feign chastity. But one does this to deceive a bird, and the other to destroy a soul. Do not trust the clay in your lifetime, and do not rely on it until you meet Christ. Do not trust that through self-control you will not fall; for even one who was eating in heaven was cast down. Some knowledgeable ones have correctly defined renunciation as hostility toward the body and warfare against the stomach. In the beginning, the body’s falls tend to occur from indulgence. In the middle stages, they happen from pride, except in the beginnings. But in those approaching perfection, they may occur from condemning one's neighbor alone.

Some have called those who are eunuchs by nature blessed because they are freed from the tyranny of the body. But I have called those who are daily eunuchs blessed, who have accustomed themselves to amputating their desires with the sword of thought. I have seen those who have fallen involuntarily, and I have seen those who desired to fall voluntarily but could not, and I have mourned more for those who fall daily because they cannot rid themselves of the stench. Wretched is the one who falls, but even more wretched is the one who causes another to fall, for he will bear the weight of both falls and the pleasure of the other. Do not try to overthrow the demon of fornication with arguments and refutations, for he has reasonable arguments, as he fights us naturally. Whoever desires to wage war against his flesh or to conquer it by himself runs in vain. For unless the Lord destroys the house of the flesh and builds the house of the soul, in vain has the person stayed awake, fasted, and demolished it. Entrust to the Lord the weakness of nature, recognizing in every respect your own incapacity, and you will receive the gift of chastity imperceptibly.

Some say that those who indulge themselves experience a bodily sensation of desire after their fall, with a bold and inhuman spirit visibly seated in the heart’s perception, preparing the afflicted to feel bodily pain like that from a fiery furnace, disregarding God, scornful of the memory of punishment, abhorring prayer, and at the very act considering the sight of dead bones as if they were lifeless stones, leading the suffering person into a state of mindless intoxication with a continuous obsession with both the rational and irrational substance. If the days were not shortened, no soul would be saved. This clay mixed with dirty blood and juice, how can it be? For everything that exists is insatiably drawn to its own kin. Blood to blood, worm to worm, and clay to clay. Thus, does not flesh to flesh? Even if we attempt to deceive the deceiver by cunning methods, those who are violent toward nature and desire the kingdom are blessed. Let us pray to be freed from such experiences altogether. Those who slip into that pit fall far from those who ascend and descend the ladder, and in order to climb up from there, they need much sweat and extreme fasting. Let us observe if, as in a physical battle, each of our enemies in the spiritual warfare approaches us with a particular strategy. This is something I have noted among those who are tempted. And I have seen falls that are more severe than other falls. Let those with understanding hear.

It is customary for the demon, especially in those who are struggling and practicing the solitary life, to prepare all his force, zeal, cunning, and evil intention to attack them only in things contrary to nature and not in those according to nature. Hence, many who live with women and are not attacked by any desire or thought toward them, consider themselves blessed, unaware that where there is greater destruction, there is no need for the lesser. I believe that for these two reasons, the malicious ones have cultivated the habit of attacking us wretches in unnatural ways, as we are always susceptible to the material of the fall. And they have acquired greater punishment as a result. These are the ones who once indulged in wild desires, who in the end, miserably surrender to them and are mocked by them, having been fed on heavenly bread, but later deprived of the good, and what is most amazing, that even after repentance, a great pillar has fallen, like Antony. But the wise one concealed the manner of his fall. For he knew that there is fornication in the body and outside of another body. There is a death within us, and a loss of the body within us, which accompanies us, especially in youth. I dared not write it down, for the one who said, “For the things done in secret by some, it is shameful even to speak, write, or hear,” restrained my hand.

This enemy, which is my flesh and yet not my own, Paul called death. “Who will rescue me from this body of death?” Another theologian called it passionate and enslaved, and nocturnal. Some have used such terms to describe it for the sake of learning. If death, as previously said, is flesh, then the one who conquers it will not die. And who then is that person who will live and not see the death of his defiled flesh? I implore you to consider who is greater, the one who died and rose, or the one who never died at all. The one who blessed the second has lied, for Christ, who died, rose again. But the one who blessed the first asks that no despair should befall those who are dying, rather than falling. For the merciful God is accused by the inhuman enemy, the master of fornication, who claims that He offers great forgiveness to this passion as it is natural. But let us observe the cunning of the demons, and after the act, we will find them accusing Him as a just judge and unforgiving. There they used this to drive us to sin, but now they want to plunge us into despair. When sorrow and despair are present, we tend to give ourselves over to our fault again. But when this is extinguished, the tyrant of mercy takes over. As the Lord is incorruptible and incorporeal, so much does He rejoice in our chastity and the incorruptibility of our bodies. There is nothing else that the demons rejoice in as much as the stench of fornication, some say. And there is nothing else that brings them closer than the defilement of the body. Chastity is the familiarization with and resemblance to God, as far as possible for humans. It is the mother of sweetness, the earth, and dew. But the mother of chastity is stillness with obedience. The one who achieves bodily impassibility through stillness, although often near the world, does not remain unmoved. But the one who achieves it through obedience is steadfast everywhere.

I have seen pride that leads to humility and have remembered the saying, “Who has known the mind of the Lord?” Pride is the pit and offspring of arrogance, and it is often the cause of humility among those who desire it. The one who wants to conquer the demon of fornication while indulging in gluttony and fullness is like one trying to extinguish a fire with oil. The one who tries to cease the battle with self-control alone is like one trying to swim with one hand and striving to cross the sea. Combine humility with self-control, for without the second, the first is useless. Whoever sees himself overcome by some passion should arm himself against it alone above all, especially the internal enemy. If this is not defeated, nothing is gained from defeating others, and if we strike it down, we will surely see God in the burning bush of humility. I have sensed the wolf testing me, bringing irrational joy, tears, and false consolation to my soul, and I thought I was bearing fruit, but it was actually corruption. If every sin that a person commits is outside the body, but the one who fornicates sins against their own body, then certainly because it is in the very nature of the flesh to be defiled by the flow, which cannot happen with any other sin. I seek to understand why we commonly say that people stumble in every sin, but when we hear that someone has fornicated, we say with pain, “He has fallen.” The fish swiftly flees the hook, and the pleasure-loving soul turns away from stillness. When the devil wants to bind some to each other in shameful bondage, he tests both sides and then starts the fire. Often, those inclined to pleasure are sympathetic, compassionate, tearful, and flattering in these matters.

A knowledgeable man once asked me a fearful question: “What sin, aside from murder and denial, is heavier than all others?” And when I said falling into heresy, he asked, “How is it that the Catholic Church accepts heretics with the proper anathema of their own heresy, considering them worthy of partaking in the mysteries, but excludes those who have fornicated even after confession and repentance from the holy mysteries for a time, according to the apostolic canons?” I was struck by this perplexity, and the paradox remains unexplained and unresolved. Let us examine and measure, and observe what sweetness we receive from the demon of fornication during psalmody, and what from the words of the Spirit and the grace and power that dwells in them.

Do not deceive yourself, young man. For I have seen some who prayed from their souls for their loved ones, and while being stirred by fornication, they thought they were fulfilling a memory and commandment of love. It is possible to be defiled through physical contact. There is nothing heavier than this sensation. Remember the one who clasped the sickle with his hand, numbing the hand over natural and unnatural, over his own and another’s body. I do not believe that anyone can be called holy if they have not first transformed this earth into holiness, if indeed it is possible to transform it. When we lie down on our beds, let us be sober, for then the mind wrestles alone with the demons, and if it is found to be pleasure-loving, it gladly becomes a traitor. Let the memory of death always sleep with you and rise with you, and let the prayer of Jesus be your constant thought. There is nothing as helpful as these in sleep. Some think that wars and emissions only come from food. But I have seen those who were ill and fasting to the extreme being strongly defiled by them. I once asked one of the most discerning and experienced monks about these things. And the blessed one taught me very clearly, saying, “Nocturnal emissions occur from an excess of food and indulgence. Another occurs from pride, when we are puffed up by our prolonged lack of emissions. And another from condemning one’s neighbor. The first two, he said, can also happen to the weak.” I think all three can. But if anyone examines himself and finds he is free from all the previously mentioned causes, blessed is he who has attained such impassibility. And if he experiences it only once due to the envy of the demons and with God’s permission, let him know it happened so that through a sinless calamity, he might acquire the highest humility.

No one should wish to reflect on the fantasies of sleep during the day. For this is also a tactic of the demons, to defile us while we are awake through dreams. Let us hear another cunning trick of our enemies. Just as harmful foods often cause illness later on, so I have seen those who indulge in the causes that defile the soul, not being immediately attacked. And I have seen those who eat with women and spend time with them without entertaining any evil thoughts. And yet, those who were deceived and trusted in their false sense of peace and safety suffered sudden destruction in their own cells. What is this destruction that occurs physically and spiritually when we are alone? The one who has been tempted knows, but the one who has not been tempted does not need to know. At such times, our best aid is sackcloth, ashes, a vigil throughout the night, a craving for bread, a burning tongue slightly cooled, dwelling among the tombs, and above all, humility of heart. And if possible, a father or a wise brother who is mature in mind to help. For I wonder if anyone can save a ship alone from the sea. The same fall often carries a hundredfold more judgment than another, due to circumstance, place, progress, and many other factors.

Someone explained to me a paradoxical and extreme point of chastity. He said, “Beholding beauty, one greatly glorified the Creator and, from the sight alone, was moved to love God and tears. It was a wonder to see one person’s pit become another’s crown, truly above nature. If this person always has such perception and practice in these matters, they have risen incorruptible before the general resurrection.” We should apply the same standard to melodies and songs. For the God-loving are moved to joy, divine love, and tears both by external things and by spiritual ones. The pleasure-lovers, however, are moved in the opposite direction. Some, as we mentioned earlier, have found that in places of stillness, they are more heavily attacked. And no wonder, for demons frequent those places much more, having been banished by the Lord to the deserts and the abyss for our salvation. The demons of fornication fiercely war against the still ones, so that nothing beneficial may come from the desert to the world. They retreat from us who live in the world, so that we may remain untroubled with worldly people. Where we are attacked, there we surely fight the enemy fiercely. For if we are not attacked, we are found to be friends of our enemies. We are protected by the hand of the Lord while living in the world out of necessity, perhaps often because of a parent’s prayer, so that the Lord may not be blasphemed because of us. Sometimes, this also happens due to insensitivity, or due to great experience and fullness of those who see and speak. Or perhaps because the demons retreat willingly, leaving us with the demon of pride, who fills the place of all others. Hear this deceitful plan and cunning of the demon, all you who aspire to practice chastity, and guard yourselves.

Someone experienced in deception told me how often the demon of the body withdraws entirely, suggesting to the monk extreme piety and perhaps even a fountain of tears at the time when he sits with women or discusses death, judgment, and chastity, prompting him to admonish them, so that through his words and false piety, the poor souls may run to him like sheep to the wolf, and once familiarity and boldness have set in, the wretched demon will bring about the fall. Let us flee, avoiding even sight and hearing, and let us resolve to no longer taste its fruit. For I wonder if we consider ourselves stronger than the prophet David, which is impossible. So high and great is the praise of chastity that some of the Fathers dared to call it dispassion. Some say it is impossible to be called chaste after tasting sin. But I contradicted them, saying, “It is possible and easy for the willing to graft a wild olive onto a cultivated one.” If the keys to the heavens were entrusted to a virgin body, then perhaps those who made the previous judgments were justified. Let those be ashamed who acquired a mother-in-law, became chaste, and bear the keys to the kingdom. The serpent of the body is multiform. To the inexperienced, it sows the notion of both experience and cessation, while to the experienced, it leads from memory back to experience. Many of the former remain unconquered due to ignorance of the evil. The latter, having experienced the filth, now endure assaults and wars. But the opposite is also true sometimes.

When we rise from sleep, peaceful and good, we unknowingly owe it to the holy angels, especially if we have slept after much prayer and vigilance. But sometimes, when we rise from sleep not peaceful, we have suffered from evil dreams. “I saw the wicked exalted and lifted up, and troubled and raging within me,” like the cedars of Lebanon. And I passed by through self-control, and behold, he was not as he was before. I sought him, having humbled my mind, and he was not found in me, neither his place nor his trace. Whoever has conquered the body has conquered nature. And whoever has conquered nature has certainly become above nature. And the one who has become so is diminished slightly below the angels, if not entirely. It is no wonder for the bodiless to fight the bodiless. But it is indeed a wonder, truly a wonder, that the bodiless enemies are defeated by the material, hostile matter. The good Lord shows great care for us even in this, by restraining the shamelessness of the female with modesty, as if with a bridle. For if it had turned toward the male, no flesh would be saved. There is a difference between temptation, struggle, consent, captivity, and what is called passion of the soul, as defined by the discerning Fathers. They define temptation as a mere word or image suddenly appearing in the heart. Struggle as conversing with the appearance, either passionately or dispassionately. Consent as a pleasurable nod of the soul to the sight. Captivity as the involuntary and violent dragging away of the heart, or a prolonged union with what is perceived, destroying our best state. They define passion as the chronic, passionately dwelling in the soul, which through habit brings it to a state of desire and willingly yields to it.

Of all these, the first is sinless, the second not always, the third according to the state of the struggler, the fourth leads to either crowns or punishments, and captivity is judged differently in the time of prayer and at other times, in the middle of the day, and differently in evil thoughts. Passion is undoubtedly subject to either repentance or future punishment. Therefore, whoever considers the first dispassionately has already cut off all the last. Among the most discerning of the knowledgeable Fathers, there is another, more subtle meaning. Some call it the darting of the mind, which without time, word, or image, signifies the passion to the sufferer more sharply, with a bare and indivisible memory, timeless and unspeakable, causing his presence in the soul to be unknown. If anyone has been able to capture this subtlety through mourning, he can teach us how it is possible for the soul to fornicate passionately through a mere glance, a bare touch, or the hearing of a single member, without any thought or reasoning. Some say that the body comes to passions from the thoughts of the heart. Others say the opposite, that evil thoughts are born from the sensations of the body. The former say that unless the mind first advances, the body would not follow. The latter attribute the evil of bodily passion to their own defense, saying, “Often from the sweetest sight or touch of the hand, or fragrant smell, or pleasant hearing, the thoughts enter the heart.” Those who can, will teach about these things in the Lord. For these are necessary and beneficial to those who practice the active life through knowledge. But to those who work in the simplicity of heart, these words mean nothing. For not all possess knowledge, nor all the blessed simplicity, which is the shield against the wiles of the evil ones. Some passions come from within, affecting the body, while others, the opposite. Among those in the world, the latter; among those who pursue the solitary life, the former due to the lack of material. I say this concerning that. “Seek understanding from the wicked, and you will not find it.”

When we have fought long and hard against the demon from the clay, driving him out of our hearts through the stones of fasting and the sword of humility, then he will sit within the body like a worm, tickling us with irrational and untimely movements, forcing us to defile ourselves. This is especially common among those who follow the demon of vanity, for having ceased from constantly contemplating lustful thoughts in their hearts, they succumb to vanity. And if this is true, whenever they take hold of some stillness, let them carefully examine themselves. They will certainly find some thought deep in their hearts, like a snake hidden in the dung, which, through their own zeal and eagerness, they believe they have perfectly achieved the work of purity in the heart, unaware of the saying, “What do you have that you did not receive as a gift,” either from God or through the cooperation and prayer of others? Therefore, let them examine themselves and with all diligence mortify the aforementioned snake in their hearts through much humility, so that, having been alienated from it, they may one day also cast off their leather garments and sing the victory hymn of chastity to the Lord, as once did the pure infants, if they are found not naked of their innocence and natural humility. This demon also watches them, much more than others, at times when we cannot pray against him physically. And then the impious one tries to war against us. The tumult of physical prayer, I mean, the stretching out of hands, striking the breast, lifting the eyes sincerely to the heavens, the noise of sighs, the constant bending of knees, which he often cannot do because of the present circumstances. Therefore, it is then that the demons try to wage war. And those who are not yet strong enough to resist with the strength of mind and the invisible power of prayer may perhaps, out of necessity, give in to their assailants. If possible, flee quickly, hide for a moment without being noticed. Shake your head, if possible, with your inner eye. If not, even with your external eye, cross your hands motionlessly so that through this sign you may shame and defeat Amalek. Call upon the One who can save, not with sophisticated words, but with humble expressions above all, beginning with “Have mercy on me, for I am weak.” Then you will be tested by the highest power and invisibly drive out the invisible by invisible help. Thus accustomed to fighting, you will quickly begin to drive out the enemies with your soul alone. For the second is given as a reward for the first by God to the workers, and rightly so.

Once, at a gathering, I noticed a zealous brother being troubled by evil thoughts. Finding no suitable place for secret prayer, he suddenly fled to the need of the stomach, as if bitten by it, and used intense prayer against his enemies. When I reproached him for the inappropriateness of the place, he replied, “I prayed in an unclean place against unclean thoughts, to be cleansed from the filth.” All those who darken the mind usually first test their own favourites and then suggest them. For if the mind does not close its eyes, the treasure will not be stolen. But the demon of fornication, more than any other, often seizes the leading mind and urges and prepares him to commit acts among people that only the insane carry out. Hence, after some time, when the mind awakens, we are ashamed not only of those who witnessed it but also of ourselves for our indecent deeds and conversations or attitudes, and we marvel at our former hardness. Therefore, some have often ceased from such discernment of evil. Turn away the enemy who prevents you from praying, fearing God, or watching after the act, remembering the saying, “Because they have given me trouble, I will avenge their soul from their enemies.” Who has conquered the body? The one who has crushed the heart. And who has crushed the heart? The one who has denied himself. How could he not be crushed who has died to his own will? There is a passionate person more passionate than another, even enjoying the very confession of his own defilements with pleasure. The unclean and filthy thoughts in the heart are born of the deceiver of the heart, the demon of fornication. These are healed by self-control and by considering them as nothing at all. But what manner and method should I use to bind this dear friend of mine, so that I may judge him according to the sequence of the rest, I do not know. For before I bind him, he is loosed. And before I judge him, I reconcile. And before I punish him, I am softened. How can I hate what I am naturally inclined to love? How can I be freed from what I am bound to for eternity? How can I destroy what rises with me? How can I show incorruptibility, having taken on a corruptible nature? What reasonable thing can I say to one who has reasonable arguments through nature? If I bind him with fasting, condemning my neighbor, I am handed over to him again. If I cease to judge and defeat him, I am humbled in my heart and fall into the same thing. For he is both an ally and an enemy, a helper and an adversary, a supporter and a plotter. When healed, he wars; when wasted, he is weak; when resting, he acts disorderly; when fawning, he cannot bear it. If I grieve, I am in danger. If I strike, I do not know how I will acquire the virtues. I both reject and embrace the same thing. What is the mystery concerning me? What is the reasoning of my constitution? How am I both an enemy and a friend to myself? Tell me, you, tell me, my spouse, my nature. For I will need to learn this from no one else but you. How can I remain invulnerable to you? How can I escape the natural danger, since I have now been ordered to be hostile to you for Christ’s sake? How will I defeat your tyranny since it has been determined that I should become your assailant?

And she, answering to her own soul, said, “I will not tell you what you do not already know, but what we both know together. I have love within me as a father, both external burning and complete comfort, both internal stirring from previous comfort and actions done. I, having conceived, give birth to falls. And those born give birth again to death through despair. If you know my and your manifest and deep weakness, you have bound my hands. If you afflict the throat, you have bound my feet so that I do not walk far. If you are yoked to obedience, you have been unyoked from me. If you have acquired humility, you have cut off my head.”

The fifteenth reward is for the one who, being in the flesh, has taken this and died, and rose, revealing the prelude of the future incorruptibility already from here.

Step 16 On Avarice

Many wise teachers have placed the present multi-headed demon of avarice right after the aforementioned tyrant. Therefore, so that we, the unwise, do not disrupt the order of the wise, we will adhere to this standard and rule. So, if it pleases, let us first briefly discuss the illness, and then shortly address the cure.

Avarice is the worship of idols, the daughter of unbelief, the excuse for infirmities, the forecaster of old age, the harbinger of famines, and the cause of droughts. The avaricious person is a mocker of the Gospels and a willing transgressor. Whoever has acquired love has scattered wealth, but the one who claims to live with both is deceiving himself. The one who mourns has renounced his own body, and even when opportunity calls, he does not spare it. Do not speak of gathering for the sake of the poor, for two mites bought the kingdom. Hospitality and avarice have met each other; the latter has called the former foolish. The one who has conquered this passion has cut off worries, but the one who is bound by it will never pray purely. The beginning of avarice is the pretext of charity, but its end is hatred for the poor. Until he has gathered, he shows himself as merciful; but when wealth is present, he closes his hands. I have seen the poor become rich in material things while living in the spiritual poverty of the kingdom, and they forgot their former poverty. A money-loving monk is free from sloth, constantly recalling the apostolic saying, "He who does not work, let him not eat." And, "My hands have served me and those with me."

The sixteenth struggle is the one in which, if victorious, one either gains love or cuts off worry.

Step 17 On Poverty: The Path to Heaven

Poverty is the abandonment of concerns, the absence of worldly cares, a traveller unhindered, faith in the commandments, and free from sorrow. A monk who is poor in material possessions is a master of the world, entrusting his care to God and through faith, possessing all as servants. He does not speak to others about his needs but accepts what comes as if from the hand of the Lord. A poor worker is a son of detachment, regarding what he has as if it were nothing, considering all things as refuse when the time comes for withdrawal. If he grieves over anything, he has not yet become truly poor. A poor man is pure in prayer, while the lover of possessions prays to images of material things. Those who live in obedience are strangers to avarice, for having surrendered even their bodies, what else remains that is their own? Such people are naturally inclined to be wronged, being always ready and willing to move on. I have seen material things breed patience in monks in certain places, but I have more greatly admired those who draw near to God. The one who has tasted the things above easily despises the things below, while the one who has not tasted them rejoices in possessions. A foolish poor man is doubly wronged, for he abstains from the present and is deprived of the future. Let us not, therefore, monks, be found less trusting than the birds, for they neither worry nor gather. Great is the one who piously renounces wealth, but holy is the one who renounces his own will. The former will receive a hundredfold, either through material things or spiritual gifts; the latter will inherit eternal life. Waves do not cease in the sea, nor do anger and sorrow depart from the avaricious. The one who has despised material things is freed from excuses and arguments, but the lover of possessions struggles to the death over a mere stick. Unwavering faith cuts off concerns, and the remembrance of death renounces even the body. In Job, there was not a trace of avarice; thus, when he was deprived, he remained calm. The root of all evil is said to be avarice, for it produces hatred, thefts, envy, separations, enmities, disturbances, grudges, heartlessness, and murders. With a little fire, some have consumed much material, and with a small virtue, they have fled all present and spoken passions. This is called detachment. Detachment is born of the experience and knowledge of God, and the concern for the account of one's departure. This concept is not unknown to the one who has read with attention about the mother of all evil. It is said that the stone of insensitivity is the second offspring of her wicked and cursed offspring. I have been prevented from assigning to it its proper place by the many-headed serpent of idolatry. I do not know how it came to be considered third among the discerning fathers in the chain of the eight vices. Having dealt with it accordingly, we now wish to speak further about insensitivity, which follows the second vice in its genesis, and then about sleep and vigilance. But let us also briefly address childish and cowardly fear, as these are introductory illnesses.

The one who has gained the seventeenth victory walks towards heaven without hindrance.

Step 18 On insensibility, that is, deadening of the soul and the death of the mind before the death of the body

Insensitivity, whether of the body or the spirit, is a deadened sense, resulting from chronic illness and negligence, leading ultimately to numbness. It is a state of disregard turned into apathy, a numbing of the mind, born of habit, a snare for zeal, a trap for courage, ignorance of compunction, a doorway to despair, the mother of forgetfulness, and after childbirth, the mother of her own mother: fearlessness. The insensible person is a foolish philosopher, self-condemned interpreter, contradictor of himself, a blind teacher of sight. He speaks about the healing of a wound while continually scratching it, he speaks against a passion but does not cease indulging in what harms him. He prays against it and immediately returns to its work. He becomes angry at his own actions but does not feel shame at his words. He cries out that he is doing wrong, yet zealously continues. His mouth prays against it, but his body fights for it. He philosophizes about death but lives as though he were immortal. He groans about separation but slumbers as though eternal. He speaks of temperance but struggles for gluttony. He reads about judgment but begins to smile about vanity, and even in the very act of reading, he is vain. He speaks eagerly about vigilance but quickly plunges himself into sleep. He praises prayer but flees from it as from a whip. He blesses obedience but is the first to disobey. He praises those who are detached, yet quarrels and holds grudges over a rag without shame. He is embittered in his anger and then becomes angry again over his bitterness, and adding defeat to defeat, he does not realize it. Sated, he repents, but soon adds to his indulgence again. He praises silence but talks incessantly about it. He teaches meekness, but even in teaching, he often becomes angry, and over his bitterness, he becomes angry again. When he recovers, he sighs and shakes his head but soon returns to his passion. He condemns laughter, yet smiles while teaching about mourning. He accuses himself of vanity over some things, but through his accusations, he gains praise for himself. He looks at a face with passion and speaks about chastity. He praises those who live in the world while remaining detached, yet does not realize his own shame. He honours the famous but despises the poor. He is always accusing himself but refuses to come to self-awareness, if I might not say, is unable to do so.

I have seen many such people who have heard about the afterlife and terrifying judgments, and they even wept. Yet, while tears were still in their eyes, they eagerly went to the table. And I marvelled at how this mistress of sorrow and storehouse of stench was able to reverse the compunction, fortified by her great insensitivity. As much as I have been able to understand the simple force of this stony, harsh, and foolish fury, I have exposed its tricks and bruises. I cannot endure long discourses with it. But whoever can in the Lord experiment with remedies for these bruises, let him not hesitate. I am not ashamed to confess my weakness in this, as I am strongly held by it. I was not even able to understand its tricks and devices from within, except when I managed to overpower it by force. And having tortured it with the aforementioned methods, I made it confess by flogging it with the fear of the Lord and unceasing prayer. Therefore, the tyrant and evildoer said: "Those who accompany me are all stony, hard, and dark. They stand at the holy table insensibly, and those who partake of the gift regard it as merely bread. When I see them moved to compunction, I mock them. All the good things born of courage and desire, I have learned to kill by the one who begot me. I am the mother of laughter, the nurse of sleep, the friend of excess, and I entwine myself with false piety. I do not feel pain when reproved. I, the wretch, amazed at the words of this fury, asked what the name of its begetter was, wanting to learn it. She replied, 'I do not have a single birth. I am a mixed and unstable offspring. Satiety strengthens me, time increases me, and evil habit makes me firm. Whoever holds onto that will never be free from me. Persevere in thinking about eternal judgment in much vigilance, and perhaps I will loosen my grip on you a little. Consider how I am born within you and strive against my mother. For I am not the same in everyone. Pray often in tombs. Engrave an indelible image of them in your heart. For if you do not inscribe it with the pen of fasting, you will never overcome me.'"

Step 19 On sleep, prayer, and psalm-singing in chapel

Sleep is somewhat like an image of death in nature, a state of inactivity of the senses. Although sleep is singular in its form, it has many causes, similar to how desires arise. I speak of sleep caused by nature, by food, by demons, or perhaps even from excessive fasting, where the body, weakened, seeks to comfort itself through sleep, much like how habitual drunkenness is linked to overindulgence. So too is the case with excessive sleep. Therefore, especially at the beginning of one’s spiritual obedience, let us strive against it, for it is difficult to cure a long-standing habit.

Let us observe and we shall find that when the spiritual trumpet sounds visibly, gathering the brothers, it also invisibly summons the enemies. This is why, upon rising from our beds, they suggest that we lay down again. "Wait," they say, "until the introductory hymns are completed, and then you may go to the church." They cause those standing in prayer to sink into sleep. Some incite the belly to discomfort by pricking it unusually hard. Others suggest making casual conversations in the Lord’s house. Some introduce shameful thoughts into the mind. Others suggest leaning against the wall as if to rest. Some cause laughter during prayer, intending to provoke God’s wrath against us through that laughter. Others rush us through the psalms out of laziness, while others encourage a more leisurely pace out of self-indulgence. There are times when they sit at our mouths, making them closed and difficult to open.

The one who considers himself to be standing before God, in a state of awareness in his heart, will be found like a pillar in prayer, unmoved by any of the aforementioned distractions. The truly obedient often suddenly become radiant and joyful during prayer, having been prepared by untainted service and pre-fired by the discipline of the fighter. It is possible for all to pray with the multitude, but it is fitting for most to pray with just one kindred spirit. Solitary prayer is the province of very few. When singing with a multitude, you cannot pray immaterially. Let the contemplation of the sayings of the hymns serve as a means of engaging the mind, or alternatively, let a specific prayer be kept in readiness as the next verse is awaited. It is fitting for no one to engage in any other task during prayer, as that would be counterproductive. This was clearly taught by the angel who appeared to the great Anthony. The furnace tests gold, and the presence at prayer tests the zeal and love for God in monks.

Blessed is the work of one who has acquired this, for he draws near to God and escapes from demons.

Step 20 On Physical Vigilance: How it Leads to Spiritual Vigilance and the Proper Way to Practice It

For earthly kings, some subjects appear before them unarmed and naked, others holding staffs, shields, or swords. The difference between the leading figures and the lesser ones is immense and incomparable; the former are closely related to the king, even his kin. Let us also examine how we present ourselves before God, our King, during evening, day, and night prayers and vigils.

Some, during the evening vigil, stand with hands outstretched in prayer, stripped of all worldly concerns, as if immaterial and free from all cares. Others are engaged in chanting psalms during their vigil. Some persist in reading, while others, due to physical weakness, bravely battle sleep through manual labour. There are also those who, by meditating on death, seek to stir their souls to compunction.

Among all these, the first and the last group endure in their vigil with divine favour. The second group walks the path of the monk, while the third takes the most humble way. Nevertheless, God receives and considers the offerings according to the intention and ability of each. A vigilant eye purifies the mind; excessive sleep hardens the soul, and a sleepy person is its companion.

Vigilance breaks the bonds of temptation, liberates from disturbing dreams, softens the eyes with tears, and the heart becomes tender. It guards thoughts, digests food, tames passions, disciplines the tongue, and drives away fantasies. A vigilant monk is a fisherman of thoughts, able to perceive and capture them easily in the calm of the night. A God-loving monk, when hearing the trumpet of prayer, exclaims, "Well done, well done," while the slothful monk laments, "Woe is me, woe is me!"

The preparation of the table tests the gluttonous, while the labour of prayer tests the God-lovers. The glutton rejoices at the sight of the table, while the latter frowns. Much sleep is a cause of forgetfulness; vigilance purifies the memory. Farmers gather wealth in barns and winepresses, while wealth and knowledge are gathered by monks during evening and night vigils and the work of the mind.

Excessive sleep is an unjust partner, robbing the slothful of half or more of their lives. A monk untested in vigilance may find his eyes heavy when the hour of prayer arrives. A lazy monk excels in idle talk, but when reading begins, he is unable to keep his eyes open due to sleep.

When the trumpet of the resurrection sounds, the dead will rise; likewise, when idle talk ceases, the sleepy will awaken. The tyrant is a deceitful friend of sleep. Often, when we are full, he retreats, but he fiercely attacks when we are hungry and thirsty.

It is suggested that we hold some work in our hands during prayer, for nothing else can destroy the prayer of the vigilant. The demon of sloth first enters the battle to induce laziness from the beginning or to prepare the way for the demon of fornication. Until we are freed from this, let us not refrain from singing with the multitude, for often, out of shame, we do not fall asleep.

The dog is hostile to hares, and the demon of vainglory is hostile to sleep. After the day, the lazy one is content, but after chanting psalms, the diligent worker counts his profit. Observe carefully after prayer while being sober, and you will see swarms of demons as if they were fought off by us during prayer, now trying to ensnare us with absurd fantasies.

Watch and see those who habitually snatch away the first fruits of the soul. It is possible, after sleep, to meditate continuously on the words of the psalms. Yet there are times when demons suggest this to us to lead us to pride. I did not wish to speak of this third aspect, but someone compelled me to speak. A soul that meditates continuously on the Lord's word by day tends to dwell on it even during sleep. Indeed, this is the true reward of the former, serving as a defense against spirits and fantasies.

Step 21 On Cowardice

If you practice virtue within a community or in a group, you are not naturally prone to be strongly attacked by cowardice. However, if you struggle in a more solitary and quiet place, beware that cowardice, born of vainglory and a daughter of disbelief, may take hold of you. Cowardice is the infantile nature of an old, vainglorious soul. It is the abandonment of faith in the anticipation of unexpected events. Fear is the anticipation of danger. Alternatively, fear can be a trembling sensation in the heart, disturbed and anxious about unknown misfortunes. Fear is the lack of confidence.

A proud soul is a servant of cowardice, relying on itself and dreading the sounds and shadows of created things. Those who mourn and are indifferent to worldly things do not possess cowardice. Often, those who are cowardly remain in a state of stupor, and rightfully so. For the Lord justly abandons the proud so that the rest of us may learn not to exalt ourselves. All cowards are vainglorious, though not all who are free from cowardice are humble, for even thieves and grave robbers are sometimes fearless.

In places where you are accustomed to being frightened, do not hesitate to be present at an unusual hour. If you waver a little, this childish and ridiculous passion will grow old with you. As you walk, arm yourself with prayer. When you arrive, stretch out your hands, and with the name of Jesus, strike your enemies. There is no stronger weapon in heaven and on earth. Once you are freed from this disease, praise the One who delivered you. For by giving thanks always, He will protect you forever.

You will never be able to satisfy your stomach completely, just as you cannot easily overcome cowardice. According to the measure of your mourning, cowardice will quickly retreat, and in proportion to the lack of it, we remain cowardly. "My hair stood on end, and my flesh trembled," said Eliphaz, describing the cunning of the demon. Sometimes the soul, and sometimes the body, becomes fearful and transfers this passion to the other. When the flesh becomes fearful, if the irrational fear does not enter the soul, the release from the disease is near. When we are truly freed from cowardice, darkness and desolation of places will not empower the demons against us, but the barrenness of our soul might.

There are times when this fear serves as a beneficial lesson in God's providence. The servant of the Lord will fear only his own Master. But he who has not yet learned to fear Him is often frightened by his own shadow. When an invisible spirit approaches, it strikes fear into the body; but when an angel approaches, the humble soul rejoices. Therefore, recognizing the presence from its effect, let us quickly leap into prayer, for our good guardian has come to pray with us.

Step 22 On the Many Forms of Vanity

Some scholars prefer to define vanity as a distinct vice, separate from pride. Therefore, they say that there are eight primary and ruling thoughts of evil. However, theologian Gregory and others have reduced these to seven, and I am inclined to agree with them. For who has ever conquered vanity and still possesses pride? These two vices differ only as much as a child differs from an adult, or as wheat differs from bread. The former is the beginning, and the latter is the end. Therefore, when the time calls for it, let us briefly speak of the beginning and the fulfillment of the passion of unholy conceit. To philosophize at length about these things is like uselessly measuring the wind.

Vanity, in its nature, is a distortion of character and a perversion of morals. In its quality, it is the scattering of efforts, the loss of sweat, the ambush of treasure, the offspring of disbelief, the forerunner of pride, a shipwreck in a harbor, an ant in a threshing floor—tiny, yet undermining every effort and fruit. Just as an ant waits for the grain to be gathered, vanity waits for the wealth to be accumulated. The ant rejoices to steal, while vanity rejoices to scatter. The spirit of despair rejoices in the increase of evil, while the spirit of vanity rejoices in the increase of virtue. The door to despair is the multitude of wounds, while the door to vanity is the wealth of labours.

Observe, and you will find vanity flourishing even at the grave, in clothes, ointments, processions, and other things. The sun shines generously on all, and vanity rejoices in every activity. Consider what I mean: I fast, and vanity boasts. I break my fast in secret, and vanity boasts again as being wise. I wear splendid clothes, and I am defeated by vanity. I change into humble attire, and vanity boasts once more. I speak, and vanity defeats me. I remain silent, and vanity defeats me again. No matter how you throw away this thorn, its point stands upright. The vain person is an idolater, pretending to worship God but truly seeking to please people rather than God. The vain person is a show-off. The fasting of the vain is without reward, and their prayer is untimely, for both are done for the praise of people.

The ascetic who is vain suffers doubly, both exhausting the body and receiving no reward. Who would not laugh at the worker of vanity standing in psalmody, moved by vanity, sometimes laughing, sometimes prepared to weep over everything? Often the Lord hides from our eyes the good that we have done, while a flatterer, or rather a deceiver, opens our eyes through praise, and once our eyes are opened, the wealth of our labours vanishes.

A flatterer is a servant of demons, a guide to pride, a destroyer of contrition, an obliterator of good, a deceiver of the way. "Those who bless you are leading you astray," says the prophet. It is the high virtue of the saints and holy ones to endure insult bravely and gladly. I have seen those who mourn being praised, and they became angry, turning their passion into a public celebration. "No one knows the thoughts of a person except the spirit within them." Let those who attempt to praise others to their faces be ashamed and silenced.

When you hear that your neighbor or friend has insulted you, either in your absence or presence, then show your love by praising them. It is a great thing to rid oneself of the praise of people from the soul. It is even greater to cast off the praise of demons. Humility is shown not by belittling oneself (for how can one not bear oneself?), but by being insulted by another and diminishing their love.

I have noticed the demon of vanity suggesting thoughts to one brother and revealing them to another, encouraging one to speak of his innermost feelings to another and making the other admire him as if he were a prophet. Sometimes, the unholy one even touches the body, causing it to tremble. Do not accept him when he suggests to you an office of oversight, leadership, or teaching. It is as difficult to drive a dog away from a butcher's table as it is to resist the suggestions of vanity.

As soon as you find yourself in a peaceful state, vanity immediately urges you to leave the desert and go into the world to save souls. "Go," it says, "for the salvation of perishing souls." Vanity assumes different forms in different places: one way in a monastery, another among those in the desert. Vanity precedes the appearance of guests in the monastery and prompts the lighter monks to go out to meet them. It prepares them to bow before the guests and humbly act, full of pride. Vanity softens the voice and demeanour, and the hands of visitors are watched carefully, as they are called lords and benefactors, second only to God for granting life. At the table, vanity encourages moderation, while mercilessly criticizing those of lower rank. Vanity makes the sluggish monks stand firm in psalmody, the mute become eloquent, and the sleepy become vigilant.

It flatters the monk on the canon, begging him to be given the first place, calling him father and teacher, until the guests have left. Vanity makes the honoured proud and the slighted resentful. Vanity, instead of honour, often becomes the cause of dishonour. When its disciples become angry, vanity brings them great shame. Vanity has made the sharp-tongued meek and the meek in spirit bold with natural gifts.

I have seen a demon grieve his own brother and drive him away. When a brother was once angry, the presence of the world caught him in the act, and the wretched one was sold to vanity out of anger. He could not serve both equally. The one sold to vanity lives a double life: in appearance, he lives with monks, but in mind and thought, he lives in the world. If we strive to please the Lord above, we will certainly taste the glory above. And whoever has tasted that glory will despise everything earthly. I wonder if anyone, after tasting the first, did not despise the second. Often, having been robbed by vanity, we turned back and more skillfully robbed it.

I have seen some begin spiritual work out of vanity, but when their initial efforts faltered, their end was praiseworthy, for they changed their intention. The one who takes pride in natural gifts—quick wit, intelligence, learning, eloquence, and such things that come to us without effort—will never encounter the blessings that are beyond nature. For the one who is unfaithful in little is also unfaithful in much and vain as well. Through supreme dispassion, the wealth of gifts, the working of miracles, and the power of foresight, some wear their bodies out foolishly, unaware that it is not effort but rather humility that is the mother of such things.

The one who considers themselves indebted will receive unexpected and sudden wealth. Do not trust the sifter who suggests that virtues should be displayed for the benefit of the listeners. "What profit is there if one gains the whole world but loses oneself?" Nothing is more beneficial than humility and unpretentious behaviour, which can edify those who observe it. It also becomes a safeguard for others never to be exalted. What greater benefit could there be?

Someone who has the ability to see has observed and described this: "As I was sitting in council, the demons of vanity and pride came and sat beside me, one on each side. One touched my side with his vain finger, urging me to speak about some insight or work I had done in the desert. When I shook him off, saying, 'Let those who seek my ruin be turned back in shame,' the one on my left quickly whispered in my ear, 'Well done, well done! You have become great by overcoming my most impudent mother.' To this, I responded promptly with the next verse, saying, 'May those who say to me, "Well done, well done!" be turned back in shame.'"

When I asked how vanity is the mother of pride, the answer was given: "Praise exalts and inflates. When the soul is exalted, pride takes it up to the heavens and brings it down to the depths." There is a glory that comes from the Lord: "For those who honour me, I will honour," He says. And there is a glory that follows from the devil's preparation. "Woe to you," He says, "when all people speak well of you."

Recognizing the first clearly, when you regard it as harmful and avoid it by all means, wherever you go, hide your way of life. The second occurs when you do something, even something trivial, to be seen by others, and the filthy one prompts us to adopt a virtue that is not ours. For He says, "Let your light so shine before others that they may see your good deeds," encouraging us. The Lord often turns those who are vain into humility through dishonour that befalls them.

The beginning of freedom from vanity is guarding the mouth and loving dishonour. The middle stage is to cut off all thoughts that foster vanity. The end (if there is an end to an abyss) is to pursue what leads to dishonour without even noticing. Do not hide your shame for fear of seeming to give offense. As for the form of the error, perhaps you should not always use the same remedy. Sometimes we invite glory, and at other times it comes to us uninvited. When we undertake some task with vanity, let us remember our own mourning and the fear of standing in our private prayer. This will certainly shame the brazen one if we truly care for genuine prayer. If not, let us quickly recall our own departure.

If not, then at least let us fear the shame that follows vanity.

"For whoever exalts themselves will certainly be humbled," both here and before the future age. When those who praise us, or rather deceive us, begin to praise us, let us quickly recall the multitude of our sins, and we will find ourselves unworthy of the things said or done. There are indeed vain people whose petitions God is bound to answer, but the Lord tends to prevent such prayers and requests, lest they add to their conceit through prayer.

Not all the simplest people are naturally prone to fall into this poison. Vanity is the loss of simplicity and a feigned manner of life. A worm, having grown wings, is lifted up into the air, and vanity, when fully grown, gives birth to pride, the beginning and the end of all evils.

The one who has overcome this disease is near to salvation, and they will soon be seen close to the glory of the saints.

Step 23 On Mad Pride and Concerning the Unclean Thoughts of Blasphemy

Pride is the denial of God, a device of demons, the scorn of men, the mother of condemnation, the offspring of praise, the mark of fruitlessness, the driving away of God's help, the forerunner of spiritual blindness, the cause of seizures, the fountain of anger, the door of hypocrisy, the support of demons, the guardian of sins, the cause of heartlessness, the ignorance of compassion, a bitter critic, an inhuman judge, an adversary of God, the root of blasphemy, the beginning of pride, and the end of vanity.

The midpoint of pride is the contempt of others, the shameless flaunting of one's own labours, praise in the heart, and the hatred of reproof. The end of pride is the denial of God's help, the exaltation of one's own efforts, and a diabolical character.

Let us all listen, those of us who wish to escape the pit of this vice. Pride often feeds on the very nourishment of gratitude. It does not immediately suggest denying God outright. I have seen people give thanks to God with their mouths while boasting in their hearts, as clearly exemplified by the Pharisee who said, "God, I thank you" . Where there is a fall, pride has already taken up residence. It is the herald of the fall.

Let me offer twelve instances where dishonour can manifest. I heard someone, who had been honoured, say, "If you love the feeling of pride, even in one of these instances, it will replace the other eleven."

A high-minded monk fiercely contradicts others. A humble monk does not know how to argue at all. Just as a cypress does not bend to the ground, neither can a proud monk attain obedience. A proud man desires to rule, for without ruling, he cannot meet his end, or rather, he does not wish to meet it. "God resists the proud." So who can have mercy on them? Every high-hearted person is unclean before the Lord, and who can cleanse such a person?

The punishment for pride is a fall, and a demon is a thorn. Abandonment is spiritual blindness. The former (a fall) has often been healed by human means, but the latter (spiritual blindness) is incurable by human hands. The one who rejects reproof signifies the presence of the passion. The one who runs to it has been freed from the bond.

If anyone fell from the heavens solely due to pride, without any other passion, it is worth considering whether one can ascend to the heavens solely by humility, without any other virtue. Pride is the loss of wealth and the fruit of labour. They cried out, but there was no one to save them, because they cried out to the Lord with pride, and He did not listen to them, because they did not cut off the causes for which they prayed.

An elder, very knowledgeable in spiritual matters, once admonished a proud brother. The blind brother replied, "Forgive me, Father, but I am not proud." The wise elder then said to him, "What clearer evidence of this passion could you give us than to say, 'I am not proud'?"

With those who suffer from pride, obedience often comes along, along with a coarser and more dishonourable way of life, and reading about the supernatural accomplishments of the Fathers. Perhaps in this way, there is some small hope of salvation for the sick. It is shameful to be arrogant over the external appearance, but ultimate folly to be boastful over the gifts of God. Exalt yourself only over the achievements made before your birth, for those after it were given by God, just as your birth was.

Whatever virtues you have achieved outside of your mind, these alone are truly yours, for the mind itself was given to you by God. Whatever rewards you have demonstrated outside of the body were achieved solely through your own effort, for the body is not yours, but God's creation.

Do not be confident until you receive the final verdict, seeing even that one who was bound hand and foot and cast into the outer darkness after reclining at the wedding banquet. Do not lift your head high, for you are of the earth. Many who were holy and incorporeal were cast down from the heavens. When the demon takes hold of his workers, he may then appear to them in dreams or even in waking hours, disguised as a holy angel or a martyr, revealing mysteries or bestowing gifts, so that the unfortunate ones, deceived, completely lose their minds.

Even if we endure countless deaths for Christ, we still have not fulfilled our duty. There is a different worth between the blood of God and the blood of servants, based on merit and not on substance. Let us always examine and judge ourselves against the Fathers and the luminaries who came before us, and we will find that we have not even set foot on the path of exact righteousness. Nor have we kept our vows holily, but continue to live in worldly ways.

A true monk is one who has an unperturbed gaze of the soul and an immovable sense of the body. A monk is one who calls the enemies, like beasts, to himself, provoking them as they flee from him. A monk is one who is constantly in a state of spiritual rapture and sorrowful life. A monk is one who delights in virtues as others do in pleasures. A monk is a ceaseless light in the eye of the heart. A monk is an abyss of humility, into which every spirit is cast down and drowned. Pride produces forgetfulness of sins, for the memory of sins is the source of humility.

Pride is the ultimate poverty of the soul. The miserable one imagines that wealth in darkness is progress, but it not only prevents advancement but also throws one down from the heights. Pride is a pomegranate rotting from within, yet outwardly shining in the light. A proud monk does not need a demon; he has become a demon and enemy to himself. Darkness is alien to light, and pride is alien to every virtue. In the hearts of the proud, words of blasphemy are born; in the souls of the humble, heavenly visions. A thief despises the sun, and the proud man scorns the meek.

I do not know how, but many find themselves full of pride. They imagine themselves to be dispassionate, yet at the end, they see their own poverty. The one caught in this will pray to the Lord, for in vain is the salvation of man with Him. I have caught the headless one wandering in my heart, riding on the shoulders of her mother. Binding her with the chain of obedience and scourging her with the whip of humility, I examined their entry into me and said, "We have neither beginning nor birth, for we have become the beginning and mother of all passions. We are not accustomed to being ruled by anything, which is why we, who once ruled in heaven, have rebelled. Therefore, we are the mothers of all those who oppose humility."

Pride leads to all sorts of disasters and failures in spiritual life. When a person sees visions of angels or saints appearing in dreams, claiming to reveal mysteries or bestow gifts, they must be wary of deception. The devil can disguise himself as an angel of light, and pride often follows such visions, leading to complete loss of spiritual sanity.

If we endure many sufferings for Christ, it still does not repay the debt we owe Him. God's blood is different in value from the blood of His servants. Constant self-examination and comparison with the Fathers and saints who came before us will reveal how far we are from their righteous way of life. True monasticism is an unshakable gaze of the soul, an immovable sense of the body, a constant state of spiritual rapture, and humility so profound that it casts down and drowns all spirits of pride.

The memory of sins fosters humility, while pride produces forgetfulness of sins. Pride is the final poverty of the soul. It imagines itself to be progressing in darkness, but it not only fails to progress but also casts one down from the heights. Pride is like a pomegranate rotting from within while shining outwardly. A proud monk needs no demon, for he has become a demon to himself. The words of blasphemy are born in the hearts of the proud, while heavenly visions are granted to the humble.

Many who imagine themselves to be dispassionate are, at the end, confronted with their own spiritual poverty. The one who has fallen into this trap will cry out to the Lord, for in vain is the salvation of man with Him. I have seen this headless pride roaming in my heart, riding on the shoulders of her mother. Binding them with the chain of obedience and scourging them with the whip of humility, I examined their entrance into me and said, "We have no beginning or birth, for we have become the beginning and mother of all passions. We are not accustomed to being ruled by anything, which is why we, who once ruled in heaven, have rebelled."

Thus, humility and constant vigilance are the only remedies against the disease of pride, which, if left unchecked, will lead to spiritual ruin. The way to conquer pride is through genuine self-reproach and humility before God, recognizing that all our virtues and accomplishments are gifts from Him and not our own doing. True victory over pride leads to spiritual freedom and the grace to ascend to the glory of the saints.

Concerning Unmentionable Blasphemous Thoughts

It has been heard from those who have advanced in spiritual warfare that there is a most terrible offspring of a terrible root and mother, which I speak of as the unspeakable offspring of blasphemy born of the vile pride. Therefore, it is necessary to bring this matter to light. For this enemy and adversary is not just any common foe, but one much more difficult than all others, and what is even more troubling is that it cannot easily be expressed, confessed, or revealed to a spiritual physician. As a result, it has often led many into despair and hopelessness. Just as a worm eats away at wood, this unholy one consumes all their hope.

This vile and utterly wicked demon often delights in profaning the Lord and the holy mysteries, even during sacred assemblies and at the very moment of the awesome mysteries. Hence, we clearly understand that it is not our soul that speaks these impious, incomprehensible, and unspeakable words within us, but rather the misotheistic demon who fled from heaven, for even there he tried to cast blasphemies against the Lord.

If those thoughts and words were truly mine, how could I receive the gift with reverence? How could I both blaspheme and bless? This deceiver and soul-destroyer has often driven many into a state of mental confusion. No other thought is so difficult to express as this one, and for this reason, it has often aged alongside many people. For nothing gives the demons and thoughts more power over us than hiding them in our hearts and keeping them secret.

Let no one consider themselves responsible for blasphemous thoughts. The Lord is the knower of hearts and knows that such words are not ours but those of our enemies. Just as drunkenness is the cause of stumbling, and pride is the cause of improper thoughts, the one who stumbles is not at fault, but the one who causes drunkenness will certainly be punished.

When we stand in prayer, those unclean and ineffable thoughts arise, and as soon as the prayer is completed, they immediately retreat. For they do not wish to fight with those who do not fight against them. The ungodly one blasphemes not only the Divine and all things holy, but also utters the most shameful and improper words within us, intending to either make us abandon our prayer or drive us to despair. He has caused many to cease praying and led others to withdraw from the mysteries. Some have wasted away their bodies with sorrow, and others have been crushed by this wicked and inhuman tyrant through fasting, without any relief. He has worked this not only among those in the world but also among those living the monastic life, suggesting to them that they have no hope of salvation left and declaring them worse than all unbelievers and pagans.

The one troubled by the spirit of blasphemy and seeking to be freed from it must recognize accurately that his soul is not responsible for such thoughts, but rather the unclean demon who once said to the Lord, "All these things will I give you if you will fall down and worship me." Therefore, let us also despise him, and by no means measure ourselves by what he suggests, saying instead, "Get behind me, Satan, for I will worship the Lord my God and serve Him only." Your pain and your words will return upon your head, and your blasphemy will descend upon your crown in this age and in the age to come.

Anyone who tries to wrestle with the demon of blasphemy outside of the aforementioned method is like one trying to grasp lightning in his hands. How can one catch, oppose, or wrestle with something that passes through the heart as quickly as the wind and a fleeting thought, uttering words as swiftly as it appears and vanishing just as quickly? All other enemies stand and engage in combat, lingering and giving time to fight against them. But not this one; as soon as it appears, it withdraws, and as soon as it speaks, it departs.

This demon often resides in the minds of the simplest and most innocent, who are more disturbed and troubled by it than others. And so, we say primarily that this does not happen out of presumption but rather out of the envy of the demons. If we cease judging and condemning our neighbor, we will not fear blasphemous thoughts, for the root and cause of the latter is the former. Just as someone locked inside a house hears the words of those passing outside without uttering them himself, so too does the soul, remaining within itself, hear the blasphemies of the demon, who speaks through it as he passes by, and is troubled. The one who despises this demon is freed from the passion, but the one who tries to wrestle with it in any other way is doomed to fail. For the one who seeks to control spirits with words is like someone trying to trap the wind.

A zealous monk, troubled by this demon for twenty years, wore down his flesh with fasting and vigil, and when he realized that he had gained no benefit, he wrote down the passion on a piece of paper and handed it to a holy man, lying prostrate and unable to lift his head. When the elder read it, he smiled and, raising the brother, said to him, "Place your hand on my neck, brother." And when the brother had done so, the great elder said, "This sin, brother, for as many years as it has existed and will continue to exist in you, shall be upon my neck, only do not measure it anymore." And it was confirmed that before he had even left the elder's cell, the passion had vanished more quickly. Having experienced this, he told me, giving thanks to Christ.

He who has gained victory over this passion has cast away pride.

Step 24 On Gentleness, Simplicity, Innocence, and the Craftiness of Deception

Just as the morning light precedes the sun, so too does gentleness precede all humility. Therefore, let us listen to the words of the Light, who arranges them in this order: "Learn from me," He says, "for I am gentle and humble in heart." Thus, it is necessary to be enlightened about gentleness before daring to gaze directly at the Sun, for it is impossible to behold the latter without first knowing the former, as the true state of affairs teaches.

Gentleness is an unwavering state of mind, maintaining the same disposition in both honour and dishonour. Gentleness is being able to pray sincerely and without disturbance for those who trouble you. Gentleness is a rock above the sea of anger, dissolving all the waves that crash against it and remaining unmoved. Gentleness is the support of patience, the door of love, or rather its mother, and the foundation of discernment. As it is said, "The Lord will teach the meek His ways," it is the source of forgiveness, boldness in prayer, and the dwelling place of the Holy Spirit. "Upon whom will I look," says the Lord, "but upon the gentle and quiet one?" Gentleness is the collaborator of obedience, the guide of brotherhood, the bridle of the mad, the cessation of anger, and the giver of joy. It is the imitation of Christ, the characteristic of angels, the bond of demons, and the shield against bitterness.

The Lord rests in the hearts of the gentle, but a troubled soul is the throne of the devil. "The meek shall inherit the earth," or rather, they shall conquer it. But the wrathful are driven out of their land. A gentle soul is the throne of simplicity, while an angry mind is the creator of evil. A gentle soul will receive words of wisdom, for "the Lord will guide the meek in judgment," or rather, in discernment. A straightforward soul is the companion of humility, while an evil soul is the companion of pride. Gentle souls will be filled with knowledge, but an angry mind dwells with darkness and ignorance. The angry and deceitful meet, but no straight word is found in their discourse. If you open the heart of the former, you will find madness; if you search the soul of the latter, you will see wickedness.

Simplicity is an unadorned habit of the soul, unmoved by malice. Wickedness is knowledge, or rather, demonic disgrace, devoid of truth and appearing to deceive many. Hypocrisy is the contrary state of body and soul, entangled in all thoughts. Innocence is a cheerful state of soul, free from any deceitful thought. Straightforwardness is a mind free from complexity, an unfeigned character, and unplanned, sincere speech. The innocent person is a pure soul by nature, engaging with everyone as one who was created in such a way. Wickedness is a deviation from straightforwardness, a deceived mind, false wisdom, twisted oaths, tangled words, an abyss of the heart, a depth of deceit, an accomplished lie, pride by nature, the adversary of humility, the hypocrisy of repentance, the mockery of sorrow, the enmity of confession, self-will, the cause of ruin, the opposition to resurrection, the smile of insults, a practiced gloom, a feigned piety, and a demonic way of life.

The wicked person is the namesake and companion of the devil. Therefore, the Lord has taught us to call him by this name, saying, "Deliver us from the evil one." Let us flee from the precipice of hypocrisy and from the pit of treachery, hearing Him who says, "Those who do evil shall be cut off," and "They shall quickly wither like the green herb." For such people are the pasture of demons. Just as God is called love, so too is He called straightforwardness. Therefore, the wise man in the Song of Songs says to the pure-hearted, "Straightforwardness has loved you." And again, his father says, "The Lord is good and upright." And He says that those who are like Him in this way will be saved, for "He saves the upright in heart." And again, "He saw straightforwardness in them and visited their countenance."

The first mark of a child's growth is simplicity, unadorned; when Adam possessed this, he did not see the nakedness of his soul nor the shame of his flesh. Simplicity, naturally present in some, is good and blessed, but not as much as that which has been grafted through labour upon wickedness. For the latter is safeguarded by much vigilance and struggle, while the former is the cause of the highest humility and gentleness. The reward for the former is not great, but for the latter, it is exceedingly praised.

All who wish to draw the Lord to themselves should approach simply and without affectation, without adornment, and without artifice, as one would approach a teacher in their studies, without curiosity. For He is simple and without composition, and He desires those who come to Him to be simple and sincere. For one cannot truly observe simplicity without also observing humility. The wicked person is a false seer, imagining that he can grasp the thoughts of others from their words and gestures. I have seen straightforward people who have learned wickedness from the wicked, and I marvel at how they were able to lose the natural grace and advantage so quickly. Just as the straightforward easily slip into wickedness, so too the opposite find it difficult to be transformed.

True exile and obedience, along with the guarding of one's lips, have often and miraculously changed and healed the incurable. If knowledge puffs up many, might not simplicity and ignorance naturally humble them? A clear example and model for us of blessed simplicity is the thrice-blessed Paul, who was simple. For no one, no one has ever seen or heard or can see such progress in so short a time. A simple monk is a rational, obedient being, laying down his own burden entirely to the one who leads him. Just as an animal does not resist the one who binds it, so too does a straightforward soul not resist the one who oversees it. It follows the one who leads it and does not know how to oppose, even unto death. "It is difficult for the rich to enter the kingdom, and for the wise fools to enter simplicity." Often, a fall has brought great understanding, and involuntary humility and innocence have been granted as salvation.

Strive to deceive your own mind, and by doing so, you will find salvation and uprightness in Christ Jesus our Lord. Amen.

The mother of all evils is pleasure and wickedness, and those who possess it will not see the Lord. Nothing from the former abstinence will benefit us except the second.

Let us take the example of the fear of the Lord from the rulers and beasts. The example of the love toward God is the type of love for bodies. There is nothing to prevent us from making the examples of virtues even from their opposites.

This present generation has become gravely wicked, and is filled with arrogance and hypocrisy; showing bodily labours perhaps like our ancient fathers, but not deemed worthy of their gifts. Yet I believe that never has nature been in such need of gifts as now. And rightly so, for it is not by labours but by simplicity and humility that God reveals Himself. Even if the power of the Lord is perfected in weakness, nevertheless, the Lord will not prevent a humble labourer. Whenever we see one of our athletes suffering physically, let us not hasten to learn the judgment of his illness out of malice; rather, with simple and sincere love, let us receive and heal him as our own member and as a soldier struck in battle. There is an illness for the cleansing of sins, and there is one for the humbling of the mind. Our good and all-good Master and Lord, when He often sees some slackening in the ascetic life, then through illness, like through a more relaxed asceticism, humbles the flesh; sometimes, too, He cleanses the soul of evil thoughts and passions.

Everything that happens to us, whether visible or invisible, can be accepted either well or poorly, and moderately. I saw three brothers suffering a loss; one was indignant, the other remained untroubled, and the third received great joy. I saw among farmers one seed being sown by all, but each had his own goal. One to repay his debts, another to store up wealth for himself, another to honour the Lord with gifts, another to seek the praise of passersby on the road of life for his good deeds, another to afflict a jealous enemy, and another not to be reproached as idle by others. These are the names of the farmer’s seeds: fasting, vigil, almsgiving, service, and the like. The brothers will diligently examine the goals in the Lord, like drawing water from springs, sometimes unknowingly, and draw up the so-called frog as well. Thus, also, when we practice the virtues, often, while hidden, the vices are intertwined with them. For example, gluttony is intertwined with vainglory; love with fornication; discernment with cunning; prudence with wickedness; meekness with hypocrisy and sluggishness, laziness, obstinacy, eccentricity, and disobedience; silence with the weight of teaching; joy with arrogance; hope with laziness; love again with judgment; silence with acedia and laziness; purity with bitterness; humility with boldness; and to all these, as a common wreath, or rather poison, vainglory is attached. Let us not be grieved in asking the Lord for requests, even if we are not heard for years. For the Lord would have all men become dispassionate in a single moment. All those who ask for requests from God and do not receive them certainly fail for one of these reasons: either because they ask prematurely, or because they ask unworthily and vainly, or because receiving them, they would have become puffed up, or would have neglected thereafter once the request was granted.

That the demons depart and the passions from the soul, either at a certain time or continuously, no one, I believe, doubts. But from how many ways their departure occurs from us, few know. The passions have departed from some, not only from the faithful but also from the unbelievers, except for one, leaving that one to them as a foremost evil, filling the place of all, even if it has such harm as to cast one down from heaven. Matter is consumed by the sulphurous fire and uprooted from the soul. Demons withdraw willingly, preparing to catch one off guard, and suddenly snatch the wretched soul, and prepare it to be most grievously affected by the passions, as from henceforth it becomes self-destructive and self-opposing.

I also know another retreat of the beasts, after the soul has become entirely accustomed, and an example of this is infants; for after long habit, even without a breast, they suckle from their own fingers. I also know a fifth dispassion in the soul, still praised due to great simplicity and integrity. "For the help of the Lord is just to such, saving the upright in heart," and they are unconsciously delivered from evil, just as infants, having undressed, are not fully aware.

Wickedness or passion does not naturally grow within nature, for God is not the creator of passions. But virtues are naturally many within us, having come from Him, from which are also these clearly: mercy, if even the Greeks have sympathy; love, if even the irrational animals have often wept over each other's loss; faith, for we all produce this from ourselves; hope, for we hope to become rich by lending, sailing, and sowing.

If, therefore, as has been demonstrated, love is established in us by nature and as a virtue, and if it is the bond and fulfillment of the law, then it is evident that virtues are not far removed from our nature. Let those who excuse themselves by claiming weakness in practicing them be put to shame. Purity, freedom from anger, humility, prayer, vigilance, fasting, and continual contrition are beyond nature. For these things, men, angels, and the Word of God Himself have been established as teachers and givers. Comparing evils, we must choose the lighter. For example, many times when we are engaged in prayer, a brother comes to us; and we are faced with two choices: either to abandon the prayer or to send the brother away without response, thereby causing him sorrow. Greater than prayer is love, for the former is partial, while the latter encompasses all.

Once, when I was still young, and found myself in a city or village, I was caught up at a table by thoughts of gluttony and vanity. Fearing the offspring of gluttony, I was instead defeated by vanity. I have recognized the demon of gluttony many times as being defeated by the demon of vanity in young people; and rightly so: among those in the world, the root of all evils is avarice, while among monks, it is gluttony. Among the spiritually inclined, there are often some most trivial passions left by God for a purpose, so that through these trivial and relatively harmless things, they may acquire great self-control. It is not possible to acquire humility at the beginning while living without submission, for everyone who has learned a skill independently tends to be puffed up.

The Fathers define practical virtue in two general categories, and rightly so. The first category eliminates pleasures, and the second secures their elimination through humility. Therefore, mourning is twofold: it eliminates sin and produces humility. It is pious to give to everyone who asks, and even more pious to give to those who do not ask. As for not demanding back from one who takes, this is perhaps characteristic of those who are dispassionate and live in solitude.

Let us never cease examining ourselves in all passions and virtues, determining where we stand: at the beginning, in the middle, or at the end. All wars of the demons against us arise from three causes: from love of pleasure, pride, or their envy. The former two are useless until the end. There is a certain perception, or rather habit, called endurance, under which the one who is not afraid of labour will never turn back or flee from hardship. The souls of martyrs, being held by this renowned endurance, easily despised tortures. The guarding of thoughts is one thing, and the keeping of the mind is another; the latter is as far above the former as the east is from the west, even if it is more difficult. Praying against thoughts is one thing, but responding to them is another; to despise them and rise above them is yet another. The first way is testified by the one who said, "O God, hasten to help me," and the like. The second way is testified by the one who said, "I will answer those who reproach me with a word of refutation." And again, "You have made us a reproach to our neighbor’s." The third way is testified by the psalmist: "I became mute and did not open my mouth, and I set a guard for myself while the sinner was before me." And again: "The proud have transgressed exceedingly; but I have not turned aside from Your law."

The middle method often uses the first due to lack of preparation, while the first method is not yet strong enough to drive away enemies in the second manner; the third spits on all demons. It is naturally impossible for the incorporeal to be controlled by the corporeal, but all things are possible with God, who created them. Just as those who have a healthy sense of smell can detect the fragrance of someone holding perfume without realizing it, so a pure soul can perceive the fragrance it has acquired from God and the stench from which it has completely been freed in others, even without their awareness. Not everyone can become dispassionate, but it is not impossible for all to be saved and reconciled with God. Let not those foreigners, who desire to pry into the ineffable providences or visions of God in men, dominate you, subtly implying that the Lord is a respecter of persons. They are the offspring of arrogance, and they are known as such.

There is a demon of avarice who often pretends to be humble, and there is a demon of vanity who incites to charity, just as there is a demon of love of pleasure. If, therefore, we cleanse ourselves of both, let us not cease to be merciful in every place. Some have said that demons oppose each other; but I have known that all seek our destruction.

Every spiritual labour, whether seen or unseen, is preceded by a proper intention and an excellent desire, which, with the cooperation of God, bring them about. For if the former are not overthrown, the latter do not naturally follow. If there is a time for everything under the sun, as Ecclesiastes says, then let us also examine and seek what is proper for each time in all our holy endeavours. There is certainly a time for dispassion among those who are struggling, due to their spiritual infancy. There is a time for tears, and a time for hardness of heart; a time for submission, and a time for command; a time for fasting, and a time for partaking; a time for war, against the enemy of the body, and a time for the death of fervour; a time for the winter of the soul, and a time for the calm of the mind; a time for sorrow of the heart, and a time for spiritual joy; a time for teaching, and a time for listening; a time for defilement, perhaps through arrogance, and a time for purification, through humility; a time for struggle, and a time for secure ease; a time for quietness, and a time for undistracted distraction; a time for continual prayer, and a time for sincere service. Let us not, then, seek things prematurely, deceived by proud zeal; let us not seek summer things in winter; nor harvest things during sowing time, for there is a time to sow labours, and a time to reap the ineffable graces. Otherwise, we will not even obtain the appropriate things at the proper time. Some received the holy rewards of their labours from God before the toils, some during the toils, some after the toils, and some at the time of death, by a hidden economy. We should seek which of these is the most humble.

There is despair from the multitude of sins, the weight of conscience, and unbearable sorrow, because the soul, being overwhelmed by the multitude of wounds and their weight, is plunged into the abyss of despair. And there is despair that comes from pride and arrogance, when one considers himself unworthy of the fall that has occurred. The observer will find this characteristic in both: one gives himself up to indifference, while the other holds onto despair in his asceticism, which is incongruous. But self-control and hopefulness heal the former, while humility and refraining from judging others heal the latter.

We should not be amazed or surprised to see some people performing evil deeds while speaking good words; for even the serpent in paradise was lost due to arrogance, having exalted itself. Let this be a model and rule for you in all your endeavours, whether seen or unseen, whether in submission or independence, whether bodily or mental, whether they are truly established according to God. I mean that whenever we engage in any endeavour, especially the beginners, if we do not gain more humility in our soul from its labour, it does not seem to me that we are accomplishing it according to God, whether it is small or great.

For us who are still immature, this is the assurance of the Lord's will; for those in the middle, perhaps the retreat of wars; and for the perfect, the addition and abundance of divine light. What is small for the great may not be small; what is great for the small may not be entirely perfect. The air, having been purified, is certainly deemed worthy of seeing divine light. Sin is one thing, laziness is another, negligence is another, desire is another, and falling is yet another. The one who is able to seek the Lord should do so clearly. Some consider the miraculous and visible gifts of the Spirit to be the greatest of all, not knowing that there are greater and more hidden things; therefore, they remain untouched. The one who is fully purified sees in himself or others what the soul is engaged in; the one who is still progressing judges this by the body. A small fire has often cleansed all material, just as a small hole has ruined all labour.

There is a rest for the enemy that lulls the power of the mind and does not arouse fervour; and there is great mortification that sometimes stirs the passions, so that "we might not rely on ourselves, but on Him who raises the dead" unknowingly. When we see some people loving the Lord, let us especially guard our boldness in them. For nothing so dissolves love as boldness and working hatred. The spiritual eye of the soul is very beautiful and transcends every concept along with the bodiless essences; thus, those who are passionate have often been able to perceive the thoughts in other souls out of great love for them, especially when they are not drowning in filth. If nothing in material nature stands against the incorporeal, let the reader understand. Observations in the world are in accord with God's providence, while within us, they are in accord with spiritual knowledge. Let those who are weak in soul, due to bodily circumstances, dangers, and external distractions, recognize the Lord's visitation toward them; and let the perfect recognize it by the presence of the Spirit and the increase of gifts.

There is a demon that comes to us when we lie down on the bed, shooting us with wicked and filthy thoughts, so that in our laziness, failing to arm ourselves against him with prayer, we fall asleep with filthy thoughts, and thus acquire filthy dreams. There is a forerunner of spirits, as it is called, who receives us immediately from sleep and defiles our first thought. Offer the first fruits of your day to the Lord. They will belong to Him who causes you to prosper.

An excellent worker once spoke a worthy word to me. He said to me, "From the very morning, I foresee the entire toil of the day." There are many paths to both piety and destruction; therefore, the one opposing another may often genuinely join with another, and the aim of both is pleasing to the Lord. The demons struggle with the temptations that happen to us, trying to make us say or do something improper. And when they fail, they quietly come and suggest to us a proud thanksgiving to God. Those who have set their minds on things above rise partially above; those who focus on things below, go down again. There is no middle ground left for those who separate. In some creatures, the existence is received from another and not from itself; and it is a wonder how it is sustained outside the being from which it received existence. The pious daughters are begotten by mothers, and the mothers by the Lord; and the opposite can be established by the same rule. The coward should not go out to battle, Moses—rather, God—commands, lest the final deception of the soul be worse than the first fall of the body, and rightly so. The visible eyes are the light of all bodily members, and the divine discernment is the light of all spiritual virtues.

On Clear Discernment

Just as the deer longs for streams of water, especially when aflame, so too do monks yearn for the understanding of the divine and good will, and also the knowledge of the mixed and contrary to it. This is indeed a profound and difficult matter for us to express, as it concerns the nature of things that are most certainly due to happen according to our needs, with the speed of all anticipation, as it is said, "He who postpones a day after day" and "a time for every matter under heaven." But what should happen with gentleness and care, as is advised, "War must be conducted with guidance" and again: "Let all things be done decently and in order."

It is not possible, no, it is not possible to discern these subtle things clearly and quickly unless one is bearing the Spirit of God within themselves. This is why even the God-bearer, who possesses the Spirit speaking within him, often prays, sometimes saying, "Teach me to do Your will, for You are my God," and sometimes, "Guide me in Your truth," and again, "Show me, Lord, the way in which I should walk, for I have lifted up my soul to You from the toil of worldly concern and passion."

All who desire to learn the will of the Lord must be prepared to die for it. Therefore, after praying with faith and pure simplicity, and consulting the souls of fathers or even brothers with humility and an unwavering heart, let them accept their advice as if it were from the mouth of God, even if the advice given is contrary to their own intention. For it is not possible for God to deceive souls that, in faith and simplicity, have subjected themselves to the counsel and judgment of their neighbor, even if those consulted are not particularly spiritual. For God is not unjust to allow souls that have humbled themselves to the advice and judgment of others to be led astray, even if those consulted happen to be irrational. The one speaking is unseen and invisible.

Those who live by the previously mentioned rule of ours are filled with great humility. If someone opened their problem in a psalter, how much more is the logical mind and spiritual soul superior to the lifeless saying? Many have not yet attained the perfect and light good mentioned above out of self-complacency, but by trying to comprehend the will of the Lord from within themselves, have devised many and very diverse judgments regarding it. Some of the disputants have abandoned all effort, allowing their thoughts to dominate them over the conflicting inclinations of the soul—the one that strives and the one that contradicts—and, after presenting their mind, stripped of their own will, to the Lord in fervent prayer for specific days, they have achieved the knowledge of His will: either by receiving a noetic communication from a spiritual mind to their own mind, or by the complete disappearance of one of the two inclinations of the soul.

Others, by the distress and confusion that followed their attempt, recognized that this was divine, according to the one who said, "We wanted to come to you—indeed, I, Paul, wanted to come again and again—but Satan hindered us." Still others, on the contrary, recognized that it was pleasing to God by the unexpected cooperation in the matter, saying, "And we know that in all things God works for the good of those who love Him, who have been called according to His purpose."

The one who has acquired God within himself through illumination does not require assurance in urgent or uncertain matters, neither through time nor by reason. To hesitate in judgments and to remain uncertain for a long time is a sign of an unilluminated and vainglorious soul. God is not unjust to close the door to those who knock with humility. The purpose is always sought from the Lord, both in urgent matters and in those that can be postponed. For everything done purely out of desire for God and free from all pollution, even if not entirely good, will still be counted as good for us. For seeking for oneself does not have a risk-free end. The judgment of the Lord concerning us is beyond understanding. Often, God wills to hide His will from us for a purpose, knowing that we might disobey even after learning it and thereby receive more wounds.

A heart immediately freed from various concerns, sailing safely in the vessel of innocence, is truly blessed.

There are brave souls who, with love and humility of heart, undertake works beyond their strength, and there are proud hearts that do the same. Often, the enemy’s goal is to suggest things beyond our power so that, failing in those, we might also fail in what is within our power, making ourselves a great laughingstock to our enemies. I have seen weak souls and bodies, burdened by many failures, attempting tasks beyond their strength and failing, to whom I have suggested that God judges repentance more by the measure of humility than by labours. Sometimes the upbringing of extreme evils is the cause, sometimes bad company, but often the perverse soul is self-sufficient for its own ruin. He who separates from the two may also be freed from the third. But he who holds onto the third is disapproved everywhere, for no place is safer than heaven.

In dealings with those who fight us wickedly, whether unbelievers or those of weak faith, let us stop after the first and second admonition. But in dealing with those who desire to learn the truth, let us not grow weary in doing good until the end. Nevertheless, let us use these experiences to strengthen our hearts in both cases.

It is utterly unreasonable to begin hearing holy things while still in the state of sin and to object to oneself. It is better that two teach you well than that you turn towards a greater understanding of yourself and the revelation of the weakness that is in you through holy humility. There are unclean demons more wicked than others, who do not suggest to us that we sin alone but encourage us to involve others in evil, so that our punishment may be all the more severe. I have seen a habit of one person learning evil from another; but when the one who taught came to a realization and began to repent, they ceased from evil. However, due to the continued practice of the student, the repentance of the teacher became ineffective. Truly, the wickedness of spirits is great and difficult to understand, and visible to only a few; I believe not even to all.

How, then, do we stay awake soberly while indulging and being filled, and how, after fasting and labouring, are we miserably overcome by sleep? Or how do we become hardened while in quietness and are moved to compunction when in company? When we are starving, we are tempted in sleep, but when we are filled, we remain untempted. In deprivation, we become gloomy and without compunction, while in indulgence, we become cheerful and full of compunction. Whoever can enlighten the unenlightened in the Lord should do so, for we are unenlightened in such matters, though we say that not all such changes are caused by demons. Sometimes it is due to the constitution given to us, bound to us by a thick, impure, and gluttonous disposition, how I do not know.

Let us earnestly and humbly ask the Lord concerning the difficulty in discerning such occurrences, and if after prayer and during the time of supplication we find that the occurrence still takes place, we will know that it is not caused by demons, but by nature. Often, divine economy desires to benefit us through opposites, restraining our arrogance by all means. It is difficult to investigate the abyss of judgments; for those who delve into it sail on a sea of conceit.

Someone once asked a person of insight, why did God, knowing the faults of some, adorn them with gifts and wonders? And he replied, "So that He may also secure other spiritual ones and show the freedom of the will and leave the fallen without excuse at judgment."

The law, as incomplete, says, "Watch yourself." But the Lord, as perfect, also commands us to correct our brother, saying, "If your brother sins against you," and so on. If your reproof is pure and humble, rather a reminder, do not hesitate to do the Lord's will, especially to those who are receptive. But if you have not yet attained this, then still practice the legal service. Do not be amazed when you see your beloved ones turning against you, for the lighter are tools of demons, especially against their enemies. I am exceedingly astonished by one thing among us: how, with the Almighty God, angels, and saints assisting us in virtues, we, on the contrary, bend more easily and eagerly toward passions, even with only one wicked demon opposing us. I cannot, nor do I wish, to speak precisely about this matter. All that has come to be, if it exists according to its nature, how is it that I am the image of God and yet mixed with clay, as the great Gregory says? If anything among the created things has been established contrary to what it was made, it must inevitably yearn insatiably for its own kind.

Let everyone use whatever means they have, as it were, so that they may bring the clay to the throne of God and enthrone it there.

Therefore, let no one make excuses regarding the ascent: the way and the door have been opened. The hearing of the spiritual accomplishments of the fathers awakens the mind and soul to zeal. The teaching of the hearing is a lamp in darkness, a return for the strayed, a light for the shortsighted. Discernment is the discoverer of health and the remover of disease. All those who are called little-wonderful by two ways are naturally affected by this: either because of extreme ignorance or by magnifying and exalting the works of others for the sake of humility. Let us strive not only to wrestle but to wage war against the demons. For the wrestler sometimes strikes and sometimes is struck, but the warrior always pursues the enemy. He who has conquered the passions wounds the demons, pretending to have the passions, thereby deceiving his enemies and remaining unwounded by them.

Once a brother was dishonoured and, without being moved at all in his heart, prayed mentally and then began to weep over the dishonour he received, hiding his dispassion by feigned passion. Another brother, entirely without desire for pre-eminence, pretended to grieve over the one who laboured for it. How then can I describe the chastity of the one who, seeming to enter a brothel for the sake of sin, actually led the prostitute to asceticism? Or again, to whom among the quiet ones a cluster of grapes was brought early: but after the one who brought it left, he ate it with a sudden rush, showing himself gluttonous to the demons? Another brother, having lost a small branch, spent the entire day lamenting his loss, showing much need for sobriety in such matters, lest they attempt to mock and be mocked. These are truly those of whom someone said, "They are deceivers, yet they are truthful."

If someone wishes to present a pure body to the Lord and show Him a clean heart, let them keep freedom from anger and self-control. Without these, all our labour is in vain. Just as different are the lights of the eyes, so many and different are the overshadowing’s of the intellectual sun in the soul. One is through bodily tears, another through spiritual tears; one through the physical eyes, another through the noetic eyes; one from hearing a word, another is the joy stirred uniquely within the soul; one from stillness, another from obedience. Above all, there is one overshadowing that, through ecstasy, presents the mind in the light of Christ unspeakably and ineffably.

There are virtues, and there are mothers of virtues. Therefore, the prudent one strives more to acquire the mothers, of which God Himself is the teacher in His own work. Many are the daughters, but God Himself is the teacher of the mothers. Let us be careful not to make up for the lack of indulgence through excessive sleep, for this is the work of fools, as is the opposite. I have seen workers, under certain circumstances, indulging their stomachs slightly. However, the brave ones tormented their wretched bodies through all-night standing and trained themselves to turn away from satiety with joy in the future.

The demon of avarice struggles fiercely with the destitute; when he does not prevail, he provokes the poor to take advantage of the opportunity, persuading the bodiless to become material again. Let us not stop considering the command of the Lord to Peter: forgive seventy times seven the one who sins. For He who commanded another to do this would certainly do much more Himself. But if we are lifted up, let us not cease to consider the entire statement of the one who said it.

Whoever fulfills the entire spiritual law but stumbles in one passion, namely, pride, has become guilty of all. There are certain situations of spiritual wickedness and envy that, involuntarily withdrawing from the saints, do so to prevent the persecuted from becoming victorious in their battles and thus being crowned. Blessed are the peacemakers, and no one contradicts. But I have also seen the peacemakers as blessed: two people had a sinful relationship, and a wise and proven man of great discernment became the instigator of enmity between them, speaking ill of each to the other until he succeeded in breaking up the relationship. He managed to defeat the malice of the demons with human cunning and ended their sinful connection.

There is one who stumbles over a commandment by the commandment itself. I have seen youths who adhered to God’s commandments yet, because of harming others and their conscience, distanced themselves from each other for a time. Just as marriage and a funeral are contrary, so are pride and despair. Both can be seen together, often due to demonic instability. Some unclean demons interpret the Scriptures for us, especially in the hearts of the vainglorious, particularly those educated in external learning, leading them gradually into heresies and blasphemies. We will recognize the theology of the demons—rather, their blasphemy—by the turmoil and unseemly joy mixed with shame during their teaching.

Every created thing has its order, beginning, and end from its Maker, but virtue has an endless goal. As the hymnographer says, "I have seen the limit of all perfection, but Your commandment is exceedingly broad." If some good workers go from the power of practice to the power of contemplation, and love never ends, "the Lord will guard the entrance of your fear and the exit of your love." Therefore, the end of this virtue is endless, and we will never reach it as we progress, not in the present age nor in the age to come, as we receive light upon light of knowledge. Even if this seems strange to many, nevertheless, according to the proof given to us, O blessed one, for I do not think that even the noetic beings are without progress; rather, I would say that they constantly receive glory upon glory and knowledge upon knowledge.

Do not be surprised if the demons often suggest good thoughts to us and even resist them intellectually, for the enemy's goal in this is to convince us that they know our innermost thoughts. Do not be a harsh judge of those who teach great things in word but are slower in action, for often the lack of deeds is made up for by the benefit of the word. Not all of us possess everything equally. In some, the word excels in action; in others, the latter surpasses the former. God did not create or make evil. Some were deceived by saying that some passions in the soul are natural, not realizing that we have turned the attributes of nature into passions. For example, the natural seed within us for procreation, we have turned into fornication. The natural anger within us against the serpent, we have used against our neighbor. Zeal within us is natural for pursuing virtues, but we use it for evil. The natural desire of the soul for glory, but for the glory above; pride, but against demons. Similarly, joy, but for the Lord and the well-being of our neighbor, and resentment, but against the enemies of the soul. We have received a desire for food, but not for gluttony.

An unyielding soul stirs up demons against itself, and as wars increase, so do the crowns. He who is not struck by enemies will not be crowned. He who does not grow weary from falls will be praised as a warrior by the angels. Some spend three nights on earth and are resurrected completely; others, having overcome in three hours, will not die afterward. If, according to a certain economy, after the sun has risen within us, "the sun knows its setting," and there was night, then the wild lions, and all the beasts of the forest of thorny passions, will come to us again, roaring to snatch the hope within us, seeking from God the food of our passions, whether through thought or action.

The sun will rise again for us through dark humility, and the beasts will gather to themselves and go into their dens, that is, into sensual hearts, and not in us. Then they will say among the demons, "The Lord has done great things with us, and we were glad." But you, being pursued, "Behold, the Lord sits on a light cloud, certainly a soul raised from all earthly desires, He will come to Egypt, the heart that was once darkened, and the handmade idols," and the thoughts of the mind will shake.

If Christ flees physically from Herod, though He is all-powerful, let the rash be taught not to throw themselves into temptations: "Do not let your foot slip, and He who keeps you will not slumber," says the angel. Pride is intertwined with courage, as the smilax with the cypress. Let our work be ceaseless, not considering that we possess any good by mere thought, but let us carefully examine whether we indeed have this characteristic within us. And then we will surely recognize our own shortcomings; seek unceasingly the signs of the passions, and you will then realize many things present in you that you could not recognize in sickness, either due to weakness or deep preconception.

God judges the intention. He looks mercifully upon those who strive according to their ability. Great is the one who does not fall short in anything within their ability, but greater is the one who undertakes tasks beyond their ability with humility. Often, demons prevent us from doing lighter and more beneficial things, urging us instead to undertake more wicked ones. I find that Joseph is blessed for turning away from sin, not for showing dispassion. We must examine in what and how many sins turning away receives a crown. It is one thing to turn away from the shadow, but higher is to run toward the sun. Darkness is the cause of stumbling, stumbling of falling, and falling of dying. Those darkened by wine have often washed themselves with water; those darkened by passions have washed themselves with tears. Confusion is different from scattering, and blindness is different. Temperance heals the first, stillness the second, obedience the third, and God, being obedient, heals.

We, from two descending forces, wisely separate the two ascending forces, as we have understood from examples. The common life according to the Lord is like a fuller’s shop, scraping away the filth, thickness, and deformity of the soul. But the retreat would be a dyeing shop for those who have laid aside lust, resentment, and anger and, from then on, go on to stillness. Some say that repeatedly falling into the same sins is due to the lack of appropriate and corresponding repentance for the first fall. We must examine whether every person who has not fallen into their own kind has truly repented. Some fall into the same sins, either thinking that God is merciful because of their indulgence or excusing themselves from their own salvation. I do not know if anyone has ever failed to blame themselves, for I would say that the inability to bind this enemy due to the strength of the habit that tyrannizes them.

We must investigate how the soul, though bodiless, is unable to see those of the same nature who dwell within it, perhaps because of the bond that only the one who bound it knows. To one who wished to learn, I say: Tell me, tell me, what someone of understanding inquired of me: what kind of spirits humble, and what kind exalt the mind over sins? And as I hesitated in my response, confirming my ignorance with an oath, the one asking taught me: "In a few words," he said, "I will leave you a seed of discernment to seek with labour: the spirits of the body, of anger, of gluttony; those of despondency, of sleep do not naturally raise the horn of the mind. But the spirit of avarice, of ambition, and of much talking, and many others, are accustomed to adding evil to evil. Therefore, the one who judges is similar to these."

If someone is present with worldly people or has accepted them, and after separating from them later, receives an arrow of sorrow instead of joy as if freed from an impediment, that person is mocked either by vainglory or by lust. Let us seek above all else from whence the wind blows: lest we be found setting our sails against it. Out of love, invite practical elders who have worn out their bodies in asceticism, offering them a little rest. Urge the young to exercise self-control, especially those who have worn out their souls in sin, by reminding them of the memory of punishment. It is not possible, as we have said elsewhere, to immediately cleanse ourselves entirely from gluttony and vainglory. But let us not seek to fight vainglory through indulgence; for gluttony begets vainglory, as I say to beginners. Instead, let us intercede against it through deprivation. The time will come, and now is for those who wish, for the Lord to also place this under our feet.

Young and old are not attacked by the same passions when approaching them. Often, they have completely opposite ailments. Therefore, blessed is holy humility, for it becomes a safe and powerful repentance for both the young and the old. Let us not be disturbed when saying, "I come." Few, however, are straightforward and free from evil, malice, and hypocrisy, whom all human association contradicts, yet they can ascend to heaven from stillness as from a harbor with their guide, and remain undisturbed and untainted by the disturbances and scandals of community life. Humans heal the lustful, angels the wicked, and God heals the proud.

Sometimes love has even appeared to be this: to leave the neighbor who insists on doing to us as they wish in everything, while still displaying all kindness. We must ask how and to what extent, and whether repentance for good deeds is as dissolving as for evil. Let us use great discernment in determining when to stand firm and how long we should contend with the materials of the passions. For it is sometimes better to flee, recognizing our weakness, so that we do not die. Let us see and observe (perhaps in time of bitterness, we can purge bile), which demons exalt, which humble, which harden, which pretend to enlighten, and which make us sluggish, malevolent, sullen, or cheerful. Let us not be amazed by the way of our life in the world. For all causes must be moved. Only then will we gain health, even if the hidden beasts are not yet visible.

When those close to perfection fall to the demons in one thing, they use every means to quickly extract a hundredfold from them. Just as the winds sometimes disturb the surface due to calm, and sometimes the depths of the sea, so consider the dark winds: the passionate ones disturb the very sense of the heart, but the more advanced disturb only the surface of the mind. Therefore, these more quickly return to their own calm, remaining undefiled.

It is the mark of the perfect always to recognize within the soul what is of the conscience, what is of God, and what is of the demons. For the demons do not always suggest contrary things from the beginning. Therefore, the problem is indeed dark.

The body is illuminated by two physical eyes, and the eyes of the heart are brightened by both noetic and visible discernment.

Step 27 On Holy Solitude of Body and Soul

We, as those who are always bought and sold by unholy passions and have become slaves to them, have thus come to understand to some extent the deceit, methods, commands, and cunning of the spirits that have ruled over our wretched souls. Others, however, have gained knowledge through the work of the Holy Spirit, having been freed from these spirits, and enlightened to their schemes. For one may understand the peace that comes from health while suffering in sickness, and another, while in the joy of health, may comprehend the distress that sickness brings.

Therefore, as weak ones, we now desire to philosophize with you about the haven of quietude, knowing that it is fitting for someone to present bread, or rather the soul, at the table of this blessed company, just as dogs snatch up bread. And such a one, taking it in his mouth, runs off and consumes it in silence. Thus, to avoid giving an opportunity to this dog through our discourse, and to avoid providing an excuse to those who seek it, we deemed it inappropriate to speak of peace to those who are now fighting as living warriors of our King. But we do say this: those who fight valiantly are crowned with the wreaths of peace and calm.

Now, in the form of discernment, let us speak briefly, as much as it does not offend anyone, leaving the discussion of these matters incomplete.

Quietude of the body is the mastery and stabilization of the passions and senses; quietude of the soul is the mastery of thoughts and a state of unshaken contemplation. A friend of quietude is a brave and resolute thought, standing steadfastly at the door of the heart, either killing or driving away those who approach. The one who quiets in the heart through spiritual perception knows this well; but the one who is still a child, inexperienced and ignorant, does not know it.

A wise quietist does not need words, for he is enlightened by the works themselves. The beginning of quietude is to drive away disturbances, as they trouble the depths; the end of quietude is to no longer fear noises but to remain unmoved by them. The one who progresses in words without progressing in deeds is gentle, a house full of love, slow to speak, and unmoved by anger. The opposite is obvious: a quietist is one who strives to confine the immaterial within a material body, which is paradoxical. Just as a cat catches a mouse, so too the quietistic mind catches subtle thoughts.

Do not dismiss this example, and if you do not understand it, you have not yet known quietude. A solitary monk is not like another solitary monk; one monk, with much vigilance and an undistracted mind, receives help from another; another is assisted by an angel. Noetic powers cooperate and find a dwelling in a spiritually quiet person. On the contrary, I will remain silent about this: the depth of doctrines is deep, and the mind of the quietist jumps into them not without danger; it is not safe to swim with clothes on, nor to touch theology while having passions.

A cell is the enclosure of the body for the quietist, containing within it a house of knowledge. The one who suffers from a spiritual passion and attempts quietude is like one who jumps out of a ship into the sea and imagines he can reach land safely on a plank. Those who struggle with clay are not yet ready for quietude unless they have gained guidance; for the isolated one needs angelic strength.

I am speaking here about those who truly quiet the body and spirit. The quietist who becomes lax will speak lies, urging others to cease their quietude through riddles, while being unaware that he has become a demon to himself. I have seen quietists, whose burning desire for God has been fully satisfied through quietude, and they have kindled fire with fire, love with love, and desire with desire.

A quietist is an earthly angel, who, by the script of love and zeal, has freed his prayer from laziness and indifference. A quietist is one who exclaims with clarity, "My heart is ready, O God." A quietist is the one who says, "I sleep, but my heart keeps watch." Close the door of your cell with your body, the door of your mouth with silence, and the inner gate with your spirit. Calmness in the noonday sun tests the endurance of a sailor; the lack of necessities reveals the perseverance of the quietist. The one who despairs is overwhelmed by water; the one who is negligent is entangled in a multitude of distractions. Do not fear the toys of noise, for fear does not know sorrow, nor is it startled.

Those whose minds have truly learned to pray speak to the Lord face to face, as if to the ear of the King. Those whose mouths pray fall down before the entire assembly of heaven. Those who remain in the world pray to the King in the midst of all the noise of the people. If you have thoroughly learned the art, you know what is being said. Sitting on high, keep watch, if indeed you know, and then you will see how, when, from where, and by how many, and by what kind of thieves, the grapes are taken.

When the watchman grows weary, he rises and prays, and then sits down again, bravely continuing his previous work.

Someone who had experience in these matters wanted to speak in detail and with precision, but he feared that he might cause laziness in the workers and frighten those who are willing by the striking force of his words. The one who explains quietude in a subtle and wise manner stirs up demons against himself, for no one else can triumph over their shamelessness. The one who has attained quietude knows the depth of mysteries; he would not have come to this understanding without first enduring the noise of waves and the winds of spirits, perhaps even seeing and hearing and being sprinkled.

Paul confirms this when he says: "If he had not been caught up into paradise as if in quietude, he would not have been able to hear inexpressible words." The ear of quietude will receive extraordinary things from God. Thus, in the Book of Job, the all-wise one says, "Will not my ear receive extraordinary things from Him?"

A quietist is one who flees without hesitation, while another runs towards God with eagerness, not desiring to gain sweetness from God through laxity. Go and scatter your possessions quickly. For the command, "Sell your possessions and give to the poor," requires time, so that through prayer you may come to quietude. Take up your cross, bearing this through obedience, and enduring the burden of cutting off your own will with strength. Then come, follow me into the blessed quietude, and I will teach you the visible work and life of the noetic powers, which will never be satisfied in praising the Creator for ages upon ages, nor will the one who has entered into the quietude of heaven cease from glorifying the Creator.

The bodiless do not concern themselves with material things, nor do the bodied immaterial ones concern themselves with food. The former will not perceive hunger, nor will the latter need sustenance. They do not care for money or possessions, nor do they concern themselves with the harm of spirits. The higher beings have no desire for visible creation, nor do those below have any longing for the sight of visible things. The former will never cease advancing in love, nor will the latter ever stop striving to match them.

Among those above, the wealth of advancement is not unknown, nor is the desire for ascent among those below. They will not stop until they reach the Seraphim; nor will they cease until they become angels. Blessed is the one who hopes; thrice-blessed is the one who becomes an angel after understanding.

On the Difference and Discernment of Quietude

In all matters of knowledge and the states of understanding, differences in opinions and intentions are evident, and this is known to all. Not everything is perfect for everyone, either due to a lack of zeal or a lack of ability. Some approach this haven, or rather this ocean, or perhaps even its depths, due to the weakness of their mouth or body because of preconceptions. Others, weak in anger and unable to control it despite much effort, are pitiable. Others, sailing more by personal preference than by the guidance of habit. Still others are unable to resist the material enticements within the midst of material things. Some strive to become diligent through their seclusion; others, unknowingly, punish themselves beyond what is necessary for righteousness. Still others seek to acquire glory for themselves through it.

There are also others (if, when the Son of Man comes, He will find such on earth) who have united with this holy quietude through the enjoyment and thirst for the love and sweetness of God, not having done so until they have completely turned away from all indifference. For this union is considered as spiritual adultery when compared to the former state. According to the plain knowledge given to me, as a wise architect, I have laid the foundation of the ladder of ascent; let each one now examine on what step they stand—whether out of personal preference, for the glory of men, due to the weakness of the tongue, or because of uncontrollable anger, excessive attachment, or to make recompense, to become diligent, or to add fire to fire.

The last will be first, and the first will be last. The seven labours of the seventh day belong to this present age; some are acceptable, others are not. The eighth, however, obviously pertains to the age to come. Watch, O monk, as a lonely observer of wild beasts, for otherwise, you will not be able to set suitable traps if indifference has completely taken hold. The work is in vain if it has already departed; but if it still remains, I do not know how you will find peace. Why is it that there were not as many shining lights among the holy Tabennisiotes as there were among the Scetis monks? Let the one who understands reflect on this, for I am unable, or rather unwilling, to explain further. Some reduce their passions, some chant psalms and persevere in prayer; others, focusing on contemplation, live as in the way of the ladder.

Lazy souls who come to the communal life and take advantage of the material comforts due to their own laziness have ended up in complete ruin. Conversely, those who have shed their laziness through the companionship of others have not only improved among the less diligent but often even among the diligent. We should apply the same rule to quietude, recognizing that many have been accepted as proven through personal habit, but were later rejected due to their attachment to pleasures, while others, out of fear and concern for their judgment, have become diligent and fervent. No one troubled by anger, arrogance, hypocrisy, or resentment should dare to assume the path of quietude, lest they fall into a state of alienation and gain nothing but harm. But if one is free from these, they will know what is beneficial.

I think even those who practice quietude with words cannot fully grasp these signs, stages, and evidence: a mind that is unshaken, thoughts purified, a rapture towards the Lord, the presence of divine judgment, a hastening towards death, unquenchable prayer, inviolable watchfulness, the death of impurity, ignorance of attachment, the death of the world, fasting from gluttony, the foundation of theology, the source of discernment, tears that bind, the loss of verbosity, and similar things that contradict the multitude of distractions. To those who do not practice quietude with words, this condition is poverty of riches, an increase in anger, a storehouse of resentment, a decrease in love, a growth in pride, and I will remain silent about the rest.

Since the discussion has come to this point, it is necessary to address those in obedience, especially since this discourse largely concerns them. Those who are lawfully and purely united in this worldly obedience and beauty have these signs and are defined by the God-bearing Fathers, though they are perfected at their own time. However, we daily strive to add to this and make progress. The increase of introductory humility and the reduction of anger—how could these not be born as the bile is drained—the lack of confusion, the reception of love, the estrangement from passions, the liberation from hatred, the reduction of lust through rebuke, the ignorance of sloth, the reception of diligence, the love of compassion, the rejection of pride—all these are to be sought by all but found by few. This is an accomplishment, and without water, the name of the spring is meaningless.

Consider the following: a virgin who does not guard her bed defiles her body; similarly, a soul that does not guard its covenant defiles its spirit. To the former follow disgrace, hatred, whips, and separations—the most pitiful of all. To the latter, defilements, forgetfulness of death, insatiable greed, lack of self-control, vanity, insatiable sleep, hard-heartedness, insensitivity, a storehouse of thoughts, and an increase in consent, captivity of the heart, the work of turmoil, disobedience, contradiction, attachment, unbelief, doubt, verbosity, and the most severe of all, a heart without compunction, which leads to insensitivity—the mother of all spiritual death.

Those who practice quietude have five spirits; those who are obedient have three out of the eight wrestling with them. The one practicing quietude often suffers from sloth, wasting time for prayer and contemplation in battles against it. Once, when I was sitting in my cell, some men approached and praised me as a quietist, and the thought of sloth quickly crept in, followed by vanity, and I marvelled at how the demon of pride opposes all spirits. Watch closely the motions, and counter-motions, and turns of your spouse, considering how and where they incline. The one who has gained peace through the Holy Spirit is not ignorant of this vision. The work of quietude is preceded by complete detachment from all things, both reasonable and unreasonable.

The one who opens the door to these earlier things will certainly fall into the latter. The second step is unwavering prayer, and the third is inviolable work of the heart. It is impossible for one who has not learned the letters to study naturally from tablets; even more impossible is it for those who have not acquired the first two to practice quietude through words. Moving to the middle, I have entered the middle, and I was enlightening the thirsty, and behold, I was again among those things. Before the visible form appeared in him, I could not teach what it was, nor was the ruler allowing it; now I asked how it was. He said it in his own words, but not in these. I said, “What is the right stance and seat on the cause?” He replied, “It is impossible to be initiated into these things by hearing alone.” What the desire drew me towards, I asked to be brought near at that time; but he had not yet said that the time had come due to a lack of the fire of incorruptibility.

Whether these things are mixed with earth, I do not know; whether they are separate from it, I cannot say. It is difficult to shake off the noonday sleep, especially in the summer, for then perhaps the work of the hands is not so easily set aside. I recognized the demon of sloth working in advance and laying the groundwork for the demon of impurity, to strongly pull the body down and baptize it in sleep, working defilements in those practicing quietude. If you resist them strongly, they will certainly fight against you with great force, so that, gaining nothing, they will cease their struggles.

Nothing so demonstrates the defeat of the demons as showing them the difficulty of their war against us. Guard well what you have gathered in your progress, for when the door is opened, the locked-up birds will fly away. And then, we will gain nothing from quietude. A small hair can disturb the eye, and a small worry can ruin quietude.

Quietude is the absence of passionate thoughts and the rejection of rational worries. The one who has truly grasped quietude will not even care for his own flesh, for the one who promised is faithful. The one who wishes to present a pure mind to God and is distracted by worries is like one who strongly binds his feet and tries to walk quickly. Few are those who reach the height of worldly philosophy; but I say, even fewer are those who master the philosophy of true quietude. The one who has not yet known God is untested in quietude and exposed to many dangers. Quietude strangles the inexperienced; for they are unacquainted with the sweetness of God, spending time in captivity, thefts, sloth, and daydreaming.

The one who has grasped the beauty of prayer will flee the crowds like a wild donkey. For what, if not this, allows the wild donkey to be free from all human company? The one encircled by passions and spending time in idle talk in solitude, as a holy elder once spoke to me and taught, I refer to George the Arsilaite, whom even your honour surely knows. He once spoke of the uselessness of a soul that, having the elements within it, leads it towards quietude.

“I have observed,” he says, “that in the morning, the demons of vanity and desire are especially present; at noon, those of sloth, sorrow, and anger; in the evening, those of gluttony and the tyrants of the stomach.” It is better to be a poor subordinate than a distracted quietist. The one who practices quietude with words and does not see the benefit daily either does not practice quietude with words or is deceived by vanity.

Quietude is an uninterrupted service and presence before God. The memory of Jesus should be united with your breath, and then you will know the benefit of quietude. Self-will is the downfall of the obedient; for the quietist, it is the separation from prayer. If you rejoice in company in your cell, know that you are only at leisure, not serving God. The wronged widow should be your example in prayer; and the great and angelic quietist Arsenius should be your model for quietude. Remember in your solitude the life of this angelic quietist and see how often he dismissed those who approached him to avoid losing the greater benefit.

I have seen the demons often sending irrational hunters to those practicing quietude with words, trying to persuade them to hinder the workers, even through small things. Mark such as these, to whom this applies, and do not hesitate to rebuke the lazy with pious severity, for perhaps they will stop their pursuit out of sorrow. Watch that you do not grieve a soul in vain, especially one coming to draw water from you in thirst. In every lamp, the life of those practicing quietude, rather of those living monastically according to conscience and awareness, should be seen.

The one who runs after matters in words, and aphorisms, and thoughts, and movements, and all actions according to the Lord and for the Lord, works with awareness of the soul and in the presence of the Lord. But if he is stolen away, he is not yet living according to virtue. Someone might say, “I will open my case in the Psalter and my will because of the incompleteness of discernment.” But I will open my will through prayer, and from there, I will receive my confirmation. Faith is the wing of prayer; without it, it will return to my bosom. Faith is the unwavering stance of the soul, not shaken by any adversity. A believer is not one who thinks God can do anything but one who believes he will obtain everything. Faith is the mother of toil and a straight heart; for one makes one unshakable, and the other produces the mother of quietists, faith. If you do not believe, how can you find quietude? If bound in chains, he has given birth to the fear of the Lord. The first does not fear the court as much as the second fears the judgment seat.

You need much fear in quietude, for nothing can so effectively drive away sloth. The condemned man constantly watches when the judge will arrive in the prison; the true worker, when the taskmaster will come. The former is burdened with a load of sorrow; the latter, with a spring of tears. If you acquire the rod of patience, the dogs will sooner stop their insolence. Patience is the unbreakable toil of the soul, not shaken by any reasonable blows. The patient worker is unassailable, and even through falls, he gains victory. Patience is the cutting off of excuses and attention to oneself; the worker will receive it not so much for his own sustenance as for his patience. On the second will be placed a crown; on the first will come destruction.

The patient man is dead before his grave, making his cell his tomb. Patience has given birth to hope and sorrow; for without both, he is a slave to sloth.

The Christian wrestler must know which of the enemies to pursue from afar and which to wrestle with up close. Sometimes the wrestling has won the crown; sometimes the refusal has made one unworthy. These things cannot be taught through words, for not all are made the same, nor are we all the same. Watch the spirits vigilantly, for they are at war with you continuously, whether standing, moving, sitting, or reclining, praying, or sleeping.

Those who sit in the path of quietude: “I set the Lord always before me,” they hold this work within themselves. Not all the loaves of spiritual work are alike, even of the heavenly wheat. Others: “In your patience, possess your souls.” Others: “Be watchful and pray.” Others: “Prepare your works for your departure.” Others: “I was humbled, and He saved me.” Some say: “The sufferings of this present time are not worthy to be compared with the glory which shall be revealed.” Others always consider: “Lest he snatch them away, and there be none to deliver.” All run, but one receives the prize. The one who has advanced works tirelessly, not only when awake but even when sleeping. Hence, some even in their sleep disdain the approaching demons and admonish unchaste women on chastity.

Do not wait or prepare for appearances; the entire state of quietude is simple and unadorned. No one who wishes to build a tower and attempts to build a cell for quietude will not first sit down and count and examine, through prayer, whether he has the means for completion, lest after laying the foundation, he becomes a laughingstock to his enemies and a hindrance to other workers.

Examine the sweetness that comes, lest it be mixed with bitter enemies, or rather, with deceivers cunningly mixed. At night, devote most of your time to prayer and a little to psalmody; during the day, prepare yourself again for the task, for reading is not the least way to enlighten and gather the mind. For these are the words of the Holy Spirit, and those who engage with them are certainly regulated. Let the things you read as a worker be practical, for this work makes the reading of the rest superfluous. Seek more to be enlightened by labours than by tablets with words of wisdom. Do not engage in words foreign to spiritual power. Words of darkness darken the powerless. One cup often signifies many tastes of wine; likewise, one word of a quietist reveals to those able to taste it his entire inner work and state.

Keep your mind’s eye untroubled by vanity, for there is nothing more destructive in theft than this. Be frugal with your words, for many labours are quickly scattered. Practice an unembellished state, for nothing can defile quietude like curiosity. Provide for those who need both body and spirit, if they are wiser, let us display our philosophy through silence. But if they are brothers in state, let us open the door moderately. However, it is better to consider all as superior. Desiring to turn me away from physical work in the assemblies of the still immature, the one who bears the sand in his cloak at night restrained me.

As there are things contrary to the word concerning the doctrines of the Holy, Uncreated, and Adorable Trinity and the Incarnate Economy of the One of the Same Ever-Praised Trinity—there what is singular, here is plural—so also the things fitting for quietude and obedience differ. The divine apostle says, “Who has known the mind of the Lord?” But I say, who has known the mind of the quietist in body and spirit?

Power to the king is wealth and abundance; power to the quietist is abundance in prayer.

Step 28 On the Holy and Mother of Virtues, the Blessed Prayer, mother of virtues, and on the attitude of mind and body in prayer.

Prayer is, by its very essence, the union and communion of man and God; in its activity, it is the establishment of the world, reconciliation with God, the mother and daughter of tears, the atonement for sins, the bridge over temptations, the partition wall of sorrows, the destruction of wars, the work of angels, the nourishment of all incorporeal beings; it is the joy of the future, boundless activity, the source of virtues, the cause of gifts, the invisible progress, the nourishment of the soul, the enlightenment of the mind, the axe of despair, the proof of hope, the dissolution of sorrow, the wealth of monks, the treasure of the quiet, the reduction of anger, the mirror of progress, the manifestation of measures, the declaration of state, the predictor of future events, the sign of glory.

Prayer is, for one who truly prays, a tribunal and a judgment seat, and the throne of the Lord before the future throne: let us rise and listen to this holy queen of virtues crying out to us in a loud voice and saying: “Come to me, all who labour and are heavy laden, and I will give you rest. Take my yoke upon you, and you will find rest for your souls.” And healing for your wounds. For my yoke is kind, and it is a remedy for great sins.

All who prepare to stand before the King and God and converse with Him should not undertake this journey unprepared, lest, when He sees us from afar without arms and the garment of royal presence, He may order His servants and ministers to bind us and cast us away from His face, and our prayers will be torn asunder and returned to us. Let your soul’s garment be fully woven before the Lord, being entirely made of the thread, or rather the fabric, of forgiveness. Otherwise, you will gain nothing from prayer.

Let the foundation of your prayer be adorned with a single word, for in one word the publican and the prodigal reconciled with God: there is one presence for those who stand, but there is much diversity and variety in it: some approach as friends and lords, offering hymns and prayers on behalf of others, not for their own need; others seek wealth, glory, and more confidence; others ask to be freed completely from their adversary; some for complete freedom from debt; some for release from prison; others for the forgiveness of crimes.

Above all, in the scroll of our prayer, let us place sincere thanksgiving. The second line should be confession and contrition of soul in awareness; then let us make known our requests to the Almighty King. For the best method of prayer is that which was revealed to a brother by an angel of the Lord.

If you have ever stood before a visible judge, you will not need another example for your prayer. If not, and you have never stood or seen those examined, learn from the petition of the sick to the doctors when they are about to be cut or burned by them.

Do not be clever in the words of your prayer: for often the simple and inarticulate babblings of children have moved their Father, who is in heaven. Do not attempt to use many words, lest the mind be distracted in the search for words. A single word of the publican reconciled him with God; and a single faithful word saved the thief. Many words in prayer often scatter and diffuse the mind, while few words often gather and focus it.

If you find sweetness or piercing in a word of prayer, stay with it: for our guardian is the One who prays with us. Do not boast, even if you possess purity; approach with much humility, and you will gain more confidence; even if you have climbed the entire ladder of virtues, pray for the forgiveness of sins: hearing Paul cry out about sinners: "Of whom I am chief." Oil and salt are meant to prepare food, and prayer is winged by chastity and tears. If you clothe yourself in all meekness and freedom from anger, you will not have much trouble in freeing your mind from captivity.

Until we have acquired a clear prayer, we are like infants being trained to walk in the preliminaries. Endeavour to raise, or rather, to shut in the mind in the words of prayer; and if it falls due to its infancy, bring it back in again: for it is the nature of the mind to be unstable. However, the One who can make all things firm can also establish it.

If you have continually fought the good fight, the One who limits the sea of the mind will dwell in you and say to it in your prayer: “Thus far you shall come, and no further.” It is impossible to bind the spirit; but where the Creator of the spirit is, all is subject.

If you have ever seen the sun, you will be able to converse with Him appropriately; but if you have never seen Him, how can you truly approach Him? The beginning of prayer is the driving away of all thoughts, pursued by monologues from the very beginning. The middle is for the mind to be solely in the words or thoughts that are said. The end is the rapture towards the Lord.

There is one joy in prayer for those who live in communion, and another for those who pray in solitude. The former perhaps has a small manifestation; the latter is entirely filled with humility. If you always train your mind not to wander, even when you set the table, it will be close to you; but if it freely wanders, it will never remain with you.

The great worker of great and perfect prayer says: “I prefer to say five words with my mind,” and so on. Such a thing is alien to the infantile; therefore, we need the quality and the quantity of the quantity as those who are incomplete; for the latter brings forth the former: for he who prays purely to the one who prays without ceasing in a defiled and laborious manner gives a different defilement to prayer, another disappearance, another theft, and another blemish.

Defilement is to stand before God and imagine improper thoughts. Disappearance is to be captured by useless worries. Theft is for the thought to be carried away imperceptibly. A blemish is a harmful thought that approaches us at that time. If we happen to be alone at the time of prayer, let us inwardly stamp the form of supplication. If the servants of praise are not present, let us also shape the outward form for intercession. For often in incomplete things, the mind is conformed to the body; all, and especially those who go to receive the remission of their debt before the King, need immense contrition.

If we are still in prison, let us hear the One who said to Peter: "Gird yourself with the towel of obedience, and strip off your will, and come naked of these things to the Lord in your prayer, calling only upon His will, and then you will receive God, who holds the rudder of your soul and guides you safely.” Having risen from worldliness and love of pleasure, cast off your cares; strip off your thoughts, deny the body; for nothing else is prayer but alienation from the visible and invisible world.

What do I have in heaven? Nothing. And what did I desire on earth from You? Nothing, but to attach myself to You without distraction in prayer forever. For some, wealth, for others, glory, for others, possessions are the object of their love: "But for me, it is desirable to cling to God, and to place my hope of impassibility in Him." Faith has winged prayer; for without it, it cannot fly to heaven. Let us, the passionate, persistently ask the Lord: for all the passionate have progressed from passion to impassibility.

Even if the judge does not fear God, yet because of the trouble caused to him by sin, and because of the soul that has been deprived of Him due to sin and falls, he will make the avenging of it from the adversary's body and his hostile spirits. The good merchant of ours attracts the grateful by quickly granting their request to His love; but He prepares the ungrateful souls of dogs to sit beside Him through prayer by means of hunger and thirst for their request. For the ungrateful dog, having received bread, immediately withdraws from the giver.

Do not say, after spending a long time in supplication, that you have accomplished nothing: for you have already accomplished it. For what is a higher good than clinging to the Lord and persistently adhering to Him in union? The condemned man is not so afraid of the verdict of his punishment as the one who cares about prayer is of its presence. Therefore, if someone is wise and prudent, by remembering it, they can subdue all reviling, anger, care, busyness, grief, satiety, temptation, and thought.

Prepare in your soul a ninefold prayer before your supplication, and you will make progress swiftly. I have seen those who, in obedience, shone brightly and did not neglect the memory of God in their minds as much as they could, and they stood quickly in prayer and soon overcame their own mind, and tears flowed like streams. For they had been prepared by holy obedience.

To one who prays with many psalms, captivity and distractions accompany; to the one who prays alone, it is not so, but sloth wages war, and zeal assists. War has revealed the love of the one who fights towards the king; and the time and presence of prayer has proved the love of the monk towards

God; your own state, your prayer will reveal to you: for the theologians have called it the mirror of the monk; the one who engages in any work and, when the hour of prayer comes, continues to occupy himself with it, is mocked by the demons. For thieves aim to steal every hour from us.

Do not neglect to pray for your soul, even if you do not have prayer; for faith often saved the one who asked with contrition. Do not boast when you pray for others and are heard; for it was their faith that worked and prevailed. Every child will unceasingly seek daily the wisdom he learned from his teacher; every mind will require in every prayer the power he received from God; therefore, one must be vigilant when praying soberly, lest one be attacked by wrath. For this is the goal of our enemies: to assault every virtue, but more so prayer, and to pray with feeling when one has overcome anger.

Those who acquired the Lord no longer weave stories of their own in prayer: for then the Spirit intercedes for them with groanings that cannot be uttered. Do not accept any sensory fantasy in prayer, so as not to be subject to ecstasy: be free from doubt, an unshakable declaration of the unclear.

Be very merciful while caring for prayer: for monks will receive a hundredfold in it; and the rest will follow in its time. Fire that has come into the heart has kindled prayer; once this is awakened and ascended to heaven, there is a fiery descent into the upper chamber of the soul. Some say that prayer for the memory of departure is better; I, however, praise the two essences of one substance. A good horse, at the time of approach, is warmed up and is inclined to add to its course; I mean the hymnody, and the brave mind as a horse; from afar it smells the battle, and once prepared, it remains invincible for all.

It is difficult to snatch water from the mouth of the thirsty; more difficult still to tear away a soul praying with contrition before it has completed the much-desired prayer. Do not withdraw until you see the fire and the water have subsided economically; for you may not receive such a time for the forgiveness of sins in your whole life. He who has tasted of prayer, and has been defiled by a single word that came before it, stood in prayer and did not usually find what he desired. It is one thing to frequently inspect the heart; it is another to inspect the heart by the ruling mind and to offer logical sacrifices to Christ as a high priest: as one of those theologians said, the holy and heavenly fire descends and burns it up due to the lack of purification; but again it illuminates due to the measure of perfection: for the same fire is both consuming and illuminating, and is called light; therefore some come out of prayer as if from a fiery furnace, feeling relief from some defilement and material; others, as if illuminated by light, and clothed in a double garment of humility and joy. Those who depart from prayer bodily, outside these two energies, not to mention, prayed Judaically and not spiritually. If a body is altered by the touch of another body in energy, how much more will one be altered who touches the body of God with innocent hands?

It is possible to behold the earthly king and our all-good King, sometimes through oneself, sometimes through a friend. Another time through a servant; and there are times when He grants His gifts to His soldiers unknowingly: and this is also according to the garment of humility fitting us, as the one who stands before the earthly king and turns his own face away and converses with the enemies of the master, he is detested; thus the Lord detests the one who stands in prayer and receives impure thoughts. When the dog comes, drive him away with a weapon; and whenever he is shameless, do not yield to him; ask through mourning, seek through obedience, knock through patience; for not everyone who asks receives, and the one who seeks finds, and to the one who knocks, it will be opened.

Take care when you pray not to overdo your intercessions for the other sex, so as not to be tricked from the right side. Do not go into detail in confessing physical acts lest you become a traitor to yourself. Do not let the time of prayer be a time of reflecting on necessary and spiritual matters. If not, you will be robbed of the better; the one who continuously holds the staff of prayer will not stumble. Even if it happens, still, he will not fall to the end; for prayer is the devout tyranny of God, its benefit is proven by the demonic obstacles at the time of assembly. Its fruit is seen in the defeat of the enemy: “In this I knew that You delighted in me because my enemy did not rejoice over me in the time of war.” I cried out, says the psalmist, with all my heart: that is, with body, soul, and spirit. For where the two latter are gathered together, there God is in their midst.

Not everything is alike, either bodily or spiritually. For some, the short, for others, the longer in psalmody is fitting; some say they fight captivity, others ignorance. If you continually entreat the King against your enemies, when they come to you, be of good courage, you will not tire; for they will quickly depart from you on their own: for the ungodly do not want to see you crowned through prayer from your battle with them; they will flee from you as if scourged by fire through prayer. Acquire all courage, and you will have God as the teacher of your prayer.

It is not by seeing that one learns through words, for this follows by nature; nor is the beauty of prayer learned through another’s teaching; for it has God as its teacher, who teaches a person knowledge and gives prayer to the one who prays, and blesses the years of the righteous.

Step 29 On the Earthly Heaven of Godlike Dispassion, Perfection, and the Resurrection of the Soul Before the General Resurrection

Behold, even we, who are placed in the deep pit of ignorance and the dark passions, and in the shadow of death of this body, begin to philosophize about the earthly heaven with boldness. For just as the firmament has the beauty of the stars, so too does dispassion adorn the virtues. I have understood dispassion to be nothing other than a heavenly state of the heart, considering the deceit of demons as mere toys. Thus, one is truly and knowingly dispassionate who has made the flesh incorruptible, has elevated the mind above creation, and has subjected all senses to this mind. Presenting the soul before the face of the Lord, it is always stretching beyond its own strength toward Him.

Some define dispassion as the resurrection of the soul before the body; others as the perfect knowledge of God, second only to that of angels. This perfect state of the perfect is, as someone who has tasted it has guided me, an incomplete perfection. It sanctifies the mind and draws it away from material things, so much so that, after gaining entrance to the heavenly harbor, it raises the mind to contemplation, causing it to stand out in heaven despite still being in the body. The one who has perhaps experienced this says somewhere that the mighty of the earth are greatly exalted before God. Such was also the Egyptian who did not allow his hands to be stretched out long in prayer whenever he prayed with others.

There is one who is dispassionate, and there is one who is more dispassionate. One strongly hates evil, and the other abounds in virtues, and purity is called dispassion, and rightly so. For it is a prelude to the general resurrection and the incorruption of the perishable. Dispassion was shown by the one who said, "I possess the mind of the Lord." Dispassion was shown by the Egyptian who said not to fear the Lord. Dispassion was shown by the one who prayed to reverse the passions. Who, before the future brilliance, has been deemed worthy of dispassion like that Syrian? For David is famous among the prophets, saying to the Lord, "Allow me to refresh myself." But the athlete of God said, "Restrain the waves of Your grace." The soul possesses dispassion, being formed in virtues as the passionate are formed in pleasures. If there is a mountain of gluttony, which does not desire to be resisted, then surely this is a mountain of self-control, which controls even a naturally hungry nature. If there is a mountain of lust, which drives one mad even towards living and non-living things, then this is a mountain of purity, which perceives all as inanimate. If this is the limit of avarice, which never ceases gathering or being satisfied, then this is the limit of poverty, which does not even spare one's own body. If this is the end of indifference, which lacks patience in every circumstance, then this is patience, which considers relief even in affliction. If this is the sea of anger, which rages even when no one is present, then this is long-suffering, which remains calm even when insulted and in the presence of the one who insults. If this is the height of vainglory, which forms the works of vainglory even when no one is present to praise, then surely this is a form of humility, which never allows thoughts to be stolen in the presence of others. If this is the form of destruction, that is, pride, which exalts itself in humble appearance, then this is the saving proof of humility, which maintains a humble mind in lofty endeavours and accomplishments. And if this is the ultimate proof of passion, to yield swiftly to all suggestions sown by demons, then I have understood this as the mark of holy dispassion: to be able to say with clear conviction, "As the evil one departed from me, I did not recognize it," nor how it came, nor for what reason, nor how it departed, but I am entirely indifferent to such things, being wholly united with and always to be with God.

The one who has been deemed worthy of such a state, while still in the flesh, has God Himself always dwelling in him, governing all his words, deeds, and thoughts. Thus, he perceives inwardly, as if from the voice of the Lord's will through enlightenment, becoming higher than all human teaching: "When will I come and appear before the face of God?" For I no longer endure the operation of desire but seek the immortal beauty that You gave me before clay. The dispassionate, what need is there to say much? "He lives, yet not he, but Christ lives in him," as the one who fought the good fight, finished the course, and kept the orthodox faith has said. A king's diadem is not composed of a single stone, and dispassion is not perfected if we neglect even one of the virtues, however minor. Dispassion, you will understand, is the palace of the heavenly king in the heavens. You will dwell in many mansions within this city. The wall of Jerusalem is the forgiveness of sins. Let us run, brothers, to obtain entry into the bridal chamber of the palace. But if, due to some burden or previous sin, oh, what misfortune! Or if we have been overtaken by time, let us at least reach some mansion around the bridal chamber. And if we are still crouching or are found idle, let us by all means find ourselves within the wall. For the one who has not entered there before the end, but rather has not passed beyond, will dwell in the wilderness. For this reason, someone prayed, saying, "With my God, I will leap over the wall." And another, as if from the face of God, says, "Are not your sins what separate you between you and Me?" Let us break down, friends, the middle wall of separation that we have poorly built through disobedience. Let us here find the release of our debt, for in Hades, no one can heal. Let us be at leisure, brothers, for we have been registered as students. There is no fall, no time, no burden to excuse. For all who have received the Lord through the washing of regeneration, He gave them the power to become children of God, saying, "Be still, and know that I am God and dispassion." To Him be the glory and power forever. Amen.

Dispassion raises the poor mind from the earth to heaven and lifts the poor from the dung of passions. Blessed dispassion, and most praised love, make it worthy to sit with the princes, the holy angels, with the rulers of the people of the Lord.

Step 30 On the Bond of the Virtuous Trinity in Virtues

Now, after all that has been previously said, three things remain, which tighten and hold together the bond of everything: faith, hope, and love. But the greatest of all is love, for God is named after it. However, I see the ray, the light, and the circle—all as one radiance and one brightness. For love can do and create all things; it surrounds with the mercy of God and makes one unashamed. It does not fall, nor does it stand still; it does not let the one who has been wounded by it rest from the blessed madness. Whoever desires to speak about the love of God attempts to speak about God Himself. But to expound upon God is dangerous and risky for those who do not pay careful attention.

The discourse about love is familiar to angels, and to them, it is revealed according to the operation of illumination. God is love, and whoever wants to define this is measuring sand in an abyss while blinded.

Love, in its quality, is the likeness of God, as much as is attainable by mortals. In its operation, it is the intoxication of the soul; in its particularity, it is the fountain of faith, the abyss of patience, and the sea of humility. Love is mainly the casting off of every contrary thought, as love does not reckon evil. Love, dispassion, and adoption as sons differ only in name. Just as light, fire, and flame merge into one operation, so too should you think of these. To the extent that there is a lack, there is fear; for one filled with fear without love has either deadened the soul or remains lacking.

There is nothing difficult in transferring human images of desire, fear, zeal, servitude, and love of God. Blessed is the one who has acquired such love for God as a passionate lover has for his beloved. Blessed is the one who fears the Lord as much as condemned men fear their judge. Blessed is the one who becomes so earnest in true zeal as loyal servants are toward their master. Blessed is the one who is as zealous in virtues as those who vigilantly guard their own spouses. Blessed is the one who stands before the Lord in prayer as servants stand before their king. Blessed is the one who serves the Lord with unceasing devotion as a son of love clings to the Lord as closely as a mother does to her nursing child. He who truly loves will joyfully embrace such closeness. He can no longer rest even in sleep from this desire but continues to speak of it to the beloved. This is how it happens with both physical and spiritual matters.

Someone who was wounded once said something I marvel at: "I sleep because of the need of nature, but my heart keeps vigil because of the abundance of love." Note this well, you who believe: whenever the soul, as a deer besieged by these wild beasts, longs for and fails to reach the Lord, it burns with the fire of love as if struck by poison. The action of hunger is somewhat hidden and insignificant; but the action of thirst is intense and evident, signalling the burning within. For this reason, the one who longs for God says, "My soul thirsts for God, for the mighty, living God." If the face of a beloved one can clearly transform all of us, making us cheerful, bright, and free from sorrow, what will the face of the Master do when He invisibly makes His presence known in a pure soul?

When fear is felt in the soul, it dissolves and consumes impurity. "Nail my flesh with Your fear," it says. Holy love has often consumed some, as one said, "You have melted our hearts, You have melted us." But sometimes it illuminates and causes rejoicing. For it says, "My heart hoped and was helped, and my flesh revived," and when the heart rejoices, the face blossoms.

When a person is completely united with the love of God, then even while in the body, he shows the brightness of the soul as if through a mirror. This is how Moses, the God-seer, was glorified. Those who have reached such a state, often forget about physical food. I believe they no longer even desire it frequently. And it is no wonder, since contrary desires often override hunger. I think that these incorruptible ones no longer nourish their bodies by chance, for they have been purified and in some way rendered incorruptible through the flame of purity, which has quenched the flame. I believe that even the food they do consume is taken with less delight. For just as water nourishes a plant's roots, so does heavenly fire nourish the soul of these individuals. The growth of fear is the beginning of love; the end of purity is the foundation of theology. He who has completely united his senses with God is initiated into His words by Him; for without this union, it is difficult to speak about God. The indwelling word perfects purity, having by its presence conquered death. With death conquered, the disciple of theology is enlightened.

The word of the Lord, remaining pure from the Lord, endures forever. For the one who does not know God speaks speculatively; purity has made the disciple a theologian, strengthening through itself the doctrines of the Trinity of the three persons. The one who loves the Lord has first loved his brother. For the second proves the first. The one who loves his neighbor will never tolerate gossip about others; instead, he will avoid it as he would fire. The one who says he loves the Lord but is angry with his brother is like someone running in his sleep. The strength of love is hope, for through it, we await the reward of love. Hope is the wealth of unseen riches; hope is an unquestioning treasure before the treasure. It is the rest from labours; it is the door to love; it removes despair; it is the image of what is absent. The lack of hope is the loss of love. Hope is tied to toil; it hangs on labours; mercy surrounds it. A hopeful monk slays indifference, turning it back with the sword of hope. Hope is born from the experience of the Lord's gifts; for the inexperienced cannot remain without doubt. Anger destroys hope, for hope does not disappoint, but an angry man is not respectable.

Love is the source of prophecy; love produces miracles; love is an abyss of illumination. Love is a fountain of fire; as much as it overflows, so much it consumes the one who thirsts. Love is the state of angels; love is the progress of the ages. Tell us, you who are good in virtues, where do you pasture your sheep? Where do you dwell at midday? Enlighten us, quench our thirst, guide us, lead us, for we now desire to ascend to you. You reign over all things. Now, my soul is overwhelmed, and I cannot contain your flame; therefore, I sing to you and proceed: You reign over the power of the sea; you calm the raging waves; you humble the proud thoughts of the arrogant. With the arm of your strength, you scatter your enemies, and you render your lovers invincible. How did Jacob see you standing on the ladder? I am eager to learn. What is the form of such an ascent? Tell your lover: What is the method and the steps of that ladder? What ascents did your lover arrange in his heart? What is their number? I thirst to know. What is the duration of the journey? For the one who learned your struggle and vision has told us the guides; nothing else is illuminated, or rather, it could be more personal, if I should speak more intimately. The queen who appeared to me as if from heaven said, "If you are not freed from the thickness, O lover, you cannot understand my time as it is." Let the ladder teach you the spiritual composition of the virtues; on it, I am supported by the peak, as my great mystic said. Now these three remain: faith, hope, and love, but the greatest of these is love.

A brief and equivalent exhortation to what has been spoken of through many journeys.

Ascend, ascend, putting ascents in your heart eagerly, brothers, hearing the one who says: Come, let us ascend to the mountain of the Lord and to the house of our God, who makes our feet like those of deer and sets us on high places, to overcome on His path. Run, I urge you, with the one who says: Let us strive until we all reach unity in the faith and the knowledge of the Son of God, to a perfect man, to the measure of the stature of the fullness of Christ, who at thirty years of age in visible maturity was baptized and fulfilled the thirtieth step on the spiritual ladder; if love is God, to Him be praise, power, and strength, to Him who is the cause of all good things, who was, and is, and will be forever.
